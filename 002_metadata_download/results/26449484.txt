Recent evidence points to the epigenetic and chromatin states of the genome as having important roles.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>Methods</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">We genotyped 355 healthy young individuals for 52 known obesity-associated SNPs and obtained DNA methylation levels in their blood using the Illumina 450 K BeadChip. Associations between alleles and methylation at proximal cytosine residues were tested using a linear model adjusted for age, sex, weight category, and a proxy for blood cell type counts. For replication in other tissues, we used two open-access datasets (skin fibroblasts, <em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;62; four brain regions, <em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;121&#x02013;133) and an additional dataset in subcutaneous and visceral fat (<em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;149).</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">We found that alleles at 28 of these obesity-associated SNPs associate with methylation levels at 107 proximal CpG sites. Out of 107 CpG sites, 38 are located in gene promoters, including genes strongly implicated in obesity (<em>MIR148A</em>, <em>BDNF</em>, <em>PTPMT1</em>, <em>NR1H3</em>, <em>MGAT1</em>, <em>SCGB3A1</em>, <em>HOXC12</em>, <em>PMAIP1</em>, <em>PSIP1</em>, <em>RPS10-NUDT3</em>, <em>RPS10</em>, <em>SKOR1</em>, <em>MAP2K5</em>, <em>SIX5</em>, <em>AGRN</em>, <em>IMMP1L</em>, <em>ELP4</em>, <em>ITIH4</em>, <em>SEMA3G</em>, <em>POMC</em>, <em>ADCY3</em>, <em>SSPN</em>, <em>LGR4</em>, <em>TUFM</em>, <em>MIR4721</em>, <em>SULT1A1</em>, <em>SULT1A2</em>, <em>APOBR</em>, <em>CLN3</em>, <em>SPNS1</em>, <em>SH2B1</em>, <em>ATXN2L</em>, and <em>IL27</em>). Interestingly, the associated SNPs are in known eQTLs for some of these genes. We also found that the 107 CpGs are enriched in enhancers in peripheral blood mononuclear cells. Finally, our results indicate that some of these associations are not blood-specific as we successfully replicated four associations in skin fibroblasts.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h2>Conclusions</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">Our results strongly suggest that many obesity-associated SNPs are associated with proximal gene regulation, which was reflected by association of obesity risk allele genotypes with differential DNA methylation. This study highlights the importance of DNA methylation and other chromatin marks as a way to understand the molecular basis of genetic variants associated with human diseases and traits.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Electronic supplementary material</h2><p id="__p5" class="p p-first-last">The online version of this article (doi:10.1186/s13073-015-0225-4) contains supplementary material, which is available to authorized users.</p></div></div></div><div id="Sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec1title">Background</h2><p id="__p6" class="p p-first">Genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have identified a plethora of common genetic variants that are associated with obesity-associated traits (e.g., body mass index (BMI) [<a href="#CR1" rid="CR1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701426">1</a>&#x02013;<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701472">11</a>], fat mass [<a href="#CR12" rid="CR12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701477">12</a>, <a href="#CR13" rid="CR13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701417">13</a>], low lean body mass [<a href="#CR14" rid="CR14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701433">14</a>], blood lipid levels [<a href="#CR15" rid="CR15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701411">15</a>], waist circumference [<a href="#CR13" rid="CR13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701430">13</a>, <a href="#CR16" rid="CR16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701507">16</a>], BMI-adjusted waist-to-hip ratio [<a href="#CR17" rid="CR17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701508">17</a>, <a href="#CR18" rid="CR18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701476">18</a>]). Some of these single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) are located near genes whose role in obesity is well established, such as <em>MC4R</em> [<a href="#CR19" rid="CR19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701504">19</a>]. However, most of these SNPs are located near genes whose role in obesity is still unclear, and the mechanisms through which they act remain unknown. Part of this lack of understanding may be due to a focus on the genes in closest proximity to these SNPs. Actually, these SNPs may regulate genes that are located quite far away, as recently demonstrated for genetic variants within <em>FTO</em>. In human brains, obesity-associated SNPs in <em>FTO</em> were found to be associated with expression of <em>IRX3</em>, a gene located more than half a million base pairs downstream of the body mass-associated genetic locus [<a href="#CR20" rid="CR20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701503">20</a>]. Another instance is the rs4537545 SNP previously associated with coronary heart disease [<a href="#CR21" rid="CR21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701447">21</a>] and located within <em>IL6R</em>: this SNP was recently found to be associated with blood mRNA levels of <em>ATP8B2</em>, a gene located 115 kb away [<a href="#CR22" rid="CR22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701458">22</a>]. Thus, obesity-associated SNPs might act through long-range interactions (for example, by disrupting enhancers) and potentially through epigenetic mechanisms.</p><p id="__p7">The epigenome represents the pattern of chemical and structural modifications to DNA that are heritable through mitosis and/or meiosis, but that do not entail changes in DNA sequence. Epigenetic mechanisms encompass DNA methylation, histone modifications, and non-coding RNAs, and have the potential to modify gene expression. Recent attention has been drawn to the possible role of epigenetics in the pathogenesis of obesity [<a href="#CR23" rid="CR23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701518">23</a>, <a href="#CR24" rid="CR24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701435">24</a>]. Moreover, while the epigenome is known to be modulated by the environment, this modulation can also be affected by genetic variants. Studies in brain [<a href="#CR25" rid="CR25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701465">25</a>&#x02013;<a href="#CR28" rid="CR28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701415">28</a>], adipose tissue [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701478">29</a>, <a href="#CR30" rid="CR30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701483">30</a>], blood [<a href="#CR26" rid="CR26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701502">26</a>, <a href="#CR31" rid="CR31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701515">31</a>, <a href="#CR32" rid="CR32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701450">32</a>], lung [<a href="#CR33" rid="CR33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701487">33</a>], fibroblasts [<a href="#CR34" rid="CR34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701414">34</a>, <a href="#CR35" rid="CR35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701460">35</a>], T cells [<a href="#CR35" rid="CR35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701514">35</a>], leukocytes [<a href="#CR36" rid="CR36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701444">36</a>], and lymphoblastoid cells [<a href="#CR35" rid="CR35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701420">35</a>, <a href="#CR37" rid="CR37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701448">37</a>] have shown that the genome contains quantitative trait loci (QTLs) for DNA methylation, also called methylation QTLs (meQTLs). DNA methylation levels correlate with the presence of specific alleles at nearby SNPs, and meQTLs tend to locate outside of promoters, especially in intergenic regions. In a study conducted in adipose tissue [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701488">29</a>], meQTLs overlapping metabolic disease loci were enriched in histone marks predictive of genetic enhancers. Interestingly, top associations from a GWAS of bipolar disorder were enriched in meQTLs [<a href="#CR38" rid="CR38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701506">38</a>], suggesting that this could be a powerful approach to better understand the molecular basis of candidate SNPs from GWASs.</p><p id="__p8" class="p p-last">In the present study, we tested associations between 52 SNPs that were previously identified in GWASs or meta-analyses to be associated with obesity traits, and proximal DNA methylation in whole blood of 355 healthy young individuals. We then tested the tissue specificity of the majority of these associations in four brain regions (<em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;121&#x02013;133), visceral adipose tissue (VAT; <em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;149), subcutaneous adipose tissue (SAT; <em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;149) and fibroblasts (<em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;62). Finally, the genomic context of associated CpG sites was explored, using chromatin segmentation on publicly available histone marks from 11 tissues and long-range interactions from five cell lines.</p></div><div id="Sec2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec2title">Methods</h2><div id="Sec3" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Discovery study group</h3><div id="Sec4" class="sec sec-first"><h4>Ethics, consent and permissions</h4><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">The discovery study group comprised two sub-groups of healthy young Caucasians from two different age ranges (Table&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4599317/table/Tab1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Tab1" rid-ob="ob-Tab1" co-legend-rid=""><span>1</span></a>). Two 6-ml blood samples were drawn for genotyping and DNA methylation measurement, at any time during the day. This chip has been shown to give a reliable and reproducible estimation of the methylation profile on a genomic scale [<a href="#CR15" rid="CR15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701451">15</a>]. First, bisulfite conversion of genomic DNA was performed using the EZ DNA Methylation-Gold&#x02122; Kit (Zymo Research) according to the manufacturer&#x02019;s protocol. Briefly, 500 ng of DNA was sodium bisulfite-treated, denatured at 98 &#x000b0;C for 10 min, and bisulfite converted at 64 &#x000b0;C for 2.5 h. After conversion, samples were desulfonated and purified using column preparation. Approximately 200 ng of bisulfate-converted DNA was processed according to the Illumina Infinium Methylation Assay protocol. This assay is based on the conversion of unmethylated cytosine (C) nucleotides into uracil/thymine (T) nucleotides by the bisulfite treatment. The DNA was whole-genome amplified, enzymatically fragmented, precipitated, resuspended, and hybridized overnight at 48 &#x000b0;C to locus-specific oligonucleotide primers on the BeadChip. After hybridization, the C or T nucleotides were detected by single-base primer extension. The fluorescence signals corresponding to the C or T nucleotides were measured from the BeadChips using the Illumina iScan scanner. Using a 95 % reference range of at least 0.20, 103,408 CpG sites were used in the covariance matrix calculation. Together, the two first principal components explain over 39 % of the total variance. We chose to use weight category instead of BMI since our study samples include individuals aged under 18 years whose BMI scales differ from the BMI scales of individuals aged over 18 years. Rare homozygous genotypes (count of less than 10) were combined with heterozygotes.</p><p id="__p38" class="p p-last">The coefficients <em>b</em><sub><em>kx</em></sub> summarize the association between methylation levels and the variables of interest. The <em>p</em> value for the SNP was determined using a likelihood ratio test, using the lrtest function of the <em>lmtest</em> package [<a href="#CR68" rid="CR68" class=" bibr popnode">68</a>], and we report the effect size as the proportion R<sup>2</sup> of the CpG methylation variance that is explained by the SNP, among the variance not already explained by the covariates. To control the proportion of false positives, q values were calculated using the qvalue function of the <em>qvalue</em> package [<a href="#CR69" rid="CR69" class=" bibr popnode">69</a>]. A SNP was considered significant if its q value was&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05.</p></div><div id="Sec29" class="sec"><h4>Enrichment of associated CpGs in genomic regions, in vivo transcribed enhancers, and chromatin states</h4><p id="__p39" class="p p-first-last">To test whether associated CpGs were enriched or underrepresented in different genomic regions (promoter, gene body, etc.), chromatin states (enhancer, TSS, heterochromatin, etc.) and in vivo transcribed enhancers, we used Fisher&#x02019;s exact test. To control the proportion of false positives, q values were calculated using the qvalue function of the <em>qvalue</em> package [<a href="#CR69" rid="CR69" class=" bibr popnode">69</a>]. Significance was considered at a q value&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05.</p></div><div id="Sec30" class="sec"><h4>Number of long-range interactions</h4><p id="__p40" class="p p-first-last">The distributions of the numbers of long-range interactions per CpG were skewed. Thus, to see whether associated CpGs had a higher or lower number of long-range interactions, we used Mann&#x02013;Whitney U-test.</p></div><div id="Sec31" class="sec sec-last"><h4>Power calculations</h4><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">We used the pwr.f2.test function of the <em>pwr</em> package in R to determine the statistical power in the replication datasets (fibroblasts, brain and SAT/VAT).</p></div></div></div><div id="Sec32" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec32title">Results</h2><div id="Sec33" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Obesity-associated SNPs associate with methylation at proximal CpGs in whole blood samples from healthy individuals</h3><p id="__p42" class="p p-first">We tested associations between 52 obesity-associated SNPs and M values of all CpG sites 500 kb upstream and 500 kb downstream of each SNP in the blood of 355 individuals (Table&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4599317/table/Tab1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Tab1" rid-ob="ob-Tab1" co-legend-rid=""><span>1</span></a>), using a linear regression model adjusted for age, sex, blood cell type surrogate, and weight category (i.e., lean, overweight, or obese) instead of BMI since our study samples include individuals aged under 18 years whose BMI scales differ from the BMI scales of individuals aged over 18 years. In order to make the link between SNP, methylation, and mRNA expression, we searched four eQTL databases (see &#x0201c;<a href="#Sec2" rid="Sec2" class=" sec">Methods</a>&#x0201d;); we browsed all associated SNPs and SNPs in strong LD with them (with r<sup>2</sup>&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;0.8), and retrieved the genes for which they were eQTLs (Additional file <a href="#MOESM8" rid="MOESM8" class=" media">8</a>). We found that associated CpGs are located in or show long-range interactions with the promoters of genes for which the corresponding SNPs are known eQTLs. For instance, rs10838738 is a known eQTL for several genes in blood, including <em>C1QTNF4</em>, <em>CELF1</em>, and <em>NUP160</em>. Interestingly, rs10838738 associated with three CpGs showing long-range interactions with <em>C1QTNF4</em> (Additional file <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a>), four CpGs showing long-range interactions with <em>CELF1</em> (Additional file <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a>), including three in enhancers in PBMCs (Additional file <a href="#MOESM6" rid="MOESM6" class=" media">6</a>), and one CpG showing long-range interactions with <em>NUP160</em> (Additional file <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a>) that was in an enhancer in PBMCs (Additional file <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a>). Another example is rs713586, a known eQTL for <em>ADCY3</em> in blood and monocytes. rs713586 associated with a CpG located in the promoter of <em>ADCY3</em> (Additional file <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a>) that was also promoter-associated in PBMCs (Additional file <a href="#MOESM6" rid="MOESM6" class=" media">6</a>).</p></div><div id="Sec40" class="sec"><h3>Genome-scale measurements are validated by bisulfite sequencing</h3><p id="__p57" class="p p-first-last">We validated one of the tested CpGs (cg15576492) by bisulfite sequencing, using DNA from 17 individuals from the discovery study group who were homozygous for rs3934834 (six <em>A</em>/<em>A</em> and 11 <em>G</em>/<em>G</em>). Our criteria for choosing this site were the following: 1) significant association with risk alleles; 2) strongest association with risk alleles; 3) located in a gene promoter and/or having long-range interactions with a gene promoter. The correlation between methylation assessed by Illumina and bisulfite sequencing was good (Pearson&#x02019;s correlation coefficient r&#x02009;=&#x02009;0.68, <em>p</em> value&#x02009;=&#x02009;0.0025; Additional file <a href="#MOESM9" rid="MOESM9" class=" media">9</a>). Methylation in <em>A</em>/<em>A</em> was higher than methylation in <em>G</em>/<em>G</em>, but the methylation difference did not reach statistical significance (<em>p</em> value&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;0.05), which could be explained by reduced statistical power (17 individuals).</p></div><div id="Sec41" class="sec sec-last"><h3>SNP&#x02013;CpG associations might not be blood-specific</h3><div id="Sec42" class="sec sec-first"><h4>Four of the initial SNP&#x02013;CpG associations in blood are replicated in skin fibroblasts</h4><p id="__p58" class="p p-first-last">The open-access dataset of skin fibroblasts consists of DNA methylation data assessed with the Illumina HumanMethylation450 BeadChip and genotype data assessed with the Illumina Human1M-Duov3 DNA Analysis BeadChip. Thus, we had data to test 65 of the 107 significant SNP&#x02013;CpG associations in skin fibroblasts (<em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;62). Fourteen SNP&#x02013;CpG associations had a raw <em>p</em> value&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05, and seven had a q value&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05, including four having a concordant effect sign with results obtained in blood (Additional file <a href="#MOESM10" rid="MOESM10" class=" media">10</a>). Notably, genotypes at rs1011731 associated with methylation at cg13446689 (regression coefficient&#x02009;=&#x02009;0.254, q value&#x02009;=&#x02009;0.012).</p></div><div id="Sec43" class="sec"><h4>The single SNP&#x02013;methylation association tested in SAT and VAT was not significant</h4><p id="__p59" class="p p-first-last">The SAT and VAT study group of 149 individuals (mostly overweight/obese) was used to test specifically the association between genotypes at rs1011731 and methylation at cg13446689, which was assessed by bisulfite sequencing. We chose to test this SNP&#x02013;CpG pair because there was an association between cg13446689 and rs1011731 in both blood and fibroblasts, and because this study group had already been genotyped for rs1011731. There was no association between methylation at cg13446689 in VAT or SAT and genotypes at rs1011731 (<em>p</em> value&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;0.05; Additional file <a href="#MOESM10" rid="MOESM10" class=" media">10</a>).</p></div><div id="Sec44" class="sec sec-last"><h4>The two SNP&#x02013;methylation associations tested in cerebellum, frontal cortex, caudal pons, and temporal cortex were not significant</h4><p id="__p60" class="p p-first-last">The open-access dataset of four brain regions consists of DNA methylation assayed using the Illumina HumanMethylation27 BeadChip, and genotype data assessed with the Illumina Human1M-Duov3 DNA Analysis BeadChip. Thus, we had data to test two of the 107 associated CpGs (cg05585544 and cg11385473). There was no association between genotypes at rs10838738 and methylation at cg05585544 in any of the four brain regions; there was no association between genotypes at rs652722 and methylation at cg11385473 in any of the four brain regions (<em>p</em> values&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;0.05; Additional file <a href="#MOESM10" rid="MOESM10" class=" media">10</a>).</p></div></div></div><div id="Sec45" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec45title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p61" class="p p-first">Our findings suggest that carriers of obesity-associated risk alleles display complex alterations of the gene regulatory landscape. We find that obesity-associated SNPs can be linked to DNA methylation levels in several proximal locations, which implies that they may affect multiple genes. These SNPs associated with proximal DNA methylation levels in whole blood of healthy individuals, but these associations might not be blood-specific. Interestingly, several obesity-associated SNPs associated with CpGs that were in the promoters of genes known to participate in the pathogenesis of obesity, or were located in regions that interact with such genes. In addition, associated CpGs were enriched in enhancers in blood, which highlights their potential in gene regulation.</p><p id="__p62">It is well established that DNA methylation levels correlate with the presence of specific alleles at nearby SNPs [<a href="#CR25" rid="CR25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701490">25</a>&#x02013;<a href="#CR37" rid="CR37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701412">37</a>], such as Grundberg et al. [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701501">29</a>], who found that 28 % of CpGs were associated with SNPs within 100 kb in adipose tissue. If we restrict our analysis to 100 kb, we find 103 SNP&#x02013;CpG associations at a q value&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05, corresponding to 27 unique SNPs (52 % of tested SNPs). It would be difficult to assess whether this percentage is particularly high, but the present study shows that obesity-associated SNPs discovered in GWASs may mediate their effect through alterations of the regulation of transcription. Indeed, the global results for each significant SNP display fascinating patterns. Several obesity-associated SNPs may affect &#x0201c;transcription factories&#x0201d;, clusters of gene promoters and their enhancers that interact in three-dimensional space and are brought together by DNA-binding proteins such as CTCF [<a href="#CR70" rid="CR70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534519479">70</a>]. The most striking example is rs7498665 since the 12 CpGs associated with this SNP are located in ten distinct gene promoters. rs3888190, one of the top loci of the most recent BMI GWAS [<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701436">11</a>], is in perfect LD with rs7498665 (r<sup>2</sup>&#x02009;=&#x02009;1) and is known to be an eQTL for five of these ten gene promoters (<em>APOBR</em> [<a href="#CR71" rid="CR71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701517">71</a>], <em>SH2B1</em> [<a href="#CR71" rid="CR71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701408">71</a>], <em>SULT1A2</em> [<a href="#CR72" rid="CR72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701470">72</a>], <em>ATXN2L</em> [<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701474">11</a>], and <em>TUFM</em> [<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701449">11</a>]). Another interesting example is rs10838738, which associated with three CpGs showing long-range interactions with <em>C1QTNF4</em>, four CpGs showing long-range interactions with <em>CELF1</em>, and one CpG showing long-range interactions with <em>NUP160</em>. rs10838738 is a known eQTL for these three genes in blood [<a href="#CR64" rid="CR64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701491">64</a>, <a href="#CR66" rid="CR66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701469">66</a>]. Thus, our results suggest that the effect of obesity-associated SNPs may be mediated by multiple and quite distant genes, as illustrated by three of our investigated SNPs (rs3934834, rs2287019, and rs7498665) that associated with CpGs interacting with no less than 15 promoters (Additional files <a href="#MOESM4" rid="MOESM4" class=" media">4</a> and <a href="#MOESM7" rid="MOESM7" class=" media">7</a>). This underlines the importance for a rational and inclusive selection process for candidate genes for GWAS hits rather than the common practice of only focusing on the closest gene.</p><p id="__p63">At a more detailed level, patterns of DNA methylation at specific CpGs between carriers and non-carriers of risk alleles were consistent with previous studies. Alleles at rs713586 explained 54 % of the variance in methylation at cg01884057, with an increase of almost 10 % methylation for each risk allele. The very same pattern was also found in adipose tissue in another study [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701461">29</a>]. More interestingly, some patterns of DNA methylation between carriers and non-carriers of risk alleles was consistent with what is known about these genes and obesity. For instance, <em>MIR148A</em> is upregulated during normal adipogenesis but downregulated in obese adipocytes [<a href="#CR73" rid="CR73" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701480">73</a>], and its expression is regulated by DNA methylation at its CpG island [<a href="#CR74" rid="CR74" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701498">74</a>]. Consistently, carriers of the risk allele at rs1055144 had higher methylation levels in the promoter of <em>MIR148A</em>. Also, carriers of the risk allele at rs10838738 had lower methylation in the promoter of <em>PTPMT1</em>, a gene that codes for a mitochondrial phosphatase whose inhibition lowers glucose concentration [<a href="#CR75" rid="CR75" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701438">75</a>] and a suggested drug target for treatment of type II diabetes [<a href="#CR76" rid="CR76" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701429">76</a>]. Last but not least, three of the associated CpGs were located within two of the numerous promoters of <em>BDNF</em>, which encodes a neurotrophin that plays several roles in regulating energy homeostasis [<a href="#CR77" rid="CR77" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701454">77</a>]. It is suggested that <em>BDNF</em> is finely regulated by DNA methylation and histone modifications [<a href="#CR78" rid="CR78" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701494">78</a>, <a href="#CR79" rid="CR79" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701462">79</a>], and differential <em>BDNF</em> transcripts are expressed at different time points and in different cellular compartments [<a href="#CR79" rid="CR79" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701419">79</a>]. Carriers of the risk allele at rs10767664 had higher methylation in the pII promoter of <em>BDNF</em>, and lower methylation in the pVI promoter of <em>BDNF</em>. However, the roles of specific <em>BDNF</em> promoters in obesity remain unexplored. Also, the SNPs may affect other genes linked to obesity: NR1H3, a member of the liver X receptors that regulate cholesterol catabolism [<a href="#CR80" rid="CR80" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_534519480">80</a>] and expressed during adipose tissue remodeling [<a href="#CR81" rid="CR81" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701512">81</a>]; PACSIN3, a kinase that induces glucose uptake by adipocytes [<a href="#CR82" rid="CR82" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701509">82</a>]; LGR4, a G protein-coupled receptor whose ablation potentiates the white-to-brown fat transition [<a href="#CR83" rid="CR83" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701427">83</a>]; POMC, a peptide that decreases food intake and increases energy expenditure [<a href="#CR84" rid="CR84" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701440">84</a>]; CLN3 and ITH4, two proteins positively associated with obesity [<a href="#CR85" rid="CR85" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701500">85</a>, <a href="#CR86" rid="CR86" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701516">86</a>]; and the developmental genes <em>HOTAIR</em> and <em>HOXC11</em>, responsible for differential fat accumulation between upper and lower body, and under epigenetic control [<a href="#CR85" rid="CR85" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701443">85</a>, <a href="#CR87" rid="CR87" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701431">87</a>].</p><p id="__p64">Our study aimed at unraveling the molecular effects of body mass-associated genetic variants on chromatin structure, with a special focus on DNA methylation. We benefited from a large sample size for the discovery analysis (<em>n</em>&#x02009;=&#x02009;355) and from the use of a large battery of open access datasets to map associated CpGs to meaningful genomic annotations, such as promoters, predicted and in vivo transcribed enhancers, and long-range interactions. In addition, we tested the tissue-specificity of 65 of the initial associations in skin fibroblasts, two of the initial associations in four brain regions, and one in SAT and VAT. It is possible that some of the associations discovered in blood are limited to this tissue, or that unmeasured environmental factors such as smoking, diet, physical activity, and tissue-specific molecular factors impacted DNA methylation at the measured CpG sites and confounded our results. It should be noted, however, that we could not test all of the 107 initial associations in the replication samples, and the sample sizes of the replication samples were smaller than the discovery study group. Analysis of statistical power (probability of detecting a &#x0201c;true&#x0201d; effect when it exists) suggests that we have a high probability of replicating our results in the VAT and SAT replication samples, where power was 95 %. In contrast, power was only 23&#x02013;25 % for cg11385473 and 42&#x02013;47 % for cg05585544 for the brain replication samples and 39 % on average for the skin fibroblast replication samples, which implies that we are likely unable to replicate our results due to too small sample groups for these conditions. Besides, pan-tissue SNP&#x02013;CpG associations are consistent with a genome-wide study where genotype-dependent methylation differences between blood and brain were associated, making genetic influence on DNA methylation in blood relevant for other tissues [<a href="#CR26" rid="CR26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701442">26</a>]. Finally, it should be kept in mind that the probes of the methylation array used in this study (Illumina 450 k) are enriched in CpG islands, gene promoters, and gene regions; it is thus possible that we missed important CpGs linked to obesity-associated SNPs.</p><p id="__p65" class="p p-last">In the paradigm of genetics&#x02013;epigenetics&#x02013;environment relationships, it is still unknown whether obesity-associated SNPs directly cause differential DNA methylation at genes and enhancers that contribute to the pathogenesis of obesity, or if the observed differential methylation levels are merely a consequence of a modified gene regulation caused by the presence of risk alleles at obesity-associated SNPs. In a recent review on the function and information content of DNA methylation, DNA methylation is thought to have both an active and passive role in gene regulation, and it seems to be highly contextual [<a href="#CR88" rid="CR88" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701441">88</a>]. In particular, it has been proposed that mutations within regulatory regions affect binding of transcription factors, which in turn influence DNA methylation [<a href="#CR88" rid="CR88" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_479701425">88</a>]. If DNA methylation does not necessarily actively impact on gene regulation, it is at least an informative marker of the underlying regulatory activity. Therefore, the differential methylation observed in carriers of risk alleles at obesity-associated SNPs in our study likely reflects allele-specific effects on gene regulatory mechanisms.</p></div><div id="Sec46" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec46title">Conclusions</h2><p id="__p66" class="p p-first-last">In this study we report strong associations between obesity-associated SNPs discovered in GWASs and methylation levels at proximal CpG sites. The methylation sites associated with alleles at obesity-associated SNPs were enriched in enhancers in PBMCs, and some of these sites were located in the promoters of genes, or were located in regions showing long-range interactions with established roles in appetite regulation as well as regulation of body mass. We also found indications that some of these genotype&#x02013;methylation associations exist in different tissues. This study has implications for understanding how obesity-associated variants mediate their effects. Further studies are needed to unravel the mechanisms that govern the interplay between genetic variants and the activity of functional DNA elements.</p></div><div id="idm140700629074272" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140700629074272title">Acknowledgements</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p108">The methylation array was performed at the Genotyping Technology Platform, (<a href="http://www.genotyping.se" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4599317&amp;issue-id=247889&amp;journal-id=863&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.genotyping.se</a>), the sequencing was performed at the Uppsala Genome Center (<a href="http://www.igp.uu.se/facilities/genome_center" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4599317&amp;issue-id=247889&amp;journal-id=863&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.igp.uu.se/facilities/genome_center</a>), Uppsala, Sweden with support from Uppsala University and the Knut and Alice Wallenberg foundation and at the Uppsala Genome Centre.
