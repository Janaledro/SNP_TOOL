However, it has been difficult to detect such an accelerated aging effect because it is unclear how to measure tissue age. Here we use a recently developed biomarker of aging (known as &#x0201c;epigenetic clock&#x0201d;) to study the relationship between epigenetic age and obesity in several human tissues. We report an unexpectedly strong correlation between high body mass index and the epigenetic age of liver tissue. This finding may explain why obese people suffer from the early onset of many age-related pathologies, including liver cancer.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">obesity, epigenetics, aging, biological age, DNA methylation</span></div></div><div id="idm140200585440816" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140200585440816title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="__p5" class="p p-first-last">Because of the dearth of biomarkers of aging, it has been difficult to test the hypothesis that obesity increases tissue age. Here we use a novel epigenetic biomarker of aging (referred to as an &#x0201c;epigenetic clock&#x0201d;) to study the relationship between high body mass index (BMI) and the DNA methylation ages of human blood, liver, muscle, and adipose tissue. A significant correlation between BMI and epigenetic age acceleration could only be observed for liver (<em>r</em> = 0.42, <em>P</em> = 6.8 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;4</sup> in dataset 1 and <em>r</em> = 0.42, <em>P</em> = 1.2 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;4</sup> in dataset 2). On average, epigenetic age increased by 3.3 y for each 10 BMI units. The detected age acceleration in liver is not associated with the Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease Activity Score or any of its component traits after adjustment for BMI. The 279 genes that are underexpressed in older liver samples are highly enriched (1.2 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;9</sup>) with nuclear mitochondrial genes that play a role in oxidative phosphorylation and electron transport. The epigenetic age acceleration, which is not reversible in the short term after rapid weight loss induced by bariatric surgery, may play a role in liver-related comorbidities of obesity, such as insulin resistance and liver cancer.</p></div></div><div id="idm140200589222608" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p6" class="p p-first">Dietary restriction in humans causes changes that protect against many age-related pathologies (<a href="#r1" rid="r1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189693">1</a>). In contrast, obesity increases the risk of chronic age-related diseases, such as type 2 diabetes, heart disease, osteoarthritis, and certain types of cancer, and thus constitutes a major and rising global health problem (<a href="#r2" rid="r2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189713">2</a>). It is a plausible hypothesis that obesity increases the risk of at least some of these diseases through accelerated tissue aging.</p><p id="__p7">Leukocyte telomere length, which is a widely used biomarker of aging, has been found to be negatively correlated with body mass index (BMI) (<a href="#r3" rid="r3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189696">3</a><a href="#r4" rid="r4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189705"></a><a href="#r5" rid="r5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189701"></a><a href="#r6" rid="r6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189708"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#r7" rid="r7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189694">7</a>). Although the observed correlation between BMI and telomere length is relatively weak (<em>r</em> = 0.12) (<a href="#r4" rid="r4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189670">4</a>), it is remarkable that these studies demonstrated that BMI is associated with an age acceleration effect in blood.</p><p id="__p8" class="p">Assessing tissue age poses a significant methodological challenge because it is not clear which biomarkers of aging are appropriate. There is a considerable debate in the literature as to what extent markers/causes of cellular senescence, such as telomere length, capture all aspects of tissue aging (<a href="#r8" rid="r8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189679">8</a><a href="#r9" rid="r9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189719"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#r10" rid="r10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189688">10</a>). Biomarkers of tissue age should ideally be validated in a wide range of tissues and cell types, provide a quantitative estimate of tissue age, and should be uniformly applicable to tissue samples from different tissue banks. S.H. recently developed a biomarker of aging (&#x0201c;epigenetic clock&#x0201d;) based on DNA methylation (DNAm) levels (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189687">11</a>). This epigenetic clock is defined as a prediction method of chronological age based on the DNAm levels of 353 CpGs. The predicted (estimated) age resulting from the epigenetic clock is referred to as &#x0201c;DNAm age.&#x0201d; Here we use DNAm age as a proxy of epigenetic tissue age. DNAm age is highly (<em>r</em> = 0.96) correlated to chronological age across sorted cell types (CD4 T cells, monocytes, B cells, glial cells, neurons), complex tissues (e.g., blood), and organs (brain, breast, kidney, liver, lung) (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189684">11</a>). The epigenetic clock is robust with respect to batch effects and can be applied to two commercially standardized platforms: the Illumina 450K array and the 27K array.</p></div><div id="s1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s1title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">Results</h2><p id="__p9" class="p p-first">DNAm age was calculated as described in ref. <a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189669">11</a> from a total of 1,215 human samples profiled with the Illumina Infinium 450K and 27K arrays (<em>n</em> = 141 liver, <em>n</em> = 274 blood, <em>n</em> = 726 adipose tissue, <em>n</em> = 74 muscle). Methylation datasets were either obtained from public data repositories or&#x02014;in the case of the replication dataset from liver, muscle, and adipose tissue&#x02014;generated for the purposes of this study. To study the relationship between epigenetic age acceleration and transcriptional changes, we also generated a novel gene-expression dataset on the Human Gene 1.1 ST Array platform from Affymetrix. The correlation of BMI with age acceleration was confirmed, yielding a similar effect size of <em>r</em> = 0.42 (<em>P</em> = 1.2 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;4</sup>) (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig02"><span>Fig. 2 <em>E</em> and <em>F</em></span></a>) compared with the discovery dataset. The correlation between age acceleration and BMI (<em>r</em> = 0.42 in the discovery and <em>r</em> = 0.42 in the replication datasets) is unexpectedly high in comparison with the previously reported correlations (<em>r</em> = 0.12) between BMI and telomere length in blood (<a href="#r4" rid="r4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189681">4</a>). We think it unlikely that changes in telomere length could explain our results in liver because telomere length only has a weak negative correlation with DNAm age after correcting for chronological age (e.g., <em>r</em> = &#x02212;0.28, <em>P</em> = 0.22 in adipose dataset 2). For a subset of the individuals from the liver datasets (discovery and replication combined), corresponding muscle (<em>n</em> = 26) and adipose tissue (<em>n</em> = 32) samples were available for analysis. In both nonliver tissues, no significant evidence of BMI-related DNAm age acceleration was observed (<em>P</em> = 0.32 and 0.68 for adipose and muscle tissue, respectively), thereby further supporting the liver-specificity of the age acceleration finding (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig02"><span>Fig. 2 <em>G</em> and <em>H</em></span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="fig02" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig02"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140200588436128" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pnas.1412759111fig02.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pnas.1412759111fig02.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/bin/pnas.1412759111fig02.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm140200588436128" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_fig02"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02">Fig. 2.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p19">Replication analysis. These analyses were enabled by the availability of a standardized histological scoring of all liver samples by a single pathologist. The following lines of evidence demonstrate that the observed age acceleration in liver is not mediated by NAFLD or NASH: First, when patients with histological evidence of NAFLD were excluded from the analysis (i.e., when the analysis was restricted to <em>n</em> = 32 controls and healthy obese subjects), BMI had a similar correlation with age acceleration (<em>r</em> = 0.49, <em>P</em> = 0.0044) (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig02"><span>Fig. 2<em>H</em></span></a>) as in the analysis that included NAFLD subjects (<em>r</em> = 0.42, <em>P</em> = 1.2 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;4</sup>) (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig02/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig02" rid-ob="ob-fig02" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig02"><span>Fig. 2<em>G</em></span></a>). Second, we find that the detected age acceleration in liver is not associated with the NAFLD Activity Score (NAS) (<a href="#r19" rid="r19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189692">19</a>) or any of its component traits (steatosis, hepatocyte ballooning, inflammation) or fibrosis (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig03/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig03" rid-ob="ob-fig03" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig03"><span>Fig. 3</span></a> and <a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Figs. S3</a> and <a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">S4</a>). Third, a multivariate linear regression model of DNAm age on various covariates reveals that BMI remains a highly significant covariate even after adjusting for chronological, NAS component traits (steatosis, ballooning, inflammation), fat percentage, and fibrosis (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Table S1</a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="fig03" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig03"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig03/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig03" rid-ob="ob-fig03"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140200580090656" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4217403_pnas.1412759111fig03.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pnas.1412759111fig03.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/bin/pnas.1412759111fig03.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140200580090656" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig03/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_fig03"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig03/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig03" rid-ob="ob-fig03">Fig. 3.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p22">Post hoc analyses of subgroups and histological characteristics of NASH. Analogous results can also be found in the individual liver datasets (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Figs. We do not find a significant association between type II diabetes status and age acceleration (<em>P</em> = 0.16) (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S5 <em>A</em> and <em>C</em></a>), but these results should be interpreted with caution because a two-group comparison based on 23 diabetes cases and 103 controls only achieves sufficient statistical power (80% at a significance level of 0.05) for detecting moderate to large effects (mean differences of 0.7 SDs).</p><p id="__p24" class="p p-last">Furthermore, it is unlikely that smoking confounds the relationship between BMI and age acceleration because (<em>i</em>) we did not observe a significant relationship between BMI and smoking status in our liver data sets, and (<em>ii</em>) smoking status was not significantly related with age acceleration in liver, muscle, or blood tissue (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S6</a>).</p></div><div id="s4" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Weight Loss Does Not Reverse Epigenetic Age Acceleration in the Short Term. </h3><p id="__p25" class="p p-first">Bariatric surgery induces a profound weight loss and reverses a number of metabolic abnormalities associated with increased BMI. For 21 subjects from the combined liver datasets, liver tissue samples before and after bariatric surgery (collected within a 9-mo period after surgery) were available. As expected, bariatric surgery led to a significant decrease in BMI (&#x00394;BMI = 14.6, <em>P</em> = 3.7 &#x000d7; 10<sup>&#x02212;7</sup>) (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig04/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig04" rid-ob="ob-fig04" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig04"><span>Fig. 4<em>A</em></span></a>). However, bariatric surgery and rapid weight loss does not reverse the DNAm age in liver tissue within a 9-mo period (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig04/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig04" rid-ob="ob-fig04" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig04"><span>Fig. 4<em>B</em></span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="fig04" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig04"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig04/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig04" rid-ob="ob-fig04"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140200580847936" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4217403_pnas.1412759111fig04.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pnas.1412759111fig04.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/bin/pnas.1412759111fig04.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140200580847936" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig04/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_fig04"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/figure/fig04/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig04" rid-ob="ob-fig04">Fig. 4.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p26">Effect of weight loss and exercise intervention on DNAm age. These 279 negatively related genes tend to be overexpressed in liver samples that do not exhibit age acceleration effects. The results of a functional enrichment analysis with the Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery (DAVID, v6.7) (<a href="#r21" rid="r21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189699">21</a>) can be found in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/table/t03/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="t03" rid-ob="ob-t03" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 3</span></a>. This finding recapitulates a previously observed conserved molecular signature of aging in which genes associated with these functions tend to be strongly underexpressed (<a href="#r22" rid="r22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189697">22</a>). The 378 positively related genes are known to play a role in nucleoside-triphosphatase regulator activity, cell adhesion, GTPase regulator activity, and response to wounding. However, these significant relationships probably reflect confounding caused by BMI, as can be seen from a structural equation model analysis (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material"><em>SI Text</em></a> and <a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>): an independence model (model 3: trait&#x02190;BMI&#x02192;age acceleration), which postulates that high BMI leads to liver pathology and age acceleration independently, achieves the best fit for the NAS score, hepatocyte ballooning, and liver inflammation. However, for steatosis, the reactive model (BMI&#x02192;steatosis&#x02192;age acceleration) provides a better fit. The results for fibrosis are ambiguous.</p><p id="__p32" class="p p-last">We caution the reader that structural equation models make various assumptions (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material"><em>SI Text</em></a>) that may not be satisfied by our observational data. As the study design is primarily observational, we cannot establish causality but provide this as a speculative model.</p></div></div><div id="s7" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s7title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">Discussion</h2><p id="__p33" class="p p-first">Aging of tissues is a complex, dynamic, and multifaceted process that is currently still poorly understood (<a href="#r23" rid="r23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189715">23</a>) because its investigation depends critically on the use of model organisms, and integration of findings with human physiology remains a challenge. Tissue aging has up until now predominantly been defined in single-tissue and organ studies, thus inhibiting global analyses of epidemiological factors and medical interventions. Genome-wide DNAm-based epigenetic analysis may provide a possible solution to this problem, as (<em>i</em>) epigenetic restructuring of the genome has been shown to play a key role in aging as demonstrated by altered global methylation levels, histone modifications and CpG methylation, and (<em>ii</em>) specific, age-related alterations in DNAm have been reported previously.</p><p id="__p34">Many groups have reported sets of CpGs that correlate with age in multiple tissues (<a href="#r15" rid="r15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189673">15</a>, <a href="#r24" rid="r24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189721">24</a><a href="#r25" rid="r25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189698"></a><a href="#r26" rid="r26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189695"></a><a href="#r27" rid="r27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189677"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#r28" rid="r28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189710">28</a>). Although these reports firmly establish the strong effect of age on epigenetic modifications, individual CpG sites are unsuitable for global comparisons between tissues in the context of epidemiological or interventional analysis, because the underlying methylation sites are highly tissue-specific. Thus, in this report, we use an aggregate measure of DNAm age that has been validated across sorted cell types (CD4 T cells, monocytes, B cells, glial cells, neurons), complex tissues, and organs (e.g., brain, breast, kidney, liver, lung) (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189691">11</a>). This biomarker enabled (as far as we are aware) the first analysis of the relation between obesity and the epigenetic ages of different human tissues. Although the epigenetic clock stands out because of its high correlation with chronological age across most tissues (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189718">11</a>), this biomarker of aging has a major limitation: it is not yet understood what it measures. Whereas many articles suggest that age-related changes in DNAm levels represent noise or epigenetic drift (<a href="#r29" rid="r29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189700">29</a>), DNAm age might measure the cumulative work of an epigenomic maintenance system (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189722">11</a>). According to the gene-enrichment signature of epigenetic age acceleration in liver (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/table/t03/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="t03" rid-ob="ob-t03" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 3</span></a>), age acceleration relates to processes, such as oxidative stress and energy metabolism. Although this epigenomic maintenance system is as yet poorly defined, the continued nutritionally driven oxidative stress and metabolic pressure might point to the mechanisms leading to the liver-specific increase of DNAm age. Our data show that high BMI relates to increased DNAm age in a tissue-specific manner. Although BMI is widely used, it is a somewhat suboptimal measure of adiposity because it is correlated both with obesity and muscle mass. Thus, a possible association of DNAm age and muscle mass should be addressed in future studies. Examining the association between epigenetic age acceleration and tissue pathology may provide an important biological link between obesity and clinical manifestations of accelerated aging. The increased age of liver tissue in obese individuals may provide insights to liver-related comorbidities of obesity, such as insulin resistance and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).</p><p id="__p35">Liver protein synthesis function, as measured on an organismal level, is quite plastic and not impaired in obesity in the absence of histopathological cirrhosis. However, even in the absence of structural liver disease, and in mild forms (such as early steatosis), the clinically relevant phenomena in these patients are insulin resistance (with its hepatic component very poorly understood) and an increased risk of HCC as the most tangible manifestations of obesity. Aging and cancer are not only intricately linked to each other (<a href="#r30" rid="r30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189707">30</a>, <a href="#r31" rid="r31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189724">31</a>) but also to obesity and metabolic syndrome (<a href="#r32" rid="r32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189716">32</a>). Obesity is an established risk factor for many types of cancers, particularly for HCC, because of its carcinogenic potential and the association with NAFLD (<a href="#r33" rid="r33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189682">33</a>). In cohort studies in men, metabolic syndrome was most strongly associated with liver cancer followed by colorectal cancer (<a href="#r34" rid="r34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189667">34</a>). Future studies will be needed to test whether tissues with increased DNAm age have a lower threshold to carcinogenesis. Although epigenetic aging may help understand carcinogenesis, it may also have practical consequences. NASH is a risk factor for liver cancer even in the absence of cirrhosis (<a href="#r32" rid="r32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189704">32</a>) and is thus recommended as a high-risk group for screening in the current American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases guidelines (<a href="#r35" rid="r35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189712">35</a>). Future HCC screening recommendations may be influenced by our findings because BMI-induced increased tissue age (<em>i</em>) is independent of the presence of overt NASH and (<em>ii</em>) is irreversible, at least in the short term.</p><p id="__p36" class="p p-last">The increased epigenetic age of liver tissue in obese individuals should provide insights into common liver-related comorbidities of obesity, such as insulin resistance and liver cancer. These findings support the hypothesis that obesity is associated with accelerated aging effects (<a href="#r3" rid="r3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189720">3</a>) and stresses once more the importance of maintaining a healthy weight.</p></div><div id="s8" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s8title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">Methods</h2><p id="__p37" class="p p-first">A detailed description of the datasets and statistical methods can be found in <a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material"><em>SI Text</em></a>. All data presented in this article are available in public data repositories. Accession numbers are presented in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/table/t01/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="t01" rid-ob="ob-t01" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>.</p><div id="s9" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Public DNAm Datasets. </h3><p id="__p38" class="p p-first-last">All genome-wide human methylation datasets with BMI, age, and sex information publicly available at the time of analysis were used (datasets 1&#x02013;6). Such datasets were available from liver, adipose tissue, muscle, and blood (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/table/t01/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="t01" rid-ob="ob-t01" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). Postbariatric patients were excluded from the liver discovery set analysis.</p></div><div id="s10" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Novel Replication Datasets: Samples. </h3><p id="__p39" class="p p-first">Liver samples were collected from German patients (all Caucasians) under the same protocol and ethics approval as reported previously (<a href="#r12" rid="r12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189676">12</a>). In brief, liver samples were obtained percutaneously for patients undergoing liver biopsy for suspected NAFLD or intraoperatively for assessment of liver histology. Normal control samples were recruited from samples obtained for exclusion of liver malignancy during major oncological surgery. A percutaneous follow-up biopsy was obtained in consenting bariatric patients 5&#x02013;9 mo after surgery. All patients provided written, informed consent. The study protocol was approved by the institutional review board (&#x0201c;Ethics commission of the Medical Faculty, University of Kiel&#x0201d; D425/07, A111/99) before the commencement of the study (<a href="#d35e855" rid="d35e855" class=" supplementary-material"><em>SI Text</em></a>). During surgery, in addition to liver tissue, subcutaneous fat and muscle tissue were obtained (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4217403/table/t01/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="t01" rid-ob="ob-t01" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>, datasets 7&#x02013;9) in a subset of patients. A standardized histological scoring of all liver samples by the same single pathologist (C.R.) according to the NAS score (<a href="#r19" rid="r19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189690">19</a>), who performed the histological scoring of the discovery dataset, thereby allowing pooling during post hoc analyses.</p><p id="__p40" class="p p-last">Ideally, one would want to carry out longitudinal studies of liver function to understand how liver function and DNAm changes with age and predicts functional change. The patients biopsied in our study are in a long-term follow up program and may help to provide such answers, which we regrettably cannot provide at this point.</p></div><div id="s11" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Preprocessing of DNAm Data. </h3><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">Samples were processed as reported previously (<a href="#r12" rid="r12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189671">12</a>). In brief, bisulfite conversion using the Zymo EZ DNA Methylation Kit (ZymoResearch), as well as subsequent hybridization of the HumanMethylation450K Bead Chip (Illumina) and scanning (iScan, Illumina), were performed according to the manufacturers protocols by applying standard settings. DNA methylation levels (&#x003b2;-values) were determined by calculating the ratio of intensities between methylated (signal A) and unmethylated (signal B) alleles. Specifically, the &#x003b2;-value was calculated from the intensity of the methylated (M corresponding to signal A) and unmethylated (U corresponding to signal B) alleles, as the ratio of fluorescent signals &#x003b2; = Max(M,0)/[Max(M,0) + Max(U,0) + 100]. Thus, &#x003b2;-values range from 0 (completely unmethylated) to 1 (completely methylated) (<a href="#r36" rid="r36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189672">36</a>).</p></div><div id="s12" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Transcriptomic Data. </h3><p id="__p42" class="p p-first-last">Novel liver messenger RNA expression datasets from the German patients were generated on the HuGene 1.1 ST gene array (Affymetrix) (<a href="#d35e1655" rid="d35e1655" class=" supplementary-material">Dataset S1</a>).</p></div><div id="s13" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Statistical Analysis. </h3><p id="__p43" class="p p-first-last">Epigenetic age was calculated as reported previously. The epigenetic clock is defined as a prediction method of age based on the DNAm levels of 353 CpGs. Predicted age, referred to as DNAm age, correlates with chronological age in sorted cell types (CD4 T cells, monocytes, B cells, glial cells, neurons) and tissues and organs, including whole blood, brain, breast, kidney, liver, lung, saliva (<a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189723">11</a>). Mathematical details and software tutorials for the epigenetic clock can be found in the supplemental files of ref. <a href="#r11" rid="r11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189703">11</a>. Many authors have described methods for dealing with the two types of probes found on the Illumina 450K array (<a href="#r37" rid="r37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189675">37</a><a href="#r38" rid="r38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189717"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#r39" rid="r39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_411189668">39</a>). This is not a concern for the epigenetic clock because it only involves type II probes.
