Groups of 8 male sheep were fed for a 10% increase or 10% decrease in body mass over 65 days. Underfeeding increased the number of apoptotic germ cells (P&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05) and increased the expression of apoptosis-related genes (P&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05) in testicular tissue. We identified 44 miRNAs and 35 putative piRNAs that were differentially expressed in well-fed and underfed males (FDR&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05). Some were related to reproductive system development, apoptosis (miRNAs), and sperm production and quality (piRNAs). Novel-miR-144 (miR-98), was found to target three apoptotic genes (<em>TP53</em>, <em>CASP3</em>, <em>FASL</em>). The proportion of miRNAs as a total of small RNAs was greater in well-fed males than in underfed males (P&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05) and was correlated (r&#x02009;=&#x02009;0.8, P&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05) with the proportion of piRNAs in well-fed and underfed males. In conclusion, the reductions in spermatozoal quality induced by under-nutrition are caused, at least partly, by disruptions to Sertoli cell function and increased germ cell apoptosis, mediated by changes in the expression of miRNAs and piRNAs.</p></div></div><div id="idm139927667947264" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p3" class="p p-first">Spermatogenesis is regulated by a specific and complex genetic program that is susceptible to many disorders that can induce infertility<sup><a href="#b1" rid="b1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264225">1</a></sup>. In addition to disease situations, spermatogenesis can be affected by environmental factors, such as nutrition, that operate through normal physiological processes to profoundly change testicular mass and the efficiency of sperm production, thus affecting sperm output<sup><a href="#b2" rid="b2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264193">2</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b3" rid="b3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373535">3</a></sup>. In the male sheep, for example, 2 months of mild under-nutrition leads to a reversible reduction in testis mass, impaired spermatogenesis, a reduction in the numbers of sperm produced per unit mass of testis (efficiency of spermatogenesis), and a reduction in sperm motility<sup><a href="#b4" rid="b4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264218">4</a></sup>. These effects seem to be mediated, at least partly, by changes in the activity of the Sertoli cells<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373536">5</a></sup>. However, a thorough understanding of the effects of nutrition on spermatogenesis is elusive because of the complexity of the process of spermatogenesis, and the breadth of the spectrum of endocrine and paracrine signals that coordinate events from the initiation of meiosis through the differentiation of germ cells to the generation of mature spermatozoa<sup><a href="#b6" rid="b6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_559642356">6</a></sup>.</p><p id="__p4">Disruption of spermatogenesis by under-nutrition also seems to involve the pathways of apoptosis<sup><a href="#b7" rid="b7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264199">7</a></sup>, a crucial event in many physiological and pathological conditions<sup><a href="#b8" rid="b8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264201">8</a></sup> that was detected long ago in the seminiferous epithelium and is thought to be an important determinant of sperm output<sup><a href="#b9" rid="b9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264179">9</a></sup>. The efficiency of spermatogenesis depends on the total number of cells at successive stages of spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#b10" rid="b10" class=" bibr popnode">10</a></sup>, so is probably regulated by programmed cell death. Indeed, factors that disrupt spermatogenesis can induce apoptosis in the testis &#x02013; for example, suppression of FSH activity, which reduces Sertoli cell proliferation and germ cell number, leads to loss of germ cells through apoptosis rather than through a decrease in proliferation<sup><a href="#b11" rid="b11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264204">11</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b12" rid="b12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264215">12</a></sup>. Similarly, selenium deficiency results in apoptosis of germ cells by arresting the cell cycles<sup><a href="#b13" rid="b13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264202">13</a></sup>. On the other hand, a relationship between the rate of apoptosis and the level of nutrition has not been reported, not even for the sheep, a model in which acute but reversible responses to nutrition are well documented<sup><a href="#b14" rid="b14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264189">14</a></sup>. The present study was therefore designed to test whether nutrition affects germ cell apoptosis in the testis of the sexually mature male sheep. It was also designed to investigate the molecular regulatory processes involved in the responses to changes in nutrition.</p><p id="__p5">Among the regulators of spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#b15" rid="b15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264164">15</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b16" rid="b16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264209">16</a></sup>, small RNAs, including microRNAs (miRNAs) and PIWI-interacting RNAs (piRNAs), have recently become prominent. miRNAs are small (~22 nucleotides) endogenous RNAs that negatively regulate gene expression by targeting the 3&#x02019;-untranslated region (3&#x02019;UTR)<sup><a href="#b17" rid="b17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264226">17</a></sup> and/or coding region<sup><a href="#b18" rid="b18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264211">18</a></sup> of mRNAs. It has been reported that a global loss of miRNAs, in germ cells or Sertoli cells, is detrimental for male fertility<sup><a href="#b19" rid="b19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264154">19</a></sup>. By contrast, piRNAs are longer (26&#x02013;32 nt) than miRNAs and can bind to PIWI, a spermatogenesis-specific protein belonging to the Argonaute protein family<sup><a href="#b20" rid="b20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264197">20</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b21" rid="b21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264177">21</a></sup>. The main function of piRNAs is to guide PIWI protein to repress the transposable elements that protect genomic integrity<sup><a href="#b22" rid="b22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264157">22</a></sup>. In addition, piRNAs derived from mRNAs play a role in the regulation of gene expression<sup><a href="#b23" rid="b23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264191">23</a></sup>. To date, piRNAs have been mainly found in the testis, suggesting their role specific to spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#b24" rid="b24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373533">24</a></sup>. Indeed, we have a confluence of hypotheses here because it has been reported that some miRNAs are crucial for the process of apoptosis<sup><a href="#b25" rid="b25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264214">25</a></sup>. For example, miR-98 expression is reduced during apoptosis<sup><a href="#b26" rid="b26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264165">26</a></sup>, miR-14 is a dose-dependent suppressor of apoptosis, miR-278 antagonizes apoptosis<sup><a href="#b27" rid="b27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373527">27</a></sup>, and transient inhibition of miR-21 in germ cell cultures increases the rate of apoptosis<sup><a href="#b19" rid="b19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264190">19</a></sup>.</p><p id="__p6" class="p">As important regulators of spermatogenesis and germ cell apoptosis, miRNAs and piRNAs should help explain the effects of nutrition on sperm production and sperm quality in males, for example, male sheep. In the present study, we used sexually mature male sheep as a model because the reversible effects nutrition on testis mass, sperm production and sperm quality are well documented<sup><a href="#b28" rid="b28" class=" bibr popnode">28</a></sup>, thus providing a solid foundation for studying the roles of small RNAs. We therefore 1) profiled miRNAs and piRNAs in sheep testis; 2) investigated the relationships among miRNA functions, spermatogenesis and germ cell apoptosis, particularly during responses to nutrition; and 3) explored the potential for gene-derived piRNAs as regulators of spermatogenesis in the testis of the sexually mature sheep.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">Materials and methods</h2><p id="__p7" class="p p-first">The experimental protocol was approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of the CSIRO Centre for Environment and Life Sciences, Floreat, Western Australia (Project No.1202). All the methods followed the approved protocol and were conducted in accordance with the approved guidelines.</p><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h3>Animals and treatments</h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">From late autumn to mid-winter, 16 Merino male sheep were housed in individual pens in a building with windows allowing good penetration of natural light (CSIRO Floreat, Western Australia, latitude 31o59&#x02019;S). On entry, the rams were 24 months old, weighed 65.7&#x02009;&#x000b1;&#x02009;4.7&#x02009;kg, and had a scrotal circumference of 31.8&#x02009;&#x000b1;&#x02009;2.5&#x02009;cm. During a 3-week acclimatization period, they were all fed daily with 750&#x02009;g oaten chaff (8.4% crude protein; 8.0&#x02009;MJ/Kg Metabolisable Energy) and 200&#x02009;g lupin grain (35.8% crude protein; 13.0&#x02009;MJ/Kg Metabolisable Energy). They were then allocated into two dietary treatments (&#x02018;high&#x02019; and &#x02018;low&#x02019;) with the groups balanced for training success to semen collection, body mass, scrotal circumference, temperament, poll-horn type, and sperm quality (the percentage of live and motile sperm, sperm concentration). The high diet was designed to allow the animals to gain 10% body mass over 65 days whereas the low diet was designed to allow 10% loss in body mass. At the start of the treatment period, individual daily allowance was adjusted to provide two dietary groups: animals fed the High diet were offered 1.2&#x02009;kg oaten chaff plus 0.3&#x02009;kg lupin grain; animals fed the Low diet were offered 0.51&#x02009;kg chaff and 0.13&#x02009;kg lupin grain. Every week, the animals were weighed and the amount of feed offered to each individual was adjusted to ensure achievement of target change in body mass. The outcomes for body and testis growth, and for sperm production, have been reported before<sup><a href="#b4" rid="b4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264210">4</a></sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h3>Tissue collection and preservation</h3><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">After 65 days of treatment, the animals were killed with an intravenous overdose of sodium pentobarbitone, and the testes were immediately removed, dissected and weighed. Three samples were chosen from the top, middle and bottom of both testes; those from the right testis were snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at &#x02013;80&#x02009;&#x002da;C for total RNA preparation; those from the left testis were washed with 0.9% saline and then fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 6&#x02009;h, dehydrated and processed for routine embedding in paraffin wax for histological analysis.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h3>Evaluation of apoptosis</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">Terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase mediated dUTP nick-end labeling (TUNEL) was performed under the instruction of the ApopTag plus peroxidase <em>in situ</em> Apoptosis Detection Kit (Chemicon International, USA). Briefly, deparaffinized tissue sections (top part of left testes) were incubated with proteinase K (20&#x02009;&#x003bc;g/ml), subjected to 3% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at 37&#x02009;&#x002da;C for 30&#x02009;min to inhibit endogenous peroxidase, and then incubated with equilibration buffer at room temperature for 1&#x02009;min. Each section was incubated with TdT (terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase) at 37&#x02009;&#x002da;C for 1&#x02009;h and then washed in stop/wash buffer for 10&#x02009;min. The sections were incubated in anti-Digoxigenin Peroxidase Conjugate at room temperature for 30&#x02009;min and were stained with diaminobenzidine (DAB) as a peroxidase substrate. After counterstaining with methyl green, numbers of TUNEL-positive cells per tubule were counted in 50 tubules per animal with the aid of a light microscope. All counting procedures were performed &#x02018;blindly&#x02019;.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><h3>Isolation of RNA and Reverse transcription</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">The trizol method was used to isolate total RNA<sup><a href="#b29" rid="b29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264168">29</a></sup> with the quality and quantity of RNA determined by Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA) and Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA), RNA with an integrity number (RIN) higher than 7.0 was used for further analysis. A high capacity RNA-to-cDNA kit from Applied Biosystems was used for reverse transcription.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="sec"><h3>Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR)</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">QRT-PCR was performed using SYBR Green (Fast SYBR&#x000ae; Green Master Mix; Applied Biosystems) to detect mRNA relative expression, with primers specifically targeting sheep fas ligand <em>(FASL)</em>, tumor protein p53 (<em>TP53)</em> and caspase3 (<em>CASP3</em>) genes designed using NCBI primer blast (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/tools/primerblast/index.cgi?LINK_LOC=BlastHome" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/tools/primerblast/index.cgi?LINK_LOC=BlastHome</a>). Primer sequences for <em>GAPDH</em> were obtained from a published source (<a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S1</a>)<sup><a href="#b30" rid="b30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264220">30</a></sup>. Fluorescence signal was detected with StepOnePlus&#x02122; Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems). The primer specificity was confirmed by PCR amplification, agarose gel electrophoresis and amplicon sequencing. The total volume of each reaction contained 10&#x02009;&#x003bc;l Fast SYBR Green Master Mix (Applied Biosystems), 1&#x02009;&#x003bc;l of each primer (20&#x02009;pmol/&#x003bc;l), 7&#x02009;&#x003bc;l of nuclease-free water, and 1&#x02009;&#x003bc;l of DNA template (50&#x02009;ng/&#x003bc;l). Samples were measured in triplicate using the following program: 95&#x02009;&#x000b0;C for 10&#x02009;min for initial denaturation and then 40 cycles of 95&#x02009;&#x000b0;C for 20&#x02009;s, followed by annealing/extension for 30&#x02009;s at 60&#x02009;&#x000b0;C. Analysis of melting curves was used to monitor PCR product purity. Previous work had shown that the level of expression of <em>GAPDH</em> is relatively high and consistent during testicular development in the sheep, indicating its suitability as a housekeeping gene expressed at similar levels in somatic and germ cell populations<sup><a href="#b30" rid="b30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264181">30</a></sup>. The &#x00394;&#x00394;Ct method was used to analyze relative gene expression<sup><a href="#b31" rid="b31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264183">31</a></sup>. The same sample was always used as calibrator. The gene expression (&#x00394;Ct value) was calculated on the basis of quantification cycles (Ct) (&#x00394;Ct&#x02009;=&#x02009;Ct <sub>target gene</sub> &#x02013; Ct <sub>GAPDH</sub>). The levels of expression of apoptosis-related genes were calculated in relation to the calibrator (&#x00394;&#x00394;Ct&#x02009;=&#x02009;mean &#x00394;Ct <sub>sample</sub> &#x02013; mean &#x00394;Ct <sub>calibrator</sub>). Relative expression (RQ) was calculated using StepOnePlus&#x02122; Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) and the formula: RQ<sub>target gene</sub>&#x02009;=&#x02009;2<sup>&#x02013;&#x00394;&#x00394;Ct</sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><h3>Small RNA library sequencing</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">In each sample, 1.0&#x02009;&#x003bc;g of total RNA was used to construct miRNA libraries with a unique index using the TruSeq Small RNA Sample Preparation kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA) according to the manufacturer&#x02019;s instruction. PCR amplification was performed for 11 cycles and gel purification was used to individually purify libraries with unique indices. Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed for library quantification using the StepOnePlus&#x02122; Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, Carlsbad, CA) and KAPA SYBR Fast ABI Prism qPCR kit (Kapa Biosystems, Woburn, MA). Individual libraries were then pooled for sequencing at G&#x000e9;nome Qu&#x000e9;bec (Montr&#x000e9;al, Canada) using the HiSeq 2000 system (Illumina) to generate 50 b single reads. All the reads were demultiplexed according to their index sequences using CASAVA version 1.8 (Illumina) and reads that did not pass the Illumina chastity filter were removed from the dataset. The small RNAs sequencing reads with good quality were subjected to 3&#x02019; adaptor sequence trimming, and all reads were mapped by blastn to the non-coding RNA sequences (Rfam) to remove sequences belonging to tRNA, snoRNA, rRNA, and other non-coding RNAs.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><h3>Identification of miRNAs</h3><p id="__p14" class="p p-first">The miRNAs were identified using the methods outlined in a previous study<sup><a href="#b32" rid="b32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264162">32</a></sup>. Briefly, known miRNAs were identified by mapping the filtered 18 to 25 nt sequences to miRBase (release version 20), a searchable database of published miRNA sequences and annotation<sup><a href="#b33" rid="b33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264207">33</a></sup>. All reads from 16 libraries were pooled to predict novel miRNA using miRDeep2 based on the reference genome sequence of OAR3.1 (<a href="http://www.livestockgenomics.csiro.au/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.livestockgenomics.csiro.au/</a>). Small RNA sequences with a miRDeep2 score higher than 5 and read numbers larger than 10 were defined as novel miRNAs in sheep. The novel miRNA precursor sequences were then combined with the known miRNA precursor sequences to form a new custom reference database. Sequencing reads from different samples were mapped to the new custom reference database to get the read number for the known and novel miRNAs for each sample. Homologous miRNAs were identified by the method described previously<sup><a href="#b34" rid="b34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264224">34</a></sup>.</p><p id="__p15">The conservation of known miRNAs was analyzed based on the definitions for &#x0201c;highly conserved&#x0201d;, &#x0201c;conserved&#x0201d;, and &#x0201c;poorly conserved&#x0201d; from Targetscan<sup><a href="#b35" rid="b35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264175">35</a></sup>. More specifically, highly conserved miRNAs are those conserved across most vertebrates; conserved miRNAs are those conserved across most mammals, but usually not beyond placental mammals; poorly conserved miRNAs are those not present in the above two groups. In this study, ovine-specific miRNAs were defined by using two criteria: 1) they belong to a poorly conserved group; 2) their seed region sequences have only been reported previously in sheep.</p><p id="__p16" class="p p-last">The genomic location of the miRNAs was searched for using the UCSC Genome Browser (<a href="http://genome.ucsc.edu/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://genome.ucsc.edu/</a>) based on the reference genome sequence of OAR3.1 (<a href="http://www.livestockgenomics.csiro.au/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.livestockgenomics.csiro.au/</a>). The miRNA genes are distributed across chromosomes either individually, or in clusters. A cluster is a group of miRNA genes located within a short distance (10&#x02009;Kb) on the same chromosome, based on the definition in the miRBase database (<a href="http://www.mirbase.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.mirbase.org</a>). In the present study, all the known and novel miRNAs were grouped into various clusters based on their genomic location.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><h3>piRNA characterization</h3><p id="__p17" class="p p-first-last">To identify piRNAs, sequencing reads that ranged from 26 to 32 nt were mapped to the ovine genome by Bowtie (version 1.0.1). Reads that could not be perfectly mapped to the genome were discarded, and the remainders were de-duplicated to unique sequences. The filtered unique reads were subjected to an online predictor (<a href="http://59.79.168.90/piRNA/analysis.php" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://59.79.168.90/piRNA/analysis.php</a>), that relies on the training sets from non-piRNA and piRNA sequences of five model species sequenced: rat, mouse, human, fruit fly and nematode, to predict piRNA candidates<sup><a href="#b36" rid="b36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264198">36</a></sup>. The positions in the ovine genome of these candidates were obtained by Bowtie and, to avoid confusion caused by multiple locations, only those with a single location were further analyzed. The piRNAs in each library were quantified by blastn and customized perl scripts. All the sequencing data were deposited in a publicly available Gene Expression Omnibus database (<a href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink-NCBI:geo&amp;article-id=4440528&amp;issue-id=247233&amp;journal-id=1579&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=Entrez%7CTerm%7CGEO%20DataSets&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>), the data are accessible through GEO accession number <a id="__tag_450264232" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE62797" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE62797"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE62797","term_id":"62797"}}</span>GSE62797</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><h3>Identification of differentially expressed (DE) miRNAs and piRNAs</h3><p id="__p18" class="p p-first-last">Differential expression (DE) of miRNA/piRNAs between the nutritional treatments was investigated using the bioinformatics tool, edgeR<sup><a href="#b37" rid="b37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264159">37</a></sup>, that utilizes a negative binomial distribution to model sequencing data. The expression of miRNAs/piRNAs in each library was normalized to counts per million reads (CPM) by the following formula: CPM&#x02009;=&#x02009;(number of miRNAs/piRNAs reads/total reads number per library)&#x02009;&#x000d7;&#x02009;1,000,000. miRNAs/piRNAs with CPM &#x0003e; 5 in at least 50% of the samples were subjected to analysis of differential expression. Fold change (FC) was defined as the ratio (low diet/high diet) of the arithmetic means of CPM values. Significant differential expression was accepted when false discovery rate (FDR) was&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05 based on Benjamini and Hochberg multiple-testing correction<sup><a href="#b38" rid="b38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264229">38</a></sup>, as well as FC&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.67 or&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;1.5<sup><a href="#b39" rid="b39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264223">39</a></sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec"><h3>Validation of miRNA expression using stem-loop qRT-PCR</h3><p id="__p19" class="p p-first-last">The TAQMAN miRNA assay was used to validate miRNA expression following the manufacturer&#x02019;s recommendation (Applied Biosystems). In brief, cDNAs were reverse transcribed from 10 ng total RNA, using 5 X specific miRNA RT primer, and then amplified using a 20 X TAQMAN miRNA assay. StepOnePlus&#x02122; Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) was used to detect the fluorescence signal. miRNAs with cycle threshold (Ct) values&#x02009;&#x0003e;&#x02009;35 were considered as having not been expressed. In this study, U6 snRNA was used as an internal control<sup><a href="#b40" rid="b40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264231">40</a></sup> and three biological replicates were performed. The 2<sup>-&#x00394;&#x00394;Ct</sup> method was used to analyze the expression level and all statistical analyses were carried out using SPSS software (Version 20). One-way ANOVA was used to compare the groups, and P&#x02009;&#x0003c;&#x02009;0.05 was considered significant. Among the remaining 8.7 million reads, we identified 1.9 million unknown small RNAs, 5.5 million known and 1.3 million novel small RNAs, resulting in the identification of 110 known miRNAs and 194 putative novel miRNAs candidates from the ovine testis. All novel miRNA candidates were mapped to all vertebrate miRNAs in miRBase, a database of published miRNA sequences and annotation, to identify the homologues of novel miRNAs (<a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S3</a>). Based on the definition in Targetscan, among the 110 known and 194 novel miRNA candidates identified in sheep testis, 41 known and 62 novel miRNA candidates were highly conserved, 28 known and 21 novel miRNA candidates were conserved, and 36 known and 42 novel miRNA candidates were poorly conserved. From the poorly conserved group, 5 known and 69 novel miRNA candidates were sheep-specific and found in all of our animals. Importantly, we have also shown how the expression of these small RNAs changes in response to under-nutrition in association with apoptosis in germ cells. Our findings strongly support the hypothesis that the decline in sperm production and sperm quality induced by under-nutrition<sup><a href="#b4" rid="b4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264200">4</a></sup> in the sexually mature sheep are mediated at least partly by increased apoptosis in germ cells.</p><div id="__sec24" class="sec"><h3>Profile of small RNAs in sheep testis</h3><p id="__p35" class="p p-first-last">Around 63% of the known miRNAs and 43% of the novel miRNAs that we detected are conserved or highly conserved, suggesting that their biological functions are conserved across species<sup><a href="#b45" rid="b45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264167">45</a></sup>. The present study also revealed 5 known and 69 novel miRNAs that are sheep-specific, most with functions focused on synthesis of lipid or hormones, or production of sperm. This differed from the conserved miRNAs, suggesting that sheep-specific miRNAs may represent an important source of novel functionalities during evolution, an idea previously raised for the human and the mouse<sup><a href="#b46" rid="b46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264188">46</a></sup>. The ten most highly ranked miRNAs are relatively well conserved across species &#x02013; for example, four miRNAs (miR-143, let-7a, let-7f, miR-148a) in pig testis<sup><a href="#b47" rid="b47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264222">47</a></sup> and two miRNAs (let-7a, let-7f) in human testis<sup><a href="#b48" rid="b48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264221">48</a></sup> ranked within the ten most highly expressed miRNAs found in this study. This suggests that they play similar roles in the control of testis function for a variety of mammalian species. To our knowledge, the present study is the first to profile piRNAs in sheep testis. About 10% of the small RNAs were predicted to be putative piRNAs, similar to pigs (13%), mice (10%) and humans (9%)<sup><a href="#b20" rid="b20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264182">20</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b48" rid="b48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264230">48</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b49" rid="b49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264171">49</a></sup>. However, in contrast to miRNAs, the sequences of the putative piRNAs are weakly conserved among species<sup><a href="#b50" rid="b50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264180">50</a></sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec25" class="sec"><h3>Impact of under-nutrition on miRNAs and apoptosis</h3><p id="__p36" class="p p-first">The predicted targets of the 44 miRNAs that were differentially expressed (DE) between well-fed and under-fed sheep were related to tissue morphology and development of the reproductive system. Among all the DE miRNAs, there were 24, including novel-miR-31, that were more highly expressed in well-fed sheep than in under-fed sheep. Novel-miR-31 is homologous with miR-34 which has been shown to enhance germ cell phenotype during the late stages of spermatogenesis in other species<sup><a href="#b51" rid="b51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264228">51</a></sup>. Under-nutrition was associated with greater expression of 20 miRNAs, including miR-99a, which reduces the expression of the tight-junction-related protein, ZO-1<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373534">5</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b52" rid="b52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264163">52</a></sup>.</p><p id="__p37">The predicted targets of top ten expressed miRNAs were functionally related to diseases of the reproductive system, connective tissue function and development, tissue morphology and cellular growth and proliferation, suggesting a critical role in regulating reproductive performance. Under-nutrition did not affect the composition of the top ten, but did affect the expression of three of them - specifically, underfed males showed higher expression of oar-miR-10b and oar-miR-26a, and lower expression of novel-miR-31 (miR-34c), than well-fed males. The genes that were up-regulated in underfed males have been implicated in the induction of apoptosis (miR-26a)<sup><a href="#b53" rid="b53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264173">53</a></sup> and testis dysfunction (miR-10b)<sup><a href="#b54" rid="b54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264170">54</a></sup>, whereas novel-miR-31(miR-34c), which was up-regulated in well-fed males, enhances germinal phenotypes in late spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#b51" rid="b51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264184">51</a></sup>. These roles are consistent with the conclusion that, during the loss of testis mass with under-nutrition, apoptosis is induced and spermatogenesis is disrupted.</p><p id="__p38">We had hypothesized that increased apoptosis is one of the main causes of the negative effect of under-nutrition on sperm production<sup><a href="#b4" rid="b4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264156">4</a></sup> and spermatogenic efficiency<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373528">5</a></sup>. Underfed sheep had more TUNEL-positive germ cells and higher expression of apoptosis-related genes (<em>FASL</em>, <em>CASP3</em>, <em>TP53</em>)<sup><a href="#b55" rid="b55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264208">55</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b56" rid="b56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264212">56</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b57" rid="b57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264158">57</a></sup> than well-fed sheep, thus supporting our hypothesis. Interestingly, TUNEL-positive germ cells were observed only in the early stages of spermatogenesis (spermatogonia and spermatocytes) and not in spermatids or spermatozoa. This observation agrees with reports for other species<sup><a href="#b58" rid="b58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264172">58</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b59" rid="b59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264205">59</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b60" rid="b60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264187">60</a></sup>, suggesting that spermatogenic cells are mostly eliminated before the first meiotic division. However, there are a few reports of apoptosis in spermatids or spermatozoa<sup><a href="#b61" rid="b61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264219">61</a></sup>. It is therefore possible that our TUNEL assay was not able to adequately label spermatids or spermatozoa due to either the compact nature of their DNA or their limited production of mRNA, generally thought to be necessary for apoptotic death<sup><a href="#b61" rid="b61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264213">61</a></sup>.</p><p id="__p39" class="p p-last">We focused our investigation on novel-miR-144 (homolog: miR-98) for two reasons: first, novel-miR-144 was up-regulated in underfed sheep; second, novel-miR-144 targets four of the apoptosis-related genes (<em>FASL</em>, <em>CASP3</em>, <em>TP53</em>, <em>BCL2L1</em>)<sup><a href="#b26" rid="b26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264217">26</a></sup><sup>,<a href="#b62" rid="b62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264155">62</a></sup>. Our working hypothesis, that the negative effects of under-nutrition on spermatogenic efficiency are mediated by increased apoptosis, is supported by studies in mice<sup><a href="#b63" rid="b63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264203">63</a></sup> where the homologue to novel-miR-144 has been shown to be pro-apoptotic. However, our finding contradicts some previous reports showing that miR-98 is up-regulated in small-cell lung cancer<sup><a href="#b64" rid="b64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264176">64</a></sup> and breast cancer<sup><a href="#b65" rid="b65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264161">65</a></sup>, two conditions associated with cell proliferation rather than apoptosis. We also found a positive correlation between expression of novel-miR-144 and the apoptosis-related genes, <em>FASL</em>, <em>CASP3</em> and <em>TP53,</em> and therefore contradict the conventional wisdom that the expression of miRNAs is negatively correlated with their target genes<sup><a href="#b66" rid="b66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264196">66</a></sup>. However, this type of positive correlation has been reported previously in mice<sup><a href="#b67" rid="b67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264227">67</a></sup> and could be interpreted as miRNAs playing a role in homeostatic mechanisms that maintain stability within the organism. The underfed sheep had increased TUNEL staining indicating increased apoptosis, most due to increased expression of the apoptosis-related genes, <em>FASL</em>, <em>CASP3</em> and <em>TP53.</em> miRNAs are thought to &#x02018;fine tune&#x02019; the physiological balance within an organism, so it is possible that the increased expression of novel-miR-144 was a response to the increase in apoptosis, rather than a cause of the apoptosis. Novel-miR-144 relationships are obviously complex, but we propose that miR-98, and thus its sheep homologue (novel-miR-144), is both pro-apoptotic and anti-apoptotic depending on the physiological condition of organism.</p></div><div id="__sec26" class="sec"><h3>Impact of nutrition on putative piRNAs</h3><p id="__p40" class="p p-first-last">Of the 35 differentially expressed piRNAs found in well-fed and underfed sheep, 60% were derived from intergenic and repeated regions of the genome. Based on observations in pigs, these DE piRNAs may have specific germline functions, including the repression of transposons and other repetitive elements<sup><a href="#b20" rid="b20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264185">20</a></sup>. The remaining 40% of the piRNAs that were differentially expressed in the current study were derived from genes. It is difficult to predict the specific functions of the piRNAs identified in the present study because our current understanding of their function is limited and their sequence identities are poorly conserved among species. We therefore focused only on the gene-derived DE piRNAs and predicted their functions based on previous references &#x02013; for example, feline leukemia virus subgroup C receptor-related protein 2 (FLVCR2), which produces piR-12568 and functions as a calcium transporter and affects reproduction and respiration<sup><a href="#b68" rid="b68" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264160">68</a></sup>. In addition, P-type Ca<sup>2+</sup>-ATPase isoform 4 of the plasma membrane (ATP<sub>2</sub>B<sub>4</sub>), which produces piR-9006, is responsible for sperm motility<sup><a href="#b69" rid="b69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264194">69</a></sup>. Furthermore, five piRNAs (piR-10216, piR-10217, piR-10729, piR-10730, piR-10731) that showed higher expression in underfed than well-fed sheep, were derived from MORF4L1, a gene that is highly expressed during male meiosis and spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#b70" rid="b70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264192">70</a></sup> and seems likely to play a crucial role in male reproduction. To date, the mechanism through which piRNAs regulate sperm production and sperm quality is not clear. However, there has been a suggestion that a piRNA pathway was active in Sertoli cells<sup><a href="#b71" rid="b71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264206">71</a></sup> and, as we have shown, Sertoli cell function is reduced in underfed males<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373531">5</a></sup>. We therefore expect that piRNAs affect male reproduction by regulating the function of Sertoli cells, a hypothesis that needs to be tested in further studies.</p></div><div id="__sec27" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Relationships between miRNAs and piRNAs</h3><p id="__p41" class="p p-first">In well-fed males, the proportion of putative piRNAs, as a percentage of total small RNAs, was greater than the proportion of miRNAs. This relationship was reversed in underfed males. These observations suggest that nutrition has distinct, differential effects on the expression of small RNAs in sheep testis. This relationship could be explained by the higher rate of sperm production in well-fed males compared to underfed males<sup><a href="#b72" rid="b72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373530">72</a></sup>, because piRNAs are specifically expressed in germ line cells<sup><a href="#b73" rid="b73" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264195">73</a></sup>. Interestingly, there was a positive correlation between the proportions of miRNAs and putative piRNAs in testicular tissue, for both underfed and well-fed males, indicating a synergistic relationship between these classes of small RNAs. Further studies are needed to test this hypothesis.</p><p id="__p42" class="p p-last">In conclusion, under-nutrition is associated with increased germ cell apoptosis, as evidenced by increases in TUNEL-positive germ cells and in the expression of genes and miRNAs that are related to apoptosis. Furthermore, in underfed males, the differential expression of miRNAs and piRNAs is likely to help explain the negative effects of nutrition on spermatogenesis, spermatogenic efficiency and sperm quality. These conclusions, combined with the outcomes of research on the effects of nutrition on the reproductive axis of the male sheep<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373529">5</a></sup>, led us to develop a working hypothesis that explains how nutrition affects testis function (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4440528/figure/f6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="f6" rid-ob="ob-f6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_f6"><span>Fig. 6</span></a>). Specifically, under-nutrition leads to a reduction in FSH secretion<sup><a href="#b74" rid="b74" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_450264166">74</a></sup>, a key hormone associated with the function of Sertoli cells and, significant changes in Sertoli cell function, including the disorganization of tight junctions and reversal of cell maturity<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373524">5</a></sup>. The outcome is impaired spermatogenesis and reductions in the number of germ cells per unit mass of testis<sup><a href="#b5" rid="b5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373525">5</a></sup>, and increases in germ cell apoptosis that dramatically reduced the quantity and quality of spermatozoa<sup><a href="#b72" rid="b72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_472373526">72</a></sup>. Small RNAs seem to be involved in these processes. Specifically, miR-98 appears to regulate germ cell apoptosis by changing the expression of <em>CASP3, TP53</em> and <em>FASL</em> in parallel with the direct effects of miR-26. Similarly, changes in expression of miR-99a seem to affect the organization of Sertoli-cell tight junctions by targeting ZO-1<sup>52</sup>. The other small RNAs that we have detected (miR-26, miR-34c, miR-10b, piR-9006, piR-12568) are involved in the control of reproductive function in other species, so might also contribute to the effects of nutrition on spermatogenesis in the sexually mature sheep. The role of piRNAs in this process is far from clear, but the distinct differences between well-fed and underfed males in piRNA expression, and in the proportion of piRNAs as a total of small RNAs, suggest that they also play an important, possibly synergistic, role.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="f6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_f6"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4440528/figure/f6/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="f6" rid-ob="ob-f6"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139927599726096" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4440528_srep10372-f6.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is srep10372-f6.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4440528/bin/srep10372-f6.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139927599726096" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4440528/figure/f6/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_f6"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4440528/figure/f6/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="f6" rid-ob="ob-f6">Figure 6</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p51">A working hypothesis of the mechanisms through which nutrition affects testis function in the sexually mature sheep, indicating roles that could be played by small RNAs.
