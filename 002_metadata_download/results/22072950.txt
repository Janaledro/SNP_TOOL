As an alternative to pathway analysis, we have developed a joint multivariate and network-based approach to analysis of a dataset of habitual dietary records, adipose tissue transcriptomics and comprehensive plasma marker profiles from human volunteers with the Metabolic Syndrome. With this approach we identified prominent co-expressed sub-networks in the global metabolic network, which showed correlated expression with habitual n-3 PUFA intake and urinary levels of the oxidative stress marker 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub>. These sub-networks illustrated inherent cross-talk between distinct metabolic pathways, such as between triglyceride metabolism and production of lipid signalling molecules. In a parallel promoter analysis, we identified several adipogenic transcription factors as potential transcriptional regulators associated with habitual n-3 PUFA intake. Our results illustrate advantages of network-based analysis, and generate novel hypotheses on the transcriptomic link between habitual n-3 PUFA intake, adipose tissue function and oxidative stress.</p></div></div><div id="idm140271310565392" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140271310565392title">Author Summary</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">A fundamental goal in the field of nutritional genomics is defining the molecular link between diet and health. Human nutritional genomic studies are frequently hindered by a high level of unexplained variation in gene expression, protein and metabolite abundance, and clinical parameters &#x02013; potentially attributable to variation in genotype, background diet, anthropometry, physical activity and health status. In our present study, relationships between adipose tissue gene expression, habitual diet and clinical markers of metabolic health were investigated in a cohort of individuals with impaired metabolic health, typical of the Metabolic Syndrome (MetS). Using multivariate statistics in conjunction with a novel approach to metabolic network analysis, we identified regions of the human metabolic network showing coordinated transcriptomic response to variation in n-3 PUFA intake and correlation with markers of metabolic health.</p></div></div><div id="s1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first">Dietary fat intake has profound effects on molecular processes of metabolic health. These effects are diverse and often subtle, representing a considerable analytical challenge in reaching system-level understanding. Transcriptomics has become a central technology in the development of molecular nutrition, having the capacity to produce expression data for every gene in a given genome. However, the major challenge is to apply appropriate techniques for extracting information from high-throughput datasets. Differentially expressed gene lists are an intuitive first choice, but they are hard to interpret in a biological context. Pathway analysis &#x02013; typically implemented using gene set enrichment analysis &#x02013; has become a standard method in the field of transcriptomic analysis <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Werner1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Werner1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483778">[1]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Song1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Song1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483784">[2]</a>. It is easy to implement and can simplify and contextualize large lists of differentially expressed genes, although this approach possesses technical limitations due to inherent redundancy among pathways and interconnectedness between one pathway and the next. Failure to appropriately account for these features can substantially limit biological interpretation of high-throughput datasets.</p><p id="__p5">Network-level analysis has revealed detailed insight on metabolic regulation in type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Liu1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Liu1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483806">[3]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Zelezniak1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Zelezniak1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483793">[4]</a>. Del Sol <em>et al.,</em> have proposed that in the emerging systems-level view of molecular biology, diseases should be viewed as a function of network perturbation rather than as isolated local changes <a href="#pcbi.1002223-delSol1" rid="pcbi.1002223-delSol1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483795">[5]</a>. Molecular networks may be classified in two categories: metabolic networks and protein interaction networks. Metabolic networks are inclusive, intuitive abstractions for representing system-level metabolism, as they incorporate all known metabolic interactions in a given species. Due to their size and complexity, however, they are analytically challenging. Previously applied analytical approaches include topological analysis (<em>e.g.,</em> identification of hub nodes and functional modules) <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Lacroix1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Lacroix1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483776">[6]</a> and reporter metabolite analysis <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Zelezniak1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Zelezniak1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483791">[4]</a>.</p><p id="__p6" class="p p-last">A number of methods exist for analyzing transcriptomic data in the context of a global interaction network <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Wu1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Wu1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483772">[7]</a>. The majority of these methods focus on protein interaction networks, and aim to partition a global network into clusters/modules of genes, and identify clusters showing coordinated transcriptomic response. When modelling transcriptomic activity in metabolic networks, however, it is instructive to use path (rather than cluster) constructs, because paths match the native pattern of energy flux through a metabolic network. Cluster-based analysis of metabolic network activity performs well in identifying regions of a network with altered transcriptomic activity, but the identified clusters may contain disconnected sections of different paths of metabolite conversion. A given path of interest may thus be fractioned across several different neighbouring clusters, making it difficult to identify coordinated alteration of activity across that entire path. We therefore defined a method that identifies altered transcriptomic activity in the context of network paths rather than clusters. With this approach, we identified local coexpressed paths in the metabolic network showing covariance with recorded dietary intake of n-3 PUFA, and correlation with a urinary marker of oxidative stress. In a parallel analysis, investigation of the promoter regions of n-3 PUFA-correlated genes highlighted significantly over-represented binding sites for transcription factors related to adipogenesis.</p></div><div id="s2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s2title">Materials and Methods</h2><div id="s2a" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Ethics statement</h3><p id="__p7" class="p p-first-last">The LIPGENE human dietary intervention study was a randomized, controlled trial that complied with the 1983 Helsinki Declarations, approved by the local ethics committees of the 8 intervention centres (Dublin, Ireland; Reading, UK; Oslo, Norway; Marseille, France; Maastricht, The Netherlands; Cordoba, Spain; Krakow, Poland; Uppsala, Sweden). Written informed consent was attained from every participant as approved by each institutional ethical committee.</p></div><div id="s2b" class="sec"><h3>Study design</h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first">The current study was conducted within the framework of the LIPGENE Integrated Project &#x0201c;Diet, genomics and the metabolic syndrome: an integrated nutrition, agro-food, social and economic analysis&#x0201d; (Clinical Trials. gov number: <a id="__tag_594483812" class="tag_hotlink" href="https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT00429195" ref="reftype=extlink-clinical-trial&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CResource%7CClinical%20Trials%7CClinicalTrials.gov&amp;rendering-type=normal"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"clinical-trial","attrs":{"text":"NCT00429195","term_id":"NCT00429195"}}</span>NCT00429195</a>) and NuGO, The Nutrigenomics Organization (<a href="http://www.nugo.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3207936&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.nugo.org</a>); both European Union FP 6 initiatives. intravenous glucose challenge.</div></div></div></div><div id="s2c" class="sec"><h3>Adipose tissue biopsy collection, RNA extraction and microarray hybridization</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first">Subcutaneous adipose tissue samples were taken from the periumbilical area of 19 volunteers from the Norwegian and Spanish cohorts (10 female, 9 male) after an overnight fast. Needle biopsies were obtained after a 5 mm transdermal incision under local anaesthesia. Samples were rinsed in saline, put in RNA later and frozen immediately (&#x02212;80&#x000b0;C) for subsequent analysis. Total RNA was extracted from adipose tissue using the RNeasy lipid tissue mini kit (Qiagen, U.K.). Briefly, 100 mg of adipose tissue was homogenised in Qiazol lysis reagent. After addition of chloroform, the homogenate was centrifuged to separate the aqueous and organic phases. Ethanol was added to the upper aqueous phase, and applied to the RNeasy spin column, where the total RNA was bound to the membrane, and phenol and other contaminants were washed away. RNA was then eluted in RNase-free water.</p><p id="__p11" class="p p-last">Extracted RNA was sent to ServiceXS (a high-throughput data service provider; <a href="http://www.servicexs.com" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3207936&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.servicexs.com</a>) for labelling with the 3&#x02019; IVT express kit and hybridization to Affymetrix arrays. The microarray platform used in this study was custom designed by NuGO, and contained 16554 probe sets. This platform is designated &#x02018;nugohs1a520180&#x02019;, and we used the &#x02018;entrezg&#x02019; version 12.1.0 annotation from the MBNI custom cdf database, reflecting the latest remapping of Affymetrix probes based on current data in the NCBI database (<a href="http://brainarray.mbni.med.umich.edu" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3207936&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://brainarray.mbni.med.umich.edu</a>). Raw and GCRMA-normalized data are available from the Gene Expression Omnibus database, under accession <a id="__tag_594483810" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28070" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28070"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE28070","term_id":"28070"}}</span>GSE28070</a>.</p></div><div id="s2d" class="sec"><h3>Microarray QC and pre-processing</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">Raw microarray data were first assessed for quality using a set of standard QC tests, including array intensity distribution, positive and negative border element distribution, GAPDH and &#x003b2;-actin 3&#x02019;/5&#x02019; ratios, centre of intensity and array-array correlation check. All QC tests were implemented in the R programming language (Version 2.11.1l, R Foundation for Statistical Computing), using the affyQCReport library. A batch effect was noted due to the arrays being hybridized on two separate days; thus, all subsequent analyses accounted for this effect by including batch number as a covariate in statistical models. It was also noted that the &#x003b2;-actin 3&#x02019;/5&#x02019; ratios were higher than recommended (<em>i.e.,</em> greater than 3-fold intensity difference) in most samples, although this ratio has been shown to be higher when cRNA is synthesized using the Affymetrix 3&#x02019; IVT express kit, particularly with low input RNA quantities (<a href="http://media.affymetrix.com/support/technical/whitepapers/3_ivtexpresskit_whitepaper.pdf" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3207936&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://media.affymetrix.com/support/technical/whitepapers/3_ivtexpresskit_whitepaper.pdf</a>). Furthermore, all samples except 2 showed 3&#x02019;/5&#x02019; GAPDH ratios within expected range of 1.25-fold, and no samples appeared suspect in RNA degradation plots. The 2 samples that did not meet the GAPDH ratio recommendation were removed from further analysis. All QC-verified samples were background corrected and normalized using the GCRMA normalization method, which accounts for nucleotide specific differences in hybridization efficiency. The normalized dataset was then filtered to remove genes with Mas5 &#x02018;absent&#x02019; call on all arrays, and those showing the lowest 10&#x00025; variance, resulting in a final dataset of 10618 genes.</p></div><div id="s2e" class="sec"><h3>Statistical analysis of diet-gene and clinical marker-gene associations</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first">Diet and plasma marker variables were first normalized with log or square root transformation as appropriate to reduce skewness and kurtosis. Sparse partial least squares regression (sPLS; <a href="#pcbi.1002223-KimAnh1" rid="pcbi.1002223-KimAnh1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483765">[11]</a>) and regularized canonical correlation analysis (rCCA; <a href="#pcbi.1002223-LeCao1" rid="pcbi.1002223-LeCao1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483808">[12]</a>) were used to assess relationships between dietary components and gene expression levels, and between clinical markers and gene expression levels, respectively. The mixOmics library of R functions was used to carry out the analysis <a href="#pcbi.1002223-LeCao1" rid="pcbi.1002223-LeCao1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483768">[12]</a>. Specifically, the <em>spls</em> function was used to fit the sPLS model, and the <em>network</em> function to produce the network of interactions. An sPLS model was fitted using dietary variables, sex, nationality and array batch number as predictors (sex, nationality and array batch number were included in order to identify and control for correlations between gene expression and these variables), and gene expression as response variables. Choice of PLS dimensions was determined using the <em>Q<sub>h</sub><sup>2</sup></em> variable previously proposed by Tenenhaus <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Tenenhaus1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Tenenhaus1" class=" bibr popnode">[13]</a> which measures the relative contribution of each dimension <em>h</em> to the predictive power of the PLS model (see 11 and 13 for further details on sPLS and use of <em>Q<sub>h</sub><sup>2</sup></em>). With this approach, we retained 5 dimensions in the model, and retained all diet-gene pairs showing a similarity score &#x0003e;0.7 (using &#x02018;threshold&#x02019; argument of the <em>network</em> function in the mixOmics library). This similarity score is a convention used in multivariate statistical methods; it ranges from 0 to 1, and corresponds to the distance between two given variables in the number of chosen dimensions <a href="#pcbi.1002223-IgnacioGonzalez1" rid="pcbi.1002223-IgnacioGonzalez1" class=" bibr popnode">[14]</a>.</p><p id="__p14" class="p p-last">The mixOmics library was used for rCCA modelling of plasma marker and gene expression data. The <em>rcc</em> function was used to define the canonical correlations and the canonical variates, <em>estim.regul</em> for estimation of regularization parameters and the <em>network</em> function to produce the network of interactions. In this case, datasets were not interpreted as predictors or responses given the more complex two-way relationship between plasma marker profile and tissue gene expression. Initial rCCA modelling including all plasma markers showed that correlations between gene expression and plasma fatty acid and lipid profile were so strong that they masked more subtle correlations between the remaining plasma markers and expression data. Consequently, separate rCCA analyses were performed: first, comparing adipose tissue gene expression to plasma fatty acids, lipids and apolipoprotein profile (including sex, nationality and array batch number as variables in the model); and second, comparing gene expression to plasma cytokines, IVGTT measurements, prostaglandin and urinary isoprostane (as before, including sex, nationality and array batch number as variables in the model). For comparison of gene expression vs. plasma fatty acids, lipids and apolipoproteins, the first 11 dimensions were retained in the model (as subsequent dimensions did not provide additional information to the model) and all gene-plasma marker pairs with a similarity score &#x0003e;0.75 were retained for subsequent analysis. Due to the very strong relationship between gene expression and plasma fatty acids, we observed that using a similarity score threshold of 0.7 resulted in a very high number of plasma marker-gene correlations (571 plasma marker-gene pairs passing threshold). Therefore, the higher threshold was chosen in this comparison in order to highlight only the strongest plasma marker-gene correlations, thereby facilitating downstream biological interpretation. For the second comparison (gene expression vs plasma cytokines, IVGTT measurements, prostaglandin and urinary isoprostane) the first 6 dimensions and all gene-plasma marker pairs with a similarity score &#x0003e;0.7, were retained in the model.</p></div><div id="s2f" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic network analysis</h3><p id="__p15" class="p p-first">We used the Edinburgh human metabolic network reconstruction <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Ma1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Ma1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483803">[15]</a>; (<a href="http://www.ehmn.bioinformatics.ed.ac.uk/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3207936&amp;issue-id=202084&amp;journal-id=328&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.ehmn.bioinformatics.ed.ac.uk/</a>), which contains reaction information for 1627 unique metabolites and 1371 unique metabolic enzymes. In its native form, this reconstruction is a metabolite-centred network (<em>i.e.,</em> nodes represent metabolites and edges are the enzymes that catalyze reactions between metabolites). For our analysis, the network was first transformed to an enzyme-centric construction (where 2 genes/proteins are linked if gene 1 produces a metabolite that is used as a metabolic substrate by gene 2). As is the norm in topology-based network analyses, we excluded currency metabolites (such as H<sub>2</sub>O, ATP and O<sub>2</sub>) from the network <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Ma2" rid="pcbi.1002223-Ma2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483800">[16]</a>.</p><div id="s2f1" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Assessment of gene-gene coexpression in the human metabolic network </h4><p id="__p16" class="p p-first-last">Coexpression was assessed for each gene-gene pair in the metabolic network reconstruction using Akaike's information criterion (AIC), a criterion used to select an optimal model among competing possibilities <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Akaike1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Akaike1" class=" bibr popnode">[17]</a>. As our network construction contains directionality information, coexpression between two genes could be assessed using a linear model of gene 2 expression as a function of gene 1 expression. In our human sample, however, we would expect the variables <em>sex</em> and <em>nationality</em> to be related to expression of some (but not all) genes in the dataset. The AIC approach allows selection of the optimal model among all possible combinations of predictor variables, thus including additional variables only where appropriate. The exact value of AIC is determined by </p><div class="disp-formula"><div class="f"><img src="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/bin/pcbi.1002223.e001.jpg" class="eq-image" alt="equation image" title="equation image" /></div></div><p>where <em>k</em> refers to the number of parameters in the statistical model, and L is the maximized likelihood value for the fitted model. An optimal model will have high likelihood while being parsimonious; thus, lower AIC values indicate a better model. As each gene pair in the network possesses directionality information (<em>i.e.,</em> gene 1 produces a compound that is metabolized by gene 2), expression level of gene 2 can be estimated as a function of gene 1 expression plus additional factors, <em>batch, sex</em> and <em>nationality: </em>
</p><div class="disp-formula"><div class="f"><img src="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/bin/pcbi.1002223.e002.jpg" class="eq-image" alt="equation image" title="equation image" /></div></div><p>where <em>Y</em> and <em>X</em> represent expression of gene 2 and 1, respectively; <em>b</em>, <em>s</em> and <em>n</em> represent batch, sex and nationality; <em>&#x003b2;<sub>0,</sub> &#x003b2;<sub>1,</sub> &#x003b2;<sub>2,</sub> &#x003b2;<sub>3</sub></em> and <em>&#x003b2;<sub>4</sub></em> represent the intercept and partial regression coefficients for each variable; and &#x003b5; represents random error. We would expect a batch effect to be present across the entire platform; thus, this variable was included in all models. The AIC values were calculated for models containing <em>b</em> plus all possible combinations of the additional factors (<em>X, s</em> and <em>n</em>); gene 1 and gene 2 were considered to be coexpressed if gene 1 expression was present as a predictor variable in the optimal model.</p></div></div><div id="s2g" class="sec"><h3>Extraction of diet-sensitive paths from the transcriptionally coordinated network</h3><p id="__p17" class="p p-first">To identify paths of interest in the global interaction network, Dijkstra's shortest paths <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Dijkstra1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Dijkstra1" class=" bibr popnode">[18]</a> were calculated from each diet-sensitive node to all others in the coexpressed subset of the global network (as determined above), taking into consideration directionality of node pair interactions. An algorithm was written in R to evaluate metabolic feasibility of each putative path &#x02013; <em>i.e.</em> whether an unbroken path of metabolite conversion could be traced from one end to another (most recent scripts available on request). This concept of metabolic feasibility is an important consideration in analysis of global networks, because a connected path through the network does not necessarily indicate an unbroken path of metabolite conversion. <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g001/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g001" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g001" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g001"><span>Figure 1</span></a> illustrates the rationale behind metabolic feasibility (see supplementary <a href="#pcbi.1002223.s001" rid="pcbi.1002223.s001" class=" supplementary-material">Figure S1</a> for detailed description of the algorithm). The output of this algorithm is a list of feasible paths of metabolite conversion, wherein each path is strongly coexpressed (<em>i.e.,</em> between each gene pair in the path) and possesses a diet-correlated gene at the upstream end. To our knowledge, this is the first metabolic network analysis algorithm that explicitly considers metabolic feasibility and adjacent pair-wise coexpression in analysis of network paths. This represents an informative alternative to network clustering analysis.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="pcbi-1002223-g001" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g001"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g001/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g001" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g001"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140271341268384" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3207936_pcbi.1002223.g001.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pcbi.1002223.g001.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/bin/pcbi.1002223.g001.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140271341268384" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g001/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g001"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g001/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g001" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g001">Figure 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Assessing metabolic feasibility in network paths.</strong><p id="__p18">The algorithm of network analysis in this study includes a two-step process: 1) extraction of connected paths from the node of interest to all others in the network; and 2) evaluation of metabolic feasibility of each candidate path. This 1,2-diacyl-sn-glycerol is further metabolized by <em>DGKA</em> to form phosphatidate. At the downstream end of this path, cytochrome p450 enzymes (<em>MYP19A1, CYP2B6</em> and <em>CYP1B1</em>) act on AA and LA as substrates to form diverse epoxyeicosatrienoic acids (EET), hydroxyeicosatrienoic acids (HETE) and epoxyoctadecenoic acids (EpOME), involved in the resolution of inflammation with subsequent relevance to cardiovascular disease <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Theken1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Theken1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483788">[22]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Tsai1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Tsai1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483794">[23]</a>. Two genes in this path &#x02013; <em>PIK3CA</em>and <em>PIP5K1A</em> &#x02013; were negatively correlated with urinary 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub>; <em>PIKFYVE, PIK3CA,</em> and <em>CEPT1</em> were positively correlated with plasma DHA, and <em>PIP5K1A</em> with plasma stearic acid and dihomo-gamma-linolenic acid.</p><p id="__p29">To assess whether a similar group of paths would be extracted from any TC network &#x02013; <em>e.g.,</em> due to higher connectivity in certain regions of the network &#x02013; we generated such a TC network from publicly available muscle tissue microarray data from obese individuals (GEO accession: <a id="__tag_594483811" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE474" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE474"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE474","term_id":"474"}}</span>GSE474</a>), and extracted paths leading from the n-3 PUFA-sensitive genes identified in the present study. In this muscle tissue TC network we found only 24 paths of maximum length three leading from ten of the n-3-PUFA-sensitive genes (supplementary <a href="#pcbi.1002223.s009" rid="pcbi.1002223.s009" class=" supplementary-material">Table S7</a>). Furthermore, these paths did not intersect to form a larger sub-network.</p><p id="__p30" class="p">To compare our network analysis with a standard approach to pathway analysis, hypergeometric tests were performed to identify KEGG pathways significantly enriched (using the <em>hyperGTest</em> function in the R &#x02018;Category&#x02019; library) for the n-3PUFA-sensitive genes identified in our sPLS analysis. This analysis returned four KEGG pathways greater than length four (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/table/pcbi-1002223-t002/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-t002" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-t002" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). Of these pathways, the top three - biosynthesis of plant hormones, biosynthesis of terpenoids and steroids and biosynthesis of alkaloids derived from terpenoid and polyketide - have 45 genes in common. Accordingly, the same 7 n-3 PUFA-sensitive genes (<em>PMVK</em>, <em>FDFT1</em>, <em>ALDOA</em>, <em>IDH3G</em>, <em>PGK1</em>, <em>SDHB</em>, <em>GGPS1</em>) were present in each pathway. Thus, the apparent enrichment of the biosynthetic plant hormones pathway is probably an artifact of the high degree of overlap with other pathways in the database. Closer inspection of these pathways in the KEGG database showed that they are large, diverse and disjointed pathways, including many parallel processes. For example, the pathway for biosynthesis of terpenoids and steroids includes of subsets of glycolysis, limonene and pinene degradation, terpenoid backbone biosynthesis, carotenoid biosynthesis and geraniol degradation. The n-3 PUFA-correlated genes were distributed across these processes rather than occurring in a single one.</p><!--table ft1--><!--table-wrap mode="anchored" t5--><div class="table-wrap table class="p p-first">
<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g004/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g004" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g004"><span>Figure 4</span></a> illustrates over-represented TFBSs among genes showing positive correlation with the intake of n-3 PUFA (from our sPLS results). The three most significantly over-represented TFBSs were those of Kr&#x000fc;ppel-like factor 4 (KLF4), specificity protein 1 (SP1) and E2F1 transcription factors. KLF4 is involved in adipogenesis, specifically by binding to the promoter of the <em>CEBPB</em> (C/EBP&#x003b2;) gene <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Birsoy1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Birsoy1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483782">[24]</a>. <em>CEBPB</em> was not present in the Edinburgh human metabolic network (as it is not a metabolic enzyme). Thus, an expanded sPLS analysis was performed, comparing dietary intake to all genes on the Affymetrix microarray. This analysis identified <em>CEBPB</em> expression to be positively correlated with n-3 PUFA intake (data not shown). Although no additional adipogenic genes were identified at this correlation threshold of 0.7, reducing the threshold to 0.6 revealed a number of additional adipogenic factors showing positive correlation with n-3 PUFA intake &#x02013; including <em>ADIPOQ, BMP2, CFD, FABP4, LIPE, LPL</em> and <em>PLIN.</em>
</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="pcbi-1002223-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g004"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g004/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g004" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g004"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140271300901920" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3207936_pcbi.1002223.g004.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pcbi.1002223.g004.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/bin/pcbi.1002223.g004.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140271300901920" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g004/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g004"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g004/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g004" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g004">Figure 4</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Significantly over-represented transcription factor binding sites in promoter regions of genes correlated with habitual n-3 PUFA intake.</strong><p id="__p32">The promoter region of each gene is depicted, with coloured boxes denoting binding site location(s) of transcription factors displayed at right. TSS: transcription start site.</p></div></div></div><p id="__p33" class="p p-last">SP1 is a broadly acting transcription factor operating in conjunction with NF-YA, SREBP and PPAR&#x003b3; in promoting lipogenesis. The NF-YA and PPAR&#x003b3; TFBSs were also significantly over-represented in our group of n-3 PUFA-sensitive genes, although SREBP TFBS was not. E2F1 is a transcription factor involved in early adipogenesis, and positively regulates transcription of PPAR&#x003b3; <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Fajas1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Fajas1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483787">[25]</a>. Interestingly, all but one PPAR&#x003b3; TFBS-containing genes in our sample also contained an E2F1 TFBS.</p></div></div><div id="s4" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s4title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p34" class="p p-first">An emerging limitation to pathway analysis of transcriptomic data is that documented pathway models tend to overlap and intersect, yielding analytical results that are biased, incomplete or both <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Hescott1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Hescott1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483767">[26]</a>. Our network approach did not segregate metabolic processes into discrete pathway models, thereby revealing inherent overlaps and intersections between pathways. Examples of this pattern are seen in the <em>ANXA3, PTEN</em> and <em>MTMR12</em> paths, which incorporate connected reactions from the metabolic pathways of inositol phosphate derivatives, glycerolipids, glycerophospholipids, arachidonic and linoleic acid. Intersections of these pathways are clear when viewed in the network context, but less evident when each canonical pathway is assessed separately. Cross-talk between metabolism of lipids/fatty acids and inositol phosphate derivatives plays an important role in the induction of signalling cascades by dietary and fat <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Newton1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Newton1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483805">[27]</a>. <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g003" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g003"><span>Figure 3B-D</span></a> illustrates paths of triglyceride metabolism including formation of intermediate metabolites such as diacylglycerol and phosphatidate. These metabolites act as signalling molecules that affect a wide range of cellular functions like insulin signalling, inflammation, cellular differentiation and proliferation and oxidative stress <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Mrida1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Mrida1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483763">[28]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Brindley1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Brindley1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483775">[29]</a>. Thus, it is of particular interest that genes in the <em>PTEN</em> and <em>MTMR12</em> paths show strong inverse correlation with urinary 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub> &#x02013; a marker of systemic oxidative stress.</p><p id="__p35">Previous work has described an inverse relationship between n-3 PUFA intake and n-6 fatty acid-derived prostaglandins (<em>e.g.,</em> PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub>) in the plasma of Alzheimer's disease patients <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Vedin1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Vedin1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483786">[30]</a>, urine of healthy males <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Mori1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Mori1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483766">[31]</a> and plasma and urine of dyslipidaemic and type 2 diabetic individuals <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Mas1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Mas1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483798">[32]</a>. A prevalent hypothesis for this relationship is that n-3 and n-6 PUFA compete for the same enzyme systems, and consequently, increased n-3 PUFA intake precludes production of n-6 fatty acid-derived prostaglandins <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Simopoulos1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Simopoulos1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483770">[33]</a>. In addition, n-3 PUFA may exert independent anti-inflammatory effects through unique receptors and enzyme systems, regardless of n-6 fatty acid intake <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Oh1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Oh1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483792">[34]</a>. Our analysis identified 17 genes showing opposing direction of correlation with n-3 PUFA intake and urinary 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g002/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g002" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g002" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g002"><span>Figure 2</span></a>). Furthermore, results from network analysis identified precise coexpressed regions of the metabolic network showing positive correlation with dietary n-3 PUFA intake and plasma DHA, and negative correlation with urinary 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub> in the cohort of MetS subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g003" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g003"><span>Figure 3</span></a>). The sub-network illustrated in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g003" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g003"><span>Figure 3A</span></a> is interesting in this context because it contains numerous members of the electron transport chain, including 20 isoforms of ATPase/ATP synthase, six of which were negatively correlated with 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub>. Further investigation of diet-dependent energy flux through these network regions may provide insight on the precise relationship between n-3 PUFA intake and adipose tissue oxidative stress. Comparing n-3 PUFA intake directly to urinary 8-iso-PGF<sub>2&#x003b1;</sub> resulted in only a near-significant trend (p&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.077; p&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.0828 after adjusting for sex); a recent publication of findings from the larger LIPGENE cohort reported a similar near-significant trend <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Petersson1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Petersson1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483789">[35]</a>. This may be due to the number of molecular intermediates between dietary intake and urinary output, highlighting the increased clarity provided by analysis of tissue-level high throughput data in the framework of a global metabolic network.</p><p id="__p36">To understand the potential regulatory consequences of dietary n-3 PUFA intake on adipose tissue biology, we analysed the promoter regions of n-3 PUFA-correlated genes to identify significantly over-represented transcription factor binding sites. Results from this analysis highlighted significantly over-represented transcription factors related to adipogenesis. The most strongly over-represented transcription factors were KLF4, SP1 and E2F1. SP1 and KLF4 share similar GC-rich target binding sites <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Kaczynski1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Kaczynski1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483780">[36]</a>, which is evident in their overlapping binding sites in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3207936/figure/pcbi-1002223-g004/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pcbi-1002223-g004" rid-ob="ob-pcbi-1002223-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pcbi-1002223-g004"><span>Figure 4</span></a>. Although limited work has focused on joint activity of these transcription factors in adipose tissue, KLF4 has been shown to inhibit SP1 activity in the gut by competitive TFBS binding <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Shie1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Shie1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483777">[37]</a>. PPAR&#x003b3; was also identified as significantly over-represented among genes correlated with habitual n-3 PUFA intake. PPAR&#x003b3; is arguably the most well-studied transcription factor in the field of diet-related transcriptomic regulation, and is the subject of many reviews on the subject <a href="#pcbi.1002223-MarionLetellier1" rid="pcbi.1002223-MarionLetellier1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483801">[38]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Siersbaek1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Siersbaek1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483769">[39]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Martin1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Martin1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483804">[40]</a>, <a href="#pcbi.1002223-White1" rid="pcbi.1002223-White1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483796">[41]</a>. In addition to its role as a central regulator of adipogenesis, lipid storage and combustion, PPAR&#x003b3; also protects against oxidative stress and inflammation <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Nunn1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Nunn1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483807">[42]</a>. Dietary n-3 PUFA are potent inducers of PPAR&#x003b3; expression in adipocytes <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Chambrier1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Chambrier1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483783">[43]</a> and preadipocytes <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Hanada1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Hanada1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483779">[44]</a>. Accordingly, n-3 PUFA supplementation has yielded positive effects on weight gain in cancer <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Colomer1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Colomer1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483797">[45]</a> and Alzheimer's patients <a href="#pcbi.1002223-Irving1" rid="pcbi.1002223-Irving1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483774">[46]</a>, and reduced lipotoxicity in a range of experimental models <a href="#pcbi.1002223-PerezMartinez1" rid="pcbi.1002223-PerezMartinez1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_594483771">[47]</a>. Future work should clarify the contribution of these adipogenic transcription factors to adipose tissue function, particularly given the positive correlation in our present study between dietary n-3 PUFA intake and expression of additional adipogenic genes, including <em>CEBPB, ADIPOQ, BMP2, CFD, FABP4, LIPE, LPL</em> and <em>PLIN1.</em>
</p><p id="__p37" class="p p-last">In conclusion, we have taken a joint multivariate and network-based approach to transcriptomic analysis, relying on known metabolic reaction information to reveal coordinated paths of metabolite conversion. This approach highlighted coexpressed regions of the metabolic network with opposing direction of correlation with habitual n-3 PUFA intake and urinary isoprotane levels - relationships that were not identified using a traditional pathway enrichment test. Promoter analysis further highlighted adipogenic transcription factors as potential transcriptional regulators of n-3 PUFA-correlated genes.</p></div><div id="s5" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s5title">Supporting Information</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="pcbi.1002223.s001"><h4>Figure S1</h4><p id="__p38">Schematic illustration of pathway extraction algorithm; data frame at right shows example network data file at each step of algorithm. The key goal of this algorithm is to assess a linked path of nodes (in this case A&#x02192;B&#x02192;C&#x02192;D), to identify if an unbroken path of metabolite conversion can be traced from the first node to the last. Given a node of interest (A) and a network path leading from A (as determined by Djikstra's algorithm; step 1) the algorithm examines each reaction pair in sequence, starting with the pair linked to the node of interest (step 2). The total list of interactions from A&#x02192;B, and B&#x02192;C are extracted from network file (step 3). Metabolites (and associated reactions) are removed from the pair of reactions if they cannot be associated with a path of conversion linking reaction 1 (A&#x02192;B) to reaction 2 (B&#x02192;C) (step 4). Self-linked reactions are included &#x02013; e.g., B&#x02192;B, where node B produces a metabolite through interaction with A, and converts the same metabolite to a different one that is further metabolized by node C (the algorithm only considers a single self-linked loop within each reaction pair, if present). If an unbroken path remains after removal of extraneous metabolites (step 4a), reaction pair is valid and algorithm continues to next pair of reactions (step 5).
