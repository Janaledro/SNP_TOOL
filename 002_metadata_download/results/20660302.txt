E-mail: <a href="mailto:dev@null" data-email="vog.hin.liam@tonik" class="oemail">vog.hin.liam@tonik</a>.</div></div><div class="togglers"><a href="#" class="pmctoggle" rid="idm139714675953504_ai">Author information <span>►</span></a> <a href="#" class="pmctoggle" rid="idm139714675953504_an">Article notes <span>►</span></a> <a href="#" class="pmctoggle" rid="idm139714675953504_cpl">Copyright and License information <span>►</span></a> <a href="/pmc/about/disclaimer/">Disclaimer</a></div><div class="fm-article-notes fm-panel hide half_rhythm" id="idm139714675953504_an" style="display:none"><div class="fm-pubdate half_rhythm">Received 2010 May 13; Accepted 2010 Jun 21.</div></div><div class="fm-cpl-info fm-panel hide half_rhythm" id="idm139714675953504_cpl" style="display:none"><div class="fm-copyright half_rhythm"><a href="/pmc/about/copyright/">Copyright</a>  &#x000a9; 2010 by The Endocrine Society </div></div></div><div id="pmclinksbox" class="links-box whole_rhythm"><div class="small"><div>This article has been <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/citedby/">cited by</a> other articles in PMC.</div></div></div></div><div class="sec"></div><div id="idm139714683570448" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm139714683570448title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">Glucocorticoids play central roles in the regulation of energy metabolism by shifting it toward catabolism, whereas AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) is the master regulator of energy homeostasis, sensing energy depletion and stimulating pathways of increasing fuel uptake and saving on peripheral supplies. We showed here that AMPK regulates glucocorticoid actions on carbohydrate metabolism by targeting the glucocorticoid receptor (GR) and modifying transcription of glucocorticoid-responsive genes in a tissue- and promoter-specific fashion. Activation of AMPK in rats reversed glucocorticoid-induced hepatic steatosis and suppressed glucocorticoid-mediated stimulation of glucose metabolism. Transcriptomic analysis in the liver suggested marked overlaps between the AMPK and glucocorticoid signaling pathways directed mostly from AMPK to glucocorticoid actions. AMPK accomplishes this by phosphorylating serine 211 of the human GR indirectly through phosphorylation and consequent activation of p38 MAPK and by altering attraction of transcriptional coregulators to DNA-bound GR. In human peripheral mononuclear cells, AMPK mRNA expression positively correlated with that of glucocorticoid-responsive glucocorticoid-inducible leucine zipper protein, which correlated also positively with the body mass index of subjects. These results indicate that the AMPK-mediated energy control system modulates glucocorticoid action at target tissues. Because increased action of glucocorticoids is associated with the development of metabolic disorders, activation of AMPK could be a promising target for developing pharmacological interventions to these pathologies.</p></div></div><div id="idm139714750204176" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p4" class="p p-first">Glucocorticoids, the end-effector hormones of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, play crucial roles in the regulation of basal and stress-related homeostasis (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456648">1</a>,<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode">2</a>). These hormones influence functions of virtually every organ and tissue and are necessary for the maintenance of many important biological activities, such as the homeostasis of the central nervous system (CNS) and cardiovascular systems, intermediary metabolism, and the immune/inflammatory reaction, influencing mRNA expression of up to 20% of the expressed genome (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456591">1</a>,<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456607">3</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>,<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456586">5</a>).</p><p id="__p5">Glucocorticoids have strong effects on the energy expenditure of the body, shifting energy balance from anabolism to catabolism by stimulating the synthesis/release of glucose in the liver and facilitating lipolysis in adipose tissues and protein degradation in various organs and tissues, including the skeletal muscles, skin, and bones (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode">2</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>). Glucocorticoids also stimulate appetite in the CNS, facilitating uptake of nutrients (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456629">6</a>). Thus, chronic excess of circulating glucocorticoids or increase of glucocorticoid sensitivity of their target tissues is associated with development of visceral obesity, diabetes mellitus type 2, and hyperlipidemia (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>,<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456654">6</a>,<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456620">7</a>,<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456623">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456656">9</a>).</p><p id="__p6">The glucocorticoid receptor (GR), which belongs to the steroid/sterol/thyroid/retinoid/orphan nuclear receptor superfamily, mediates most of the known actions of glucocorticoids (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456604">3</a>,<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456597">10</a>). The prototype human GR consists of 777 amino acids and three domains: the N-terminal domain; middle, DNA-binding; and C-terminal, ligand-binding domains (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456614">10</a>). Upon hormone binding, the GR translocates from the cytoplasm into the nucleus, and binds its specific DNA recognition sequences, the glucocorticoid response elements (GREs), in the regulatory regions of glucocorticoid-responsive genes or interacts with other transcription factors and modifies their actions on their target genes (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456608">10</a>,<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456638">11</a>). DNA-bound GR modulates the transcription of regulated coding sequences by attracting numerous cofactor and coregulator proteins and protein complexes, such as the histone acetyltransferase coactivator complexes, and the mating-type switching/sucrose non-fermenting (SWI/SNF) and vitamin D receptor-interacting protein/thyroid hormone receptor-associated protein chromatin-remodeling complexes, eventually influencing the activity of RNA polymerase II and its ancillary factors (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456559">10</a>,<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456603">12</a>).</p><p id="__p7">Because glucocorticoids are essential for survival and strongly influence the energy balance of the body, several distinct signaling pathways regulate the transcriptional activity of the GR by affecting various activation steps of this receptor (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456557">3</a>,<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456563">10</a>). Such major mechanisms include posttranslational modifications of the GR, such as phosphorylation, acetylation, methylation, nitrosylation, and small ubiquitin-like modifier protein (SUMO)ylation (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456633">10</a>). Indeed, several kinases, including the cell cycle-related and mitogen-activated kinases, located downstream of cell surface receptors for growth factors and cytokines, phosphorylate specific serine or threonine residues of the GR and change its transcriptional activity (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456573">13</a>,<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456626">14</a>,<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456554">15</a>,<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456617">16</a>,<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456598">17</a>,<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456599">18</a>,<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456645">19</a>,<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456606">20</a>). In addition, we recently found that the cyclin-dependent kinase 5, a brain-specific serine/threonine kinase essential for fetal brain development and function, phosphorylated the GR at multiple serine residues located in the N-terminal domain and modulated GR-induced transcriptional activity in a promoter-specific fashion (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456585">21</a>,<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456549">22</a>).</p><p id="__p8">AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), also an evolutionarily well-conserved serine/threonine kinase, plays central roles as a cellular sensor of metabolic needs and regulator of the body&#x02019;s energy balance (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456556">23</a>). AMPK is composed of three subunits, &#x003b1;, &#x003b2;, and &#x003b3;, each of which consists of multiple isoforms with different biological activities (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456596">23</a>,<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456653">24</a>). Depletion of intracellular ATP and hence elevation of AMP leads to activation of AMPK by upstream kinases, such as AMPK kinase, liver kinase B1, and calmodulin kinase kinase, through phosphorylation of threonine 172 of the &#x003b1;-subunit of the human AMPK, in cooperation with other activation mechanisms, including allosteric activation and inhibition of dephosphorylation by protein phosphatases (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456610">24</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456547">25</a>).</p><p id="__p9">This effect of AMP can be mimicked by its synthetic analog 5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide-1-&#x003b2;-d-ribonucleoside (AICAR), which is generally used experimentally as a stimulator of AMPK (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456576">24</a>,<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456636">26</a>,<a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456566">27</a>,<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456592">28</a>). Activation of AMPK by AMP or AICAR phosphorylates downstream substrate molecules, such as the insulin receptor substrate-1, the p38 MAPK, the acetyl-coenzyme A carboxylase, the 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaryl-coenzyme A reductase, and the hormone-sensitive lipase (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456551">24</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456590">25</a>,<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456569">29</a>,<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_309307165">30</a>,<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456560">31</a>,<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456649">32</a>,<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456630">33</a>,<a href="#B34" rid="B34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456581">34</a>,<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456611">35</a>).</p><p id="__p10">Activation of these substrates by AMPK then shift the functions of various organs toward increasing energy supply and reducing anabolism (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456659">23</a>). Such alterations include an increase of appetite in CNS, stimulation of glucose uptake in the heart and skeletal muscles, inhibition of gluconeogenesis in the liver, inhibition of insulin release from the pancreas, and suppression of fat accumulation/synthesis in adipose tissue (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456624">23</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456589">25</a>). Interestingly, glucocorticoids also strongly influence all of these activities (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode">2</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>).</p><p id="__p11" class="p">We hypothesized that these two major signaling pathways crosstalk at numerous points. We demonstrate here that AMPK differentially modulates glucocorticoid action by phosphorylating the human GR at serine 211 indirectly through the activation of p38 MAPK. This regulation is evident in the liver because AMPK suppresses glucocorticoid-induced gene expression of rate-limiting enzymes for gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis, ultimately attenuating glucocorticoid-induced hepatic steatosis and the elevation of blood glucose levels.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">Results</h2><div id="__sec2" class="sec sec-first"><h3>AMPK attenuates dexamethasone-induced elevation of serum glucose and liver steatosis in rats</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first">To examine our hypothesis, we first studied the influence of AMPK on the effect of glucocorticoids <em>in vivo</em> by injecting the synthetic glucocorticoid dexamethasone and/or the AMPK activator AICAR into rats (Fig. 1<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -0.5em;">1).</span></span><span></span></a>). Interestingly, we found that GILZ mRNA expression also correlated positively with BMI, suggesting that subjects with higher BMI have elevated GR-induced transcriptional activity or hypersensitivity to glucocorticoids (Fig. 8C<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1em;">8C</span></span><span></span></a>).</p><p id="__p40">Comparison between AMPK&#x003b1;1 and GILZ mRNA expression revealed that these two parameters also correlated with each other (Fig. 8D<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1em;">8D),</span></span><span></span></a>), suggesting that AMPK may act as a positive regulator for tissue glucocorticoid action in PBMCs. These results are consistent with those of the animal study and cellular-based experiments that AMPK enhances GR-induced transcriptional activity in some tissues and cell types.</p><p id="__p41" class="p p-last">We further compared mRNA expression of AMPK&#x003b1;1 and AMPK&#x003b1;2 with that of the growth arrest-specific 5 (Gas5). Gas5 is a noncoding RNA, accumulating inside cells in response to starvation-related growth arrest, and functions as a transcriptional repressor for the GR by inhibiting the binding of GR to its cognate DNA sequences (<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_309307162">47</a>). These results suggest that the energy sensor/regulator AMPK system and the Gas5-mediated growth arrest/starvation-related regulatory pathway communicate with each other to adjust local glucocorticoid action.</p></div></div><div id="__sec11" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec11title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p42" class="p p-first">We showed that AMPK, a central player of the adaptive response to energy depletion, regulated GR-induced transcriptional activity by phosphorylating its serine residue at amino acid 211, through activation of its downstream substrate p38 MAPK. This indirect interaction between AMPK and GR observed at the cellular level was also evident at the whole animal level: AMPK reversed dexamethasone-induced steatosis in rat liver and suppressed elevation of blood glucose and hepatic glycogen by dexamethasone, most likely through regulating the action of this steroid on key glucose metabolic enzymes PEPCK and G6Pase in the liver. In mRNA expression analysis of glucocorticoid-responsive genes in various tissues and microarray analyses performed on rat liver, AMPK differentially regulated GR-induced transcriptional activity in a gene- and organ-specific fashion. Thus, our results suggest that the AMPK-mediated energy-sensing pathway communicates with the GR/glucocorticoid-mediated stress system through their molecular effectors AMPK and GR. Such pathway interaction may also be observed in human subjects with various BMI values.</p><p id="__p43">Previous reports suggested that glucocorticoids exerted their metabolic effects, such as deposition of lipid in the liver, through activation of AMPK in this organ (<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456553">48</a>), whereas they stimulated mRNA expression of neuropeptide Y and the agouti-related peptide, which play important roles in the stimulation of appetite, through activation of AMPK in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus (<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456609">49</a>). These reports indicate that glucocorticoids may stimulate the activity of AMPK, although the underlying molecular mechanisms have not been elucidated as yet.</p><p id="__p44">Our results, together with these previous reports, suggest that glucocorticoids and AMPK influence each other&#x02019;s activity. Such mutual regulation between the energy sensing and the stress-responsive systems would have been expected from the major overlap of their broad biological actions. For example, depletion of energy is an important stressor for humans and animals, whereas the adaptive response to stress includes major alterations of energy balance in the organism (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode">2</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>,<a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode">50</a>). The directions, as well as the duration and timing, of their mutual effects appear to be specific for the organs/tissues regulated, and the interactions between them appear to be at several steps of their signaling systems, including the communication between AMPK and GR that we showed here. AMPK was recently reported to regulate directly the promoter activity of the known glucocorticoid target proopiomelanocortin gene, another route through which energy and metabolism may be regulated (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456634">51</a>).</p><p id="__p45">We demonstrated the molecular mechanism through which AMPK regulates the transcriptional activity of the GR, <em>i.e.</em> phosphorylation of the GR at serine 211 through activation of the AMPK substrate p38 MAPK&#x003b1;. Serine 211 of the human GR is conserved in the rat and mouse GRs (serines 232 and 220, respectively) and is also phosphorylated by the yeast cyclin-dependent kinase p34CDC28 (<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456579">18</a>,<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456646">22</a>). In addition to serine 211, p38 MAPK is reported to phosphorylate serine 226 of the human GR (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456616">15</a>), but the latter does not appear to be functional in AMPK-mediated regulation of GR transcriptional activity because replacement of serine 211 by alanine in the human GR completely abolished AICAR-mediated regulation of GR transcriptional activity. AMPK-mediated phosphorylation of serine 211 of the human GR resulted in altered accumulation of cofactors, p300 and the SNF2 component of the SWI/SNF complex, to ligand-activated GR bound on GREs of the glucocorticoid-responsive genes <em>PEPCK, G6Pase, GILZ</em>, and MMTV, possibly by preferential attraction/dissociation of phosphorylation site-specific cofactors.</p><p id="__p46">These results are consistent with our previous report that showed that cyclin-dependent kinase 5, which plays important roles in the morphogenesis and functions of the nervous system and whose aberrant activation is associated with the development of neurodegenerative disorders, phosphorylated multiple serines of the human GR including serine 211, and the changed GR-induced transcriptional activity in a tissue- and gene-specific fashion (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456613">21</a>,<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456558">22</a>).</p><p id="__p47">We demonstrated that dexamethasone injection produced a severe steatotic change in the rat liver, whereas AICAR antagonized this dexamethasone effect. Steatosis is one of the most common complications of glucocorticoid excess in humans (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>,<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456651">7</a>) and is characterized by massive accumulation of glycogen and lipids inside the hepatocytes (<a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456571">52</a>). Dexamethasone also increased blood glucose levels, whereas AICAR strongly suppressed this change. Alteration of blood glucose levels and induction/attenuation of liver steatosis by dexamethasone and/or AICAR appear to be mediated by their differential regulations on the <em>PEPCK</em> and the <em>G6Pase</em> genes in the liver, muscle, and fat. Dexamethasone stimulated the mRNA expression of these genes in the liver and resulted in an increase in both the synthesis and release of glucose into the circulation.</p><p id="__p48">AICAR strongly suppressed dexamethasone-stimulated mRNA expression of these genes, reducing blood glucose levels and antagonizing dexamethasone-induced accumulation of hepatic glycogen. AICAR, on the other hand, strongly stimulated G6Pase mRNA expression in the skeletal muscle, whereas simultaneous treatment of dexamethasone and AICAR strongly suppressed AICAR-stimulated G6Pase mRNA expression in this tissue. Taken together, these findings suggest that AMPK mainly suppresses the catabolic effects of glucocorticoids on glucose metabolism in the liver, skeletal muscle, and fat.</p><p id="__p49">Diabetes mellitus type 2, hyperlipidemia, and liver steatosis are major metabolic complications associated with excess amounts or increased sensitivity of tissues to glucocorticoids (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>,<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456588">7</a>,<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456582">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456575">9</a>). Thus, it is possible that pharmacological activation of AMPK, for example, by the use of metformin, thienopyridones, berberine, or AICAR-like nucleoside analogs, is a promising therapeutic target for eliminating these glucocorticoid-associated metabolic complications (<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_309307161">53</a>,<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456652">54</a>,<a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456565">55</a>,<a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456545">56</a>). However, AMPK and glucocorticoids both increase appetite by influencing the CNS appetite center (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456640">6</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456567">25</a>); thus, selective AMPK activators functional only in peripheral tissues, especially in the liver, would be appropriate for suppressing metabolic action of glucocorticoids.</p><p id="__p50" class="p p-last">Our results in rat spleen and brain and human PBMCs strongly suggest the presence of functional interactions between AMPK and GR in these organs. Because glucocorticoids have numerous important actions in the regulation of the immune and the CNS (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode">2</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode">4</a>), it would be interesting to examine further how the energy sensing system, represented by AMPK, alters glucocorticoid actions in these tissues. Indeed, AMPK can inhibit glucocorticoid-induced apoptosis in the thymus, an organ in which phosphorylation of GR by p38 MAPK is strongly present (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456577">13</a>,<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456627">28</a>). It is also quite interesting to examine how the AMPK-mediated energy sensing system cooperates with other regulatory pathways, such as Gas5/starvation/growth arrest-mediated one, for controlling net glucocorticoid action at local tissues.</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec12title">Materials and Methods</h2><div id="__sec13" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Plasmids and Reagents</h3><p id="__p51" class="p p-first">pRShGR&#x003b1;, which expresses the human GR, and pRSVerbA<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>, which contains the thyroid hormone receptor cDNA in an inverse orientation and was used as a negative control for pRShGR&#x003b1;, were both gifts from Dr. R. ASP07-016 and ASP09-030). Male Sprague Dawley rats (180&#x02013;200 g) were injected with either AICAR (0.7 g/kg animal) or physiological saline into their peritoneal cavities. Animals in each group were also injected im with dexamethasone (1.5 mg/kg) or normal saline. Twenty-four hours after the injection, levels of blood glucose were measured using the Freestyle GlucoMeter (Abbott Laboratories, Abbott Park, IL) by sampling blood from their tails. Animals were subsequently euthanized using CO<sub>2</sub>, and their livers, skeletal muscles, sc fat, spleens, and brains were harvested for hematoxylin and eosin or PAS staining.</p><p id="__p54">Sera were also obtained, and the levels of serum and liver cholesterol and triglycerides were determined by using their specific ELISA or calorimetric reactions. Serum insulin and liver glycogen levels were determined by using the rat insulin enzyme immunoassay kit (SPI Bio, Montigny le Bretonneux, France) and the glycogen assay kit (BioVision, Inc., Mountain View, CA), respectively. For Western blots examining the phosphorylation of GR in response to AICAR, the same experiments were performed, whereas the animals were euthanized 2 h after the injection of AICAR and/or dexamethasone. Their livers and whole homogenates were then harvested and were produced as previously reported (<a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456642">62</a>). The extraction and purification of total RNA and/or separation of nuclear extracts for ChIP analyses were, respectively, performed in the indicated rat organs 8 and 2 h after the injection of these compounds.</p><p id="__p55" class="p p-last">C57BL/6J adult male mice (15&#x02013;20 g/animal) were injected ip with 1 &#x000d7; 10<sup>6</sup> infectious units of virus per animal of control lentiviral particles or those expressing the shRNAs for the AMPK&#x003b1;1/AMPK&#x003b1;2 catalytic subunits and/or p38 MAPK&#x003b1;. Twenty-four hours after the injection of the lentiviral particles, animals were injected ip with dexamethasone (1.5 mg/kg) and/or AICAR (0.7 g/kg). After an additional 8 h, the mice were euthanized using CO<sub>2</sub>, and their livers were harvested for purification of total RNA.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="sec"><h3>SYBR Green-based real-time PCR</h3><p id="__p56" class="p p-first-last">Total RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA, as previously described (<a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456637">63</a>,<a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456658">64</a>). p-first-last">Five milligrams of total RNA purified from the rat liver were used for producing probes with the one-cycle target labeling and control reagents kit (Affymetrix). The Rat Genome 230 2.0 tiled array (Affymetrix) was then labeled with the prepared probes, washed, and stained in the Affymetrix working station. Detailed data analysis steps were performed as previously described (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456619">21</a>). Briefly, all hybridizations are normalized with the RMA method using Bioconductor package (http:/www.bioconductor.org). Followed by generation of gene expression ratios from treated <em>vs.</em> control samples, a one-sample Student <em>t</em> test was performed based on comparisons of gene expression values (log ratio) of the same probe set among all replicates with a critical value of <em>P</em> &#x02264; 0.05. Candidate genes were identified by using the z test by calculating the 95% cutoff interval. The Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery (DAVID) Bioinformatics Resources (http://david.abcc.ncifcrf.gov) was used to perform functional analysis (Fig. 3F<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1em;">3F)</span></span><span></span></a>) of all the candidate genes. The microarray data discussed in this publication have been deposited in the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) of the National Center for Biotechnology Information (www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/) and are accessible through GEO Series accession number <a id="__tag_197054090" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE22647" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE22647"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE22647","term_id":"22647"}}</span>GSE22647</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec17" class="sec"><h3>Cell culture, transfection, and treatment with chemical compounds</h3><p id="__p58" class="p p-first">Murine preadipocyte 3T3-L1, human hepatoma HepG2 and African green monkey kidney CV-1 cells were all purchased from American Type Culture Collection (Manassas, VA). The 3T3-L1, HepG2, and CV-1 cells were maintained in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 100 U/ml penicillin, and 100 &#x003bc;g/ml streptomycin. The COS7/MMTV cells, which have stably transfected and integrated MMTV promoter, were described previously and were cultured in DMEM with the same supplements and 0.2 mg/ml neomycin (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456632">21</a>,<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456561">57</a>). 3T3-L1 and HepG2 cells express endogenous, functional GR, whereas CV-1 and COS7/MMTV cells do not (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456564">21</a>,<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456552">57</a>,<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456574">61</a>). 3T3-L1 cells were transfected with the indicated plasmids or siRNAs with the Nucleofector system (solution-R and program U-30; Amaxa GmbH, Cologne, Germany) with more than 80% transfection efficiency (data not shown). CV-1 and COS7/MMTV cells were transfected with the indicated plasmids with Lipofectin (Invitrogen), as previously described (<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456618">61</a>,<a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456587">64</a>).</p><p id="__p59" class="p p-last">Cells were cultured with the compounds indicated in the presence or absence of 10<sup>&#x02212;6</sup> <span class="small-caps">m</span> of dexamethasone for 24 h. If transfection was applied, such compounds were added with dexamethasone into culture media 24 h after the transfection. Cells were subsequently lysed for purification of total RNA or measurement of the luciferase and &#x003b2;-galactosidase activities. Total RNA was purified using the RNeasy minikit (QIAGEN, Valencia, CA).</p></div><div id="__sec18" class="sec"><h3>Luciferase and &#x003b2;-galactosidase assays</h3><p id="__p60" class="p p-first-last">Luciferase and &#x003b2;-galactosidase activities were determined, as previously described (<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456550">61</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><h3>Western blots</h3><p id="__p61" class="p p-first">HepG2 cells were plated in 25-mm<sup>2</sup> flasks at the density of 1 &#x000d7; 10<sup>6</sup> cells/flask and incubated with the compounds indicated in the presence or absence of 10<sup>&#x02212;6</sup> <span class="small-caps">m</span> of dexamethasone for 2 h. Cells were then lysed in buffer containing 50 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 150 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> NaCl, 0.1% sodium dodecyl sulfate, 1% Nonidet P-40, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate, and one tablet per 50 ml Complete tablet (Roche Applied Science, Indianapolis, IN), and the cell extracts were obtained by centrifuging cell lysates at 10,000 &#x000d7; <em>g</em> for 15 min. Cell extracts were run on 4&#x02013;20% SDS-PAGE gels, and separated proteins were blotted to nitrocellulose membranes. GR and its phosphorylated form at serine 211 or 232 were visualized with anti-GR&#x003b1; antibody (Santa Cruz Biotechnology) and an antibody specifically recognizing the human or rat GR phosphorylated at serine 211 or 232 (a gift from Dr. M. J. Garabedian, New York University, New York, NY), respectively.</p><p id="__p62" class="p p-last">p38 MAPK&#x003b1;, the AMPK&#x003b1; catalytic subunit, and their phosphorylated forms were detected with their specific antibodies (Santa Cruz Biotechnology). Nuclear extracts obtained from livers of the rats injected with dexamethasone and/or AICAR were also run on 4&#x02013;20% SDS-PAGE gels and blotted on nitrocellulose membranes. GR, p38 MAPK&#x003b1; and the AMPK&#x003b1; catalytic subunit, and their phosphorylated forms were detected with their specific antibodies (Santa Cruz Biotechnology).</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec"><h3>Kinase assay for p38 MAPK</h3><p id="__p63" class="p p-first-last">Kinase activity of the p38 MAPK was measured using the p38 MAPK assay kit (Cell Signaling, Danvers, MA), as described previously (<a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456655">65</a>). Briefly, livers of the rat treated with AICAR and/or dexamethasone were lysed, and phosphorylated p38 MAPK was precipitated with the antiphospho-p38 MAPK (threonine180/tyrosine 184) antibody. The precipitated proteins were then incubated with ATF-2, and phosphorylation of ATF-2 at threonine 71 was evaluated in Western blots using the antibody that reacted with the phosphorylated form.</p></div><div id="__sec21" class="sec"><h3>ChIP assay</h3><p id="__p64" class="p p-first">ChIP assay was performed in rat liver and muscle obtained in the animal study treated with AICAR and/or dexamethasone for 2 h or in COS7/MMTV cells. Samples were fixed in 10% formaldehyde, and DNA and bound proteins were cross-linked, and ChIP assays were performed by coprecipitating the DNA/protein complexes with anti-p300, anti-SNF2 or anti-GR&#x003b1; antibodies, or rabbit control IgG (Santa Cruz Biotechnology).</p><p id="__p65" class="p p-last">The &#x02212;421 to &#x02212;296, &#x02212;447 to &#x02212;281, &#x02212;2452 to &#x02212;2314, and &#x02212;219 to &#x02212;47 of the rat PEPCK, G6Pase, GILZ, and MMTV promoters containing functional or putative tandem GREs (<a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456546">37</a>,<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456605">38</a>,<a href="#B66" rid="B66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456622">66</a>) were amplified from the prepared DNA samples using specific primer pairs (PEPCK promoter: 5&#x02032;-GAATTAAAGGTGTATGC-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-CTTTGAACCTGGTATTTGC-3&#x02032;, G6Pase promoter: 5&#x02032;-CATCACTCTCCACAGTAGC-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-GCTTGACAGTGCTAAGG-3&#x02032;, GILZ promoter: 5&#x02032;-GAATTAAAGGTGTATGC-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-CTTTGAACCTGGTATTTGC-3&#x02032;, MMTV promoter: 5&#x02032;-AACCTTGCGGTTCCCAG-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-GCATTTACATAAGATTTGG-3&#x02032;) in the SYBR Green-based real-time PCR using the SYBR Green PCR master mix (Applied Biosystems) and a 7500 real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems) (<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200456595">57</a>). The obtained cycle threshold values of ChIP samples were normalized for those of corresponding inputs and their relative precipitation were demonstrated as fold precipitation above the baseline.</p></div><div id="__sec22" class="sec"><h3>Determination of AMPK&#x003b1;1/AMPK&#x003b1;2, Gas5, and GILZ mRNA expression in obese and anorectic lean individuals</h3><p id="__p66" class="p p-first-last">Whole-blood samples were collected from subjects of different BMI (14.65&#x02013;34.43 kg/m<sup>2</sup>, aged 8&#x02013;14 yr) under a protocol approved by the Ethics Committee of the Children&#x02019;s Hospital Aghia Sophia (Athens, Greece). The mRNA levels of the AMPK&#x003b1;1 and AMPK&#x003b1;2 catalytic subunits, Gas5, glucocorticoid-responsive GILZ, and RPLP0 were measured with the SYBR Green-based real-time PCR, as described above. The fold induction of AMPK&#x003b1;1/AMPK&#x003b1;2 and GILZ mRNA expression was calculated against the values of subject 1 (BMI 18.98 kg/m<sup>2</sup>).</p></div><div id="__sec23" class="sec"><h3>Statistical analysis</h3><p id="__p67" class="p p-first-last">Statistical analyses were carried out by unpaired Student <em>t</em> test with the two-tailed <em>P</em> value or ANOVA, followed by a Student <em>t</em> test with Bonferroni correction for comparison of the results obtained in the presence of increased concentrations of AICAR (in Fig. 4B<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2940476/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1em;">4B).</span></span><span></span></a>).
