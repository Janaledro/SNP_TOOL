To achieve this goal, we aimed at identifying expression changes in genes which could be mediated by virgin olive oil phenol compounds in the human.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">Postprandial gene expression microarray analysis was performed on peripheral blood mononuclear cells during postprandial period. Two virgin olive oil-based breakfasts with high (398 ppm) and low (70 ppm) content of phenolic compounds were administered to 20 patients suffering from metabolic syndrome following a double-blinded, randomized, crossover design. To eliminate the potential effect that might exist in their usual dietary habits, all subjects followed a similar low-fat, carbohydrate rich diet during the study period. Microarray analysis identified 98 differentially expressed genes (79 underexpressed and 19 overexpressed) when comparing the intake of phenol-rich olive oil with low-phenol olive oil. Many of these genes seem linked to obesity, dyslipemia and type 2 diabetes mellitus. Among these, several genes seem involved in inflammatory processes mediated by transcription factor NF-&#x003ba;B, activator protein-1 transcription factor complex AP-1, cytokines, mitogen-activated protein kinases MAPKs or arachidonic acid pathways.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusion</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">This study shows that intake of virgin olive oil based breakfast, which is rich in phenol compounds is able to repress <em>in vivo </em>expression of several pro-inflammatory genes, thereby switching activity of peripheral blood mononuclear cells to a less deleterious inflammatory profile. These results provide at least a partial molecular basis for reduced risk of cardiovascular disease observed in Mediterranean countries, where virgin olive oil represents a main source of dietary fat. Admittedly, other lifestyle factors are also likely to contribute to lowered risk of cardiovascular disease in this region.</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec4" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec4title">Background</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first">Metabolic syndrome (MetS) is a very common condition that is associated with increased cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk and type 2 diabetes mellitus, itself a risk factor for CVD. Its diverse clinical characteristics illustrate the complexity of the disease, involving several dysregulated metabolic pathways and multiple genetic targets. When caloric intake exceeds caloric expenditure, a positive caloric balance and subsequent storage of fat in adipose tissue appears; this often is cause of adipocyte hypertrophy and obesity. Depending on interaction of genetic and environmental factors, this hypertrophy can be followed by macrophage accumulation within adipose tissue leading to local hypoxia, inflammation, and oxidative stress. These deleterious effects favor adipose functional failure resulting in changes in systemic energy delivery; these also impair glucose consumption and activate self-regulatory mechanisms that extert influence over whole body homeostatic systems and link obesity to numerous health problems associated with MetS [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432277">1</a>]. A low-level systemic chronic inflammation, with abnormal cytokine production in adipose tissue, increased acute-phase reactants, and activation of inflammatory signaling pathways, often acting over a period of many years, appears to constitute a potential link to atherosclerosis development in these patients [<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432238">2</a>]. In addition, changes in postprandial metabolism undergoing every time we have a meal as well as alterations in this state may play an important role in the development of cardiovascular and cardiovascular associated diseases [<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432270">3</a>]. Moreover, Van Oostromet et al. [<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432251">4</a>] provides evidence that postprandial triglyceridemia is related to a pro-inflammatory state due to high expression of markers of neutrophilic and monocyte activation.</p><p id="__p5">The anti-atherogenic effects associated with Mediterranean Diet (MD) rich in Virgin Olive Oil (VOO) consumption, could contribute in explaining the low rates of cardiovascular mortality found in Southern European Mediterranean countries, in comparison with other Western populations [<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432237">5</a>]. It has been demonstrated recently that inhibition of circulating immune cell activation could be a protective mechanism by which MD exerts its healthy effects [<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432235">6</a>]. Bonani et al. [<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432253">7</a>] showed that extra VOO consumption reduces inflammatory markers and increase serum antioxidant capacity at postprandial state. Previously, our group has showed how a VOO-rich MD, during postprandial state, reduces inflammatory response of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) mediated by transcription factor NF-&#x003ba;B, when compared to, butter and walnut-enriched diets or Western diets [<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_322807174">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432225">9</a>]. These results have been replicated by others and support the hypothesis that decreasing NF-&#x003ba;B pathway activation is a mechanism involved in the anti-inflammatory effect of a VOO-rich MD [<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432264">10</a>,<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432267">11</a>]. Nevertheless, it has been proposed that healthy effects of VOO may be due not only to its oleic acid content but also to the antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capacity of minor components, especially the phenol-rich fraction [<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432274">12</a>,<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432252">13</a>]. Our group demonstrated that the VOO phenol fraction also improves endothelial dysfunction and haemostatic profile during postprandial state [<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432265">14</a>,<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432229">15</a>]. Other studies showed how these compounds ameliorate lipid profiles and decrease oxidative stress <em>in vivo </em>[<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432243">12</a>]. However, in animal models, it has been showed that hydroxytyrosol, a phenol found in olive oil, administered after being extracted from its original matrix could be not only non beneficial but indeed harmful for health [<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432234">16</a>].</p><p id="__p6" class="p p-last">It has been speculated that potentially beneficial effects could be due to olive oil modulation on genes involved in proliferative, antioxidant and inflammatory pathways. In order to clarify this question, some human, animal models and <em>in vitro </em>studies were undertaken using gene expression microarray platforms [<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432258">17</a>]. These studies showed that olive oil is able to modify gene expression coding for proteins participating in cellular mechanisms involved in oxidative stress resistance [<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432240">18</a>], lipid metabolism [<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432256">19</a>] and other atherosclerosis-related traits/pathways [<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432275">20</a>,<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432271">21</a>]. Nonetheless, it is unresolved whether these changes in gene expression are mediated by oleic acid or executed by olive oil polar minor components, maybe as a consequence of their antioxidant capacity, or by interacting directly with receptors, enzymes or transcription factors. To date, no intervention studies in humans have thoroughly explored the effects of VOO phenol compounds on gene expression. Thus, the main purpose of this study was to identify genes which undergo changes in expression in PBMCs in patients with MetS, after acute intake of breakfast based in phenol-rich virgin olive oil, compared to phenol-poor olive oil breakfast.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec5title">Results</h2><div id="__sec6" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Postprandial metabolic parameters</h3><p id="__p7" class="p p-first">No significant differences were observed in any of the iAUCs of the main metabolic variables (glucose, insulin, non-sterified fatty acids, serum triglycerides and high density lipoprotein cholesterol) after intake of phenol-rich virgin olive oil compared with olive oil with lower content of these compounds (Table <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2874810/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3em;">(Table1</span></span><span>1</span></a>).</p><!--table ft1--><!--table-wrap mode="anchored" t5--><div class="table-wrap table anchored whole_rhythm" id="T1"><h3>Table 1</h3><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p8">Postprandial metabolic parameters.</p></div><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139738919690256" class="xtable"><table frame="hsides" rules="groups" class="rendered small default_table"><thead><tr><th rowspan="1" colspan="1"></th><th align="center" rowspan="1" colspan="1">Low-phenol<br />OO breakfast</th><th align="center" rowspan="1" colspan="1">High-phenol<br />OO breakfast</th><th align="center" rowspan="1" colspan="1">p value</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left" rowspan="1" colspan="1">iAUC Glucose (mg.dL-1. min-1)</td><td align="center" rowspan="1" colspan="1">1775 &#x000b1; Microarray analysis showed a correlation index &#x0003e;90% of the raw log-intensity signal among replicates in the array in all cases. Mean coefficient of variation of the log-signal probe values was lower than 0.1 for intra-array replicates. Cellular functions most strongly associated with the differentially expressed genes were cell death (41 genes, <em>p </em>= 7.53E-11), cell migration (24 genes, <em>p </em>= 3.88E-8), cell division (23 genes, <em>p </em>= 6.29E-8), cell proliferation (32 genes, <em>p </em>= 9.90E-7) and transcription (25 genes, <em>p </em>= 3.55E-05). Network of phenol-rich VOO modulated genes. Gray symbols denote that the gene was found over-expressed or under-expressed by phenols in microarrays analysis.</p></div></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec10" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec10title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p16" class="p p-first">In the present study, we have observed that the phenol fraction of VOO <em>in vivo </em>is able to repress the expression of several genes related to inflammation pathways in patients with MetS during postprandial period (Additional File <a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">1</a>). This finding draws interest since pro-inflammatory state remains as one component of MetS [<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432227">23</a>] and low-grade inflammation is often associated with endothelial dysfunction [<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432262">24</a>], which by itself is associated to the development of atherosclerosis [<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432266">25</a>].</p><p id="__p17">The <em>PTGS2 </em>gene encodes prostaglandin-endoperoxide synthase 2 (COX-2), an inducible isozyme involved in prostaglandin biosynthesis using arachidonic acid as substrate. In macrophages, and other cells, COX-2 activity is rapidly increased by various stimuli, such as pro-inflammatory cytokine IL1&#x003b2;. Substantial evidence indicates that up regulated <em>PTGS2 </em>expression and prostaglandin synthesis indeed influence chronic inflammatory conditions [<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432241">26</a>]. De la Puerta et al. observed that murine macrophages showed significantly reduced IL1&#x003b2; production and COX-2 activity after olive oil-enriched diet [<a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432233">27</a>]. Recently, it has been demonstrated that hydroxytyrosol, one of the most important phenol compounds found in virgin olive oil, attenuates <em>in vitro </em>LPS-induced transcription of <em>PTGS2 </em>[<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432269">28</a>]. Our study showed that <em>in vivo </em>intake of phenol-rich virgin olive oil in humans is associated with a decreased expression of both <em>IL1B </em>and <em>PTGS2</em>, as compared to low-phenol olive oil intake. Those effects could contribute to reduced inflammation during postprandial state in agreement with anti-inflammatory effects observed after VOO-rich MD consumption [<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_322807172">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432242">9</a>].</p><p id="__p18">The cytokine-cytokine interaction pathway contains a network of proteins (chemokines and their receptors) involved in the recruitment and activation of leukocytes during inflammatory response. Expression of genes such as <em>CCL3</em>, <em>CXCL1</em>, <em>CXCL2</em>, <em>CXCL3</em>, <em>CXCR4</em>, <em>IL1B</em>, <em>IL6</em>, and <em>OSM </em>is described under-expressed after acute intake of phenol-rich olive oil in our intervention study on diet. <em>CCL3 gene</em>, which codes for macrophage inflammatory protein-1 (MIP-1), has been implicated in monocyte infiltration of adipose tissue, an action that could significantly influence a pro-inflammatory pattern within endothelial cells [<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432248">29</a>]. <em>CXCL1</em>, <em>CXCL2 </em>(<em>MIP2A</em>), and further, <em>CXCL3 </em>(<em>MIP2B</em>) are genes for small and structurally related chemokines that regulate cell trafficking of various types of leukocytes through interactions with a subset of G protein-coupled receptors. Elgazar-Carmon et al. have demonstrated in mice that early neutrophil infiltration of adipose tissue may be mediated by <em>CXCL1</em>, a process that would precede macrophage infiltration after long-term consumption of a high-fat diet [<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432260">30</a>]. <em>IL6 </em>encodes a cytokine which is secreted to serum and induces a transcriptional response involved in a wide variety of inflammation-associated conditions, including MetS and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) [<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432255">31</a>]. On the other hand, it has been proposed that <em>IL6 </em>and <em>OSM</em>, which encodes oncostatin M, a growth regulator and member of the <em>IL6 </em>group of cytokines, can contribute to the increased cardiovascular risk in obese patients by up regulating PAI -1 in adipose tissue [<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432245">32</a>].</p><p id="__p19">Activation of NF-&#x003ba;B and MAPK pathway, a cascade of phosphorilation events that result in the activation of transcription factors like CREB and AP-1, synergize for expression of inflammatory genes through coordinate bindings of transcription factors to &#x003ba;B and AP-1 sites which have been found together in the promoters of e.g. IL6 and TNF-&#x003b1;, and many other inflammatory genes [<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432257">33</a>]. Chemokine repression found in our study could be consequence of phenols interaction with this inflammation signaling system, since expression of some genes involved in NF-&#x003ba;B/MAPK/AP-1 signaling pathways was also modulated after phenol-rich olive oil based breakfast. NF-&#x003ba;B is a transcription factor activated by pro-inflammatory cytokines [<a href="#B34" rid="B34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432259">34</a>] and oxidative stress mediators [<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432276">35</a>]. Recently Pierce et al. have demonstrated that NF-&#x003ba;B activation is important in mediating vascular endothelial dysfunction in obese humans [<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432250">36</a>]. The product of <em>SGK1 </em>gene, encoding a serum/glucocorticoid regulated kinase with a role in stress response and by itself being a downstream target for PI<sub>3</sub>K signaling, enhances nuclear NF-&#x003ba;B activity by phosphorylating an inhibitory kinase IKK&#x003b1; [<a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432231">37</a>]; so repression on expression of <em>SGK1 </em>gene by olive oil phenols would decrease the NF-&#x003ba;B activation. In addition, <em>NFKBIA </em>gene, which encodes to I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1;, a member of an inhibitory I&#x003ba;B family that retains NF&#x003ba;B into the cytoplasm, remained under-expressed after acute intake of phenol-rich olive oil. It has been reported that NF-&#x003ba;B binds to the I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1; promoter in order to activate its transcription [<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432236">38</a>]. Thus, this negative feedback mechanism results in rapid cycles of inhibition and stimulation of NF-&#x003ba;B where a decrease on NF-&#x003ba;B activation is accompanied by a reduction on <em>NFKBIA </em>gene expression, as observed in our results. The hypothesis that NF-&#x003ba;B activation is decreased by olive oil phenols is also supported by two <em>in vivo </em>studies which showed reduced NF-&#x003ba;B activation after olive oil consumption [<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_322807173">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432273">9</a>]. Additionally, <em>in vitro </em>studies showing attenuated NF-&#x003ba;B activation by resveratrol support the hypothesis that this pathway employs a shared mechanism by which polyphenols reduce expression of genes encoding inflammatory cytokines and adhesion molecules [<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432263">39</a>].</p><p id="__p20">After intake of virgin olive oil with high content in phenolic compounds we found a decreased postprandial expression of <em>DUSP1 </em>and <em>DUSP2</em>. Those genes encode dual serine-threonine phosphatases, which down regulate members of p38, MAPK/ERK and SAPK/JNK, the three final effectors of the MAPK pathway [<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432249">33</a>]. In addition, <em>TRIB1</em>, another gene repressed by phenol-rich olive oil, is involved in MAPK signaling, participating in the activation of ERK proteins [<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432268">40</a>] and being up regulated in human atherosclerotic plaques [<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_322807171">41</a>]. Thus, reduction of <em>TRIB1 </em>expression by olive oil phenols could promote decreased ERK activation. This observation agrees with <em>in vitro </em>studies demonstrating that phenol compounds of green tea down regulate <em>PTGS2 </em>expression by decreasing ERK and p38 MAPK activation [<a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432244">42</a>]. Our results allow us to hypothesize that olive oil phenols influence activation of AP-1, which consists of a variety of heterodimers of Jun, Fos and activating transcription factor families [<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432230">43</a>], by means of two different mechanisms: a) one direct, involving repression of <em>JUN</em>, <em>JUNB </em>and <em>FOSB </em>as observed after phenol-rich olive oil intake; and/or b) another one indirect, through MAPK pathway, where the relative intensity and duration of activation determine the type of response.</p><p id="__p21" class="p p-last">Lastly, biomedical literature and text mining tasks were performed to identify interactions of differentially expressed genes in PBMCs as response to phenol-rich VOO with conditions clustered around MetS such as obesity, hypertension, dyslipemia, hyperglycemia, or T2DM. Recently, Chen et al. have described a macrophage-enriched gene network (MEGN) of ~1237 genes referred as having causal relationship with complex-disease traits associated with MetS [<a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432254">44</a>]. Thirteen genes share our set of differentially expressed genes in PBMCs after acute intake of phenol-rich olive oil and MEGN: <em>JUN</em>, <em>RGS1</em>, <em>CXCL2</em>, <em>ANXA3</em>, <em>RASGEF1B</em>, <em>CD83</em>, <em>CA2</em>, <em>EGR2</em>, <em>DIAPH3</em>, <em>CCL3</em>, and <em>TLR7</em>, <em>PSAP </em>and <em>IFIT3</em>. De Mello et al. assessed individuals with both impaired glucose metabolism and MetS on how long-term weight loss affects expression of cytokines in PBMCs. Weight reduction resulted in a decrease in of <em>IL1B </em>expression [<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432239">45</a>]. Kaiser et al. showed by microarrays analysis in PBMCs a set of 22 over-expressed genes in T1DM and T2DM compared to healthy subjects [<a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432247">46</a>]. Interestingly, 8 of the identified 22 over-expressed genes in T2DM were repressed by olive oil phenols, according to our intervention study (<em>IL1B</em>, <em>EGR2</em>, <em>EGR3</em>, <em>PTGS2</em>, <em>FOSB</em>, <em>CXCL1</em>, <em>SGK</em>, and <em>TRIB1</em>). In addition, <em>PBEF1</em>, another gene involved in the pathogenesis of T2DM [<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432232">47</a>], was also found repressed after consumption of phenols-rich olive oil. Taken together, this finding could lead to potential therapeutic implications in T2DM.</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec11title">Conclusions</h2><p id="__p22" class="p p-first-last">Our study shows that intake of breakfast based in virgin olive oil being rich in phenol compounds is able to repress expression of several pro-inflammatory genes <em>in vivo</em>, thereby switching activity of PBMCs to a less deleterious inflammatory profile. These results provide at least a partial molecular basis for risk reduction of cardiovascular disease observed in Mediterranean countries, where VOO represents a main source of dietary fat. Admittedly, other lifestyle factors are also likely to contribute to lowered risk of CVD in this region. Nonetheless, our data suggest that mechanisms by which these micronutrients in phenol-rich olive oil would exert their anti-inflammatory effect could involve pathways related to NF-&#x003ba;B/AP-1, cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, arachidonic acid metabolism, and MAPK. These findings strengthen the relationship between inflammation, obesity and diet and provide evidence at transcription level of control of healthy effects derived from VOO consumption in humans. However, it would be interesting to evaluate whether these beneficial effects are maintained after prolonged feeding and if these effects are carried out by one or several olive oil phenolic compounds, or if they are consequence of a synergic effect of the total phenolic fraction.</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec12title">Methods</h2><div id="__sec13" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Subjects</h3><p id="__p23" class="p p-first">Twenty (56 years old; range, 40-70) subjects (9 men, 11 women) from the Lipids and Atherosclerosis Unit at Hospital Universitario Reina Sof&#x000ed;a (Cordoba, Spain) participated in this study.</p><p id="__p24" class="p p-last">All subjects fulfilled three or more of the proposed criteria proposed by the Third Report of the National Cholesterol Education Program (NCEP) Expert Panel on Detection, Evaluation, and Treatment of High Blood Cholesterol in Adults (Adult Treatment Panel III) for MetS: a) central obesity (waist circumference &#x0003e; 102 cm in men or &#x0003e; 88 cm in women); b) high blood pressure &#x02265; 130/85 mmHg or documented use of antihypertensive therapy; c) high fasting glucose (&#x02265; 100 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup>); d) hypertriglyceridemia (&#x02265; 150 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup>), and e) low high density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDLc) (&#x0003c; 40 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup> for males or 50 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup> for females). Subjects had an average body mass index (BMI) of 38.98 (27.95-44.42) kg.m<sup>-2</sup>, waist perimeter of 132.2 cm (103.0-137.0), systolic blood pressure of 146 &#x000b1; 19 mmHg, diastolic blood pressure of 89 &#x000b1; 10 mmHg, plasma glucose levels of 102.8 &#x000b1; 17.0 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup>, plasma insulin levels of 11.5 &#x000b1; 6.5 mU.L<sup>-1</sup>, total cholesterol (TC) plasma levels of 201.7 &#x000b1; 27.1 mg.dlL-1 triacylglyceride (TAG) plasma levels of 185 &#x000b1; 78 mg.mL-1, low density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDLc) levels of 120.3 &#x000b1; 19.7 mg.dL<sup>-1</sup> and high density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDLc) levels of 50.7 &#x000b1; 11.8 mg.dL-1. No subjects showed evidence of chronic diseases (hepatic, renal, thyroid, cardiac), smoking, alcohol consumption, or family history of cardiovascular disease of early onset, nor were they taking drugs. The Human Investigation Review Committee at Reina Sofia University Hospital approved this study. All participants gave informed consent before joining the study.</p></div><div id="__sec14" class="sec"><h3>Study design and dietary intervention</h3><p id="__p25" class="p p-first-last">Before the first breakfast intervention, participants followed a 6-week washout period in which they were instructed to not take vitamins, soy supplements, or any drug. To eliminate the potential effect that might exist in their usual dietary habits, all subjects followed a low-fat, carbohydrate rich (CHO) diet during this period through the end of the study. Compliance with the CHO diet was assessed after two and four weeks using a three-day recording and a food frequency questionnaire. 24 hours prior to each breakfast intervention, participants were instructed to avoid consuming phenol-rich foods such as fruit or juices, wine, grape juice, chocolate, coffee, tea, olive oil, or soy, and to refrain from intense physical exercise. After a 12-h fast and following a randomized sequential crossover design with one-week washout period, participants reported to the hospital and received two fat meals consisting of 60 grams of white bread, 40 mL of VOO (CANOLIVA<sup>&#x000ae;</sup>, Antonio Cano e Hijos&#x02122;, Cordoba, Spain) with high (398 ppm) or low (70 ppm) content in phenolic compounds, and 60,000 IU of vitamin A per m<sup>2 </sup>of body surface. Throughout the 4-hour duration of the study session, subjects performed no physical activity, nor did they consume anything but water.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="sec"><h3>Olive oil characteristics</h3><p id="__p26" class="p p-first-last">Olive oil with low content in phenolic compounds was obtained by physical extraction of most phenolic compounds from the high-phenol olive oil, so that both oils retained a similar composition of the remaining macro- and micro-nutrients with the exception of phenolic content (70 ppm vs 398 ppm) (data not shown). Hydroxytyrosol content was 0.2 &#x003bc;mol.g<sup>-1 </sup>and 45.4 &#x003bc;mol.g<sup>-1</sup>, respectively in low-phenol and high-phenol olive oil. High-phenol olive oil was washed in separating funnels with the same volume of distilled water. This operation was repeated seven times at room temperature under reduced light to avoid oxidation. Fatty acid composition of oils was determined by gas chromatography in a Perkin-Elmer Autosystem (SGE Scientific, Australia) according to EU Regulation 2568/91 (CE, 1991). Tocopherol composition was analyzed by HPLC in a Perkin-Elmer HPLC, applying IUPAC method 2432 (IUPAC, 1992).</p></div><div id="__sec16" class="sec"><h3>Sample collection</h3><p id="__p27" class="p p-first-last">Venous blood samples were obtained at 0, 30, 60, 120 and 240 min after consumption of each breakfast. Samples from fasting and postprandial states were collected in tubes containing 1 g EDTA/L and stored in containers with iced water and kept in the dark. Special care was taken to avoid exposure to air, light, and ambient temperature. Plasma was separated from whole blood by low-speed centrifugation at 1500 &#x000d7; <em>g </em>for 15 min at 4&#x000b0;C within the hour after extraction.</p></div><div id="__sec17" class="sec"><h3>Lipid analysis and biochemical determinations</h3><p id="__p28" class="p p-first-last">Lipid parameters were assessed with the modular autoanalyzer DDPPII Hitachi (Roche, Basel, Switzerland), using specific reagents (Boehringer-Mannheim, Mannheim, Germany). Determinations of TC and TAG levels were made by colorimetric enzymatic methods [<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432228">48</a>,<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432261">49</a>]; of HDLc by colorimetric assay [<a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432272">50</a>]; of LDLc by the Friedewald formula based on CT, TAG, and HDLc values [<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_135432226">51</a>]. Plasma glucose concentrations were measured with a Hitachi 917 analyzer (Boehringer Mannheim, Mannheim, Germany) by the glucose oxidase method (GOD-PAP). Plasma insulin concentrations were measured by microparticle enzyme immunoassay (Abbott Diagnostics, Matsudo-shi, Japan). Nonsterified fatty acid concentrations were measured by enzymatic colorimetric assay (Roche Diagnostics, Penzberg, Germany).</p></div><div id="__sec18" class="sec"><h3>Isolation of PBMCs</h3><p id="__p29" class="p p-first-last">PBMCs were isolated within 2 hours after blood draw from 30 mL EDTA anticoagulated blood samples taken 4 hours after consumption of each olive oil-based breakfast. Buffy coats were diluted 1:2 in PBS, and cells were separated in 5 mL Ficoll gradient (lymphocyte isolation solution, Rafer) by centrifugation at 2000 &#x000d7; <em>g </em>for 30 min. PBMCs were collected and washed twice with cold PBS. Harvested PBMCs were preserved in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80&#x000b0;C prior to RNA extraction.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><h3>RNA extraction and microarray samples preparation</h3><p id="__p30" class="p p-first-last">Total RNA was extracted from mononuclear cells with TRI Reagent (Sigma-Aldrich, Inc., St. Louis, MO, USA) and purified with RNeasy MiniElute Cleanup Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Recovered RNA was quantified using a Nanodrop ND-1000 v3.5.2 spectrophotometer (Nanodrop Technology<sup>&#x000ae;</sup>, Cambridge, UK). RNA integrity was assessed using 1.6% agarose gel, 1&#x000d7; TBE. RNA was judged suitable for array hybridization only if samples exhibited intact bands corresponding to 18S and 28S ribosomal RNAs.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec"><h3>Microarray analysis design</h3><p id="__p31" class="p p-first-last">Gene expression profiles were generated using the <em>4x44K </em>glass slide <em>Whole Human Genome Oligo Microarray G4112A </em>(Agilent Technologies Inc., Santa Clara, CA, USA). Each microarray uses 45,220 probes to interrogate 30,886 unique human genes and transcripts. Two sets of dye-swapped experiments were performed to total four replicate hybridizations per subject (totals 80 microarray experiments). Each array compared total RNA from PBMCs obtained 4 hours after intake of virgin olive oil with high phenolic content with RNA obtained 4 hours after low-phenol virgin olive oil consumption.</p></div><div id="__sec21" class="sec"><h3>Synthesis and labeling of cDNA</h3><p id="__p32" class="p p-first-last">RNA samples were labeled using the <em>SuperScript Indirect RNA Amplification System </em>(Invitrogen Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. To avoid confounding by extraneous factors, all experiments were performed in a single batch and processed by one researcher on the same day for each step. Dye incorporation rates were assessed with a Nanodrop<sup>&#x000ae; </sup>ND-1000 v3.5.2 spectrophotometer (Nanodrop Technology<sup>&#x000ae;</sup>, Cambridge, UK) and found to be between 8 and 12 pmol. &#x003bc;L<sup>-1</sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec22" class="sec"><h3>Microarray hybridation and scan protocol</h3><p id="__p33" class="p p-first-last">Differentially labeled aRNA samples were cohybridized on microarray slides. Hybridization was performed using the <em>Gene Expression Hybridization Kit </em>(Agilent Technologies Inc., Santa Clara, CA, USA) following the manufacturer's instructions. Microarray images of each slide were obtained with a Gene Pix 4000B scanner (Axon Instruments, Union City, CA, USA). Image quantization was performed using Agilent Feature Extraction Software v9.5 (Agilent Technologies Inc., Santa Clara, CA, USA).</p></div><div id="__sec23" class="sec"><h3>Preprocessing and normalization of the microarray data</h3><p id="__p34" class="p p-first-last">Background adjusted signals (BGSubSignal) were calculated by the Agilent Feature Extraction Software (v9.5) and filtered on the flag IsWellAboveBG. Log<sub>2 </sub>dye swapped (red/green) mean-centered signal ratio data were normalized within-array and between-array by Lowess and quantile methods respectively, both implemented in the R Limma package (GLP).</p></div><div id="__sec24" class="sec"><h3>Differential gene expression analysis</h3><p id="__p35" class="p p-first-last">Identification of genes whose expression could be regulated by virgin olive oil phenols was done by comparing microarray results from PBMCs obtained at 4 hours after high and low phenol olive oil ingestion. Differential gene expression analysis was performed by fitting of gene-wise linear models to microarray data with R Limma package (GLP). For considering a gene as differentially expressed, filtering criteria were used combining M value (signal log<sub>2 </sub>ratio) greater than 0.4 (over-expressed) or lower than -0.4 (under-expressed) (by 1.32 and -1.32-fold changes, respectively), and statistical significance (<em>p</em>) of the fitted linear model of &#x02264; 0.01 for every gene. Results were adjusted by dye-swap effects and by False Discovery Rate (FDR) using Benjamini and Hochberg method.</p></div><div id="__sec25" class="sec"><h3>MIAME Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database</h3><p id="__p36" class="p p-first-last">Raw microarray data have been curated and accepted in GEO (a public repository for microarray data, aimed at storing MIAME (Minimum Information About Microarray Experiments) compliant data in accordance with MGED (Microarray Gene Expression Data) recommendations. Access to data concerning this study may be found under GEO experiment accession number <a id="__tag_135432279" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15812" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15812"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE15812","term_id":"15812"}}</span>GSE15812</a> <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?token=pjihtumyoyogmhy&#x00026;acc=GSE15812" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2874810&amp;issue-id=184100&amp;journal-id=32&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?token=pjihtumyoyogmhy&#x00026;acc=<a id="__tag_135432278" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15812" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15812"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE15812","term_id":"15812"}}</span>GSE15812</a></a>.</p></div><div id="__sec26" class="sec"><h3>qRT-PCR analysis of differentially expressed genes</h3><p id="__p37" class="p p-first-last">PCR analyses were performed using Mx3005 Thermocycler (Agilent Technologies, La Jolla, CA) and <em>iQ SYBR</em><sup>&#x000ae;</sup><em>Green Supermix </em>(Bio-Rad Laboratories, Inc., Hercules, CA, USA) commercial kit in a final volume of 20 &#x003bc;l with 10 pmol of each primer. Each reaction was performed on 1 &#x003bc;l of 1:5 (v/v) dilution of the first cDNA strand, synthesized using 1 &#x003bc;g of total RNA by the commercial kit <em>iScript</em><sup>&#x000ae; </sup><em>cDNA Synthesis Kit </em>(Bio-Rad Laboratories, Inc., Hercules, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer's instruction. The reaction was incubated at 96&#x000b0;C for 3 min, followed by 40 cycles of 30 s at 96&#x000b0;C, 30 s at 62&#x000b0;C, 20 s at 72&#x000b0;C and 10 s at 80&#x000b0;C where fluorescence was measured to avoid primer-dimer and background signals. Primers used were: ERG1-Up (5'-CTACGAGCACCTGACCGCAGAG-3'), ERG1-Low (5'-CCAGGGAAAAGCGGCCAGTATAG-3'), JUN-Up (5'-GACCTTCTATGACGATGCCCTCAA-3'), JUN-Low (5'-ACGTCGGGCGAGGTGAGGAGGTC-3'), IL1B-Up (5'-GGGACAGGATATGGAGCAACAAGT-3'), IL1B-Low (5'-GCTTATCATCTTTCAACACGCAGGA-3'), PTGS2-Up (5'-ATGAGTTATGTCTTGACATCCAGATCAC-3'), PTGS2-Low (5'-CAAACATCATGTTTGAGCCCTGG-3'), RPL13-Up (5'-CCTGGAGGAGAAGAGGAAAGAGA-3') and RPL13-Low (5'-TTGAGGACCTCTGTGTATTTGTCAA-3'). Specificity of PCR amplifications was verified by a melting curve program (60-95&#x000b0;C with a heating rate of 0.5&#x000b0;C/s and a continuous fluorescence measurement) and analyzed by electrophoresis on a 1.6% agarose gel, TBE 1&#x000d7;. Primer efficiencies were: EGR1, 98%; PTGS2, 101%; IL1B, 96%; JUN, 108%; RPL13a, 95%. Expression values were obtained as relative expression of the target gene versus the constitutively expressed RPL13a gene (relative expression = 2<sup>-(Ct, Target gene-Ct, Reference gene)</sup>).</p></div><div id="__sec27" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Statistical analysis</h3><p id="__p38" class="p p-first">Several variables were transformed to define the metabolic postprandial response after acute intake of both types of olive oil. Incremental area under plasma concentration curve (iAUC) was calculated for all measured metabolic variables by the trapezoidal method. The normality of data was assessed using the Saphiro-Wilk test. Log transformation of data was performed when those variables were not normally distributed. Differences between iAUC after intake of high-phenol and low-phenol olive oils were analyzed using Wilcoxon-paired test.</p><p id="__p39">For data analysis, gene expression data were presented in M-values [log<sub>2</sub>(ratio)] expressing a fold change after high-phenol olive oil intake compared to low-phenol olive oil consumption. The corresponding <em>p</em>-value, B-value, and B-probability are shown (Additional File <a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">1</a>). The data input for correlation analysis using Spearman's test were the log<sub>2</sub>(ratio) value for microarrays (M-value) and qRT-PCR in subject individuals.</p><p id="__p40" class="p p-last">All data presented in the text and tables are expressed as mean &#x000b1; SD unless otherwise specified. Significance levels were set to <em>p</em>-values less than 0.01 for microarray data and less than 0.05 for all other analyses. All statistical tests were performed using R programming and statistical environment software (GLP).</p></div></div><div id="__sec28" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec28title">Abbreviations used</h2><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">ppm: part per million; MetS: metabolic syndrome; CVD: cardiovascular disease; VOO: virgin olive oil; MD: Mediterranean Diet; PBMC: peripheral blood; mononuclear cells; MEGN: macrophage-enriched gene network.</p></div><div id="__sec29" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec29title">Authors' contributions</h2><p id="__p42" class="p p-first-last">AC and JR contributed equally to this work.
