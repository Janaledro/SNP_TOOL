Several of these genes control the metabolism of simple carbohydrates and are direct targets for the SREBP1, a metabolic transcription factor also differentially expressed between our study populations.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusions/Significance</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">These data support the concept of stable patterns of gene transcription unique to a geographic ancestral lineage. Differences in expression of several carbohydrate metabolism genes suggest both genetic and transcriptional mechanisms contribute to these patterns and may play a role in exacerbating the disproportionate levels of obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease observed in Americans with African ancestry.</p></div></div></div><div id="s1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p5" class="p p-first">Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are multifactorial conditions with strong genetic and environmental influences <a href="#pone.0008183-Kurian1" rid="pone.0008183-Kurian1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030559">[1]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Smith1" rid="pone.0008183-Smith1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_674940949">[2]</a>. Despite many advances in diagnosis and treatment, significant challenges remain in understanding, treating and possibly preventing these conditions <a href="#pone.0008183-Flordellis1" rid="pone.0008183-Flordellis1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030564">[3]</a>. Most forms of CVD are multi-factorial, influenced by genetic predispositions as well as environmental factors. On a genetic level, the contribution of any single gene is often small, making investigations of candidate genes difficult to draw any conclusions towards the etiology of CVD <a href="#pone.0008183-Arnett1" rid="pone.0008183-Arnett1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030557">[4]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-VanRegenmortel1" rid="pone.0008183-VanRegenmortel1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030522">[5]</a>. Initial attempts to characterize the underlying causes of CVD have identified a plethora of heterogeneous risk factors including: demographic factors such as family history of premature CVD, gender, and race; behavioral factors including smoking, diet, and activity level; metabolic/biochemical factors related to adiposity, plasma homocysteine, cholesterol levels; and the presence of co-morbid conditions (for example diabetes and hypertension). Whereas individual risk factors often lack significance in terms of predictive power for any given illness, assessment of several risk factors allows appropriate medical interventions both for prevention and treatment of CVD <a href="#pone.0008183-Grundy1" rid="pone.0008183-Grundy1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030521">[6]</a>.</p><p id="__p6">The study of ancestry and genetics is a highly controversial subject <a href="#pone.0008183-Duster1" rid="pone.0008183-Duster1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030541">[7]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Goodman1" rid="pone.0008183-Goodman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030566">[8]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Ossorio1" rid="pone.0008183-Ossorio1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030560">[9]</a>. However, studies have shown that Americans of African ancestry have up to a 2.5-fold increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes, five-fold increased risk of CVD, and eight-fold increase in mortality from CVD compared to Americans of European ancestry <a href="#pone.0008183-Brancati1" rid="pone.0008183-Brancati1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030546">[10]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Williams1" rid="pone.0008183-Williams1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030539">[11]</a>. The molecular basis for the increased frequency of these disease occurrences in Americans of African ancestry remains unclear and cannot be adequately explained by social marginalization or various theories of access to health care <a href="#pone.0008183-Kurian1" rid="pone.0008183-Kurian1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030554">[1]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Williams1" rid="pone.0008183-Williams1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030519">[11]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Sequist1" rid="pone.0008183-Sequist1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030529">[12]</a>.</p><p id="__p7" class="p p-last">The purpose of this study was to identify differential transcriptional signals associated with CVD susceptibility and ancestry. Using genetic samples obtained from a cohort of subjects undergoing cardiac-related evaluation, a strict algorithm that filtered for genomic features at multiple levels identified 151 differentially-expressed genes between Americans of African ancestry and those of European ancestry. Many of the genes identified were associated with glucose and simple sugar metabolism, suggestive of a model whereby selective adaptation to the nutritional environment differs between populations of humans separated geographically over time. These observations represent promising preliminary data indicating that gene expression profiles can be used to phenotypically describe ancestral populations. Furthermore, the data offer at least one potential explanation for the rising incidence of obesity, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome and CVD in the American population as a whole.</p></div><div id="s2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s2title">Materials and Methods</h2><div id="s2a" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Study Guidelines and Processing</h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">Subjects were enrolled in the University of North Carolina Institutional Review Board approved &#x0201c;SAMARA&#x0201d; study (IRB 04-MED-471). Exclusion criteria included pregnancy, lymphoma, leukemia, chronic immunosuppressive therapy, infection with HIV or HCV, history of solid organ transplant, and anemia. Blood was drawn early in the day from fasted subjects to minimize signals associated with nutritional and diurnal cycle and processed within fifteen minutes. Plasma samples were obtained and RNA and DNA recovered from leukocytes using a modified one-step acid guanidinium thiocyanate-phenol-chloroform extraction (RNA-STAT60, Tel-Test, TX).</p></div><div id="s2b" class="sec"><h3>Microarray and qRT-PCR Analysis</h3><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">Labeled cRNA was co-hybridized to Agilent G4112A Whole Human Genome 44K oligonucleotide arrays with equimolar amounts of Cyanine-3 labeled Universal Human Reference RNA (UHRR, Stratagene, LaJolla, CA) as previously described <a href="#pone.0008183-Charles1" rid="pone.0008183-Charles1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030530">[13]</a>. Complete, MIAME-compliant datasets were deposited with the Gene Expression Omnibus of the National Center for Biotechnology Information and can be accessed through GEO Series accession number <a id="__tag_671030567" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE12959" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE12959"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE12959","term_id":"12959"}}</span>GSE12959</a>. Ten micrograms of total RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the High Capacity cDNA Reverse Transcription Kit (ABI, Applied Biosystems, Framingham, MA) and quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) reactions were performed using the ABI PRISM&#x000ae; 7900 HT sequence detection system, software and reagents; see <a href="#pone.0008183.s002" rid="pone.0008183.s002" class=" supplementary-material">Table S1</a> for primer and probe information. RNA input was calibrated with <em>18S</em> expression levels and relative mRNA levels were normalized to levels from the UHRR.</p></div><div id="s2c" class="sec"><h3>Genotype Analysis</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">DNA labeling, hybridization, and data extraction were performed by the DNA Array Core Facility at The Scripps Research Institute (Jupiter, FL). The Genome-Wide Human SNP Array 6.0 (Affymetrix&#x000ae;) was used for hybridizations. Identification of local elements associated with expression (eQTLs) was performed with linear modeling tools in the software package R. For a given gene, all SNPs within 10 kb of the untranslated region were tested. Each SNP was tested by grouping the expression values based on the genotype and assuming an additive relationship between number of &#x02018;B&#x02019; alleles and expression level. The genes were selected for differential expression between ancestries, and PCA illustrated segregation of ethnicities based on the genotypes. This combination may inflate the theoretical number of false positives from the linear model. In order to minimize bias, the eQTL procedure was repeated after randomizing the gene-SNP pairs. After 100 such randomizations these permuted statistics were compared to actual statistics in order to estimate the empirical false discovery rate at each theoretical p value threshold. This permutation procedure is specific for identifying local-acting SNPs since it assumes no distant-acting SNPs, and thus is a conservative estimate in the presence of the potential selection bias.</p></div><div id="s2d" class="sec"><h3>Statistical Methods</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">Microarray data were normalized via the loess local intensity normalization method of Smyth and Speed <a href="#pone.0008183-Smyth1" rid="pone.0008183-Smyth1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030517">[14]</a>, and probes were filtered for features having a normalized intensity of &#x0003c;30 aFU in both channels. Probes were removed if &#x0003c;70&#x00025; of the data were present across all samples. Missing data points were imputed using the k nearest-neighbors algorithm (k&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;17). 18,375 probes passed these filters, and were subsequently used for analysis. Scripts written in the R Statistical Language and Environment (&#x0201c;R&#x0201d;; Version 2.2.1, build r36812, release date 2005-12-20.) and Perl (ActiveState Perl 5.8.1, build 807, release date 2003-11-6) were used to standardize (&#x003bc;&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0, &#x003c3;&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;1) the data set. Samples were tested for processing time-dependent correlation with gene expression and found to be clear of any technical confounding variables <a href="#pone.0008183-Akey1" rid="pone.0008183-Akey1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030558">[15]</a>. Furthermore, to avoid any potential analysis bias, ancestry was not associated with subject ID number. Lists of differentially expressed genes were identified using the statistical analysis of microarray algorithm <a href="#pone.0008183-Tusher1" rid="pone.0008183-Tusher1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030520">[16]</a> (SAM, Version 2.21, release date 2005-8-24; typical false discovery rate of 1&#x00025; and 10&#x00025;), and custom R scripts written in our laboratory. Unsupervised, semi-supervised, and supervised clustering analysis were performed on gene lists essentially as described <a href="#pone.0008183-Eisen1" rid="pone.0008183-Eisen1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030503">[17]</a> using Cluster (Version 2.11, <a href="http://rana.lbl.gov/EisenSoftware.htm" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2790609&amp;issue-id=182901&amp;journal-id=440&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://rana.lbl.gov/EisenSoftware.htm</a>). Heatmaps of cluster analyses were visualized with JavaTreeView (Version 1.0.12, release date 2005-3-14; <a href="http://sourceforge.net/projects/jtreeview/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2790609&amp;issue-id=182901&amp;journal-id=440&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://sourceforge.net/projects/jtreeview/</a>) <a href="#pone.0008183-Saldanha1" rid="pone.0008183-Saldanha1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030500">[18]</a>. Nearest centroid classification was performed by calculating two centroids, or vectors of the class mean (AA or CAU) of each gene. Test cases were assigned the class of the most similar centroid as measured by Euclidean distance.</p></div><div id="s2e" class="sec"><h3>Plasma Fructosamine Assays</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">Plasma fructosamine levels were determined using the Kamiya Biosciences (Seattle, WA) Fructosamine Assay Kit, following the manufacturer's recommended protocol. Ten microliters of archived plasma from each subject were utilized for analysis.</p></div><div id="s2f" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Immunoblotting</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">Plasma protein concentration was determined for each archived plasma sample (Bio-Rad Quick Start Bradford Assay, Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). Twenty-five micrograms of total protein were reduced, denatured, and resolved on 4&#x02013;12&#x00025; NuPAGE&#x000ae; Novex Bis-Tris Gels (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) in the MES/SDS buffer system. Proteins were transferred to PVDF membranes, reacted with chicken anti-human haptoglobin (NB300-330, Novus, Littleton, CO) and detected with rabbit anti-chicken IGY HRP-conjugate (Sigma, St. Louis, MO). Bands were visualized with Pierce ECL Substrate (Pierce, Rockford, IL). Relative levels of haptoglobin were quantified using Image J (NIH, Bethesda, MD).</p></div></div><div id="s3" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s3title">Results</h2><div id="s3a" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Demographics and Covariates Analyses</h3><p id="__p14" class="p p-first">One hundred and sixty-three subjects referred to cardiology services at UNC between the ages of 18 and 50 years enrolled in Phase One of the SAMARA (Supporting a Multi-disciplinary Approach to Researching Atherosclerosis) study were used for this analysis. Using unsupervised clustering and principal components analysis, the variation in gene expression data among the study subjects resulted in a binary segregation of subjects based on self-reported race, either &#x0201c;African American&#x0201d; (AA) or &#x0201c;Caucasian&#x0201d; (CAU). Exclusion of gender and coronary artery disease as confounding factors limited the initial analysis to a &#x0201c;discovery set&#x0201d; of 17 AA and 30 CAU subjects, with equal contributions of gender per cohort.</p><p id="__p15">Within the discovery set of subjects, four demographic variables differed significantly in AA versus CAU subjects: lower smoking pack years and hematocrit levels, and higher occurrence of hypertension and fructosamine levels (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/table/pone-0008183-t001/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-t001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-t001" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). These findings are in line with other studies performed in the United States that report increased diagnosis of hypertension and decreased mean hematocrit values and smoking rates in Americans of African ancestry versus those of European decent <a href="#pone.0008183-Kurian1" rid="pone.0008183-Kurian1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030524">[1]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Williams1" rid="pone.0008183-Williams1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030504">[11]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Chen1" rid="pone.0008183-Chen1" class=" of gene expression within the discovery set, we investigated gene expression patterns associated with hematocrit levels, smoking pack-years, hypertension, or fructosamine. A two-class SAM analysis compared the bottom quartile subjects to top quartile subjects and negatives to positives for the continuous and categorical variables, respectively. This method failed to identify any differentially expressed genes (false discovery rate &#x0003c;20&#x00025;). Alternatively, performing SAM as a quantitative analysis on the continuous variables yielded the same results, indicating these clinical and demographic features are unlikely to impair detection of distinct ancestral transcriptional profiles.</p></div><div id="s3b" class="sec"><h3>Differences in Glucose Homeostasis</h3><p id="__p18" class="p p-first-last">Despite the numerous studies associating increased rates of metabolic syndrome in persons of African descent, there was no significant difference in clinical diagnosis of diabetes mellitus or mean fasting plasma glucose between AA and CAU subjects (data not shown). We used the measurement of plasma fructosamine as a surrogate marker for functional diabetes, using a threshold value of 2.6 mM/L <a href="#pone.0008183-Baker1" rid="pone.0008183-Baker1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_674940950">[20]</a>. Fructosamine measures the concentration of glycated protein adducts in the blood to assess regulation of glucose levels in the diabetic patient over a time period of weeks. Consistent with clinical diagnosis and fasting blood glucose data there was no significant difference between AA and CAU subjects in the number of subjects with fructosamine levels above threshold. However, when fructosamine was analyzed as a continuous variable, we identified significantly higher concentrations in AA compared to CAU subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/table/pone-0008183-t001/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-t001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-t001" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>), suggesting a sub-clinical predisposition to dysglycemia in AA subjects. Overall, the observed differences in fructosamine and other variables (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/table/pone-0008183-t001/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-t001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-t001" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>) within the discovery set of this study agrees with previously published reports on the same topic, implying that, although the number of cohorts in each group was relatively small, the two study groups used in this report are largely representative of their respective populations in the United States. Importantly, the lack of correlation between fructosamine levels and gene expression across our subjects lessens the probability of long-term glucose homeostasis impairment confounding ancestry-dependent expression analyses.</p></div><div id="s3c" class="sec"><h3>Identification of Transcriptional Expression Patterns Associated with Ancestry</h3><p id="__p19" class="p p-first">In this discovery set, the SAM statistical technique <a href="#pone.0008183-Tusher1" rid="pone.0008183-Tusher1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030518">[16]</a> identified 2521 probes, corresponding to 2331 genes, that were significantly differentially expressed between CAU and AA groups, using a false discovery rate of 1&#x00025; (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g001/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g001" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g001"><span>Figure 1</span></a>, <a href="#pone.0008183.s003" rid="pone.0008183.s003" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>). Given this large number of differentially expressed genes between the study groups, we refined these data by concentrating our focus on genetic differences that had been identified previously between similar populations represented in the HapMap project. The HapMap project is a collection of genetic differences, <em>i.e.</em> single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP), that have been identified between human populations of different geographical regions <a href="#pone.0008183-Thorisson1" rid="pone.0008183-Thorisson1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030533">[21]</a>. Using this approach, we identified the differentially expressed genes from the SAM analysis that contained at least one SNP (within 10kb of the untranslated regions) that distinguishes two HapMap populations with similar ancestral origins as our AA and CAU study groups, the Yoruba people in Ibadan, Nigeria (abbreviation: YRI) and the CEPH population (Utah residents with ancestry in northern and western Europe, abbreviation: CEU), respectively. This analysis uncovered 897 genes (of the 2331 differentially expressed genes in the discovery set, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g001/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g001" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g001"><span>Figure 1</span></a>) that had single nucleotide polymorphisms (12,276 total SNPs) that were statistically different between YRI versus CEPH populations (p value&#x0003c;1.25E-07, Bonferroni's corrected p value of 0.01, <a href="#pone.0008183.s003" rid="pone.0008183.s003" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>). Further refining the 897 gene list to those genes that had an absolute mean fold change (MFC) cutoff of greater than 1.3 in our discovery set resulted in the identification of 151 genes; we define Other studies identified genetically linked gene expression differences between various HapMap populations <a href="#pone.0008183-Stranger1" rid="pone.0008183-Stranger1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030537">[23]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Spielman1" rid="pone.0008183-Spielman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030532">[24]</a>. Likewise, we used the assumption that the ancestry of AA and CAU subjects in this study was similar to the YRI and CEPH populations, respectively, to generate our list of geo-ancestral genes. However, to test that this assumption was correct, DNA from our discovery set was genotyped using the Affymetrix&#x000ae; Genome-Wide Human SNP Array 6.0, which allowed comparison of principle component analysis of our data with 90 representative samples from each of the YRI and CEU populations. Sorting by the first and second component identified 26 of 30 CAU subjects as more similar to the CEPH versus YRI population and AA subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g002/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g002" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g002" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g002"><span>Figure 2</span></a>). Likewise, 16 of 17 AA subjects associated more with YRI population than the CEPH population and CAU subjects. The alignment of our CAU and AA study cohorts with CEPH and YRI populations previously identified by the HapMap study once again lends credence to accuracy of ethnic identification in the present study. Western blot analysis of total plasma isolated from the subjects used in our study revealed a 2.9&#x000b1;0.5 fold increase in circulating HP in CAU versus AA subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g003" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g003"><span>Figure 3B</span></a>), consistent with both microarray and qRT-PCR analysis (<a href="#pone.0008183.s003" rid="pone.0008183.s003" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g003" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g003"><span>Figure 3A</span></a>). Ancestral-based differences in the levels of plasma haptoglobin are well described in the literature, and correlate with a multitude of genetic distinctions: allelic differences in the coding regions of <em>HP</em>
<a href="#pone.0008183-Carter1" rid="pone.0008183-Carter1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030552">[28]</a>, SNPs in the upstream promoter sequences <a href="#pone.0008183-Grant1" rid="pone.0008183-Grant1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030510">[30]</a>, and intronic regulatory elements <a href="#pone.0008183-Hatada1" rid="pone.0008183-Hatada1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030507">[31]</a>. Importantly, a number of recent studies implicate the absolute amount and quality of the <em>HP</em> gene product as an independent risk factor for a multitude of diseases including: diabetes <a href="#pone.0008183-Levy1" rid="pone.0008183-Levy1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030528">[32]</a>; atherosclerosis <a href="#pone.0008183-Levy2" rid="pone.0008183-Levy2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030501">[33]</a>; poor clinical outcome following myocardial infarction <a href="#pone.0008183-Carter1" rid="pone.0008183-Carter1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030527">[28]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Blum1" rid="pone.0008183-Blum1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030511">[34]</a>; and percutaneous coronary interventions <a href="#pone.0008183-Blum1" rid="pone.0008183-Blum1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030545">[34]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Roguin1" rid="pone.0008183-Roguin1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030505">[35]</a>. In all of these cases, lower levels of functional haptoglobin increase the likelihood of developing diabetes and cardiovascular disease.</p></div><div id="s3f" class="sec"><h3>Validation of Ancestral Patterns of Gene Expression</h3><p id="__p29" class="p p-first">In order to determine how predictive our geo-ancestral gene set was of the general population, we used an independent validation set comprised of 112 unrelated subjects, similarly classified by self-reported ancestry (32 AA and 80 CAU), to validate the 151 geo-ancestral genes. A two-tailed Student's T test identified 102 of the 151 genes (67.5&#x00025;) as differentially expressed at a p value of &#x02264;0.05 (range: p&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;8.32&#x000d7;10<sup>&#x02212;16</sup> (<em>PSPHL</em>) to p&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;4.96&#x000d7;10<sup>&#x02212;2</sup> (<em>STX3A</em>); <a href="#pone.0008183.s003" rid="pone.0008183.s003" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>). Furthermore, using the 151 genes for supervised principle component analysis, AA and CAU subjects successfully separated both discovery and validation sets. As expected, principal component analysis successfully grouped the discovery set subjects, with less than 7.0&#x00025; misclassification (1/17 AA and 2/30 CAU, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g004/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g004" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g004"><span>Figure 4A</span></a>). Parallel analysis on the validation set led to a similar level of ancestral discrimination in the independent subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g004/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g004" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g004"><span>Figure 4B</span></a>). A simple nearest centroid classifier built from all 151 genes yielded 84&#x00025; accuracy in the validation set. These data validate the gene expression patterns observed in the discovery set of 47 subjects, and demonstrate that these geo-ancestral genes are in fact stable phenotypes in Americans of African and European ancestry. Understanding the functional relationships within this gene set could potentially help in explaining the disproportionate predisposition of CVD and other diseases between these populations, a topic that we explore below.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="pone-0008183-g004" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g004"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g004/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g004" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g004"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139992726764944" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=2790609_pone.0008183.g004.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is pone.0008183.g004.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/bin/pone.0008183.g004.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139992726764944" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g004/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_pone-0008183-g004"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g004/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g004" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g004">Figure 4</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Validation of geo-ancestral genes.</strong><p id="__p30">The 151 geo-ancestral genes were used to perform supervised principle components analysis of the discovery set of 47 subjects (<strong>A</strong>) and the validation set of 112 unrelated subjects (<strong>B</strong>). Interestingly, the decreased expression of these genes in the AA cohort was notable because of the increased plasma fructosamine levels in these same subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/table/pone-0008183-t001/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-t001" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-t001" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). These results suggest that differences in glucose metabolism between Americans of African and European may reside at the transcriptional level. The <em>down-regulation</em> of these genes in the AA cohorts argues against these changes being a compensatory response to hyperglycemia and suggests instead a genetic adaptation to changes in the availability of dietary sugars that may no longer be appropriate to a Western Diet. In order to explore this idea further and to determine the functional importance of the genetic differences we identified, we used hyperclustering analysis of our geo-ancestral gene set to test for differential expression of gene sets that underlie common biological process. Hyperclustering is a method of associating genes with significant enrichments in Gene Ontologies, KEGG pathways, and TRANSFAC analysis <a href="#pone.0008183-Charles1" rid="pone.0008183-Charles1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030543">[13]</a>. Of the eight GO categories and four KEGG pathways enriched at a threshold of p&#x02264;0.01, half belonged to the Carbohydrate Metabolism hypercluster. These overrepresented KEGG pathways and Gene Ontologies within the Carbohydrate Metabolism hypercluster reaffirm the initial observation of differential expression of carbohydrate metabolic genes, and begin to shed light on factors that may affect glycemic regulation in different ancestral populations.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" This analysis led to the identification of significantly enriched binding sites (p&#x02264;0.02) of four predicted transcription factors in the gene set: AML6, HNF3&#x003b1;, E2F1, and SREBP1. Although transcription factor activity can be influenced by several factors, such as post-transcriptional and post-translational modifications and the availability of co-activators and co-repressors, the direction of change in overall activity predicts a complementary change in expression of target genes. The only significant enrichment in either up- or down-regulated target genes of the four transcription factors was SREBP1, exhibiting a 2.9-fold enrichment in down-regulated genes (p&#x0003c;0.05, <a href="#pone.0008183.s004" rid="pone.0008183.s004" class=" supplementary-material">Table S3</a>). Consistent with this observation, microarray and qRT-PCR analysis identified expression for the gene encoding for SREBP1, <em>SREBF1</em>, as significantly decreased by 0.3&#x000b1;0.1-fold in AA relative to CAU subjects (t-test p&#x0003c;0.001, SAM q-value of zero, qRT-PCR p&#x0003c;0.05, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g003/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g003" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g003" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g003"><span>Figure 3A</span></a>, <a href="#pone.0008183.s003" rid="pone.0008183.s003" class=" supplementary-material">Table S2</a>).</p><p id="__p34" class="p p-last">Although SREBP1 was initially characterized as a primary regulator of cholesterol anabolic genes <a href="#pone.0008183-Brown1" rid="pone.0008183-Brown1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030534">[36]</a>, recent studies in animal models detail the critical role SREBP1 plays in the long-term control of both lipid and <em>glucose</em> homeostasis in an insulin-dependent manner. As such, SREBP1 mediates the regulation of insulin and glucose responsive genes in a variety of tissues, including skeletal muscle, liver, adipose, and the pancreatic islets of Langerhans <a href="#pone.0008183-Gosmain1" rid="pone.0008183-Gosmain1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030551">[37]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Qi1" rid="pone.0008183-Qi1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030556">[38]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Diraison1" rid="pone.0008183-Diraison1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030542">[39]</a>. Promoters of five of the eight genes in the carbohydrate metabolic hypercluster (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g005/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g005" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g005" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g005"><span>Figure 5</span></a>) contain SREBP1_Q6 binding motifs. Importantly, while a sequence algorithm identified potential SREBP1 binding sites in these genes, ChIP analysis and DNase footprinting determined SREBP1 <em>directly interacts</em> with the promoters and mediates the transcription of both <em>HKII</em>
<a href="#pone.0008183-Gosmain2" rid="pone.0008183-Gosmain2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030561">[40]</a> and <em>PGD</em>
<a href="#pone.0008183-Rho1" rid="pone.0008183-Rho1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030508">[41]</a>, which encode the first enzymes in glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway, respectively. These data provide a mechanism by which a decrease in SREBP1 expression and transcriptional activity promotes the differential expression of several geo-ancestral genes including multiple carbohydrate metabolic genes.</p></div><div id="s3i" class="sec sec-last"><h3>The Influence of <em>cis</em>-Acting Elements Associated with Gene Expression</h3><p id="__p35" class="p p-first">Gene expression is influenced by a variety of factors, such as the thousands of common <em>cis</em>-acting variations that occur in the population as well as <em>trans</em>-acting factors, such as the activity of transcription factors, RNA processing, and signaling molecules <a href="#pone.0008183-Rockman1" rid="pone.0008183-Rockman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030540">[42]</a>. Expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) analysis combines gene expression and genotyping (<em>i.e.</em> SNP) data to determine if changes in gene expression correlate to variations in genomic sequence. We used local eQTL analysis to identify <em>cis</em>-acting genetic contributions to the differential expression pattern of the geo-ancestral genes.</p><p id="__p36">Differentially expressed genes and SNP associations were both identified with respect to ancestry; as such, the association between genotype and gene expression may be artificially increased (<a href="#pone.0008183.s001" rid="pone.0008183.s001" class=" supplementary-material">Figure S1</a>). This potential bias was minimized by permutation of the SNP &#x02013; gene pairs. Association of a SNP with expression after this permutation is assumed to be due to the selection bias. This procedure generates a distribution from which to calculate the expected false discovery rate for a threshold and corresponding set of candidate eQTLs. Comparing the number of observed p values versus expected p values from permutation resulted in more eQTL associations than expected at reasonable thresholds (<em>e.g.</em> 16 observed eQTLs compared to 3 expected SNP; FDR&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;15.8&#x00025;, p&#x0003c;0.00025, <a href="#pone.0008183.s005" rid="pone.0008183.s005" class=" supplementary-material">Table S4</a>). This knowledge not only informs the disease data analysis process, it provides important insight into the range of baseline transcriptional regulation in human populations. The International HapMap Project characterizes the scope of genetic differences by genomic sequencing human populations from different geographical areas: Europe, Asia, and Africa. It is important to emphasize that the HapMap Project is highly informative, despite small numbers of subjects from different ancestries: for example, the YRI and CEU datasets derive from 90 total subjects each (30 trios of two parents and an adult child). This effort tabulated millions of single nucleotide polymorphisms within these populations <a href="#pone.0008183-Thorisson1" rid="pone.0008183-Thorisson1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030523">[21]</a>. Several groups have used these data to explore the genetic components of multi-factorial diseases <a href="#pone.0008183-Kim1" rid="pone.0008183-Kim1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030515">[43]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-TaillonMiller1" rid="pone.0008183-TaillonMiller1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030553">[44]</a>. Recently, whole genome scans identified single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) within the p21.3 region of chromosome 9 that are associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease and myocardial infarction in Caucasian populations <a href="#pone.0008183-McPherson1" rid="pone.0008183-McPherson1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030526">[45]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Helgadottir1" rid="pone.0008183-Helgadottir1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030562">[46]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Samani1" rid="pone.0008183-Samani1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030565">[47]</a>. Although there is no mechanistic data on the association of these non-coding SNPs with disease, it is likely that these silent polymorphisms are associated with transcriptional control of gene expression <a href="#pone.0008183-Drake1" rid="pone.0008183-Drake1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030509">[48]</a>. The burgeoning correlations between whole-genome SNP patterns and transcriptional regulation is redefining the use of integrative genomics to understand multi-factorial diseases, such as cardiovascular and metabolic diseases <a href="#pone.0008183-Glinsky1" rid="pone.0008183-Glinsky1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030563">[49]</a>.</p><p id="__p41">We acknowledge that multi-center genome-wide association studies on cardiovascular disease and diabetes include very large cohorts; however, our approach was designed to better understand disease biology by identifying heritable traits that influence gene expression, not to identify genetic markers solely based on their predictive power of a disease state. Using this approach, the largest transcriptional difference observed in this study was associated with the self-reported ancestry of the subjects. It can be argued that the concept of race, especially self-reported race can be unreliable. However, the correlation between genetic data obtained from our study cohorts respective of self-proclaimed race and data reported from other groups studying similar ancestral populations supports the validity of our cohort partitioning. Indeed, an integrative data analysis, incorporating SNPs identified in the HapMap project, identified differentially expressed genes between Americans of African (AA) and European (CAU) ancestry in the United States that were also structurally distinct between European and African populations (as identified in the HapMap project) that we classified as &#x0201c;<em>geo-ancestral genes</em>&#x0201d;. Many of the geo-ancestral genes expressed at lower levels in AA compared to CAU subjects were associated with carbohydrate and glucose metabolism. This subset of genes contained local eQTLs (<em>cis</em>-acting) as well as predicted and/or confirmed binding sites for the metabolic transcription factor, SREBP1 (<em>trans</em>-acting), also expressed lower in AA subjects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2790609/figure/pone-0008183-g007/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="pone-0008183-g007" rid-ob="ob-pone-0008183-g007" co-legend-rid="lgnd_pone-0008183-g007"><span>Figure 7</span></a>). These results are consistent with the observations that Americans of African ancestry are disproportionately affected by obesity, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease <a href="#pone.0008183-Kurian1" rid="pone.0008183-Kurian1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030538">[1]</a> as well as recent studies classifying <em>SREBF1</em> as a candidate gene both at an expression and genetic level for these same diseases <a href="#pone.0008183-Laudes1" rid="pone.0008183-Laudes1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030531">[50]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Felder1" rid="pone.0008183-Felder1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030547">[51]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Harding1" rid="pone.0008183-Harding1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030512">[52]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Grarup1" rid="pone.0008183-Grarup1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030499">[53]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Mingrone1" rid="pone.0008183-Mingrone1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030549">[54]</a>. Studies suggest that variations at <em>cis</em>-regulatory polymorphisms account for more of the population differences in prevalence of complex diseases versus <em>trans</em> effects <a href="#pone.0008183-Stranger1" rid="pone.0008183-Stranger1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030555">[23]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Spielman1" rid="pone.0008183-Spielman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030525">[24]</a>, <a href="#pone.0008183-Rockman1" rid="pone.0008183-Rockman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030502">[42]</a>. Likewise, future studies including analysis of <em>SREBF1</em> polymorphisms within our study populations and distant eQTL studies to identify other loci that contribute to the regulation of carbohydrate metabolic gene expression should be considered.</p><p id="__p42" class="p p-last">A study of the nutritional patterns and diabetes risk among American children demonstrated that, despite better overall compliance with the FDA recommended &#x0201c;Food Pyramid,&#x0201d; American children of African ancestry remained at higher risk for the development of diabetes and pre-diabetic conditions <a href="#pone.0008183-Lindquist1" rid="pone.0008183-Lindquist1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030544">[55]</a>. One interpretation of our findings is that differences in metabolic expression profiles between AA and CAU subjects may not be the sole result of differing nutritional and dietary practices between the study groups. Likewise, diabetics studied within the Seventh Day Adventist Church revealed less benefit for American patients of African versus European ancestry when both groups adhered to the religious dietary practices of the denomination <a href="#pone.0008183-Montgomery1" rid="pone.0008183-Montgomery1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_671030514">[56]</a>. More focused studies are needed to determine and identify the contribution of genetics to dietary responses, in particular subjects at high risk for multi-factorial diseases such as cardiovascular disease and diabetes. Our study identifies ancestral-dependent patterns of gene expression that may contribute to the differential adaptations of dietary changes and if better understood, could help therapeutically.</p></div><div id="s5" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s5title">Supporting Information</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="pone.0008183.s001"><h4>Figure S1</h4><p id="__p43">Illustrating the p-value distributions from different association tests.
