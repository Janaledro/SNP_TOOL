Reactive oxygen species (ROS), antioxidant defences and cellular redox status play a key function in gene expression regulation and are involved in diabetes and metabolic syndromes as in ageing.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>Methods</h2><p id="Par2" class="p p-first-last">We have, therefore, designed an in vitro cell model of oxidative stress by exposing hESCs to hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) during 72&#x000a0;h, in order to resemble the period of preimplantation embryonic development.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="Par3" class="p p-first-last">We have analysed the global gene expression profiles of hESCs (HUES3) exposed to non-cytotoxic H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations, using Illumina microarray HT-12 v4, and we found the differential expression of 569 upregulated and 485 downregulated genes. The most affected gene ontology categories were those related with RNA processing and splicing, oxidation reduction and sterol metabolic processes. We compared our findings with a published RNA-seq profiling dataset of human embryos developed in vitro, thereupon exposed to oxidative stress, and we observed that one of the common downregulated genes between this publication and our data, NEDD1, is involved in centrosome structure and function.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h2>Conclusions</h2><p id="Par4" class="p p-first-last">Therefore, we assessed the presence of supernumerary centrosomes and showed that the percentage of cells with more than two centrosomes increased acutely with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatment in hESCs (HUES3 and 7) and in a control somatic cell line (Hs27), inducing a premature entry into senescence.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Electronic supplementary material</h2><p id="__p1" class="p p-first-last">The online version of this article (doi:10.1186/s13287-017-0602-6) contains supplementary material, which is available to authorized users.</p></div></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">hESC, Oxidative stress, Microarray, Centrosome, NEDD1</span></div></div><div id="Sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec1title">Background</h2><p id="Par5" class="p p-first">Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are constantly produced by cells and in a normal physiological environment they are efficiently regulated by intracellular antioxidant systems. However, when ROS production exceeds cellular defences, these unstable compounds can damage proteins, nucleic acids and lipids [<a href="#CR1" rid="CR1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861638">1</a>]. This situation is known as oxidative stress, and it has been related to the ageing process and to multiple diseases like diabetes mellitus type 2, cancer, metabolic syndromes and neurodegenerative complications [<a href="#CR2" rid="CR2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861639">2</a>, <a href="#CR3" rid="CR3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861614">3</a>]. The study of the mechanisms implicated in oxidative stress in different cell models could greatly help to design preventive antioxidant treatments for these diseases [<a href="#CR4" rid="CR4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861598">4</a>].</p><p id="Par6">Cell culture has been used for many decades to decode the mechanisms involved in oxidative stress and to analyse the protective effect of antioxidants, providing a vast amount of information. However, very different data can be found in the literature in respect to pro-oxidants doses and effects [<a href="#CR5" rid="CR5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861609">5</a>]. The effects can also differ enormously according to the cell line; thus, it would be very interesting to compare different cell types in the same study. Furthermore, most of our knowledge in this field is derived from differentiated cells while limited information is available on human embryonic stem cells (hESCs). Some studies have suggested that somatic and ESCs respond in different ways to oxidative stress [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861602">6</a>, <a href="#CR7" rid="CR7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861575">7</a>] although the mechanisms underlying this different response remain unknown. The interest in the response of hESCs to oxidative conditions lies in their peculiar nature since they reflect the same features of the early pluripotent cells of human embryos, showing a similar mitochondrial morphology and mass, and meeting their energy requirements predominantly via anaerobic glycolysis [<a href="#CR8" rid="CR8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861616">8</a>, <a href="#CR9" rid="CR9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861592">9</a>]. The implications of an exposure to common situations in assisted reproduction procedures (ART) or in pregnant diabetic mothers are still largely unknown in human and remain to be elucidated. Thus, since for ethical reasons the availability of human oocytes and embryos for research is limited, hESCs are suitable in the studies of human development-linked genetic disorders. Therefore hESCs provide the in vitro model to study how early human embryonic cells react to an adverse environment during the critical preimplantation window when environmental alterations like oxidative stress can lead to future disorders, according to the Developmental Origins of Health and Disease theory (DOHaD) [<a href="#CR10" rid="CR10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861627">10</a>].</p><p id="Par7">When cells affront physiologic stresses, they tend to adapt [<a href="#CR11" rid="CR11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861617">11</a>], but if this adaptive capability is outstripped or if the external stress is too adverse, cell damage initiates. Acute or persistent stress causes an ample spectrum of metabolic, genetic and cellular responses. The most extreme outcome is necrosis that implicates irreversible injury and cell death [<a href="#CR12" rid="CR12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861612">12</a>, <a href="#CR13" rid="CR13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861608">13</a>]. In order to induce oxidative stress in a cell culture in vitro model, one of the pro-oxidant agents widely used is hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), which at physiological concentrations is involved in signalling mechanisms and cell metabolism. However, it also can produce ROS, which can easily penetrate cell membranes, directly damaging lipids, nucleic acids and other macromolecules. Moreover, it can react with partially reduced transition metals such as Fe<sup>2+</sup> or Cu<sup>2+</sup>, generating the highly reactive hydroxyl radical (OH<sup>-</sup>) that increases the oxidative damage in the cell [<a href="#CR14" rid="CR14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861558">14</a>, <a href="#CR15" rid="CR15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861564">15</a>]. H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> intracellular concentration is controlled by different enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidant mechanisms and it is assumed to range between 1 and 700 nM [<a href="#CR16" rid="CR16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861583">16</a>] depending on the cellular type and the specific system; so intracellular steady-state concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> above 1&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M are considered to induce oxidative stress, triggering arrest of development and cell death [<a href="#CR17" rid="CR17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861642">17</a>, <a href="#CR18" rid="CR18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861628">18</a>]. In a preliminary study, we identified the non-cytotoxic window of exposure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> of hESCs (HUES3 and HUES7) and somatic cells (HS27 and HUVEC), that is was recorded in between 4 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> for Hs27 and HUES cells, and up to 32&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M for HUVEC cells. We previously demonstrated that in this range there are no modifications of cell number and morphology, but significantly increased levels of ROS were observed [<a href="#CR19" rid="CR19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_624234967">19</a>].</p><p id="Par8">Cells in normal physiological conditions possess precise checkpoint mechanisms in order to prevent replication with damaged DNA or other cellular defects. These checkpoints can arrest the cell cycle, until abnormal cells are either corrected or removed [<a href="#CR20" rid="CR20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861559">20</a>]. However, under more severe environmental conditions, and in response to specific stresses, cells undergo senescence, an irreversible growth arrest state between the G1 and S phase of the cell cycle, and can persist as such for a long time metabolically active [<a href="#CR21" rid="CR21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861630">21</a>]. A critical cell-cycle progression organelle is the centrosome that is the primary microtubule-organizing centre (MTOC) of the cell, and it has been described that cell-cycle arrest and senescence can be originated by defects in this organelle [<a href="#CR22" rid="CR22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861640">22</a>]. A typical centrosome is made up by a pair of perpendicularly oriented cylindrical structures called centrioles, surrounded by the pericentriolar material. Defects in the structure of the centrosome can predispose to arrest of the cell cycle and senescence [<a href="#CR22" rid="CR22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861562">22</a>]. In most eucariotic cells, the main function of the centrosomes is contributing to the mitotic spindle by attaching and nucleating microtubules and by building its bipolar organization [<a href="#CR23" rid="CR23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861637">23</a>]. Several studies have shown that the abnormalities in the chromosomes might be caused by an abnormal number of centrosomes [<a href="#CR24" rid="CR24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861603">24</a>].</p><p id="Par9">Human embryonic stem cells are particularly sensitive to chromosome instability [<a href="#CR25" rid="CR25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861599">25</a>, <a href="#CR26" rid="CR26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861635">26</a>] and a considerable percentage of cultured hESCs after long-term passages exhibit supernumerary centrosomes during mitosis [<a href="#CR27" rid="CR27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861606">27</a>]. Chromosome abnormalities have been largely studied in human embryos [<a href="#CR28" rid="CR28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861595">28</a>], but the status of their centrosome number, structure and the percentage of centrosome abnormalities are still largely unknown. To determine if aberrant centrosomes contribute to senescence in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated cells, we analysed &#x003b3;-tubulin, an essential protein for correct centrosomal structure and function, and which is an important target of the protein encoded by neural precursor cell-expressed developmentally downregulated gene 1 (NEDD1) [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861632">29</a>, <a href="#CR30" rid="CR30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861622">30</a>]. Centrosome disruption can be originated by oxidative stress [<a href="#CR31" rid="CR31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861605">31</a>]; however, the mechanisms linking modifications of this organelle to senescence are still poorly understood.</p><p id="Par10" class="p p-last">Microarray technology has been widely used to generate global transcriptome profiles, to unravel the process of early development from the oocyte to the preimplantation stage in various animal models and also in human embryos [<a href="#CR32" rid="CR32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861613">32</a>, <a href="#CR33" rid="CR33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861573">33</a>]. The aim of this study was therefore to analyse using microarray the response of human ESCs against H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> oxidant agents in an oxidative stress model through the investigation of the global gene expression profile at three key non-cytotoxic H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations (4, 8 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M). We demonstrate that somatic and ESCs show different responses compared to endothelial cells, and we provide further data to show how the oxidative environment can affect the early embryonic cells. On one hand, our data suggest that heterogeneity across different hESC lines is reflected in global transcriptional profiles in agreement with human embryonic development [<a href="#CR34" rid="CR34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861591">34</a>]. On the other hand, we demonstrate the modification of crucial genes involved in pathways related with oxidation reduction, glucose metabolism, epigenetics and DNA repair. One of these altered genes in common with human embryos developed in vitro, NEDD1, led us to unravel the role of centrosome modifications in human somatic cells, fibroblasts and endothelial cells, and in ESCs exposed to an in vitro oxidative stress treatment induced by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exposure.</p></div><div id="Sec2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec2title">Methods</h2><div id="Sec3" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Cell culture</h3><p id="Par11" class="p p-first">Human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) (HUES3 and HUES7 cell lines, obtained from Harvard Stem Cells Institute) [<a href="#CR26" rid="CR26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861618">26</a>] were first cultured on a feeder layer of mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) inactivated by mitomycin C (Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy) in KO-DMEM medium (Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy) supplemented with 10% serum replacement (Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy), 4.3&#x000a0;mg/ml bovine serum albumin (BSA) (Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy), 2&#x000a0;mM glutamine (L-alanyl-L-glutamine, Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy), 1% non-essential amino acids (Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy), 0.055&#x000a0;mM beta-mercaptoethanol (Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy), 63&#x000a0;mg/ml penicillin, 70&#x000a0;mg/ml streptomycin, and 10&#x000a0;ng/ml bFGF (PeproTech, Milan, Italy). To perform the experiments, hESCs were adapted to grow in feeder-free conditions in mTeSR&#x02122;1 medium (Stemcell Technologies, obtained from Voden Medical Instruments, Milan, Italy). The medium was changed every day and cells were passaged 1:4 with PBS/EDTA every 3 or 4&#x000a0;days.</p><p id="Par12">Human fibroblasts (Hs27 cell line, obtained from Biobanking of Veterinary Resources, IZSLER, Brescia, Italy) were cultured in Dulbecco&#x02019;s modified Eagle&#x02019;s medium (DMEM, high-glucose, GlutaMAX&#x02122; supplement, Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy), supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVEC line, obtained from Biobanking of Veterinary Resources, IZSLER, Brescia, Italy) were cultured in Medium 200 that is supplemented with 2% Low Serum Growth Supplement (Gibco Invitrogen, Milan, Italy). Cells were passaged 1:4 by 0.05% trypsin/EDTA incubation at 37&#x000a0;&#x000b0;C for 5&#x000a0;min every 3 or 4&#x000a0;days.</p><p id="Par13" class="p p-last">For microarray analysis the exposure to 4, 8 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> started 24&#x000a0;hours (h) after plating and medium was changed daily during the following 72&#x000a0;h, ending at day 4 after plating&#x000a0;(Additional file <a href="#MOESM1" rid="MOESM1" class=" media">1</a>: Figure S1). For centrosome fragmentation analysis the exposure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> started 24&#x000a0;h after plating and medium was changed once, treating the cells during 2&#x000a0;h and 24&#x000a0;h. For gene expression analysis Hs27 and HUVEC cells were grown in 60&#x000a0;mm dishes and HUES cells in 24-well plates. For immunofluorescence detection cells were seeded on 6&#x000a0;mm diameter glass cover slides at different concentrations depending on treatment timing: 2 and 24&#x000a0;h treatment 80,000 cells/ml; and to reach the optimal cell confluence after 72&#x000a0;h treatment, cells were plated at different concentrations: somatic cells (Hs27 and HUVEC) were plated at 60,000 cells/ml and hESCs (HUES3 and HUES7) at 40,000 cells/ml. At the end of the treatment, cell samples were frozen for RT-PCR, and then stored in RNA protect Cell Reagent (Qiagen, Milan, Italy) for the microarray analysis or fixed in PAF (4% in PBS) for immunocytochemistry analysis.</p></div><div id="Sec4" class="sec"><h3>RNA isolation, cDNA synthesis and qPCR</h3><p id="Par14" class="p p-first">The RNA from cells treated with the different H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentrations was extracted, from three biological replicates, using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Milan, Italy) following the manufacturer&#x02019;s instructions. Each replicate was processed independently. After extraction, the reverse transcription reaction was performed with iScript&#x02122; cDNA Synthesis Kit (Bio-Rad, Milan, Italy) following the manufacturer's instructions. Tubes were first incubated at 25&#x000a0;&#x000b0;C for 5&#x000a0;min and then at 42&#x000a0;&#x000b0;C for 30&#x000a0;min to allow the reverse transcription of mRNA, followed by 85&#x000a0;&#x000b0;C for 5&#x000a0;min to denature the enzyme. After the retrotranscription of mRNA transcripts, the three control cDNA replicates and three replicates of each treatment condition (4, 8 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) were sent to conduct microarray experiments by Cambridge Genomic Services using Illumina HumanHT-12 v4 expression BeadChIP.</p><p id="Par15" class="p p-last">Validation of microarray data was performed by real-time qPCR. For each biological experiment, three PCR replicates were conducted for the genes of interest. We compare the relative levels of each transcript with those of the housekeeping gene in each sample. PCR was carried out with the PCR mix iTaq&#x02122; Universal SYBR Green Supermix (Bio-Rad, Milan, Italy) containing the specific primers (Additional file <a href="#MOESM2" rid="MOESM2" class=" media">2</a>: Table S1) in a MyiQ Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, Milan, Italy). Data was analysed with the iQ Optical System Software (Bio-Rad) with the ddCt method. 18S was used as housekeeping reference gene, and all the genes were normalized to the non-treated control.</p></div><div id="Sec5" class="sec"><h3>Database submission of microarray data</h3><p id="Par16" class="p p-first-last">The microarray data were deposited in the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database: <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=5497375&amp;issue-id=283375&amp;journal-id=1238&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>. The GEO accession number for the platform is <a id="__tag_623861646" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE90999" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE90999"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE90999","term_id":"90999"}}</span>GSE90999</a> and the NCBI tracking system number is 18185372.</p></div><div id="Sec6" class="sec"><h3>Microarray gene expression studies</h3><p id="Par17" class="p p-first-last">Preprocessing of Illumina microarray data and quality control (QC) were performed using R/Bioconductor packages BeadArray (Dunning et al., 2007) and lumi (Du et al., 2008). QC steps undertaken (i) exclusion of unexpressed genes/probes if their detection <em>p</em>&#x02009;&#x02264;&#x02009;0.05 and (ii) quantile normalization of data, followed by log2 and variance-stabilizing transformation (VST) implemented in the lumi Bioconductor package. In the circumstance of genes represented by multiple distinct oligonucleotide probes, the probe with the highest average expression across all samples was selected for use in further analyses. Normalized data were exported directly from the lumi Bioconductor package.</p></div><div id="Sec7" class="sec"><h3>Identification of differentially expressed genes</h3><p id="Par18" class="p p-first-last">Differential gene expression between sample groups was identified with multiple hypothesis testing via the R/Bioconductor package limma (Smyth, 2004) using methods &#x0201c;lmFit&#x0201d;, &#x0201c;makeContrasts&#x0201d; and &#x0201c;eBayes&#x0201d; with default parameters. The empirical Bayes approach employed shrinks sample variance towards a pooled estimate, leading to more robust estimation of differential expression. Differentially expressed (DE) genes are denoted significant if their <em>p</em>&#x02009;&#x02264;&#x02009;0.05.</p></div><div id="Sec8" class="sec"><h3>Identification of pivot genes associated with human preimplantation embryonic development</h3><p id="Par19" class="p p-first">Transcriptomic data of human early embryonic development were derived from a published study of single-cell RNA-seq profiling of human preimplantation embryos [<a href="#CR34" rid="CR34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861561">34</a>], which include seven developmental stages of preimplanted embryos (oocyte, zygote, 2-cell, 4-cell, 8-cell, morula and blastocyst). The processed gene counts files were downloaded from the GEO database (<a id="__tag_623861645" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE36552" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE36552"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE36552","term_id":"36552"}}</span>GSE36552</a>). Gene expression levels were evaluated by calculating TPM [<a href="#CR35" rid="CR35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861576">35</a>], and the genes with TPMs higher than one in more than 50% replicates in at least one stage were kept as the expressed ones in human early embryonic development. Human protein-protein interaction (PPI) data were downloaded from BioGrid database (version 3.1.141) [<a href="#CR36" rid="CR36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861579">36</a>] including both curated physical and genetic interactions. Redundant interactions and self-interactions were deleted.</p><p id="Par20" class="p p-last">The co-expression level for each interacted gene pair in the PPI network was evaluated by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC). The protein-protein interactions with significant PCCs (BH [<a href="#CR37" rid="CR37" class=" bibr popnode">37</a>] corrected <em>p&#x02009;&#x02264;</em>&#x02009;0.01) are kept for the construction of the co-expression network. Then, the co-expression network was clustered by using the Markov cluster algorithm (MCL) [<a href="#CR38" rid="CR38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861568">38</a>] with default parameters, and the clusters with at least ten genes, which are significantly enriched with GO biological processes evaluated by using a hypergeometric test, are selected as co-expression functional gene modules. The inter-modular hubs are defined as the genes locating between the functional modules, which interacted with at least ten genes in the co-expression network. The pivot genes were selected from the inter-modular hubs, which have significantly more interactions with a functional module evaluated by a hypergeometric test (<em>p&#x02009;&#x02264;</em>&#x02009;0.05). The differential expression of the pivot genes between the 4-cell and 8-cell stages were evaluated by using <em>t</em> test with the BH corrected <em>p</em> values lower than 0.05 and the fold changes higher than 1.4 as suggested by MAQC consortium [<a href="#CR39" rid="CR39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861631">39</a>].</p></div><div id="Sec9" class="sec"><h3>Immunofluorescence</h3><p id="Par21" class="p p-first-last">In order to localize &#x003b3;-tubulin, a highly conserved protein within the microtubule-organizing centres, cells grown in glass cover slides were washed once with PBS and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (VWR, Milan, Italy) for 30&#x000a0;min at room temperature (RT). Then, they were permeabilized by incubation in 0.5% Triton (Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy) in PBS for 15&#x000a0;min at RT and blocked in 10% normal goat serum (Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy) in PBS for 1&#x000a0;hour at RT. After that, cells were incubated for 2&#x000a0;h at RT in 1:1500 mouse monoclonal anti-&#x003b3;-tubulin (Clone GTU-88, Sigma-Aldrich, Milan, Italy). Following incubation, cells were washed three times and incubated in PBS containing 1:200 Texas Red anti-mouse antibodies (Jackson ImmuneResearch, Milan, Italy) for 1&#x000a0;h in the dark at RT. We demonstrated that the non-lethal doses of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> resulted in an increase in oxidative stress in treated cells. To evaluate the nominal concentration-effect relationship for the cytotoxic action of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, human somatic cells (Hs27 and HUVEC) and embryonic stem (HUES3 and HUES7) cell lines were exposed to increasing concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> between 4 and 768&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M during 72&#x000a0;h and by AlamarBlue&#x000ae; reduction we analysed cell viability and normalized to the untreated control samples of each cell line.</p><p id="Par24">Gene expression profiles from in vitro untreated control and 4, 8 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-treated HUES3 cells were obtained using Illumina HT-12 v4 microarrays, each array included approximately 48&#x000a0;k transcripts. No global differences were observed in the transcriptome profile between samples of different H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> conditions (Additional file <a href="#MOESM3" rid="MOESM3" class=" media">3</a>: Figure S2), suggesting that the global gene expression of human ES cells is stable upon hydrogen peroxide treatment.</p><p id="Par25" class="p p-last">We next sought to determine whether any of genes were observed as differentially expressed across all H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatments. CNBP and NEDD1 gene expression analysis in treated hESC and somatic cells</h3><p id="Par29" class="p p-first">It is well known that during in vitro culture the oocytes and afterwards the early cleaving embryos are exposed to oxidative stress that accumulated over time [<a href="#CR40" rid="CR40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861572">40</a>]. To further validate and characterise the effect of oxidative stress on embryonic cells, we conducted a comparative analysis of our microarray data with the single-cell RNA-seq profiling data obtained from in vitro cultured human preimplantation embryos [<a href="#CR34" rid="CR34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861626">34</a>], which also are exposed to some extent to oxidative stress conditions. These data included seven early stages of human preimplantation embryonic development: oocyte, zygote, 2-cell, 4-cell, 8-cell, morula and blastocyst. We found two top genes overlapping between comparisons made in the two datasets: CNBP (CCHC-type zinc finger, nucleic acid binding protein), also known as ZNF9, and NEDD1 (neural precursor, developmentally downregulated 1).</p><p id="Par30">In the human IVF embryo transcriptome data, we saw that both genes were highly expressed in oocyte, prior to downregulation at 4-cell stage. CNBP gene showed an increased expression at 8-cell and morula stages compared to 4-cell stage, whereas NEDD1 gene was downregulated at 8-cell and blastocyst stage compared to 4-cell stage. Both genes have been identified as pivot genes, taking into account both the expression data and the curated physical and genetic interaction network downloaded from BioGrid. CNBP has significantly more interactions with a functional gene module, which is significantly involved in the biological processes such as translational termination, SRP-dependent co-translational protein targeting to membrane, translational elongation, nuclear-transcribed mRNA catabolic process, nonsense-mediated decay, translational initiation and viral transcription. And NEDD1 is a pivot gene which crosstalks with two functional gene modules: one is involved in the biological processes of toxin transport, binding of sperm to zona pellucida and &#x02018;de novo&#x02019; posttranslational protein folding; while the other is related with cilium assembly, G2/M transition of mitotic cell cycle, centriole replication, spindle assembly, microtubule bundle formation and determination of left/right symmetry.</p><p id="Par31" class="p p-last">Viewing from the gene-to-gene co-expression functional network, CNBP interacts with RPL23A, RPS16 and RPS19, which means that CNBP shows significant co-expression with the ribosomal proteins (Fig.&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC5497375/figure/Fig3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Fig3" rid-ob="ob-Fig3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_Fig3"><span>3a</span></a>). We checked the BioGrid dataset, which we used to construct the co-expression network and we found that all the interactions between CNBP gene and the ribosomal proteins are physical interactions. It suggests that the proteins encoded by CNBP bind to the ribosomal proteins. NEDD1 crosstalks with the functional gene module of &#x0201c;toxin transport, binding of sperm to zona pellucida and de novo posttranslational protein folding&#x0201d; through the anti-correlated co-expressions with several members of the chaperonin-containing TCP1 complex such as CCT2, CCT3, CCT4 and so on, while it also interacts with the functional module of &#x0201c;cilium assembly, G2/M transition of mitotic cell cycle, centriole replication, spindle assembly, microtubule bundle formation and determination of left/right symmetry&#x0201d; through the correlated co-expressions with the genes CEP152, CEP192, C14orf145, KIAA1009, KIAA0562, NME7, ODF2, CCDC77, OFD1, CP110 and PLK4 (Fig.&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC5497375/figure/Fig3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Fig3" rid-ob="ob-Fig3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_Fig3"><span>3b</span></a>), which suggests that NEDD1 plays roles in centrosome-related pathways such as centriole replication, spindle assembly and microtubule bundle formation through the interactions with the proteins CEP152, CEP152, OFD1, CP110 and PLK4. NEDD1 showed downregulation in embryonic stem cell lines HUES3 and HUES7 at 8 and 16&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M compared with control (Fig.&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC5497375/figure/Fig4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Fig4" rid-ob="ob-Fig4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_Fig4"><span>4c</span></a>) and in somatic cell line Hs27 at 8, 16 and 32&#x000a0;&#x003bc;M (Fig.&#x000a0;<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC5497375/figure/Fig4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="Fig4" rid-ob="ob-Fig4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_Fig4"><span>4d</span></a>). In this work we propose a 72-hour oxidative stress in vitro model and we provide an analysis of changes in global gene expression of hESCs exposed to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> within the non-cytotoxic range. Furthermore, we present a comparative study between hES and somatic cells under oxidative stress conditions and we describe the presence of centrosome abnormalities as senescence markers.</p><p id="Par37">The way in which ESCs respond to oxidative stress is still largely unknown. Although other authors have reported that ESCs have a remarkable recovery capacity against oxidative stress and are more resistant than their differentiated fibroblastic progenies [<a href="#CR7" rid="CR7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861581">7</a>, <a href="#CR41" rid="CR41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861586">41</a>], we have observed the toxic effect of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> on cell viability at similar concentrations in both Hs27 and hESCs. This can be explained by the ESC sensitivity when the exposure to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> occurs continuously [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861566">6</a>]. Besides that, it has been suggested that the defensive mechanisms could be more effective in cells that have been more exposed to acute variations in the environment during evolution and specially in cells that carry a huge sensitivity to particular stresses and engage more in maintaining homeostasis [<a href="#CR42" rid="CR42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861634">42</a>]. In our study, HUVEC showed higher resistance to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exposure and this finding could be linked to the fact that endothelial cells in vivo are constantly exposed to shear stress, thereby producing an impact on cellular metabolism, structure and function [<a href="#CR43" rid="CR43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861597">43</a>], ultimately making them stronger against damage caused by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and potentially more active in its elimination.</p><p id="Par38" class="p">It has been described that oxidative stress induces an altered redox status in the cell, modifying DNA by transcriptional mediators such as high ROS levels [<a href="#CR15" rid="CR15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861580">15</a>]. Given the oxidative stress status, the challenge for the cell is to develop mechanisms to counteract the negative oxidative effects in order to survive. The variability in gene expression that we have seen between different HUES3 replicates agrees with their heterogeneity in development, which is an important feature of early human embryos [<a href="#CR44" rid="CR44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861567">44</a>] and also of individual ES cells. On the other hand the focus on the non-cytotoxic range of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> of exposure that was chosen in this study implies that only subtle but critical changes, specifically due to oxidative stress, were the target of our investigation. In any case, with our oxidative stress model we have identified an important number of molecular pathways modified and reported differences in gene expression between control and non-cytotoxic doses of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. More specifically, we have identified a total of 1054 dysregulated genes, being 569 upregulated and 485 downregulated in comparison to control cells. The substantial differences in transcript number in each category of the Venn diagrams illustrates the magnitude of the variation between treatments conditions that, however, do not induce gross morphological changes in the exposed cells.</p><div id="Sec19" class="sec"><h3>Redox processes</h3><p id="Par39" class="p p-first">The importance of diverse components involved in the correct oxidation-reduction processes in the pluripotent embryo stages has been described in previous studies [<a href="#CR33" rid="CR33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861619">33</a>, <a href="#CR45" rid="CR45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861571">45</a>]. The activity of many eukaryotic crucial cellular nucleophiles and their capacity to interact with critical enzymes is modulated by redox changes as previously reported [<a href="#CR46" rid="CR46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861557">46</a>]. In our study, NADH dehydrogenase (ubiquinone) 1 alpha subcomplex 3 (NDUFA3), peroxiredoxins 2 and 5 (PRDXs 2 and 5) and cytochrome C oxidase assembly protein (SCO2) were upregulated in H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treated cells, a finding that agrees with several of the reported studies demonstrating changes of gene expression in response to oxidant agents [<a href="#CR47" rid="CR47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861604">47</a>, <a href="#CR48" rid="CR48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_624234966">48</a>].</p><p id="Par40">Furthermore, oxidative stress is also associated with several age-related diseases and the physiological process of ageing [<a href="#CR2" rid="CR2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861601">2</a>]. NDUFA3 together with SCO2 are essential enzymes in the electron transport chain that are responsible for maintaining the proton gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane, process that is essential for the aerobic production of ATP. Their upregulation could contribute to telomere shortening and therefore pathological ageing under oxidative stress conditions [<a href="#CR18" rid="CR18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861629">18</a>, <a href="#CR49" rid="CR49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861590">49</a>], modifications that could have long-term deleterious effects especially in the pluripotent cells of the early embryo [<a href="#CR50" rid="CR50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861644">50</a>].</p><p id="Par41" class="p p-last">Considering peroxiredoxin enzymes, their increased expression levels in embryonic cells support the DOHaD concept and the existence of an interesting association between expression perturbation of PRDX isoform 2 and long-term metabolic diseases and cardiovascular risks [<a href="#CR51" rid="CR51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861624">51</a>] and can suggest a novel therapeutic strategy for treating metabolic disorders [<a href="#CR52" rid="CR52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861625">52</a>].</p></div><div id="Sec20" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic pathways</h3><p id="Par42" class="p p-first">Pyruvate, produced through the metabolism of glucose, and glucose itself constitute the main sources of energy for oocytes and preimplantation embryos [<a href="#CR53" rid="CR53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861565">53</a>]. The sub-optimum glucose culture conditions for oocytes, embryos and stem cells have been suggested to promote failure of resumption and completion of meiosis, decreased cytoplasmic maturation and diminished developmental potential [<a href="#CR54" rid="CR54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861588">54</a>]. In the quite complex glucose metabolism, many genes and enzymes contribute to the serial reactions necessary for its glycolytic breakdown [<a href="#CR54" rid="CR54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861570">54</a>]. In our study we found several upregulated genes related with sugars and sterol metabolism such as aldo-keto reductase family 1 member A1 (AKR1A1) and transmembrane 7 superfamily member 2 (TM7SF2). Previous studies in AKR1 family reductases revealed its involvement in diabetes complications in human and porcine models [<a href="#CR55" rid="CR55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861610">55</a>] and moreover its upregulated expression has been observed in rat tissues treated with submicromolar concentrations of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, suggesting that this enzyme family acts as a defence system against oxidative stress [<a href="#CR56" rid="CR56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861633">56</a>]. Meanwhile the upregulation of TM7SF2 has been implicated in oncogenesis and cancer progression [<a href="#CR57" rid="CR57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861596">57</a>], but further studies are needed to understand this phenomenon and its role during oxidative stress challenge.</p><p id="Par43">Other genes such as aldehyde dehydrogenase 1 family, member B1 (ALDHB1B1), dolichyl-phosphate &#x003b2;-glucosyltransferase (ALG5) and sorbitol dehydrogenase (SORD) were found to be downregulated after H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exposure. The aldehyde dehydrogenases family is responsible for metabolizing endogenous and exogenous aldehydes and thereby mitigating oxidative/electrophilic stress in the organism. Many forms of ALDHs have appeared during evolution in single and multicellular organisms, and mostly have analogous functions, providing protection against environmental stresses [<a href="#CR58" rid="CR58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861585">58</a>]. Interestingly, for the validation of our oxidative stress model, the alterations of ALDHs are associated with several human pathological conditions, mainly related with oxidative stress and lipid peroxidation [<a href="#CR59" rid="CR59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861623">59</a>]. In mammals the downregulation of ALDH family members has been described under ethanol stress, leading to the downregulation of mitochondrial glutathione levels [<a href="#CR60" rid="CR60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861611">60</a>]; although the underlying mechanisms is yet to be fully elucidated, such downregulation would induce ROS accumulation and, afterwards, increased oxidative stress [<a href="#CR61" rid="CR61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861593">61</a>].</p><p id="Par44" class="p p-last">It is well known that hyperglycaemia increases the rates of glucose metabolism via glycolysis and the SORD gene pathway [<a href="#CR62" rid="CR62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861556">62</a>, <a href="#CR63" rid="CR63" class=" bibr popnode">63</a>], but the importance of SORD downregulation and its contribution to oxidative stress and diabetic complications in human in vivo and vitro models remains unclear.</p></div><div id="Sec21" class="sec"><h3>Other important pathways affected by oxidative stress</h3><p id="Par45" class="p p-first">TNFSF9 gene encodes for a cytokine belonging to the tumour necrosis factor (TNF) ligand family, which are one of the stronger physiological inducers of the nuclear transcription factor NF-kappa B [<a href="#CR64" rid="CR64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861641">64</a>]. In our HUES3-treated cells we have seen an increase of TNFSF9 gene expression that other authors have demonstrated previously in the microvascular cells of glycemic rats compared to the retinas obtained from normal animals. This mechanism has been related to the regulation of the apoptosis machinery, contributing to delay the progression of diabetic retinopathy [<a href="#CR65" rid="CR65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861578">65</a>].</p><p id="Par46">Related to the mismatch repair (MMR) system, we have shown in treated cells the downregulation of the PMS1 gene, involved in the single-base mismatches correction and small insertion/deletion loops that arise during DNA replication. This finding agrees with the hypothesis that oxidative stress together with chronic inflammation might damage some components of the MMR protein system, causing its functional inactivation [<a href="#CR66" rid="CR66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861584">66</a>]. This suggests that non-cytotoxic levels of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can block and inactivate the MMR system in a dose-dependent way.</p><p id="Par47">Related to the upregulation of the Jumonji domain-containing 6 (JMJD) gene expression shown in this study, recent literature provides evidences of its implication as an oxygen sensor in the human placenta, functioning as a mediator of the hypoxic gene expression [<a href="#CR67" rid="CR67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861589">67</a>]. However, its upregulation in embryonic cells under oxidative stress conditions remains to be elucidated.</p><p id="Par48" class="p p-last">Finally, accordingly to previous results [<a href="#CR6" rid="CR6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861582">6</a>], oxidative stress did not alter the expression of pluripotency genes, demonstrating that the oxidative treatment, in the not cytotoxic range, does not affect embryonic stem cells pluripotency and most likely, although not tested in this study, the ability to differentiate into cells and tissues of the three primary germ layers.</p></div><div id="Sec22" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Correlation between a published single-cell RNA-seq profiling dataset of IVF-derived human embryos and our microarray data of hESC exposed to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></h3><p id="Par49" class="p p-first">It is well known that during in vitro culture the oocytes and afterwards the early cleaving embryos are exposed to oxidative stress that accumulated over time. Here we propose a novel comparative analysis integrating an already published global gene expression by single-cell RNA-seq profiling of individual human oocytes and embryos derived from assisted reproduction procedures (ART) [<a href="#CR34" rid="CR34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861560">34</a>] and the global gene expression profiling of hESCs exposed to non-cytotoxic oxidative stress conditions obtained in this study. The top two common and differentially expressed genes were CNBP (CCHC-type zinc finger, nucleic acid binding protein) or also called ZNF9, and NEDD1 (neural precursor, developmentally downregulated 1).</p><p id="Par50">CNBP is implicated in the regulation of numerous genes, such as the c-myc proto-oncogene promoter [<a href="#CR68" rid="CR68" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861594">68</a>] although in another study it has been the significant reduction of CNBP transcripts occurred after the repression of c-myc, which indicated that other mechanisms, besides lowering CNBP transcript numbers, contribute to trigger the downregulation of c-myc. In any case, the fast decrease of c-myc levels is considered a crucial step causing apoptosis in cancer [<a href="#CR69" rid="CR69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861587">69</a>], Alzheimer disease [<a href="#CR70" rid="CR70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861615">70</a>] and cellular stress [<a href="#CR71" rid="CR71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861636">71</a>]. Our results demonstrate that the H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> oxidative stress treatment induces a downregulation in CNBP gene expression in hESC and Hs27 fibroblasts. Interestingly, HUVEC was the only cell line in which we have not seen this lower gene expression in treated cells. The absence of effects on CNBP gene expression could be explained by the more gradual slope of the viability curve of HUVEC in comparison with the other somatic and embryonic cells lines [<a href="#CR72" rid="CR72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861574">72</a>].</p><p id="Par51">NEDD1 protein is located in the centrosome and binds the &#x003b3;-tubulin ring complex with the mitotic spindle, which is responsible of the nucleation of microtubules [<a href="#CR73" rid="CR73" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861620">73</a>]. Given that the reduction of NEDD1 expression is responsible for centrosomal abnormalities [<a href="#CR31" rid="CR31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861563">31</a>] in our study the integrity of the centrosome was evaluated at different timing of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatments in both embryonic and somatic lines at non-cytotoxic and cytotoxic concentrations. Our results demonstrate that the non-cytotoxic treatment induces a downregulation of NEDD1 gene expression in hESC and Hs27 fibroblasts and consequently an increased number of cells with supernumerary centrosomes at 2&#x000a0;h, 24&#x000a0;h and 72&#x000a0;h treatments, although this increase was only significant at 24&#x000a0;h for a concentration range between the non-cytotoxic and the cell injury starting point (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> dose-response curves viability for HUES3, HUES7, Hs27 and HUVEC treated during 24&#x000a0;h shown in Additional file <a href="#MOESM2" rid="MOESM2" class=" media">2</a>: Figure S1). As NEDD1 is important in the processes of centriole assembly and duplication [<a href="#CR29" rid="CR29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861577">29</a>], it would be logical to expect that its downregulation may result in a decreased number of centrosomes, instead of more centrosomes spots as we detected in our study. Moreover higher centrosome number is frequently linked to tumorigenesis [<a href="#CR74" rid="CR74" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861607">74</a>], therefore it was not an expected finding in senescence cells. To justify our observations, we hypothesize that the abnormal centrosomes observed in cells cultured under H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> conditions may derive from fragmentation or incorrect assembly, rather than overamplification. In fact, one of the characteristics of senescent cells in culture displaying an abnormally high proportion of centrosomal structures is that these dots are often smaller in treated cells than in non-treated ones, an observation that supports the hypothesis of fragmented centrosomes. Interestingly, the loss of NEDD1 function has been indicated as a possible cause of centrosome fragmentation in senescent mouse embryonic fibroblasts [<a href="#CR31" rid="CR31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861643">31</a>] and in HeLa cells [<a href="#CR75" rid="CR75" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861621">75</a>]. In our study we found a relatively low percentage of cells with supernumerary centrosomes: 6% for hESC and 12% for Hs27 cells, this is likely an underestimation because abnormal centrosomes can produce aberrant mitosis with non-viable daughter cells, and consequently these abnormal cells may have degenerated before the analysis. Our results agree with a work done in human Chang liver cells where the authors have demonstrated that 20% of cells exposed to low doses of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> accumulated an increased number of centrosomes, compared with 5&#x02013;7% of controls [<a href="#CR76" rid="CR76" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861600">76</a>].</p><p id="Par52" class="p p-last">Curiously, in agreement with the gene expression results previously described for CNBP, HUVEC was the only cell line in which we have not detected either a decrease of NEDD1 expression, or an increase of centrosome fragmentation at any H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatment time. Again, this no effect on endothelial cells could be explained by the different gradual slope of the viability curve in comparison with the other somatic and embryonic cells lines [<a href="#CR72" rid="CR72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_623861569">72</a>]. This confirms that endothelial cells have special, or at least different, damage removal systems in order to avoid oxidative damage of cell membrane and signal transduction pathways finally being more resistant to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exposure.</p></div></div><div id="Sec23" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="Sec23title">Conclusions</h2><p id="Par53" class="p p-first-last">Our data provides relevant knowledge on the gene expression profiles of hESC exposed to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> non-cytotoxic concentrations during 72&#x000a0;h. Moreover, the observation that embryonic cells accumulate supernumerary centrosomes under oxidative stress conditions in a different way than somatic cells, contributes to the argument that ES cells are a valuable and unique model to study the consequences of an oxidative environment on the early embryo. Therefore this study offers new opportunities to investigate how an oxidative stress environment can affect the pluripotent cells of the early embryo in vitro; the findings reported can also be a basis to facilitate the identification of antioxidant treatments in order to improve embryo culture conditions.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec6title">Additional files</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div id="Sec24" class="sec sec-first-last"><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">
