We have tested the hypothesis that the effects of <em>Cd36</em> mutation on the features of metabolic syndrome are contextually dependent on genomic background. We have derived two new congenic strains by introgression of limited chromosome 4 regions of SHR origin, both including the defective <em>Cd36</em> gene, into the genetic background of a highly inbred model of insulin resistance and dyslipidemia, polydactylous (PD) rat strain. We subjected standard diet-fed adult males of PD and the congenic PD.SHR4 strains to metabolic, morphometric and transcriptomic profiling. We observed significantly improved glucose tolerance and lower fasting insulin levels in PD.SHR4 congenics than in PD. One of the PD.SHR4 strains showed lower triglyceride concentrations across major lipoprotein fractions combined with higher levels of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol compared with the PD progenitor. The hepatic transcriptome assessment revealed a network of genes differentially expressed between PD and PD.SHR4 with significant enrichment by members of the circadian rhythmicity pathway (Arntl (Bmal1), Clock, Nfil3, Per2 and Per3). In summary, the introduction of the chromosome 4 region of SHR origin including defective <em>Cd36</em> into the PD genetic background resulted in disconnected shifts of metabolic profile along with distinct changes in hepatic transcriptome. The synthesis of the current results with those obtained in other <em>Cd36</em>-deficient strains indicates that the eventual metabolic effect of a deleterious mutation such as that of SHR-derived Cd36 is not absolute, but rather a function of complex interactions between environmental and genomic background, upon which it operates.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">functional genomics, metabolic syndrome, animal model, transcriptomics</span></div></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first">Metabolic syndrome is a complex human condition with rising prevalence worldwide. Recently, several previously used definitions have been merged into a unified definition of the metabolic syndrome (<a href="#bib3" rid="bib3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433426">Alberti <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>). This definition lists dyslipidemia, obesity, hypertension and fasting glucose levels among the syndrome's major components. In spite of clear evidence of the ecogenomic (genome &#x000d7; environment interactive) nature of the pathogenesis of the syndrome and its individual aspects, the identification of causal genetic variants remains elusive even in the era of large, genome-wide association studies (<a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433485">Kraja <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). To date, one of the few successfully and positionally cloned culprits linked to many facets of the metabolic syndrome is a gene coding for fatty acid translocase <em>Cd36</em>, mutation of which has been originally found in the spontaneously hypertensive rat (SHR) (<a href="#bib2" rid="bib2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433459">Aitman <em>et al.</em>, 1997</a>, <a href="#bib1" rid="bib1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433474">1999</a>; <a href="#bib28" rid="bib28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433460">Pravenec <em>et al.</em>, 2001</a>). Interestingly, the complex nature of mutation of <em>Cd36</em> (a chimeric gene owing to unequal recombination between the <em>Cd36</em> gene and one of its pseudogenes) in SHR (<a href="#bib12" rid="bib12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433444">Glazier <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>) does not result in complete lack of expression, but rather in severe, tissue-specific downregulation of the gene (<a href="#bib5" rid="bib5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433461">Bonen <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>). Although rare, complete deficiencies of Cd36 were reported in humans (<a href="#bib39" rid="bib39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433462">Yamamoto <em>et al.</em>, 1994</a>; <a href="#bib15" rid="bib15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433432">Kashiwagi <em>et al.</em>, 1996</a>) and a murine model featuring complete knockout of <em>Cd36</em> is available (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433469">Febbraio <em>et al.</em>, 1999</a>), models carrying the &#x02018;incomplete' type of mutation may relevantly represent the more subtle functional consequences of common polymorphisms in human <em>Cd36</em> gene. Actually, several single-nucleotide polymorphisms in the human <em>Cd36</em> gene were recently associated with lipid levels, metabolic syndrome (<a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433484">Love-Gregory <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>; <a href="#bib25" rid="bib25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433483">Noel <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>) or obesity (<a href="#bib4" rid="bib4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433433">Bokor <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>), although the latter has not been unanimously confirmed (<a href="#bib7" rid="bib7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433427">Choquet <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>).</p><p id="__p3" class="p p-last">We have previously shown that introgression of rat chromosome 4 segment including the mutant <em>Cd36</em> gene from SHR into the Brown Norway genome results in deterioration of insulin sensitivity and increase in triacylglycerol and free fatty acid levels in the BN.SHR4 congenic strain (<a href="#bib34" rid="bib34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433473">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). Furthermore, we have found this limited portion of genome to be crucial for several pharmacogenetic interactions involving the insulin sensitizer rosiglitazone (<a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433470">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2003a</a>, <a href="#bib35" rid="bib35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433489">2008</a>) and glucocorticoid dexamethasone (<a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433477">Krupkova <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). In SHR, <em>Cd36</em> was established as a key determinant of the insulin-sensitizing actions of thiazolidinediones using SHR transgenic and congenic strains expressing wild-type <em>Cd36</em> (<a href="#bib29" rid="bib29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433437">Qi <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). In this study, we have tested the metabolic and transcriptomic impact of the presence of defective <em>Cd36</em> within the genomic background of a highly inbred model of metabolic syndrome, the polydactylous rat (<a href="#bib19" rid="bib19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433451">Kren, 1975</a>; <a href="#bib36" rid="bib36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433472">Sedova <em>et al.</em>, 2000</a>). The choice was driven by our previous differential linkage study conducted in PD &#x000d7; BN.SHR4 cross, in which we mapped several suggestive and significant quantitative-trait loci of metabolic syndrome-related traits in the region of <em>Cd36/Fat</em> gene (<a href="#bib33" rid="bib33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433441">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2003b</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec2title">Materials and methods</h2><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Rat strains</h3><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">The polydactylous rat strain (PD/Cub, PD hereafter, Rat Genome Database (<a href="#bib8" rid="bib8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433488">Dwinell <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) (RGD) ID no. 728161) is a highly inbred strain (<em>F</em>&#x0003e;90) showing metabolic syndrome attributes (<a href="#bib36" rid="bib36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433425">Sedova <em>et al.</em>, 2000</a>; <a href="#bib32" rid="bib32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433467">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2005</a>), kept since 1969 at the Institute of Biology and Medical Genetics, First Faculty of Medicine, Charles University in Prague (<a href="#bib19" rid="bib19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433482">Kren, 1975</a>). In this study, we have established two new congenic strains combining the genomic information of two established genetic models of metabolic syndrome, PD and the spontaneously hypertensive rat (SHR/OlaIpcv, SHR hereafter, RGD ID no. 631848). class="p p-first-last">Abbreviations: PD, polydactylous; SHR, spontaneously hypertensive rat.</p></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h3>Experimental protocol</h3><p id="__p5" class="p p-first-last">Our study was performed in conformity with the Animal Protection Law of the Czech Republic (311/1997), which is in compliance with the European Community Council recommendations for the use of laboratory animals 86/609/ECC and was approved by the Ethical committee of the First Faculty of Medicine. Adult rat males were held under temperature- and humidity-controlled conditions on a 12-h/12-h light&#x02013;dark cycle. Throughout the study, the animals had free access to food (standard chow) and water. At the age of 4 months, adult males of both congenic strains and the parental strain PD (<em>n</em>=6&#x02013;8 per strain) were subjected to oral glucose-tolerance test after overnight fasting and the blood samples for other metabolic measurements were drawn. Then the animals were killed and the total weight and the weight of heart, liver, kidneys, adrenals, epididymal and retroperitoneal fat pads were determined; liver tissue was snap frozen in liquid nitrogen for further analyses of gene expression.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><h3>DNA extraction, genotyping</h3><p id="__p6" class="p p-first-last">PCR was used for genotyping markers polymorphic between progenitor strains. We tested the DNA of both congenic strains (PD.SHR4a, <em>n</em>=8; PD.SHR4b, <em>n</em>=6) and the progenitor strains PD/Cub and SHR. The rat DNA was isolated by a modified phenol-extraction method from tail-incision samples. Nucleotide sequences of primers were obtained from public databases (RGD, <a href="http://rgd.mcw.edu/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3375406&amp;issue-id=211056&amp;journal-id=1477&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://rgd.mcw.edu/</a>, The Welcome Trust Centre for Human Genetics, <a href="http://www.well.ox.ac.uk/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3375406&amp;issue-id=211056&amp;journal-id=1477&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.well.ox.ac.uk/</a> or Whitehead Institute/MIT Center for Genome Research, <a href="http://www-genome.wi.mit.edu/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3375406&amp;issue-id=211056&amp;journal-id=1477&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www-genome.wi.mit.edu/</a>). The PCR products were separated on polyacrylamide (7&#x02013;10&#x00025;) gels, detected in ultraviolet light after ethidium-bromide staining using Syngene G:Box.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic measurements</h3><p id="__p7" class="p p-first-last">The oral glucose-tolerance test (OGTT) was performed after overnight fasting and the blood samples for the glycemia determination (Ascensia Elite Blood Glucose Meter; Bayer HealthCare, Mishawaka, IN, USA, validated by Institute of Clinical Biochemistry and Laboratory Diagnostics of the First Faculty of Medicine) were obtained from the tail vein at intervals of 0, 30, 60, 120 and 180&#x02009;min after intragastric glucose administration to conscious rats (3&#x02009;g&#x02009;kg<sup>&#x02212;1</sup> body weight, 30&#x00025; aqueous solution). The lipid profile (cholesterol and triglyceride blood concentration in 20 lipoprotein fractions, glycerol level and chylomicron, very-low-density lipoprotein (VLDL), low-density lipoprotein (LDL) and high-density lipoprotein particle sizes) was assessed by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) as described previously (<a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433449">Krupkova <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>; <a href="#bib37" rid="bib37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433465">Usui <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). Serum free fatty acids were determined using an acyl-CoA oxidase-based colorimetric kit (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Mannheim, Germany). Serum insulin concentration was determined using an ELISA kit for rat insulin assay (Mercodia, Uppsala, Sweden). Serum levels of adiponectin were determined using Rat Adiponectin ELISA kit (B-Bridge International, Cupertino, CA, USA).</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><h3>Transcriptomic profiling and quantitative real-time PCR</h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first">Total RNA was extracted with TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) and purified with the RNeasy MinElute cleanup kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, USA) following the manufacturer's recommendations. The quality of the total RNA was evaluated on an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer system (Agilent, Palo Alto, CA, USA). Microarray experiments were performed using the GeneChip Rat Exon 1.0 ST array (interrogates over 850&#x02009;000 exon clusters), with approximately four probes per exon and roughly 40 probes per gene (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA, USA). Following the ribosomal reduction procedure (Invitrogen), from 1&#x02009;&#x003bc;g total RNA, each sample was processed using the GeneChip Whole Transcript Sense Target Labeling Assay (Affymetrix). Briefly, after the rRNA reduction procedure, double-stranded cDNA was synthesized with random hexamers tagged with a T7 promoter sequence, then it was amplified by T7 RNA polymerase producing complementary RNA; in the second cycle of cDNA synthesis, complementary RNA was used to produce sense single-stranded cDNA and 5.5&#x02009;&#x003bc;g was fragmented, labeled and hybridized onto the chip (PD.SHR4a&#x02013;2 chips; PD&#x02013;3 chips). The whole hybridization procedure was performed using the Affymetrix GeneChip system according to the protocol recommended by Affymetrix. The hybridization was evaluated with Affymetrix GeneChip Command Console Software (AGCC) and the quality of the chips with Affymetrix Expression Console. Partek Genomics Suite (Partek, St Louis, MO, USA) was used for data analysis. The data were normalized by using Robust Multichip Average (RMA) algorithm, which uses background adjustment, quantile normalization and summarization.</p><p id="__p9" class="p p-last">To validate microarray gene expression data, quantitative real-time PCR (SYBR-Green) was used. Total RNA (2&#x02009;&#x003bc;g) was reverse-transcribed with random primers using the SuperScript VILO cDNA Synthesis Kit (Invitrogen). Primers were designed using Primer3 (<a href="#bib30" rid="bib30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433468">Rozen and Skaletsky, 2000</a>) and synthesized by Integrated DNA Technologies. The primer sequences are listed in <a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 2</a>. Real-time PCR reaction was performed in quadruplicate with EXPRESS SYBR GreenER qPCR SuperMix with Premixed ROX Kit according to the manufacturer's protocol (Invitrogen, Burlington, ON, Canada) using Applied Biosystems (Burlington, ON, Canada) Real-Time PCR System. Results were analyzed using the Pfaffl analysis method (<a href="#bib27" rid="bib27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433463">Pfaffl, 2001</a>) with reference genes peptidylprolyl isomerase B and 18S.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><h3>Statistical and pathway analyses</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">The metabolic and morphometric data were compared by one-way analysis of variance with strain as main factor followed by Tukey's honest significance difference test for detailed pair-wise comparison.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Transcriptomic data</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">After evaluation of the hybridization, the quality control and the data normalization by robust multi-array analysis (RMA), the gene expression was compared between the PD.SHR4a and PD strains using Partek Genomics Suite (Partek). The transcripts found to be significantly differentially expressed between PD and PD.SHR4a strains (FDR&#x0003c;0.1, <em>n</em>=172) were included in the gene enrichment and pathway/network analyses, which were performed using web-based DAVID functional enrichment analysis (<a href="#bib14" rid="bib14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433464">Huang da <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) and Ingenuity Pathway Analysis software v.9 applications.</p></div></div><div id="__sec10" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec10title">Results</h2><div id="__sec11" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Genomic characteristics of the differential segments in the new PD.SHR4 congenic strains</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">Our genotyping scan of 54 polymorphic microsatellite markers revealed the extent of the chromosome 4 differential segments of SHR origin in the two PD.SHR4 congenic strains (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl1" rid-ob="ob-tbl1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). In PD.SHR(D4Arb13-D4Rat125)&#x02014;PD.SHR4a hereafter&#x02014;the SHR-derived segment spans about 21&#x02009;Mb, while in PD.SHR(cen-<em>Lmbr</em>; D4Rat147&#x02013;D4Rat151)&#x02014;PD.SHR4b hereafter&#x02014;the introgressed segment is discontinuous as it extends 37&#x02009;Mb from the centromere with a limited interruption at 1.3&#x02013;2.4&#x02009;Mb (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl1" rid-ob="ob-tbl1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). Several total genome scans conducted throughout the PD.SHR4 strains derivation eventually excluded the presence of other non-PD alleles than those fixed on chromosome 4, confirming the congenicity of the new strains. The SHR-derived segments on chromosome 4 hence represent the only genomic differences between PD and PD.SHR4 congenic strains.</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="sec"><h3>Morphometry and basic metabolic profile</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">The PD and the two PD.SHR4 strains did not show any differences in body weight, adipose tissue distribution or relative weights of internal organs except for the slightly lower liver weight in PD.SHR4a (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl2" rid-ob="ob-tbl2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). After filtering the results for quality parameters and adjusting statistical significance levels for multiple comparisons (see Materials and methods), we found 172 transcripts to be differentially expressed between PD and PD.SHR4a (<a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Data Set</a>), with 48 of the transcripts showing more than a twofold difference (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl4/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl4" rid-ob="ob-tbl4" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 4a</span></a>). The numbers of transcripts up- and downregulated in PD.SHR4a vs PD were roughly balanced (83 up- vs 89 downregulated). Apart from the expected substantially reduced expression of <em>Cd36</em> in the congenic strain, there was no other differentially expressed gene located in the segment of chromosome 4 of SHR origin (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl4/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl4" rid-ob="ob-tbl4" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 4</span></a> and <a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplemental Data Set</a>). The rest of the differentially expressed genes were spread across almost all the other chromosomes. differentially expressed genes, we carried out a systematic search for their enrichment in ontological categories, canonical pathways or disease-related gene sets as well as their potential functional connections. First, we examined the degree of over-representation of our set of genes in the canonical pathways using the relevant module in the Ingenuity Pathway Analysis v.9 software. After correction for multiple testing using Benjamini&#x02013;Hochberg false discovery rate, we identified four pathways significantly enriched by the genes most distinctly expressed in PD vs PD.SHR4: circadian rhythm signaling, xenobiotic metabolism signaling, PXR/RXR activation and LPS/IL-1-mediated inhibition of RXR function (<a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 2</a>). In the subsequent toxicity functions analysis (as implemented in IPA), only liver regeneration, liver steatosis and renal tubule injury surpassed the statistical threshold for significant over-representation both in the total sample of 172 genes and in the subset upregulated in PD.SHR4. Then, we proceeded to assess the potential functional relations among the genes differentially expressed between PD.SHR4a and PD using dynamic pathway modeling. As we have previously shown the concentrations of insulin, glucose, free fatty acids and cholesterol to be comparable in PD and SHR strains (<a href="#bib36" rid="bib36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433456">Sedova <em>et al.</em>, 2000</a>), the current results indicate the presence of an interaction between gene(s) within the introgressed segments and genomic background of the congenic strains.</p><!--table ft1--><!--table-wrap mode="anchored" t5--><div class="table-wrap table anchored whole_rhythm" id="tbl5"><h3>Table 5</h3><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Comparison of effect of <em>Cd36</em> deficiency in different genetic models</strong></div><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139793994952336" class="xtable"><table frame="hsides" rules="groups" border="1" class="rendered small default_table"><thead valign="bottom"><tr><th align="left" valign="top" charoff="50" rowspan="1" colspan="1">&#x000a0;</th><th align="center" valign="top" charoff="50" rowspan="1" colspan="1"><em>BN.SHR4 vs BN</em></th><th align="center" valign="top" charoff="50" rowspan="1" colspan="1"><em>PD.SHR4 vs PD</em></th><th align="center" valign="top" charoff="50" rowspan="1" colspan="1"><em>Cd36&#x02212;/&#x02212; The arrows indicate the respective change of the metabolic parameter in the <em>Cd36</em>-deficient animals versus their wild-type controls: &#x02191;, increase; &#x02193;, decrease; &#x02194;, no change.</p></div></div></div><p id="__p18">Although many of the studies including ours demonstrated the association of lack of Cd36 with metabolic syndrome features like insulin resistance or dyslipidemia (<a href="#bib2" rid="bib2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433452">Aitman <em>et al.</em>, 1997</a>, <a href="#bib1" rid="bib1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433445">1999</a>; <a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433438">Febbraio <em>et al.</em>, 1999</a>; <a href="#bib28" rid="bib28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433430">Pravenec <em>et al.</em>, 2001</a>; <a href="#bib34" rid="bib34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433442">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>), there are also reports suggesting that, in certain circumstances, the expression level of Cd36 actually correlates positively with unfavorable metabolic profile. Recently, it has been shown by <a href="#bib22" rid="bib22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433457">Love-Gregory <em>et al.</em> (2011)</a> that in African-Americans the Cd36 variants that reduce protein expression appear to promote a protective metabolic profile, particularly concerning high-density lipoprotein and VLDL lipid levels (<a href="#bib22" rid="bib22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433436">Love-Gregory <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). Also, the <em>Cd36</em> knockout mice fed chow diet displayed lower fasting concentrations of glucose and insulin compared with wild-type controls (<a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433487">Hajri <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>), similarly to PD.SHR4 congenics in the current study. On the other hand, their triglyceride and free fatty acid levels were increased in contrast to the decrease or no change observed in our study (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl5/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl5" rid-ob="ob-tbl5" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 5</span></a>). Absence of Cd36 also protects mice from insulin resistance associated with high fat diet-induced obesity and hyperlipidemia (<a href="#bib16" rid="bib16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433439">Kennedy <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>) and, conversely, increased expression of hepatic Cd36 in response to high fat diet-induced obesity was found to be sufficient to exacerbate hepatic triglyceride storage and secretion (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433455">Koonen <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>). One of the mechanisms responsible may lie in the fact that human islets express Cd36 in the plasma membrane as well as in the insulin-secretory granules, and Cd36 activity was deemed important for uptake of fatty acids into &#x003b2;-cells as well as for mediating their modulatory effects on insulin secretion (<a href="#bib26" rid="bib26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433434">Noushmehr <em>et al.</em>, 2005</a>) .</p><p id="__p19">Altogether, it is apparent that the eventual metabolic effect of <em>Cd36</em> deficiency is tightly linked to a particular setting of both genomic background (for example, PD.SHR4 vs BN.SHR4 (<a href="#bib34" rid="bib34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433450">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>, <a href="#bib33" rid="bib33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433448">2003b</a>); <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl5/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl5" rid-ob="ob-tbl5" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 5</span></a> and <a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 3</a>) and environmental factors, particularly diet (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433466">Febbraio <em>et al.</em>, 1999</a>; <a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433478">Hajri <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>; <a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433429">Koonen <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>; <a href="#bib16" rid="bib16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433475">Kennedy <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>) or medication (<a href="#bib29" rid="bib29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433428">Qi <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>; <a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433443">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2003a</a>; <a href="#bib35" rid="bib35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433435">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>; <a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433480">Krupkova <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). Therefore, the apparently controversial issue of causal relation between level of Cd36 expression and metabolic outcome may be resolved by adoption of broader conceptual framework incorporating other (eco)genomic factors.</p><p id="__p20">Apart from <em>Cd36</em>, significant overexpression of <em>Igfbp2</em> in PD.SHR4a (yet genetically of PD origin in both strains) represents a possible mediator of the enhanced insulin sensitivity, as was reported, for example, in <em>Igfbp2</em> transgenic mice (<a href="#bib38" rid="bib38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_433231510">Wheatcroft <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>). The observation of concurrent upregulation of <em>Abcg5</em> and downregulation of <em>Insig1</em> in PD.SHR4 congenic follows well their assumed respective roles in cholesterol efflux and its cellular feedback regulation. This shift, reported also in <em>Insig1&#x02212;/&#x02212;Insig2&#x02212;/&#x02212;</em> mice (<a href="#bib10" rid="bib10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433454">Engelking <em>et al.</em>, 2005</a>), may suggest a tendency toward increasing lipid deposition in liver, although we did not validate this parameter directly.</p><p id="__p21">One of the intriguing results in the current study was the identification of a differentially expressed set of circadian clock-related genes (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/figure/fig3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig3" rid-ob="ob-fig3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig3"><span>Figure 3</span></a>). The fundamental interrelatedness of circadian rhythmicity, inter- and intra-organ desynchrony with the metabolic and signaling pathways involved in the pathogenesis of cardiometabolic diseases has been supported by an ever-growing body of evidence, reviewed recently in detail elsewhere (<a href="#bib9" rid="bib9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_433231509">Eckel-Mahan-Sassone-Corsi, 2009</a>; <a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433431">Maury <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). The gene most downregulated in PD.SHR4a liver compared with PD was <em>Arntl</em> (also known as <em>Bmal1</em>), an essential component of master circadian pacemaker (<a href="#bib6" rid="bib6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_364813741">Bunger <em>et al.</em>, 2000</a>), together with upregulated <em>Per2</em> and <em>Per3</em> genes, two natural inhibitors of <em>Bmal1</em> and parts of its negative-feedback loop. There is a certain resemblance to the studies in liver-specific <em>Bmal1</em> knockout mice, which show hypoglycemia restricted to the fasting phase of the daily cycle and enhanced glucose clearance after an overnight fast (<a href="#bib21" rid="bib21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433447">Lamia <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>), but not reduced fasting-insulin levels, found in both PD.SHR4 congenic strains and complete <em>Bmal1</em> knockout (<a href="#bib21" rid="bib21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433479">Lamia <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). We are not aware of any previous report functionally linking the metabolic outcomes of <em>Cd36</em> polymorphisms to circadian-clock circuitry.</p><p id="__p22" class="p p-last">Finally, we observed several differences in lipid levels and liver weight between the two congenic strains. There are several plausible candidates for these effects within the three regions differing between PD.SHR4a and PD.SHRb, namely <em>Insig1</em>, serotonin receptor <em>Htr5a</em> or carnitine <em>O</em>-octanoyltransferase (<em>Crot</em>) as indicated in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3375406/table/tbl1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="tbl1" rid-ob="ob-tbl1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>. We cannot completely exclude the possibility that some of the other genes of SHR origin are present in the differential segment(s) of the congenic PD.SHR4 strains contributed to the observed metabolic effects commonly present in both congenics and SHR. However, the transcriptome analysis and functional annotation of all the genes in the segment reinforce rather the effect of the <em>Cd36</em> mutation. Within the differential segment of the PD.SHR4a strain, there are 58 well-documented protein-coding genes (<a href="#sup1" rid="sup1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplemental Table 3</a>) among about 120 transcripts. Of those, only <em>Cd36</em> was found to be differentially expressed between PD and PD.SHR4a. Further studies are needed to assess the effect of Cd36 and the whole differential segment under distinct nutritional and pharmacological challenges, especially given our previous extensive documentation of nutrigenetic and pharmacogenetic interactions of a similar genomic region in the BN.SHR4 congenic strain (<a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433486">Seda <em>et al.</em>, 2003a</a>, <a href="#bib35" rid="bib35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433440">2008</a>; <a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_263433476">Krupkova <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). In sum, the introduction of mutated <em>Cd36</em> into the genomic background of an inbred model of metabolic syndrome resulted in disconnected shifts of metabolic profile along with distinct changes in hepatic transcriptome. Our network analysis revealed possible pathways underlie the improvement of insulin sensitivity toward a shift in lipid profile. The synthesis of the current results with those obtained in other <em>Cd36</em>-deficient strains indicates that the eventual metabolic effect of a deleterious mutation such as that of SHR-derived <em>Cd36</em> is not absolute, but rather a function of complex interactions between environment and genomic background, upon which it operates.</p></div><div id="__sec17" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec17title">Data archiving</h2><p id="__p23" class="p p-first-last">Complete microarray datasets have been submitted to the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) under the record <a id="__tag_263433545" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE33597" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE33597"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE33597","term_id":"33597"}}</span>GSE33597</a>.
