sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140515152022384title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><div id="S1" class="sec sec-first"><h2>Aims/hypothesis</h2><p id="P1" class="p p-first-last">Pregnancies complicated by diabetes have a higher risk of adverse outcomes for mothers and children, including predisposition to disease later in life, e.g. metabolic syndrome and hypertension. We hypothesised that adverse outcomes from diabetic pregnancies may be linked to compromised placental function, and sought to identify cellular and molecular abnormalities in diabetic placenta.</p></div><div id="S2" class="sec"><h2>Methods</h2><p id="P2" class="p p-first-last">Using a mouse model of diabetic pregnancy, placental gene expression was assayed at mid-gestation and cellular composition analysed at various stages. Genome-wide expression profiling was validated by quantitative PCR and tissue localisation studies were performed to identify cellular correlates of altered gene expression in diabetic placenta.</p></div><div id="S3" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="P3" class="p p-first-last">We detected significantly altered gene expression in diabetic placenta for genes expressed in the maternal and those expressed in the embryonic compartments. We also found altered cellular composition of the decidual compartment. In addition, the junctional and labyrinth layers were reduced in diabetic placenta, accompanied by aberrant differentiation of spongiotrophoblast cells.</p></div><div id="S4" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusions/interpretation</h2><p id="P4" class="p p-first-last">Diabetes during pregnancy alters transcriptional profiles in the murine placenta, affecting cells of embryonic and maternal origin, and involving several genes not previously implicated in diabetic pregnancies. The molecular changes and abnormal differentiation of multiple cell types precede impaired growth of junctional zone and labyrinth, and of placenta overall. Regardless of whether these changes represent direct responses to hyperglycaemia or are physiological adaptations, they are likely to play a role in pregnancy complications and outcomes, and to have implications for developmental origins of adult disease.</p></div></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">Diabetic placenta, Diabetic pregnancy, Gene expression profiling, Gene regulation, Hyperglycaemia, Junctional layer, Maternal diabetes, Placental insufficiency, Spongiotrophoblast, Transcription factors</span></div></div><div id="S5" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S5title">Introduction</h2><p id="P5" class="p p-first">Diabetes in pregnancy is a well established risk factor for diabetic embryopathy, a spectrum of congenital malformations that include heart defects and neural tube defects [<a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282320">1</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R3" rid="R3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282312">3</a>]. Exposure to maternal diabetes during pregnancy also predisposes to disease later in life, e.g. metabolic syndrome, obesity, diabetes and hypertension [<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282336">4</a>, <a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282305">5</a>]. While mechanistic studies on developmental origins of adult disease are still in early stages, substantial progress has been made in identifying causes of the congenital abnormalities. Thus in rodent models, metabolic perturbations and nutrient deficiencies have been demonstrated, including perturbed prostaglandin metabolism and inositol deficiency. Oxidative stress and hypoxia have also been documented as causes [<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282328">6</a>], and antioxidant administration reduced the incidence of fetal malformations. In addition to increased apoptosis, developmental regulators [<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282307">7</a>] and signalling pathways [<a href="#R8" rid="R8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282298">8</a>, <a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282331">9</a>] are perturbed in exposed embryos, implicating these mechanisms in abnormal morphogenesis and congenital malformations in diabetic embryopathy.</p><p id="P6">There is, however, little knowledge about the basis for increased disease susceptibility later in life in progeny exposed to maternal diabetes. Once an embryo has escaped the critical time window of vulnerability to congenital defects, it nevertheless remains exposed to the diabetic environment. We have recently shown that this leads to reduction of intrauterine growth during later stages of gestation [<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282313">10</a>], possibly due to hyperglycaemia-induced changes in the placenta. It has been proposed that placental changes could be central to developmental programming of disease susceptibility of the offspring [<a href="#R11" rid="R11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282322">11</a>, <a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_398698501">12</a>], but the underlying molecular mechanisms are unknown. Altered placental weight has been reported for diabetic rats [<a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282311">13</a>], and for human type 1 [<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282315">14</a>] and gestational diabetic [<a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282289">15</a>] pregnancies. Human type 1 diabetic placenta undergoes increased angiogenesis and exhibits vascular dysfunction, mediated through vascular endothelial growth factor and nitric oxide in early pregnancy, and through hyperinsulinaemia in the later trimesters [<a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282281">16</a>]. Furthermore, enrichment of glycogen [<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282285">17</a>] and excess lipid accumulation [<a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282327">18</a>, <a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282332">19</a>] have been described in rat diabetic placenta.</p><p id="P7" class="p p-last">To identify the molecular basis of placental abnormalities in diabetic pregnancies in the mouse, we used a streptozotocin-induced model of diabetes. Our results demonstrate that altered gene expression at mid-gestation precedes cellular and structural abnormalities in diabetic mouse placenta at later stages.</p></div><div id="S6" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S6title">Methods</h2><div id="S7" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Animals</h3><p id="P8" class="p p-first-last">All experiments were conducted in accordance with the United States Public Health Service Policy on Humane Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (<a href="http://grants1.nih.gov/grants/olaw/references/phspol.htm" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://grants1.nih.gov/grants/olaw/references/phspol.htm</a>) in a facility approved by the American Association for Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care. Diabetes was induced in 7- to 9-week-old FVB female mice (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA, USA) by two i.p. injections of streptozotocin (100 mg/kg body weight) and matings were set up as described previously [<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282318">9</a>]. Dams whose glucose levels in tail vein blood (Contour Glucometer; Bayer, Tarrytown, NY, USA) exceeded 13.9 mmol/l were considered diabetic; average glucose levels were 8.22 mmol/l (&#x000b1;1.0 SD) before streptozotocin treatment, 18.722&#x000b1;4.4 mmol/l in diabetic dams on the day of mating and 29.33&#x000b1;3.9 mmol/l on the day of placenta removal (from dams at gestational day E10.5, counting the day of detection of vaginal plug as E0.5).</p></div><div id="S8" class="sec"><h3>Microarray analysis</h3><p id="P9" class="p p-first-last">Whole placentae were dissected at E10.5; only specimens associated with morphologically normal embryos were used. Decidua were removed from the uterus, and the yolk sac and embryos were dissected out; placenta samples consisted of embryonic and maternal tissue. Placentae were processed as individual samples following published methods [<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282284">9</a>]. Samples were hybridised to 10 Affymetrix Mouse 430 2.0 arrays (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA, USA), which were scanned using a scanner (GeneChip3000, Affymetrix). Quality control was done with GeneChip Operating Software (Affymetrix) and statistical analyses were performed as previously described for embryos [<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282304">9</a>]. The primary data files are available at the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus repository (accession number GSE 28277).</p></div><div id="S9" class="sec"><h3>Quantitative real-time</h3><p id="P10" class="p p-first-last">PCR Quantitative real-time PCR was performed as described [<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282282">9</a>] using the ABI Prism 7900HT device (Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Primers (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">electronic supplementary material [ESM] Table 1</a>) were positioned to span exon&#x02013;exon junctions and amplicons were designed to originate from a different transcript region than that detected by microarray probes. Complementary DNA samples were derived from individual E10.5 placentae from different diabetic and control dams than those from which samples were assayed on microarrays. For normalisation, we used measurement of polymerase epsilon 4 (<em>Pol&#x003b5;4</em>, also known as <em>Pole4</em>) expression; <em>Pol&#x003b5;4</em> expression proved to be unaffected by maternal diabetes on the arrays.</p></div><div id="S10" class="sec"><h3>Annotation for molecular function and mutant mouse phenotypes</h3><p id="P11" class="p p-first-last">Molecular function attributes were based on Gene Ontology annotation (NetAffx <a href="http://www.affymetrix.com/analysis/netaffx/index.affx" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.affymetrix.com/analysis/netaffx/index.affx</a>), supplemented with information from PubMed and Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery (DAVID) (<a href="http://david.abcc.ncifcrf.gov/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://david.abcc.ncifcrf.gov/</a>), which was also used to examine for enrichment of specific categories. Information on mouse mutants was obtained from Mouse Genome Informatics (MGI) (<a href="http://www.informatics.jax.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.informatics.jax.org</a>; accessed 20 December, 2010). For transcription factor binding site (TFBS) prediction, Whole Genome rVISTA (<a href="http://genome.lbl.gov/vista/index.shtml" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://genome.lbl.gov/vista/index.shtml</a>) was used to identify motifs that are conserved between mouse and human and are over-represented in the 5 kb upstream regions of diabetes-affected genes, relative to all 5 kb upstream regions in the genome (<em>p</em>&#x0003c;0.005).</p></div><div id="S11" class="sec"><h3>Histology</h3><p id="P12" class="p p-first-last">Placentae from normal and diabetic pregnancies were embedded in paraffin using a Shandon Excelsior tissue processor (Thermo, Waltham, MA, USA) and an embedder (Histocentre 3, Thermo), and sectioned at a thickness of 6 &#x003bc;m. Deparaffinisation and staining with haematoxylin/eosin was done in a Special Stainer device (ST5020; Leica Microsystems, Bannockburn, IL, USA), followed by coverslipping (CV5030; Leica). Slides were scanned on a digital pathology station (NDP Nanozoomer; Hamamatsu, Bridgewater, NJ, USA).</p></div><div id="S12" class="sec"><h3>In situ hybridisation</h3><p id="P13" class="p p-first-last">In situ hybridisations were performed as published [<a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282280">20</a>] to either 20 2m frozen sections or 6 2m sections of paraffin-embedded tissues. Processing of slides was done together for diabetic and control samples in the same vessel for all fixation, wash, hybridisation, incubation and development steps. Plasmids for probe generation were obtained from Open Biosystems (Huntsville, AL, USA). For comparison of control and diabetic tissues, photography was done with the same magnification and exposure.</p></div><div id="S13" class="sec"><h3>Cell counting and statistical analysis</h3><p id="P14" class="p p-first-last">All sections were from the middle of the respective placentae. The sizes of placental compartments were determined as the respective area fractions in ImageJ (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA; <a href="http://rsbweb.nih.gov/ij/download.html" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3882064&amp;issue-id=231775&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://rsbweb.nih.gov/ij/download.html</a>, accessed 14 March 2011). Cell cluster counting was done by selecting the labyrinth only and counting of pixels with <em>Tpbpa</em> signal above a uniform threshold was performed. The validity of these methods was confirmed manually by two individuals, independently. For each placenta, data from ten sections were averaged and adjusted for size of the whole placenta. We included six placentae each in the control and diabetic groups for gestational day E15.5 and three specimens per group were used for E18.5. Each placenta came from a different pregnancy. Embryo and placenta size were averaged to each dam before averaging for the group. Statistical analysis was done by two-sided <em>t</em> test.</p></div><div id="S14" class="sec"><h3>Mouse model of diabetic pregnancy</h3><p id="P15" class="p p-first-last">We employed the well-established streptozotocin-induced mouse model of diabetes, using the FVB inbred strain of mice. Embryos and placentae were collected at embryonic day 10.5 (E10.5) because at this stage, neural tube defects are easily detectable. Samples associated with malformed embryos were excluded from the analysis. Control embryos (<em>n</em>=65 embryos from nine dams) had a crown&#x02013;rump length of 5.17&#x000b1;0.34 mm (mean&#x000b1;SD). In diabetes-exposed embryos (<em>n</em>=97 embryos from 13 dams) crown&#x02013;rump length was 5.07&#x000b1;0.39 mm. Differences were not statistically significant (<em>p</em>=0.54). Placentae looked normal at this time point, but they differed (<em>p</em>=0.0038) in size, with control placenta (<em>n</em>=31 placentae from seven dams) having a wet weight of 42.87&#x000b1;2.01 mg and diabetic placentae (<em>n</em>=61 placentae from nine dams) weighing 37.92&#x000b1;3.96 mg. From five control dams and five diabetic dams, we collected one placenta each of a weight close to the mean for the respective group and performed ten independent microarray assays (Affymetrix 430 2.0). Separate placentae were used for validation studies by quantitative real-time PCR. There were no significant differences in litter size at any of the gestational points investigated, except at E18.5, where control dams on average had 9.8&#x000b1;2.31 embryos and diabetic dams had 8.1&#x000b1;3.04 progeny.</p></div><div id="S15" class="sec"><h3>Altered gene expression profiles in placentae from diabetic dams</h3><p id="P16" class="p p-first">From the 45,401 probe sets represented on the microarrays, we identified 158 genes where expression levels in placentae differed significantly between the experimental and control groups by more than 1.5-fold (<a href="#SD2" rid="SD2" class=" supplementary-material">ESM Table 2</a>). Of these 158 genes, 73 (46.2%) were expressed at lower levels and 85 (53.8%) genes at higher levels in diabetic placentae (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1a</span></a>). This is in contrast to the diabetes-exposed embryo (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>), where more than 85% of the differentially expressed genes were decreased in expression compared with controls [<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282334">9</a>]. Even where identical genes exhibited differential expression on embryo and placenta microarrays under conditions of maternal diabetes (<a href="#SD3" rid="SD3" class=" supplementary-material">ESM Table 3</a>), 62.5% of these displayed discordant regulation, reflecting opposite directions of misregulation in embryo and placenta. These results underscore the notion that responses of placenta and embryo to maternal diabetes are fundamentally different at the molecular level, probably implicating different molecular mechanisms of regulation.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140515152757840" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3882064_nihms-313942-f0001.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms-313942-f0001.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/bin/nihms-313942-f0001.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140515152757840" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Fig. 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p9">Comparison of gene expression profiles in placentae and embryos under conditions of maternal diabetes. These results suggest that oxidative stress pathways could be involved in deregulated gene expression in the diabetic placenta.</p></div><div id="S18" class="sec"><h3>Functional roles of genes deregulated in diabetic placentae</h3><p id="P22" class="p p-first-last">Conceivably, misregulated genes in diabetic placentae could be involved in altered placental function. This proposition is supported by mouse mutant phenotype information (MGI), which is available for 68 of the 158 genes. Of these 68 genes, seven have been shown to play a role in abnormal development of extra-embryonic tissues, uterus and/or the placenta (<a href="#SD4" rid="SD4" class=" supplementary-material">ESM Table 4</a> plus references cited there). Knockout of <em>Incenp</em> and <em>Lims1</em> affects extra-embryonic tissues, and <em>Bmp2</em> deficiency delays allantois development, while <em>Star</em> deletion is associated with abnormal uterus morphology. The labyrinth is disorganised in mutants that are heterozygous for a mutant <em>Cdx2</em> allele on a <em>Cdx4</em>-deficient background. Animals with knockout of <em>Socs3</em> and <em>Ascl2</em> exhibited altered placental morphology in the labyrinth layer, with the latter gene also affecting trophoblast giant cells and spongiotrophoblasts. However, for many other diabetes-affected placental genes, functional annotation is not yet available. Interestingly, the number of genes we identified as altered in diabetic placenta is only a small fraction of the 458 genotypes listed in MGI with placental phenotypes. This strongly suggests that diabetes affects specific pathways in the placenta, such as cell adhesion, growth factor responses and nutrient transport. Collectively, our results are consistent with the notion that misregulation of gene expression compromises development and function of the placenta in diabetic mouse pregnancy.</p></div><div id="S19" class="sec"><h3>Expression studies identified abnormalities in the maternal and embryonic compartments of the diabetic placenta</h3><p id="P23" class="p p-first">To identify the cell types with altered gene expression, we performed in situ hybridisation studies. <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Figure 2</span></a> depicts hybridisation patterns in normal E10.5 placenta for selected genes for which placental expression has not been described previously. <em>Mmp9</em> was expressed in a few cells of the junctional zone, thrombospondin-domain 4 exhibited a diffuse pattern, and in the decidual compartment we detected <em>Sfrp5</em> and <em>Rik9130008F23</em>. In the diabetic placentae, the ectopic <em>Tpbpa</em>+clusters were significantly larger (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5n</span></a>) and persisted to E18.5. The mislocalisation of these cells outside the spongiotrophoblast layer might explain its reduced thickness. Interestingly, Yu et al. [<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282321">22</a>] reported moderate enlargement of the junctional layer in E17.5 diabetic mouse placentae, based on isolectinB4 staining, but they included glycogen-containing cells in their measurements. These cells, which display light cytoplasms on haematoxylin&#x02013;eosin-stained sections [<a href="#R45" rid="R45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282306">45</a>], exhibit weak <em>Tpbpa</em> labelling (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5e, f</span></a>) and were excluded from our analysis because they differ in histological appearance from the cells with the strongest <em>Tpbpa</em> signal at E15.5 (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5e, f</span></a>) and at E18.5. The precise relationship of the ectopic clusters in the labyrinth to trophoblast progenitors, spongiotrophoblasts [<a href="#R46" rid="R46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282335">46</a>] and glycogen cells [<a href="#R45" rid="R45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282316">45</a>] remains to be defined. The persistence of large clusters of inappropriately differentiated cells may affect the functional integrity of the labyrinth and the vasculature, a possibility that deserves further investigation. Taken together, our results show that maternal diabetes impairs growth and alters spongiotrophoblast differentiation in the placenta, which is likely to affect signalling pathways important for placental growth and may have a detrimental effect on nutrient supply to the embryo.</p><p id="P33">Mislocalisation of spongiotrophoblast cells to the labyrinth has been reported in only a few instances to date. &#x02018;Islands of SP-like cells&#x02019; [<a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282329">47</a>] in the labyrinth were detected with targeted disruption of the genes encoding haemoxygenase 1, prolyl hydroxylase domain 2 and protocadherin 12, and aberrant expression of the imprinted gene <em>Phlda2</em> in mice (<a href="#SD4" rid="SD4" class=" supplementary-material">ESM Table 4</a>). Effects on labyrinth size were not consistent, but in the latter, fetal growth was delayed, resulting in a 13% smaller size by E18.5. These results implicate ectopic spongiotrophoblast differentiation in intrauterine growth reduction and support the intriguing possibility that the reduced spongiotrophoblast layer and ectopic spongiotropho-blast localisation in diabetic placentae could be responsible for the reduced embryonic growth we previously observed [<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282309">10</a>] at later stages of mouse diabetic pregnancies.</p><p id="P34" class="p p-last">Finally, although spongiotrophoblasts originate from extra-embryonic progenitors, their differentiation may be influenced by maternal factors. For example, the spongiotrophoblast layer was reduced in mutants with uterus-specific deletion of the <em>Nr2f2</em> gene (encoding COUPTFII), demonstrating a maternal effect on spongiotrophoblast growth [<a href="#R48" rid="R48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_348282279">48</a>]. Given that we found altered cell composition in the decidual compartment of diabetic placentae, it will be interesting to investigate which role specific decidual cell types have in spongiotrophoblast differentiation and placenta growth. Collectively, our results show that maternal diabetes causes misregulation of distinct gene repertoires in embryo and placenta, bringing about developmental defects, intrauterine growth reduction and abnormal placental cell differentiation, respectively. Perturbed cellular interactions in the diabetic placenta and subsequent impairment of fetal growth could be the underlying cause for life-long elevated disease risk for mother and offspring of a pregnancy complicated by diabetes. </p></div></div><div id="SM" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="SMtitle">Supplementary Material</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="SD1"><h4>electronic supplementary material [ESM] Table 1</h4><div class="sup-box half_rhythm" id="d36e779"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/bin/NIHMS313942-supplement-electronic_supplementary_material__ESM__Table_1.pdf">Click here to view.</a><sup>(59K, pdf)</sup></div></div><div class="sec suppmat" id="SD2"><h4>electronic supplementary material [ESM] Table 2</h4><div class="sup-box half_rhythm" id="d36e783"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/bin/NIHMS313942-supplement-electronic_supplementary_material__ESM__Table_2.pdf">Click here to view.</a><sup>(125K, pdf)</sup></div></div><div class="sec suppmat" id="SD3"><h4>electronic supplementary material [ESM] Table 3</h4><div class="sup-box half_rhythm" id="d36e787"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/bin/NIHMS313942-supplement-electronic_supplementary_material__ESM__Table_3.pdf">Click here to view.</a><sup>(38K, pdf)</sup></div></div><div class="sec suppmat" id="SD4"><h4>electronic supplementary material [ESM] Table 4</h4><div class="sup-box half_rhythm" id="d36e791"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3882064/bin/NIHMS313942-supplement-electronic_supplementary_material__ESM__Table_4.pdf">Click here to view.</a><sup>(123K, pdf)</sup></div></div></div><div id="S23" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S23title">Acknowledgements</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p1">We are grateful for expert technical assistance by J.
