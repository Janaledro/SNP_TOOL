in PMC.</div></div></div></div><div class="sec"></div><div id="idm139699265702480" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm139699265702480title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><div id="__sec1" class="sec sec-first"><h2>Background</h2><p id="__p1" class="p p-first-last">Obesity is a multifactorial disorder influenced by genetic and environmental factors. Animal models of obesity are required to help us understand the signaling pathways underlying this condition. Zebrafish possess many structural and functional similarities with humans and have been used to model various human diseases, including a genetic model of obesity. The purpose of this study was to establish a zebrafish model of diet-induced obesity (DIO).</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">Zebrafish were assigned into two dietary groups. One group of zebrafish was overfed with <em>Artemia </em>(60 mg dry weight/day/fish), a living prey consisting of a relatively high amount of fat. The other group of zebrafish was fed with <em>Artemia </em>sufficient to meet their energy requirements (5 mg dry weight/day/fish). Zebrafish were fed under these dietary protocols for 8 weeks. The zebrafish overfed with <em>Artemia </em>exhibited increased body mass index, which was calculated by dividing the body weight by the square of the body length, hypertriglyceridemia and hepatosteatosis, unlike the control zebrafish. Calorie restriction for 2 weeks was applied to zebrafish after the 8-week overfeeding period. The increased body weight and plasma triglyceride level were improved by calorie restriction. We also performed comparative transcriptome analysis of visceral adipose tissue from DIO zebrafish, DIO rats, DIO mice and obese humans. This analysis revealed that obese zebrafish and mammals share common pathophysiological pathways related to the coagulation cascade and lipid metabolism. Furthermore, several regulators were identified in zebrafish and mammals, including APOH, IL-6 and IL-1&#x003b2; in the coagulation cascade, and SREBF1, PPAR&#x003b1;/&#x003b3;, NR1H3 and LEP in lipid metabolism.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusion</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">We established a zebrafish model of DIO that shared common pathophysiological pathways with mammalian obesity. The DIO zebrafish can be used to identify putative pharmacological targets and to test novel drugs for the treatment of human obesity.</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec4" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec4title">Background</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first">According to the World Health Organization, an estimated 310 million people worldwide are obese [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798161">1</a>]. Such estimates are particularly alarming given the strong association between obesity and various adverse health consequences, including atherosclerosis, hypertension, type 2 diabetes and certain types of cancer [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798080">1</a>-<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798152">3</a>]. While the exact causes remain elusive, it is now accepted that obesity is a complex, multifactorial disease that develops from an interaction between the genotype and the environment [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798088">1</a>,<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798106">2</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798114">4</a>].</p><p id="__p5">A number of genes involved in monogenic, syndromic and polygenic obesity have been identified [<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798166">2</a>,<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798092">5</a>]. In addition to genetic predisposition, environmental and behavioral factors resulting in increased physical inactivity and calorie intake also contribute to the development of obesity [<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798063">6</a>]. Numerous studies in rodents have attempted to characterize the functions of obesity-related genes and whole-animal responses to high-calorie and high-fat diets [<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798130">7</a>]. Such studies have generally shown that the obesity phenotype can differ based on the functions of knockout genes, genetic background, and dietary protocols [<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798085">6</a>-<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798159">10</a>]. This suggests the importance of developing and analyzing genetic and diet-induced models of obesity.</p><p id="__p6">Although rodent models have greatly contributed to our understanding of human obesity [<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798129">11</a>], experiments using rodent models require considerable staff and infrastructural support, and are relatively expensive. Therefore, the development of simple and inexpensive animal models of obesity to complement the currently used rodent models has been anticipated. Recent studies on energy homeostasis in worms, fly and zebrafish have shown that these lower organisms can be used to unravel the metabolic processes underlying obesity [<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798140">12</a>,<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798142">13</a>].</p><p id="__p7">As vertebrates, zebrafish possess many structural similarities with humans that worms and flies do not [<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798170">13</a>] and have been used to model various human diseases [<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798111">14</a>-<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798081">19</a>]. For example, zebrafish digestive organs, adipose tissues (AT), and skeletal muscle are physically arranged in a manner similar to their human counterparts [<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798095">13</a>]. Neural and endocrine signals regulating food intake are also conserved in zebrafish, including agouti-related protein (AgRP) [<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798162">15</a>,<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798158">20</a>], leptin [<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798135">21</a>] and adiponectin [<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798165">22</a>]. Although zebrafish larvae have been used in genetic and chemical screening experiments to identify novel genes involved in the regulation of energy homeostasis and potential therapeutic targets to treat obesity [<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798155">13</a>,<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798082">23</a>], it remains unclear whether zebrafish can be used as a model for diet-induced obesity (DIO), similar to that observed in mammals.</p><p id="__p8" class="p p-last">We have developed a zebrafish model for DIO and validated the model by several methods, including biochemical and histological analyses, diet therapies and DNA microarray analysis of visceral AT with comparison to those of mammalian obesity. These studies revealed that DIO zebrafish and obese mammals share common pathophysiological pathways, suggesting that zebrafish can be used as an alternative model of DIO.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec5title">Results</h2><div id="__sec6" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Zebrafish overfed with <em>Artemia </em>become obese</h3><p id="__p9" class="p p-first">To develop a zebrafish model of DIO, we overfed the fish with freshly hatched nauplii of brine shrimp <em>Artemia</em>, a common food source for aquaculture [<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode">24</a>]. Given the hierarchy of macronutrient effects on the perception of hunger (i.e. fat is least satiating while protein is most satiating [<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798125">4</a>]), it is plausible that zebrafish would overfeed on <em>Artemia </em>and become obese. Indeed, increases in the amount of dietary fat are associated with the risk of developing obesity [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798107">1</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798128">25</a>].</p><p id="__p10">We first assessed the consumption of <em>Artemia </em>by zebrafish. It was previously reported that the energy requirement for a Leopard <em>Danio</em>, a spotted color morph of zebrafish, is under 30 cal [<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798109">26</a>]. Therefore, we fed zebrafish at 3.5 months post-fertilization (mpf) with 5 or 60 mg of freshly hatched <em>Artemia </em>to meet their energy requirements (control) or to overfeed (OF), respectively. Zebrafish fed 5 or 60 mg of <em>Artemia </em>per day consumed about 80 or 50% of the provided <em>Artemia</em>, respectively (Methods). Because 1 mg of <em>Artemia </em>contains approximately 5 calories, it was estimated that a zebrafish would obtain 20 calories from 5 mg of <em>Artemia </em>at 80% consumption, and 150 calories from 60 mg of <em>Artemia </em>at 50% consumption. Zebrafish were fed under these dietary protocols for 8 weeks.</p><p id="__p11" class="p">As shown in Figures <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">Figures1A</span></span><span>1A</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and1B,</span></span><span>1B</span></a>, significant (p &#x0003c; 0.05) increases in body mass index (BMI), which was calculated by dividing the body weight (g) by the square of the body length (cm), were evident in male (1.1-fold) and female (1.3-fold) zebrafish in the OF group compared with those in the control group within 1 week of the dietary protocol. This trend was maintained throughout the 8-week dietary protocol. As shown in Figures <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">Figures1C</span></span><span>1C</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and1D,</span></span><span>1D</span></a>, plasma TG levels were also significantly (p &#x0003c; 0.05) higher in male (2.4-fold) and female (1.8-fold) zebrafish in the OF group than in the control group at week 8, but not at week 1. Lipid-specific Oil Red O staining of frozen liver sections revealed abnormalities consistent with hepatosteatosis in both male (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure1G)</span></span><span>1G</span></a>) and female (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure1H)</span></span><span>1H</span></a>) zebrafish in the OF group at week 8, but not in the control group. These results indicate that zebrafish overfed with <em>Artemia </em>develop obesity in a manner similar to those observed in transgenic zebrafish overexpressing AgRP [<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798096">15</a>] and in mammalian models of DIO [<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798141">8</a>].</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139699266936144" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=2972245_1472-6793-10-21-1.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is 1472-6793-10-21-1.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/bin/1472-6793-10-21-1.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139699266936144" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Figure 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p12"><strong>Assessment of BMI, plasma TG and hepatic steatosis in zebrafish overfed with <em>Artemia</em></strong>. Gray denotes genes that were not spotted in the microarray.</p></div></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec9" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec9title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p30" class="p p-first">In this study, we developed a zebrafish model of DIO. DIO zebrafish exhibited increased BMI, hypertriglyceridemia and hepatosteatosis. Comparative transcriptome analysis using visceral AT revealed that the DIO zebrafish and obese mammals share common pathophysiological pathways. These findings suggest that the DIO zebrafish model can be used to identify putative pharmacological targets and to test novel drugs for the treatment of human obesity.</p><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><h3>Zebrafish can be used as a useful animal model of DIO</h3><p id="__p31" class="p p-first">The regulation of feeding behavior occurs via close interactions between peripheral regions and the brain [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798087">1</a>-<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798156">4</a>,<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798084">35</a>-<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798115">38</a>]. Peripheral endocrine and metabolic factors convey information regarding nutritional status to the brain. The peripheral signals include satiety signals, such as PYY [<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798105">39</a>], GLP-1 [<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798143">40</a>] and ghrelin [<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798150">41</a>], which originate from the gastrointestinal tract, as well as adiposity signals, such as adiponectin [<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798164">22</a>] and insulin [<a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798074">42</a>], the blood levels of which are proportional to body nutrient stores [<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798122">38</a>]. The brain then processes this peripheral information and induces neuropeptide signaling (for example, via NPY [<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798120">43</a>], &#x003b1;-MSH [<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798153">20</a>], AgRP [<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798078">20</a>] and orexin [<a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798110">44</a>]), mainly from the hypothalamus, to stimulate or inhibit feeding. These peripheral and central factors controlling food intake are well conserved in zebrafish [<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798091">20</a>,<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798097">22</a>,<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798102">39</a>-<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798147">45</a>].</p><p id="__p32">Probably because of the low satiating effect of fat consumption [<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798154">4</a>], high dietary fat intake is associated with an increased risk of obesity [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798070">1</a>,<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798157">25</a>]. Because of the relatively high fat content in <em>Artemia</em>, as compared with flake foods, we fed the zebrafish a diet consisting solely of <em>Artemia </em>to induce obesity. We have demonstrated that zebrafish overfed with <em>Artemia </em>showed significant increases in BMI and plasma TG levels and hepatosteatosis, consistent with obesity observed in humans and rodent models of DIO [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798099">1</a>,<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798098">6</a>,<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798066">7</a>]. Of note, there seemed to be no decrease in physical activity in the zebrafish overfed <em>Artemia </em>(data not shown), indicating that the development of obesity is most likely to be a result of the increased intake of a high-fat diet.</p><p id="__p33">There are several advantages to the DIO zebrafish model. First, zebrafish (<em>AB </em>line) respond well to the <em>Artemia </em>diet because almost all of the zebrafish overfed <em>Artemia </em>developed obesity. The C57BL/6J (B6) line has been widely used for DIO in mice because these mice are very susceptible to obesity when fed a high-fat diet [<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798139">6</a>-<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798118">9</a>]. However, there are variations in adiposity among individual B6 mice [<a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798132">46</a>,<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798094">47</a>]. Similarly, when outbred Sprague-Dawley rats are fed a high-fat diet, about half become obese, while the other half are resistant to DIO [<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798112">6</a>-<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798101">10</a>]. The relatively homogenous responses of zebrafish to overfeeding with <em>Artemia </em>suggest that these fish represent an excellent alternative model species for experimental research on DIO. Furthermore, the dietary protocol to induce obesity in zebrafish is simple and can be applied to other zebrafish lines. For example, we are currently applying the protocol to the <em>Casper </em>line, which have transparent abdomens, even in the adult stage [<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798124">48</a>]. Using DIO <em>Casper </em>zebrafish, visceral AT can be visualized in a living animal under a fluorescent microscope by staining the AT with a fluorescent dye, such as Nile Red [<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798069">49</a>]. This feature makes it possible to monitor the short- and long-term effects of a therapeutic intervention on the amount of visceral AT in live DIO zebrafish. Finally, zebrafish are small and easy to maintain in large stocks because of their high fecundity, thus making zebrafish amenable for medium-to-high throughput screening for early drug discovery [<a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798134">50</a>].</p><p id="__p34" class="p p-last">One limitation of the zebrafish DIO model is the apparent absence of brown AT [<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798100">51</a>]. The development of obesity in mammals not only depends on the balance between food intake and calorie utilization, but also on the balance between white AT and brown AT [<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798149">51</a>]. Therefore, zebrafish DIO may not be suitable to identify signaling pathways related to brown AT.</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Comparative transcriptomics of visceral AT revealed common pathophysiological pathways in zebrafish and mammalian obesity</h3><p id="__p35" class="p p-first">Genome-wide expression assays using DNA microarrays allow rapid screening and quantification of differences in large groups of functionally related genes and are thus well-suited to studies of pathways dysregulated in obesity [<a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798093">52</a>]. We compared the visceral AT expression profiles of zebrafish, rat, mouse and human obesity. The comparative transcriptome analysis revealed that several genes involved in blood coagulation, platelet activation, fatty acid metabolism, cholesterol efflux, and triglyceride metabolism were dysregulated in both zebrafish and mammalian obesity. IL-6, IL-1&#x003b2; and APOH were identified as regulatory factors involved in blood coagulation and platelet activation in zebrafish and mammalian obesity. Similarly, SREBP1, PPAR&#x003b1;/&#x003b3;, NR3H1 and LEP were identified as common regulatory factors for fatty acid metabolism, cholesterol efflux and triglyceride metabolism in zebrafish and mammalian obesity.</p><p id="__p36">Visceral AT dysfunction can play a causal role in the prothrombotic state observed in obesity by affecting hemostasis, coagulation and fibrinolysis [<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798104">53</a>-<a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798146">55</a>]. It has also been shown that IL-6 and IL-1&#x003b2;, secreted from visceral AT, induce the biosynthesis of fibrinogen from visceral AT and liver. Fibrinogen is a substrate of F2 (thrombin) in the final step of the coagulation cascade, and its presence is essential for platelet aggregation [<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798137">53</a>]. Of interest, plasma fibrinogen levels were found to be significantly higher in obese subjects than in age- and sex-matched non-obese individuals [<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798144">53</a>]. Significant correlations have been reported between fibrinogen and BMI and the waist-to-hip ratio [<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798136">53</a>]. It has also been reported that substantial weight loss reduces fibrinogen levels more effectively than modest weight reduction [<a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798073">53</a>]. Consistent with these reports, OF and CR significantly induced and reduced, respectively, the expression of fibrinogen in zebrafish (Figures <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -4em;">(Figures3A</span></span><span>3A</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and5A,</span></span><span>5A</span></a>, Additional files <a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">1</a> and <a href="#S2" rid="S2" class=" supplementary-material">2</a>: Supplemental Table S1 and S2). APOH binds to activated protein C (APC), an anticoagulant enzyme that is activated by activation of the protein C zymogen by the thrombin-thrombomodulin complex on the surface of endothelial cells, platelets and monocytes [<a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798090">56</a>]. However, the effect of APOH binding to APC is inconclusive and the functional role of APOH in obesity remains to be elucidated.</p><p id="__p37" class="p p-last">Dyslipidemia is commonly seen in obesity [<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798126">57</a>,<a href="#B58" rid="B58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798123">58</a>] and is characterized by an increased flux of free fatty acids, elevated TG levels, low high-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels and increased low-density lipoprotein levels [<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798062">57</a>]. Fatty acid metabolism, cholesterol efflux and triglyceride metabolism are closely related to these pathways. This study revealed that SREBP1, PPAR&#x003b1;/&#x003b3;, NR3H1 and LEP are key regulatory factors in these pathways and are expressed in zebrafish and mammalian obesity. PPARs mediate adaptive metabolic responses to increased systemic lipid availability and are activated by endogenous or dietary lipids [<a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798065">59</a>]. PPAR&#x003b1; promotes lipid clearance by increasing tissue fat oxidization [<a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798077">59</a>,<a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798168">60</a>] while PPAR&#x003b3; promotes lipid storage in white AT, as well as preadipocyte differentiation to mature adipocytes [<a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798083">59</a>]. SREBP1 is an important transcription factor that regulates the transcription of many lipid genes and participates in adipocyte differentiation by stimulating PPAR&#x003b3; [<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798169">61</a>,<a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798116">62</a>]. NR3H1, also known as liver X receptor A (LXRA), has been shown to regulate lipid and carbohydrate homeostasis [<a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798064">63</a>]. LEP, an adiposity hormone produced by white AT, reflects total fat mass [<a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798127">64</a>]. Although we did not detect a statistically significant difference between OF8W and OF1W in terms of the mRNA expression level of <em>lep </em>measured by qPCR (data not shown), the expression of <em>apoa1</em>, a transcriptional target gene of leptin [<a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798131">65</a>], was significantly induced and reduced by OF and CR, respectively (Figures <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -4em;">(Figures4A</span></span><span>4A</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and5B)</span></span><span>5B</span></a>) [Additional files <a href="#S1" rid="S1" class=" supplementary-material">1</a>, <a href="#S2" rid="S2" class=" supplementary-material">2</a> and <a href="#S7" rid="S7" class=" supplementary-material">7</a>: Supplemental Tables S1 and S2, and Figure S1], suggesting that leptin protein levels were likely to be increased in zebrafish DIO. It is noteworthy that the functional importance of SREBP1, PPAR&#x003b1;/&#x003b3;, NR3H1 and LEP in obesity has been shown in many genetic studies [<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798138">2</a>,<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798075">11</a>,<a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798121">63</a>,<a href="#B66" rid="B66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798068">66</a>].</p></div></div><div id="__sec12" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec12title">Conclusions</h2><p id="__p38" class="p p-first-last">Here, we have shown that the DIO zebrafish model shares common pathophysiological pathways with mammalian obesity and can be used to identify putative pharmacological targets of human obesity. For example, we are currently performing genome-wide expression profiling of the DIO zebrafish liver. The zebrafish model can also be used to study the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, the main link between the central nervous and the endocrine system [<a href="#B67" rid="B67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798113">67</a>]. Further studies are needed to examine whether complications related to obesity such as insulin resistance, cardiovascular diseases and cancer can be studied in DIO zebrafish.</p></div><div id="__sec13" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec13title">Methods</h2><div id="__sec14" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Feeding zebrafish</h3><p id="__p39" class="p p-first">Adult zebrafish (<em>AB </em>line, ZIRC, Eugene, OR, USA) were kept at 28&#x000b0;C under a 14-h light:10-h dark cycle, and water conditions of environmental quality were maintained according to an established protocol [<a href="#B68" rid="B68" class=" bibr popnode">68</a>]. Zebrafish at 3.5 mpf were assigned into two dietary groups (overfeeding and maintenance groups) with approximately five fish per 2-L tank. Zebrafish in the overfeeding group were fed three times per day with freshly hatched <em>Artemia </em>(corresponding to 60 mg cysts/fish/day). Zebrafish in the maintenance group were fed freshly hatched <em>Artemia </em>(corresponding to 5 mg cysts/fish/day) once per day. For calorie restriction, the zebrafish were fed freshly hatched <em>Artemia </em>(corresponding to 2.5 mg cysts/fish/day) for 2 weeks after being overfed for 8 weeks. Commercial flake food (Hikari Tropical Fancy Guppy, Kyorin, Hyogo, Japan) consists of 9% fat, 17% carbohydrate and 59% protein, whereas <em>Artemia </em>nauplii consist of 22% fat, 16% carbohydrate and 44% protein (dry weight basis).</p><p id="__p40" class="p p-last">The percentage of consumed <em>Artemia </em>was estimated by counting <em>Artemia </em>before and after feeding. Briefly, 1 mL of water was collected from 1700 mL of water containing freshly hatched <em>Artemia </em>corresponding to 100 or 8.5 mg cysts. The numbers of hatched <em>Artemia </em>were counted three times to determine a mean count. After counting, the samples were returned to the 2-L tank. Then, five fish were transferred to the tank for feeding. After 2 hours, the number of <em>Artemia </em>was counted as before feeding. Zebrafish fed 5 or 60 mg of <em>Artemia </em>per day consumed about 80 or 50% of the provided <em>Artemia</em>, respectively. Zebrafish fed 2.5 mg of <em>Artemia </em>per day consumed almost all of the provided <em>Artemia</em>.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="sec"><h3>Measurement of BMI and plasma TG</h3><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">The body weight and length of zebrafish were measured weekly throughout the study. Zebrafish length was measured from the head to the end of the body. Blood was withdrawn from the dorsal artery of the zebrafish at the indicated times after an overnight fast to measure plasma TG (TG L-type assay, Wako, Tokyo, Japan).</p></div><div id="__sec16" class="sec"><h3>Oil Red O staining</h3><p id="__p42" class="p p-first-last">Zebrafish were fixed using 4% formaldehyde solution in PBS. The fixed samples were rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen-cooled isopentane, embedded in Tissue-Tek (Sakura Finetek Europe, Zoeterwoude, Netherlands) and cut using a cryostat. After drying, the sections were fixed in 4% formalin and rinsed with distilled water. The sections were then immersed in a working solution of Oil Red O (Wako) for 15 min and rinsed with distilled water. Sections were also counterstained using Mayer's hematoxylin to visualize the nuclei.</p></div><div id="__sec17" class="sec"><h3>Nile Red staining</h3><p id="__p43" class="p p-first-last">A stock solution of Nile Red (Wako) was prepared by dissolving 10 mg of Nile Red in 10 mL acetone. Zebrafish were transferred into 1000 mL of swimming water containing 100 &#x003bc;g of Nile Red and incubated overnight in the dark at 28&#x000b0;C. After incubation, the fish were rinsed in fresh water and observed with a fluorescence microscope (MZ 16F, Leica, Tokyo, Japan) using a GFP2 filter (Leica).</p></div><div id="__sec18" class="sec"><h3>DNA microarray analysis of visceral AT of DIO zebrafish</h3><p id="__p44" class="p p-first-last">The visceral (omental) AT of male DIO zebrafish stained with Nile Red was collected by surgical extraction under a fluorescence microscope. The AT was stored in RNA-later (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). Total RNA was then extracted using an RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, USA), qualified by an Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) and quantified using a spectrophotometer (NanoDrop ND-100, Wilmington, DE, USA). Three hundred nanograms of total RNA from each visceral AT depot were converted into labeled cRNA using the Low RNA Input Fluorescent Linear Amplification Kit (Agilent). Cy3-labeled cRNA (1.5 &#x003bc;g) was hybridized to Agilent Zebrafish Whole Genome Oligo Microarrays (G2518A) according to the manufacturer's protocol. The microarray has 22,000 different probes. Each probe is spotted twice distantly in a microarray. The hybridized microarrays were scanned (Agilent G2565BA) and analyzed using Feature Extraction software (Agilent). The data were normalized according to the manufacturer's protocol (Agilent). RankProd analysis [<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798117">28</a>] was performed using Bioconductor [<a href="#B69" rid="B69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798072">69</a>] to identify differentially expressed genes between two groups by calculating the FDR. Differentially expressed genes (FDR &#x0003c;15% for both duplicated probes) were then converted to human orthologs using the Life Science Knowledge Bank (World Fusion, Tokyo, Japan). The gene symbols of human orthologs were used for functional analysis.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><h3>DNA microarray analysis of visceral AT in mammalian obesity</h3><p id="__p45" class="p p-first">The microarray data ([GEO:GDS8700] [<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798079">30</a>], [GEO:<a id="__tag_200798183" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE11790" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE11790"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE11790","term_id":"11790"}}</span>GSE11790</a>] [<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798167">31</a>] and [GEO;<a id="__tag_200798171" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15524" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15524"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE15524","term_id":"15524"}}</span>GSE15524</a>] [<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798086">32</a>]) were downloaded from Gene Expression Omnibus [<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798089">29</a>]. We used microarray data that were filtered and normalized and applied the data to RankProd analysis [<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_200798160">28</a>] to identify differentially expressed genes between obese and control mammals. The conversion to human orthologs and functional analysis were performed as described above for visceral AT from DIO zebrafish. The microarray data of zebrafish DIO have been deposited in NCBI GEO and can be downloaded from GEO main page (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2972245&amp;issue-id=185097&amp;journal-id=58&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>) with accession number <a id="__tag_200798186" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE18566" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE18566"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE18566","term_id":"18566"}}</span>GSE18566</a>. GSEA and SNEA were performed using Pathway Studio 7 (Ariadne Genomics, Rockville, MD, USA). Pathway Studio is a program used to visualize and analyze biological pathways and gene regulation networks. This program includes the ResNet database of more than 500,000 functional relationships and the MedScan tool for automatic extraction of information from the scientific literature. Pathway Studio 7 draws networks based on the extracted information and was used to draw Figures <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">Figures3,</span></span><span>3</span></a>, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -0.5em;">,4</span></span><span>4</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2972245/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and5</span></span><span>5</span></a>.</p><p id="__p46" class="p p-last">GSEA was used to determine the list of biological pathways dysregulated in obesity using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov enrichment algorithm with 400 random permutations to determine statistical significance. SNEA was used to identify molecules regulating expression of genes in the coagulation cascade (blood coagulation and platelet activation) and in lipid metabolism (fatty acid metabolism, cholesterol efflux and triglyceride metabolism), both of which were identified by GSEA to be dysregulated in zebrafish and mammalian obesity. SNEA built sub-networks of genes related to the coagulation cascade or the lipid metabolism and identified the central "seed" of each network prioritized by the p-value based on the criterion "Expression Targets" in the ResNet database.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec"><h3>qPCR analysis</h3><p id="__p47" class="p p-first-last">Total RNAs from zebrafish visceral AT were used to generate cDNAs using an iScript Select cDNA Synthesis Kit (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). qPCR was done using an ABI Prism 7300 (Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA, USA) with SYBR Green Realtime PCR Master Mix Plus (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan). The thermal cycling condition comprised an initial step at 95&#x000b0;C for 1 min followed by 40 cycles of 95&#x000b0;C for 15 sec, 60&#x000b0;C for 15 sec and 72&#x000b0;C for 45 sec. The primers used in this study are shown in [Additional file <a href="#S8" rid="S8" class=" supplementary-material">8</a>: Supplementary Table S7]. Data were normalized by the quantity of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (<em>gapdh</em>). This allowed us to account for any variability in the initial template concentration as well as the conversion efficiency of the reverse transcription reaction.</p></div><div id="__sec21" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Statistical Analysis</h3><p id="__p48" class="p p-first-last">Results are expressed as means &#x000b1; SEM. Differences between groups were tested for statistical significance using Student's t-test.</p></div></div><div id="__sec22" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec22title">Authors' contributions</h2><p id="__p49" class="p p-first-last">TO carried out the experiments and helped to draft the manuscript.
