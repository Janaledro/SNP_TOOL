Here, using an enriched environment (EE)-induced weight loss model, we show that maternal weight loss improves general health and reprograms metabolic gene expression in mouse offspring, and the epigenetic alterations can be inherited for at least two generations. EE in mothers induced weight loss and its associated physiological and metabolic changes such as decreased adiposity and improved glucose tolerance and insulin sensitivity. Relative to controls, their offspring exhibited improved general health such as reduced fat accumulation, decreased plasma and hepatic lipid levels, and improved glucose tolerance and insulin sensitivity. Maternal weight loss altered gene expression patterns in the liver of offspring with coherent down-regulation of genes involved in lipid and cholesterol biosynthesis. Epigenomic profiling of offspring livers revealed numerous changes in cytosine methylation depending on maternal weight loss, including reproducible changes in promoter methylation over several key lipid biosynthesis genes, correlated with their expression patterns. Embryo transfer studies indicated that oocyte alteration in response to maternal metabolic conditions is a strong factor in determining metabolic and epigenetic changes in offspring. Several important lipid metabolism-related genes have been identified to partially inherit methylated alleles from oocytes. Our study reveals a molecular and mechanistic basis of how maternal lifestyle modification affects metabolic changes in the offspring.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">DNA Demethylation, DNA Methylation, Embryo, Epigenetics, Fertilization, Obesity, Oocyte, Reprogramming, Epigenetic Inheritance, Weight Loss</span></div></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p6" class="p p-first">Obesity and related metabolic syndromes are globally prevalent and rapidly becoming major global health, social, and economic problems (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016711">1</a>, <a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016726">2</a>). They contribute to the early emergence of type 2 diabetes and its epidemic spreading (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016747">3</a>). Having either parent with obesity is an independent risk factor for obesity in children (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016745">4</a>). Although the impact of diet-induced paternal obesity on metabolism and adiposity has been demonstrated, increasing evidence indicates a significant role of maternally derived factors in obesity and metabolic programming of the offspring (<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016703">5</a>). Human obesity appears to be mostly related to complex interactions between genetic backgrounds and environmental factors (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016740">6</a>). Although some alleles associated with obesity have been identified and can be inherited from parents (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016713">7</a>), maternal environmental exposures also play an important role in affecting the offspring phenotypes (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016734">8</a>, <a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016724">9</a>) with the potential to contribute to the rapid increase in obesity. In humans, maternal obesity during pregnancy can lead to enhanced birth weight; increased circulating insulin, glucose, and triglycerides; and glucose intolerance as well as obesity in offspring (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016772">10</a>, <a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016773">11</a>). In rats, maternal obesity leads to early onset obesity, altered glucose metabolism, and insulin sensitivity (<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016702">12</a>, <a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016728">13</a>). In mice, offspring of obese mothers are heavier, diabetic and insulin-resistant, and produce second generation offspring with glucose intolerance and insulin resistance (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016722">14</a>, <a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016706">15</a>). Therefore, maternal obesity may play an important role not only in programming obesity in offspring but also in the intergenerational transmission and amplification of the obesity epidemic. Collectively, these facts indicate that mothers can initiate intergenerational transmission of obesity and increase the risk for metabolic diseases in offspring.</p><p id="__p7">To overcome the increased prevalence of obesity and its complications, weight loss is becoming more and more popular in modern society, particularly in young women. Although it is appealing and has health benefits for an individual (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016749">4</a>, <a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016743">16</a>), how and to what extent it affects the offspring is still poorly understood. We reasoned that if maternal obesity contributes to obesity and its related metabolic disorders in offspring then maternal weight loss could improve the general health and metabolic parameters in the offspring, and if it does, then the mechanisms that mediate these effects can be characterized. In humans, the increased prevalence of obesity is closely correlated with the lifestyle changes in modern society such as the increase in sedentary behavior and office work. Correspondingly, outdoor exercise and playing physical games with friends are recognized as effective means to increase energy expenditure leading to weight loss. Mice living in a standard laboratory environment provide an ideal non-genetic obese model system (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016725">17</a>, <a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016755">18</a>). They live in a small cage and have sufficient food but little exercise, therefore optimally mimicking the modern lifestyle of humans such as sedentary behavior and office work (<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016716">19</a>). Mice living in an enriched environment can be considered as a suitable weight loss model (<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016723">20</a>, <a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016727">21</a>). They live in a larger space, allowing more physical activity and social stimulation. This condition mimics weight loss behaviors in humans (such as outdoor exercise and playing physical games with friends). Importantly, these animals exhibit a significantly leaner phenotype than those living in standard laboratory conditions although they are fed <em>ad libitum</em> on identical diets (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016771">17</a>). We therefore asked whether simply placing female mice in an enriched environment may induce effects that are sufficient to significantly improve the metabolic health in the offspring.</p><p id="__p8" class="p">In this study, we describe a physiological and genomic screen for transgenerational effects of maternal weight loss on metabolism and gene expression in offspring of mice (see <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1</span></a> for a schematic). EE<sup><a href="#FN3" rid="FN3" class=" fn">2</a></sup> in mothers induced improved general health such as reduced fat accumulation, enhanced metabolic parameters, and improved glucose tolerance and insulin sensitivity in offspring. The expression of thousands of genes was altered in the liver of offspring from weight loss mothers with coherent down-regulation of genes involved in lipid and cholesterol biosynthesis. Epigenomic profiling in offspring livers revealed numerous changes in cytosine methylation depending on maternal weight loss, including promoter hypermethylation of several important genes involved in lipid and cholesterol biosynthesis, correlated with their expression patterns. Embryo transplantation studies revealed the important role of oocyte alteration in determining the metabolic and molecular changes in offspring. Several genes whose methylation status was significantly altered by maternal weight loss have been identified to partially resist global demethylation postfertilization and to some extent inherit methylated alleles from oocytes. Interestingly, maternal weight loss also altered the overall transcriptome patterns in oocytes with reproducible changes of several genes involved in lipid metabolism and chromatin modification. These facts indicate that the oocyte may be a key nexus that integrates maternal metabolic information to control offspring phenotypes. Overall, this study provides a molecular basis of how a maternal lifestyle change affects the offspring and may have implications explaining the epidemiology of several major human diseases.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702598128768" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580001.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580001.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580001.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702598128768" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">FIGURE 1.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p9"><strong>Experimental schematic.</strong> Control or EE-induced weight loss females were mated with males. All animal care and use procedures were in accordance with guidelines and approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the Institute of Zoology, Chinese Academy of Sciences. Maternal weight loss was performed in EE cages (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a><em>A</em>). Female F<sub>0</sub> founders were first raised in a standard laboratory environment from weaning until 12 weeks of age at which point they were randomly placed into EE cages or maintained in normal cages for 4 weeks. For EE groups, mice were placed into large cages (four to six mice per cage) of 310 &#x000d7; 450 &#x000d7; 490 mm supplemented with running wheels, toys, stairs, huts, and a seesaw in addition to standard lab chow and water. All of the EE devices and plastic toys were strictly selected to be free of bisphenol A or other toxic chemicals. At 16 weeks, female F<sub>0</sub> founders of EE or control were mated with males in standard cages under standard conditions (note that EE mothers were no longer returned to EE cages after mating). Offspring were delivered and raised in standard cages under standard conditions until 16 weeks of age.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702597793808" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580002.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580002.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580002.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702597793808" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F2"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2">FIGURE 2.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p11"><strong>EE leads to physiological and metabolic changes in mothers.</strong>

Each EE had its age-matched unique control. For standard EE, the control mice were aged to 16 weeks, whereas for short term EE, the control mice were aged to 14 weeks.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Animal Care </h5><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">Female F<sub>0</sub> founders were raised on standard diet in a normal cage until 12 weeks of age at which point they were placed into the EE cage or still maintained in the normal cage, chosen at random, for 4 weeks. Males were always raised on standard diet in the standard cage. At 16 weeks, female F<sub>0</sub> founders were mated with males in standard cages under controlled temperature (22 &#x000b1; 2 &#x000b0;C) and lighting (12:12-h light/dark cycle) and had free access to sterile water and pellet food <em>ad libitum</em>. Control and weight loss mating cages were always interspersed with one another. After 1 or 2 days, males were removed, and pregnant females were left alone with standard diet in the standard cage until their litters were 3 weeks of age. Note that a single male was mated with a single female for only one time and thus produced only one litter. Only litter sizes between 8 and 12 were included, and litters were standardized to 10 pups at day 1 within mother groups to avoid a nutrition imbalance. Offspring were always housed in standard cages (four to six mice per cage). Note that we always used virgin males to avoid confounding effects brought about by the males (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016750">22</a>, <a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016756">23</a>). Moreover, males mated with two female groups did not differ in phenotypic data (body weight, adiposity, and fasting blood glucose and insulin levels). At 3 weeks of age, a portion of the offspring were sacrificed, and the median lobe of liver was rapidly dissected out and flash frozen in liquid N<sub>2</sub>, each from an independent mother. Animals were sacrificed between 8 and 9 a.m. to avoid any mismatches in circadian cycle. Phenotype data from one offspring per mother, chosen at random, were generated at 16 weeks of age. Serum biomarkers, including blood glucose (Roche Diagnostics Accu-Chek), plasma insulin (ALPCO Diagnostics), leptin (ALPCO Diagnostics), triglyceride (Wako Diagnostics), total cholesterol (Wako Diagnostics), and free fatty acid (Wako Diagnostics) were determined after overnight fasting. Liver triglyceride and cholesterol were extracted in chloroform/methanol (2:1, v/v) (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016751">24</a>) and measured as above. Body weight and food intake were monitored weekly, the latter by collecting and weighing food remaining after 24 h. Food intake was averaged across animals housed with four to six per cage to reduce stress. Diets were obtained from Beijing Vital River Co., and detailed compositions are listed in <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S1</a>. For hormonal and metabolic parameters, litters ranging from five to 20 were included depending on the different tests, and one offspring per sex per litter was used for each test. For all comparisons shown, <em>n</em> represents the number of animals used in each test. For simplicity, body weight, weight of white adipose tissue (WAT), and serum biomarkers are displayed as percentage of control in figures. However, detailed data are also available in <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Glucose Tolerance Test (GTT) and Insulin Tolerance Test (ITT) </h5><p id="__p14" class="p p-first-last">For GTT, mice were injected intraperitoneally with glucose (2 g kg<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>) after overnight fasting. Blood glucose was measured at the indicated time points. For ITT, we injected insulin (1 unit kg<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>) intraperitoneally into mice at 2 h of fasting. We collected blood and determined glucose using a glucometer (Roche Diagnostics Accu-Chek). Serum insulin levels were measured using an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay kit (ultrasensitive mouse insulin ELISA kit, ALPCO Diagnostics). Blood glucose or insulin levels were measured before injection (time 0) and at 15, 30, 60, and 120 min after injection. Single time measurements were analyzed by analysis of variance or, if appropriate, by two-tailed Student's <em>t</em> test, and time courses were analyzed by repeated measures analysis of variance.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Microarray Expression Profiling </h5><p id="__p15" class="p p-first-last">Total RNA was extracted from the median lobe of liver using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen). Samples from five control and four weight loss offspring, each from an independent mother, were chosen for microarray analysis using NimbleGen Mouse Gene Expression 12 &#x000d7; 135K Array (Roche NimbleGen) according to the manufacturer's instructions. A series of software (NimbleScan software and Agilent GeneSpring GX software) was used for data analysis. After adjustment and normalization by the robust multichip analysis method, the data were analyzed by Significance Analysis of Microarrays (SAM) software (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016712">25</a>). All array data and additional details on samples and analysis are available in the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) under accession number <a id="__tag_428016782" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE40479","term_id":"40479","extlink":"1"}}</span>GSE40479</a>. Differentially expressed genes were determined based on a -fold change &#x0003e;2 at a false discovery rate correction of 5%. Differentially expressed genes were functionally annotated according to gene ontology terms or Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathways using DAVID (Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery). Additionally, differentially expressed genes were hierarchically clustered using Cluster 3.0.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Quantitative Real Time PCR (qRT-PCR) </h5><p id="__p16" class="p p-first-last">We analyzed mRNA levels by qRT-RCR after reverse transcription as described before (<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016762">26</a>). For liver tissues, total RNA was extracted from the median lobe of liver using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) and quantified by absorbance at 260 and 280 nm in a PerkinElmer Life Sciences spectrophotometer. It was then used as a template for complementary DNA (cDNA) synthesis by SuperScript III first strand synthesis (Invitrogen) with random hexamers. For oocytes, amplification of small amounts of the cDNA of the cells was based on a previous protocol (<a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016717">27</a>). mRNA quantity was determined with the Roche LightCycler 480 II quantitative RT-PCR system (Roche Applied Science) using primer sequences summarized in <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S1</a> and SYBR Green SuperMix-UDG (Invitrogen). PCR was performed in a final volume of 25 &#x003bc;l consisting of diluted cDNA sample, 1&#x000d7; SYBR Green Mix (Invitrogen), primers optimized for each target gene, and nuclease-free water. The PCR condition was 40 cycles at 95 &#x000b0;C for 30 s, 55 &#x000b0;C for 1 min, and 72 &#x000b0;C for 30 s. Gene transcript levels were normalized to the housekeeping gene &#x003b2;-actin. The results were expressed as 2<sup>&#x02212;(target gene number of cycles &#x02212; &#x003b2;-actin number of cycles)</sup>. Note that for <em>Lpin1</em>, the &#x003b2; isoform (transcript variant 2, GenBank<sup>TM</sup> accession number <a id="__tag_428016788" class="tag_hotlink" href="/nuccore/NM_015763.4" ref="/nuccore/NM_015763.4"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"NM_015763.4","term_id":"194353982","term_text":"NM_015763.4"}}</span>NM_015763.4</a>) was used for analysis.</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">DNA Methylation Profiling </h5><p id="__p17" class="p p-first-last">The same animals used in microarray analysis were used for the methylated DNA immunoprecipitation (MeDIP) study. We used the NimbleGen MM8 385K Promoter Plus CpG Island Microarray (Roche NimbleGen) to perform methylation analysis. The array contains 15,963 annotated CpG islands and covers all known Reference Sequence gene promoter regions (from &#x02212;1300 to +500 bp relative to the transcription start site). The MeDIP method was adapted from a previous study (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016758">28</a>). 4 &#x003bc;g of purified genomic DNA was fragmented to a size of 100&#x02013;500 bp using a Covaris machine. After denaturation, the DNA was immunoprecipitated with 10 &#x003bc;g of monoclonal antibody against 5-methylcytidine (Diagenode) in 300 &#x003bc;l of IP buffer (10 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> sodium phosphate at pH 7.0, 140 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> NaCl, 0.05% Triton X-100) for 5 h at 4 &#x000b0;C and then washed three times with 800 &#x003bc;l of IP buffer. Immunoprecipitated DNA was recovered with proteinase K digestion followed by column-based purification (DNA Wizard, Promega). The purified immunoprecipitated DNA was used for microarray hybridization. For methylation analysis of the microarray, we adopted a method from a previous study (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016760">28</a>). We defined promoters that gain methylation in EE groups as follows: log<sub>2</sub> ratio in control group &#x0003c;0.4, &#x00394;log<sub>2</sub> ratio (EE <em>versus</em> control) &#x0003e;0.25, and <em>p</em> &#x0003c; 0.05. All MeDIP-chip data and additional details on samples and analysis are available in the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) under accession number <a id="__tag_428016781" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE40479","term_id":"40479","extlink":"1"}}</span>GSE40479</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Bisulfite Sequencing </h5><p id="__p18" class="p p-first-last">Bisulfite genomic sequencing was performed as described previously (<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016768">29</a>). Bisulfite modification was accomplished using the EZ DNA Methylation kit (Zymo Research). The converted DNA was then amplified by PCR with primers summarized in <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S1</a>. The obtained PCR products were purified using the MinElute Gel Extraction kit (Invitrogen) and cloned into the pMD18-T Vector (Takara). Individual clones were grown, and the plasmids were purified using the PureLink Miniprep kit (Invitrogen). The positive clones were confirmed by PCR, and no fewer than 10 clones randomly selected for each subject were sequenced using an automatic sequencer (ABI PRISM-77). Sequencing results were analyzed using MethTools 2.0. The regions that were examined by bisulfite sequencing are as follows: <em>Hmgcr</em>, Chromosome 13: 97441152&#x02013;97441498; <em>Lss</em>, Chromosome 10: 75994273&#x02013;75994536; <em>Sqle</em>, Chromosome 15: 59146023&#x02013;59146335; and <em>Lpin1</em>, Chromosome 12: 16596388&#x02013;16596703.</p></div><div id="__sec13" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">mRNA-Seq </h5><p id="__p19" class="p p-first-last">For mRNA-Seq, pooled mRNA from three animals (equally from each animal) for each group was used for analysis, and the average expression level for each group was evaluated. Briefly, naturally ovulated mature oocytes (metaphase of the second meiosis (MII stage)) were collected from three control and three EE female F<sub>0</sub> founders (three oocytes per mouse, nine oocytes for each group). Cumulus cells were removed from oocytes by treatment with hyaluronidase. Amplification of small amounts of the cDNA of the cells was based on a previous protocol (<a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016754">27</a>). The cDNAs were evenly amplified by PCR for 20 cycles. Libraries were purified and sequenced on an Applied Biosystems SOLiD sequencing system. Sequencing data were mapped to the mouse genome to extract the whole transcriptome information of oocytes. Mapped read data were analyzed by the DEGseq package as described previously (<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016720">30</a>). The detected genes with corresponding read counts greater than 200 were chosen for further analysis. The -fold change selection method was used for differentially expressed gene selection. Genes with log<sub>2</sub> values greater than 0.584 or less than &#x02212;0.584 (-fold change &#x0003e;1.5) were considered as up-regulated or down-regulated, respectively.</p></div><div id="__sec14" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Embryo Transfer </h5><p id="__p20" class="p p-first-last">The estrous female F<sub>0</sub> founders were mated with males. Successful mating was determined by the presence of the vaginal plug, and the morning of the vaginal plug presence was designated day 0.5. One-cell stage embryos at day 0.5 were obtained from control or EE females and then transferred into oviducts of day 0.5 pseudopregnant females, respectively. Pregnant females were left alone in standard conditions until their litters were 3 weeks of age. Only mothers with litter sizes of 8&#x02013;12 were included, and litters were standardized to 10 pups at day 1 within mother groups to avoid a nutrition imbalance. One offspring per mother chosen randomly was used for metabolic and molecular studies as described for naturally fertilized animals. For blastocysts used for bisulfite sequencing, embryonic day (E) 3.5 blastocysts were collected at day 3.5 by flushing the oviducts with M2 medium. Each bisulfite treatment was performed on 18 pooled blastocysts derived from three animals (six blastocysts randomly selected from each animal) for each group.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Oocyte in Vitro Maturation </h5><p id="__p21" class="p p-first-last">Ovaries were isolated from 3-month-old female mice 46&#x02013;48 h after intraperitoneal injection of 10 international units (IU) of pregnant mare's serum gonadotrophin. Ovaries were then placed in a Petri dish with prewarmed M2 medium supplemented with 2.5 &#x003bc;<span class="small-caps">m</span> milrinone to prevent oocytes from undergoing germinal vesicle breakdown. Cumulus oocyte complexes were recovered from ovaries by repeatedly puncturing antral follicles with a fine steel needle under the visual field of a dissecting microscope. After washing three times in M2 medium, the cumulus oocyte complexes were cultured for maturation in M16-based medium (containing 10% FBS) supplemented with or without glucose (5 m<span class="small-caps">m</span>), insulin (5 &#x003bc;g/ml), or leptin (10 ng/ml), respectively. All oocyte culture experiments were performed under mineral oil at 37 &#x000b0;C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO<sub>2</sub> in air for 16 h. Cumulus cells were removed from oocytes by treatment with hyaluronidase, and the oocytes with a first polar body were used for further studies.</p></div><div id="__sec16" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Statistical Analyses </h5><p id="__p22" class="p p-first-last">Measurements at single time points were analyzed by analysis of variance or, if appropriate, by two-tailed Student's <em>t</em> test. Time courses were analyzed by repeated measurements analysis of variance. All data are shown as mean &#x000b1; S.E. <em>p</em> &#x0003c; 0.05 was considered statistically significant.</p></div><div id="__sec17" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Accession Numbers </h5><p id="__p23" class="p p-first-last">All microarray data and deep sequencing data used in this study have been deposited in the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus under accession number <a id="__tag_428016787" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40479"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE40479","term_id":"40479","extlink":"1"}}</span>GSE40479</a>.</p></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec18" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec18title">RESULTS</h2><div id="__sec19" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec20" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec21" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h5 class="inline">EE-induced Maternal Weight Loss Leads to Physiological and Metabolic Changes in Offspring </h5><p id="__p24" class="p p-first">Female F<sub>0</sub> founders were first raised in a standard laboratory environment from weaning until 12 weeks of age at which point they were randomly placed into EE cages or maintained in normal cages for 4 weeks. Both control and EE mice had free access to normal diet and water. After 4 weeks of EE, female F<sub>0</sub> founders displayed a &#x0223c;17% decrease in body weight compared with controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a>, <em>A&#x02013;D</em>, and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). Accordingly, female EE F<sub>0</sub> founders exhibited decreased WAT mass and plasma glucose, insulin, leptin, cholesterol, and triglyceride levels (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a>, <em>D</em> and <em>E</em>, and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). No changes were observed in energy intake between EE mice and controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a><em>F</em>), indicating that the reduced fat seen in EE mice was due to the increased energy expenditure. Moreover, upon GTTs and ITTs, EE mice showed improved glucose tolerance as well as enhanced insulin sensitivity compared with controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a>, <em>G&#x02013;I</em>).</p><p id="__p25" class="p">To determine the potential effects of maternal weight loss on offspring, we mated female F<sub>0</sub> founders of EE or control mice at 16 weeks of age with males in a standard laboratory environment (EE mothers were no longer returned to EE cages after mating) and examined physiological and metabolic changes in their offspring at 16 weeks of age. Maternal EE did not alter litter size or sex ratios. As several studies identified distinct phenotypes between sexes in offspring (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016757">15</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016721">31</a>), we first assessed female offspring after weaning onto a normal diet in a standard laboratory environment for up to 16 weeks of age. We found that female offspring of EE mothers exhibited a leaner phenotype accompanied by reduced weight gain and a marked reduction in WAT mass (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a>, <em>A</em>, <em>B</em>, and <em>D</em>, and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). Furthermore, these mice had less food intake compared with controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a><em>E</em>). Maternal EE led to a sharp decrease in leptin abundance corresponding to the reduced adipose tissue mass seen in these animals (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a><em>C</em>). Accordingly, serum concentrations of glucose, insulin, cholesterol, triglyceride, and free fatty acid were all significantly decreased (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a><em>C</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). GTT and ITT revealed significant augmentation in glucose metabolism in female offspring (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a>, <em>F&#x02013;H</em>). In addition, the homeostasis model assessment of insulin resistance index was significantly decreased in female offspring (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a><em>I</em>). Furthermore, regression analysis showed that the body mass index (BMI) and WAT weight in offspring were significantly positively correlated with the BMI in mothers (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3</span></a>, <em>J</em> and <em>K</em>). We then examined the physiological and metabolic changes in male offspring and found that maternal EE led to changes in male offspring (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4</span></a>) similar to those observed in females, indicating that maternal EE contributed equally to both sexes of offspring. These results suggest that maternal weight loss plays a potential antiobesity role in offspring.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702635687632" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580003.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580003.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580003.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702635687632" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F3"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3">FIGURE 3.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p26"><strong>Maternal weight loss leads to physiological and metabolic changes in female offspring.</strong>

Overall, these molecular findings are consistent with physiological and metabolic changes.</p></div><div id="__sec23" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Maternal Weight Loss Alters the Methylation Patterns of Lipid Metabolic Genes in Offspring </h5><p id="__p32" class="p p-first">Cytosine methylation is a widespread DNA modification that is critical for the regulation of gene expression and carries heritable information between generations (<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016710">35</a>,<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016705">&#x02013;</a><a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016770">37</a>). We then turned to genome-wide studies to search for differentially methylated loci between control and weight loss offspring. We performed MeDIP-chip to characterize cytosine methylation across all known Reference Sequence gene promoters and 15,963 annotated CpG islands. The same animals used in the microarray analysis were used for the MeDIP-chip study. Maternal weight loss altered the methylation of 2,218 promoters and 1,438 CpG islands (<a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S5</a>) in the liver of offspring. Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathway analysis indicated that no pathways were significantly affected in either promoter regions or CpG island-related genes as evidenced by an adjusted <em>p</em> value or false discovery rate markedly higher than 0.05 (<a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S6</a>). We then turned our attention to genes closely related to cholesterol biosynthesis and lipid metabolism to explore the correlation between hepatic gene expression and methylation changes. We found substantial increases in methylation at promoter regions of <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, and <em>Sqle</em> and CpG islands of <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Idi1</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, <em>Hsd17b7</em>, and <em>Gpam</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Fig. 6</span></a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>, and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S5</a>). Overall, of the 13 identified significantly down-regulated genes involved in cholesterol biosynthesis, five genes showed significantly increased methylation levels. Moreover, we also found substantial decreases in methylation at promoter regions of <em>Cpt2</em> and <em>Lpin1</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Fig. 6</span></a><em>A</em>) whose expression levels were significantly up-regulated. To validate the methylation data, we performed bisulfite sequencing on the promoters of <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, and <em>Lpin1</em>. Pooled DNA from the same animals used for MeDIP-chip (equally from each animal) was used for analysis, and the average methylation level for each group was evaluated. The bisulfite sequencing further confirmed their methylation differences with markedly increased methylation levels of <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, and <em>Sqle</em> and the markedly decreased methylation level of <em>Lpin1</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Fig. 6</span></a><em>C</em>). Together, these results indicate that epigenetic regulation is an upstream event that could drive a substantial fraction of the observed expression changes in offspring.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702581216448" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580006.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580006.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580006.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702581216448" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F6"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F6/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6">FIGURE 6.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p33"><strong>Maternal weight loss alters the methylation patterns of metabolic genes in offspring.</strong>

We expected that a short term EE has a similar effect on offspring. The results were quite unexpected. Short term EE led to lower body weight and reduced fat in mesenteric WAT and retroperitoneal WAT in offspring similar to that observed in the EE mice, but the gonadal WAT in offspring was largely unaffected in contrast to the significant decrease in the EE mice (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7</span></a><em>C</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). Although short term EE also showed altered serum biomarkers in offspring, the pattern was quite distinct from that of standard EE mice. Furthermore, although the effect of the maternal uterine environment on the metabolism of offspring has been widely accepted, increasing evidence suggests that maternal physiological and metabolic changes can lead to oocyte alterations (<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016776">38</a>, <a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016718">39</a>). These facts raise the possibility that maternal metabolic changes may affect oocytes and thereby offspring. To determine whether oocyte alteration in response to maternal metabolic conditions contributes to certain phenotypic changes in offspring, we performed embryo transplantation experiments. We transplanted weight loss or control fertilized eggs into oviducts of foster mothers, respectively. Offspring were delivered and raised on a normal diet after weaning in a standard laboratory environment until 16 weeks of age. No differences were observed in litter size or sex ratios between weight loss and control groups after embryo transfer. We first assessed physiological and metabolic changes after embryo transfer. Analysis of the offspring revealed that, although body weight and weight of mesenteric WAT were largely unaffected, the weights of retroperitoneal WAT and gonadal WAT were significantly reduced (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). Among the serum biomarkers examined, glucose and insulin were largely unaffected, whereas leptin, cholesterol, triglyceride, and free fatty acid were significantly decreased (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>B</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). Meanwhile, the hepatic levels of cholesterol and triglyceride exhibited a dramatic decrease (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>C</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). In addition, GTT and ITT revealed significantly improved glucose tolerance and enhanced insulin sensitivity (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a>, <em>D</em> and <em>E</em>). We then assessed molecular changes in the offspring after embryo transfer. Because the cholesterol and triglyceride levels were significantly decreased in both plasma and liver, we focused on cholesterol biosynthesis and lipid metabolism-associated genes. Of particular interest, analysis of the liver of offspring after embryo transfer revealed that the cholesterol biosynthetic genes exhibited an expression pattern similar to that of naturally fertilized animals (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>F</em>). Of the identified 13 significantly decreased cholesterol biosynthetic genes in offspring derived from natural fertilization, 10 were also significantly down-regulated to a comparable level after embryo transfer (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>F</em>). Meanwhile, analysis of the lipid metabolism-associated genes revealed that of the identified 12 differentially expressed lipid metabolism-associated genes in offspring derived from natural fertilization seven were also induced in offspring after embryo transfer (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>G</em>). Moreover, <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, and <em>Lpin1</em>, all of which were differentially methylated in the liver of offspring of weight loss mothers, exhibited a similar cytosine methylation pattern after embryo transfer (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Figs. 9</span></a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>, and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F10/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F10" rid-ob="ob-F10" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F10"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and10,</span></span><span>10</span></a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>). Together, these results indicate that oocyte alterations in response to maternal metabolic conditions contribute to certain phenotypic changes in offspring.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702597617824" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580008.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580008.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580008.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702597617824" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F8"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F8/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8">FIGURE 8.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p37"><strong>Metabolic and gene expression changes in offspring derived from embryo transfer.</strong>

We therefore isolated DNA from naturally ovulated mature oocytes (MII stage) from control or weight loss mothers. We assayed the <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, and <em>Lpin1</em> promoters for methylation by bisulfite sequencing. Importantly, we found a substantial increase in methylation at the promoter regions of <em>Hmgcr</em> and <em>Lss</em> in oocytes from weight loss mothers (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Figs. 9</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F10/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F10" rid-ob="ob-F10" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F10"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and10</span></span><span>10</span></a><em>A</em>) found to be hypermethylated in livers of weight loss offspring. We also found a substantial decrease in methylation at the promoter region of <em>Lpin1</em> in oocytes from weight loss mothers (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a><em>B</em>). In contrast, methylation of <em>Sqle</em> was unaltered between control and weight loss oocytes (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F10/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F10" rid-ob="ob-F10" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F10"><span>Fig. 10</span></a><em>B</em>), indicating that differential methylation at this locus observed in livers is established at some point during development. To test whether genes differentially methylated are <em>de novo</em> methylated or potentially inherit DNA methylation from oocytes, we performed bisulfite sequencing on E3.5 blastocysts after embryo transfer of fertilized eggs. Both <em>Hmgcr</em> and <em>Lss</em> showed higher methylation levels in weight loss samples compared with controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Figs. 9</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F10/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F10" rid-ob="ob-F10" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F10"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and10</span></span><span>10</span></a><em>A</em>), and <em>Lpin1</em> showed lower methylation levels in weight loss samples compared with controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a><em>B</em>), suggesting that these genes partially inherit methylated alleles from oocytes. Together, these results indicate that for certain genes or regions the cytosine methylation status in oocytes is strongly predisposed toward methylation in blastocysts perhaps by incomplete postfertilization demethylation of methylated cytosines.</p></div><div id="__sec27" class="sec"><p></p><h5 class="inline">Maternal Weight Loss Alters Overall Transcriptional Patterns in Mature Oocytes </h5><p id="__p41" class="p p-first">RNA is another potential epigenetic information carrier that plays an important role in transmitting heritable information between generations (<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016778">40</a>,<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016752">&#x02013;</a><a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016763">42</a>). To globally investigate effects of maternal weight loss on oocyte RNA transcription, we surveyed transcriptional patterns across the entire transcriptome via mRNA-Seq. Pooled naturally ovulated mature oocytes (MII stage) from three control and three weight loss female F<sub>0</sub> founders (three oocytes randomly selected for each animal and a total of nine oocytes for each group) were used for analysis. We identified &#x0223c;800 differentially expressed genes in mature oocytes from weight loss compared with control groups (<a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>). Interestingly, weight loss oocytes consistently exhibited decreased expression of cholesterol and lipid metabolism-associated genes (<em>Apoe</em>, <em>Insig1</em>, <em>Cd36</em>, <em>Elovl1</em>, <em>Gnpat</em>, <em>Acoxl</em>, <em>Plcb3</em>, and <em>Aldh5a1</em>; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>). We also observed down-regulation of a number of glucose catabolic genes such as <em>Pdha1</em> and <em>Gpd2</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>). Moreover, a number of transcription factors (<em>Mapk3</em>, <em>Gtf3a</em>, <em>Fus</em>, and <em>Tlr3</em>) and chromatin regulators (<em>Carm1</em>, <em>Men1</em>, <em>Epc2</em>, and <em>Hdac4</em>) were also differentially expressed (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a><em>A</em> and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>). Whether this reflects inheritable information is presently unknown, although we confirmed expression of these genes by qRT-PCR (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a><em>A</em>). To better define the alterations, we quantified the transcript levels of representative genes important for lipid biosynthesis (<em>Apoe</em>), transcription regulation (<em>Mapk3</em>), and chromatin modification (<em>Hdac4</em> and <em>Carm1</em>) in an additional six weight loss and five control animals that were not included in the mRNA-Seq study. Importantly, we observed reproducible lower levels of <em>Apoe</em> and <em>Hdac4</em> and higher levels of <em>Mapk3</em> and <em>Carm1</em> in independent weight loss mothers (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a>, <em>B&#x02013;E</em>). Moreover, short term EE led to fewer changes compared with standard EE (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><span>Fig. 11</span></a>, <em>B&#x02013;E</em>), indicating that in short term EE fewer effects on oocytes are accumulated, which may be the reason for the phenotypic differences seen in their offspring. Together, these results indicate that oocyte RNA transcription can be regulated by maternal metabolic conditions and may represent environmental information participating in early steps of development and thereby affecting offspring. However, the molecular mechanism for these observations is still unclear.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F11" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F11"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702639382992" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4335202_zbc0111509580011.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580011.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580011.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702639382992" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F11"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F11/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F11" rid-ob="ob-F11">FIGURE 11.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p42"><strong>Maternal weight loss alters overall transcriptional patterns in mature oocytes.</strong>

We then asked whether the beneficial metabolic and molecular changes in the offspring (F<sub>1</sub> generation) can be passed to the next generation (F<sub>2</sub> generation). We mated the female F<sub>1</sub> generation of weight loss or control mice with normal males and examined metabolic, transcriptional, and epigenetic changes in their offspring. We first assessed physiological and metabolic changes in the F<sub>2</sub> generation at 16 weeks of age and found that the F<sub>2</sub> generation also exhibited reduced body weight; decreased WAT; decreased serum concentrations of glucose, insulin, cholesterol, triglyceride, and free fatty acid; and decreased liver concentrations of cholesterol and triglyceride as seen in the F<sub>1</sub> generation (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F12" rid-ob="ob-F12" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F12"><span>Fig. 12</span></a>, <em>A&#x02013;C</em>, and <a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S2</a>). We then assessed how weight loss in the F<sub>1</sub> generation females causes molecular changes in their offspring. We randomly selected six cholesterol biosynthetic genes and six lipid metabolism-related genes that were significantly affected by maternal weight loss from the gene expression data. qRT-PCR analysis showed that all of these genes exhibited a similar expression pattern compared with the F<sub>1</sub> generation (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F12" rid-ob="ob-F12" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F12"><span>Fig. 12</span></a>, <em>D</em> and <em>E</em>). Moreover, we examined the methylation status of <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, and <em>Lpin1</em> (whose methylation status was significantly altered by maternal weight loss) in the liver of the F<sub>2</sub> generation by bisulfite sequencing and found a similar methylation status compared with the F<sub>1</sub> generation (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F12" rid-ob="ob-F12" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F12"><span>Fig. 12</span></a><em>F</em>). Together, these results indicate that the beneficial effects and epigenetic marks induced by maternal weight loss can be inherited for at least two generations. Because all F<sub>1</sub> generation animals lived in standard laboratory housing, the observed metabolic and molecular changes in the F<sub>2</sub> generation are independent of enriched environment-mediated and other environmental factors. Therefore, the beneficial effects seen in offspring are most likely due to the weight loss-associated physiological and metabolic conditions in mothers.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F12" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F12"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F12" rid-ob="ob-F12"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702601299808" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580012.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580012.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580012.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702601299808" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F12"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F12/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F12" rid-ob="ob-F12">FIGURE 12.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p44"><strong>Effects of maternal weight loss on metabolic and molecular changes in the F<sub>2</sub> generation.</strong>

Independent of <em>in utero</em> exposure, our results reveal that oocyte alteration in response to environmental conditions is a strong factor in determining metabolic changes in offspring. Overall, our study reveals a molecular and mechanistic basis of how maternal lifestyle modification affects metabolic changes in the offspring.</p><p id="__p46">Obesity induces a variety of metabolic disorders such as diabetes and fatty liver disease. In contrast, weight loss ameliorates obesity-induced disorders by enhancement of numerous parameters, including decreased plasma glucose, insulin, leptin, cholesterol, and triglyceride levels. The reduced fasting glucose and insulin levels indicate improved glucose metabolism, which would play an important role in decreasing the risk of susceptibility to diabetes. Leptin is a hormone released from adipocytes that acts in the brain. It plays a critical role in energy homeostasis (<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016730">43</a>). The reduced food intake observed in the weight loss offspring is potentially due to the decreased leptin concentrations. High levels of serum triglycerides have been associated with atherosclerosis and fatty liver (<a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016719">44</a>). Weight loss can significantly decrease the serum lipid levels and thus can ameliorate these obesity-related disorders. Moreover, our results suggest that not only are these beneficial effects present in the F<sub>0</sub> generation but also in at least subsequent two generations, indicating that the mother can initiate intergenerational transmission of weight loss and its related advantages.</p><p id="__p47">Our results clearly identified certain physiological pathways whose expression is sensitive to maternal weight loss. Specifically, we found that hepatic expression of genes involved in lipid and cholesterol biosynthesis can be regulated by maternal weight loss, and these molecular changes are correlated with physiological and metabolic changes in individuals. The reprogrammed gene expression state is most likely affected by epigenetic modifications. Our epigenomic profiling of offspring livers revealed numerous changes in cytosine methylation depending on maternal weight loss, including reproducible changes in promoter or CpG island methylation over several key lipid and cholesterol biosynthesis genes. Notably, of the 13 identified significantly down-regulated genes involved in cholesterol biosynthesis, we found that five genes (<em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, <em>Sqle</em>, <em>Idi1</em>, and <em>Hsd17b7</em>) showed significantly increased methylation levels, leading to the hypothesis that epigenetic regulation is an upstream event that affects the entire gene expression status. However, changes in promoter or CpG island methylation did not globally correlate with changes in gene expression, indicating that gene expression is unlikely to be epigenetically specified at each individual gene. In fact, gene expression is regulated by a complicated network, which involves upstream and downstream factors and other regulators, and widespread expression differences can be caused by small numbers of upstream regulators (<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016748">45</a>, <a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016708">46</a>). Moreover, although we focused on the effect of cytosine methylation, which is critical for the regulation of gene expression, we cannot neglect other important epigenetic regulations, including histone modification, chromatin remodeling, non-coding RNA regulation, and others. Further analyses of the epigenetic modifications mentioned above will enhance our understanding of the transcriptional regulation affected by maternal lifestyle changes.</p><p id="__p48">How maternal weight loss affects oocyte epigenomes is an intriguing question. As the oocyte matures, it accumulates epigenetic marks on both DNA and histone until the final phase of maturation before ovulation (<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016744">47</a>). The follicular fluid surrounding the oocyte varies according to maternal physiological and metabolic conditions (<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016753">48</a>), providing a unique microenvironment that reflects the outside world (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F13/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F13"><span>Fig. 13</span></a>). In responding to these environmental signals, the oocyte epigenome exhibits a moderate degree of plasticity (<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016777">49</a>, <a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016741">50</a>). In females, increased BMI has been demonstrated to be correlated with elevated glucose, insulin, leptin, triglycerides, and inflammatory biomarkers in follicular fluid (<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016774">48</a>). Corresponding with this, the receptors of glucose, insulin, and leptin have been identified in oocytes (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016732">51</a>,<a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016704">&#x02013;</a><a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016764">53</a>). It is conceivable that the environmental signals enter the oocyte and further affect the epigenome of the oocyte (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F13/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F13"><span>Fig. 13</span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F13" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F13"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F13/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702680125248" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580013.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580013.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580013.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702680125248" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F13/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F13"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F13/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13">FIGURE 13.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p49"><strong>Potential mechanism of weight loss-induced epigenomic changes in oocytes.</strong> Developing oocytes are suspended in follicular fluid, which provides a unique nutritional microenvironment that represents maternal metabolic states, for example lipid and biomarkers. During oocyte maturation, its epigenetic marks are established until the final phase of maturation before ovulation. Environmental signals, which vary according to maternal metabolic conditions, may enter the oocyte and result in epigenomic alterations in oocytes.</p></div></div></div><p id="__p50">During oocyte maturation, the <em>de novo</em> DNA methyltransferases <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> are necessary for the establishment of DNA methylation (<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016742">54</a>, <a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016779">55</a>). We asked whether the expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> are affected by maternal weight loss. The oocyte mRNA-Seq data showed that <em>Dnmt3a</em> exhibited a slightly decreased expression level in weight loss oocytes with expression at 81.9% when compared with the control (<a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>), whereas <em>Dnmt3l</em> showed a moderately increased expression level with expression being 1.63 times that of the control (<a href="http://www.jbc.org/cgi/content/full/M114.605642/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4335202&amp;issue-id=249632&amp;journal-id=567&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">supplemental Data Set S7</a>). In an additional experiment, we confirmed the mRNA-Seq results by qRT-PCR, which showed a similar expression difference between groups in both genes normalized to the housekeeping gene &#x003b2;-actin (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Fig. 14</span></a><em>A</em>). Although the expression change is less than 2-fold, it should be noted that a slight perturbation for key genes during critical developmental stages may have important effects. However, the biological implications for these observations are presently unknown. We next asked whether the expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> is affected by environmental signal differences that potentially exist in follicular fluid due to maternal metabolic variations. In an additional experiment, we determined the expression levels of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> in oocytes after introduction of glucose, insulin, or leptin into the <em>in vitro</em> maturation culture system, respectively. The qRT-PCR results showed that glucose supplementation (5 m<span class="small-caps">m</span>) induced a significantly increased expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> but with no alterations of expression of <em>Dnmt3l</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Fig. 14</span></a><em>B</em>). Insulin supplementation (5 &#x003bc;g/ml) led to no significant difference in the expression of both <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Fig. 14</span></a><em>C</em>). The presence of leptin (10 ng/ml) resulted in no alteration in <em>Dnmt3a</em> but a significant decrease in the expression of <em>Dnmt3l</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Fig. 14</span></a><em>D</em>). These results suggest that the environmental signals potentially affect the expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em>, which may further influence the epigenomic modification of oocytes. However, it is difficult to definitively demonstrate the causal relationships between the environmental and epigenetic changes because of the complexity of the environmental factors and the numerous epigenetic alterations in the vast genome. Moreover, previous work suggests that regional methylation levels can be secondary consequences of alterations in DNA-binding factors or histone modifications (<a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016766">55</a>, <a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016759">56</a>). Thus it is possible that the differential methylation observed in our study is secondary to these upstream events. Further studies are required to completely clarify this issue.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139702642637904" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4335202_zbc0111509580014.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zbc0111509580014.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/bin/zbc0111509580014.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139702642637904" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F14"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4335202/figure/F14/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14">FIGURE 14.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p51"><strong>Effects of different supplementations during <em>in vitro</em> oocyte maturation on the expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em>.</strong>
<em>A</em>, to validate the RNA-Seq data, quantitative RT-PCR was performed on <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l</em> relative to the housekeeping gene &#x003b2;-actin, which showed a similar expression difference between groups compared with the RNA-Seq data. <em>B</em>, effects of glucose supplementation on the expression of <em>Dnmt3a</em> and <em>Dnmt3l. The <em>dotted line</em> (set as 1) represents the average of expression levels of each gene from controls. *, <em>p</em> &#x0003c; 0.05.</p></div></div></div><p id="__p52">Maternal lifestyle could potentially affect the offspring's metabolism via a number of different mechanisms. For instance, in mice, high fat intake during pregnancy increases metabolic syndromes in several generations of offspring (<a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016733">57</a>), and maternal postpartum behavior can influence multiple aspects of neurobiology and behavior in offspring (<a href="#B58" rid="B58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016737">58</a>). Here we focused on the hypothesis that environmental information does reside in oocyte epigenetic information carriers to control the offspring's phenotype (<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016729">49</a>). First, decreased ovarian temperature resulting from less fat accumulation and lower concentrations of serum biomarkers may affect DNA reprogramming and RNA transcription of the gamete (<a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016775">59</a>, <a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016739">60</a>). Second, several studies reported that RNA molecules in gametes can affect the offspring phenotype (<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016746">40</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016761">41</a>), and our transcriptome analysis found numerous differentially expressed genes in oocytes of weight loss mothers. Third, a subset of epigenetic modifications in gametes is known to be heritable (<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016765">35</a>, <a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016709">36</a>, <a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016738">61</a>,<a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016767">&#x02013;</a><a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016769">63</a>), and we observed that a number of chromatin regulators tend to be differentially expressed in oocytes of weight loss mothers. Moreover, our methylation analysis also found that several genes tend to be differentially methylated in oocytes of weight loss mothers. Specifically, we observed that certain genes (such as <em>Hmgcr</em>, <em>Lss</em>, and <em>Lpin1</em>) can partially resist global demethylation postfertilization and largely inherit cytosine methylation from oocytes, further suggesting that there is intergenerational transmission of cytosine methylation at a substantial fraction of the genome. These results extend findings of researchers from a previous study (<a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016707">64</a>) that identified some non-imprinted sequences that resist demethylation in preimplantation embryo development. Together with previous work showing that histone modification can also be transmitted from gametes to embryos (<a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016736">62</a>, <a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_428016715">65</a>), this indicates that epigenetic information linked to environmental factors participates in early steps of development and thereby affects offspring.</p><p id="__p53">For mammalian studies, transgenerational epigenetic inheritance is broadly used to describe the non-sequence-based effects that can be inherited from one generation to the next. However, it is important to distinguish the descriptive transgenerational effects from the truly transgenerational effects. When the F<sub>0</sub> gestational female is exposed to the environment, both the F<sub>1</sub> embryo and its germ line (which will produce the F<sub>2</sub> generation) are exposed. Therefore, phenotypes from F<sub>1</sub> and F<sub>2</sub> may result from gestational environmental exposure, and only the F<sub>3</sub> and later generations can be considered as truly transgenerational effects. Our embryo transfer studies indicate that maternal weight loss-associated metabolic changes affect epigenomics in oocytes and can be potentially transmitted to offspring. Because the F<sub>2</sub> generation also exhibited significant metabolic differences, it would be expected that the epigenetic alterations still exist in oocytes of F<sub>2</sub> females and would be potentially transmitted to the F<sub>3</sub> generation. Further assessment on the F<sub>3</sub> generation will be required to clarify this issue.</p><p id="__p54" class="p p-last">Together, we have shown that maternal weight loss reprograms metabolic gene expression and improves metabolic parameters in offspring and that oocyte alteration in response to maternal physiological and metabolic conditions might be a strong candidate contributor. Environmentally induced maternal metabolic changes affect epigenetic marks and can be inherited for at least two generations. These findings may have implications in explaining the prevalence of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and other chronic metabolic disease. This may also imply that in the near future epigenetic factors that are potentially heritable should be regarded as important in determining the risk of certain diseases. Epigenetic problems may be effectively solved by environmental protocols, and our present findings imply that maternal weight loss may be considered as a potential strategy to reverse prevalent obesity and related metabolic syndromes.</p></div><div id="idm139702601968352" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm139702601968352title">Acknowledgments</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p67">We thank Qing-Hua Zhang at the State Key Laboratory of Reproductive Biology for assistance with photo collections.
