id="idm140034038113104title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><div id="__sec1" class="sec sec-first"><h2>Background</h2><p id="__p1" class="p p-first-last">Obesity and metabolic syndrome are important public concerns, and there is increasing demand for effective therapeutic strategies. Flavonoids are expected to improve the risk factors associated with metabolic syndrome. Anthocyanidins are a kind of flavonoids; well known for their anti-oxidative, anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor properties. However, their effects on adipocytes and molecular systems are not well defined. In this study, we examined the effects of anthocyanidins-enriched bilberry extracts on adipocyte differentiation.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>Methods</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">Utilizing 3T3-L1 cell line, we investigated that bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins induced inhibition of lipid accumulation during adipogenesis. To identify what is the most important bilberry mediated-effect, we analyzed the expressions of key transcriptional factors associated with adipocyte differentiation by Real Time (RT)-PCR. From the results of RT-PCR, we hypothesized that bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins blocks insulin signal, we determined the phosphorylation of tyrosine residues of insulin receptor substrate 1 (IRS1) protein by western blotting analysis. In addition, we compared the whole-genome expression profiles of early stage of adipocyte differentiation under four different growth conditions (DMSO, bilberry, two anthocyanidins) by microarray analyses and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA).</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">Exposure to bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins during adipocyte differentiation inhibited 3T3-L1 differentiation. During this period, bilberry extracts and anthocyanidin significantly decreased a key adipocyte differentiation-associated marker, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor- <em>&#x003b3; </em>(Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>) and sterol regulatory element-binding protein 1c (Srebp1c). Western blotting analysis showed that bilberry extracts and anthocyanidin decreased the phosphorylation of tyrosine residues of IRS1. In addition, microarray experiments and GSEA data revealed significantly altered expression of the known genes of the insulin pathway in cells treated with bilberry extracts or anthocyanidins in the early differentiation stages.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusions</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">Our data demonstrate that anthocyanidin enriched bilberry extracts strongly inhibit the adipocyte differentiation via the insulin pathway. Furthermore, bilberry extracts might be used as a potential complementary treatment for the obese patients with metabolic syndrome.</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec5" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec5title">Background</h2><p id="__p5" class="p p-first">Obesity is the main cause of metabolic syndrome. It can lead to various complications, including hardening of the arteries and an increased risk of cardiovascular diseases, and therefore has a large impact on healthcare in both developed and developing countries [<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096117">1</a>]. The development of obesity is characterized by both increased adipocyte number (hyperplasia) and size (hypertrophy) [<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096104">2</a>], which is regulated by genetic, endocrine, metabolic, neurological, and nutritional factors [<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096111">3</a>,<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096101">4</a>]. Accordingly, understanding the mechanisms by which particular nutrients affect adipocyte differentiation would help prevent obesity and its associated diseases.</p><p id="__p6">A variety of naturally occurring flavonoids have been of recent interest because accumulating evidence indicates that these compounds have beneficial health effects and are relatively safer than drugs [<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096112">5</a>,<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096102">6</a>]. Bilberry contains rich flavonoids [<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096103">7</a>], specifically anthocyanidins. Anthocyanidins have a wide range of biological functions and health benefits, such as anti-oxidative, anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor properties [<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096107">8</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096108">9</a>]. Interestingly, when rats fed on hyperlipidemic diets supplemented with bilberry extracts, their plasma triglyceride levels decreased in proportion with the amount of bilberry extracts [<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096100">10</a>]. Also, anthocyanin (glycoside of anthocyanidin)-rich diets from purple corn or Moro orange have been shown to ameliorate obesity and insulin resistance in mice fed on a high-fat diet [<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096097">11</a>-<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096105">13</a>]. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying these properties remain unknown and require further investigation in cells.</p><p id="__p7">Adipocyte differentiation requires the synergistic action of multiple adipogenic transcription factors [<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096118">14</a>]. The first indication that this transcriptional network is activated is the involvement of CCAAT/enhancer-binding protein (C/ebp) family. C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b4; </em>are expressed in the early phase of differentiation, and they subsequently induce transcription of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b1; </em>[<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096116">15</a>]. Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b1; </em>are two master regulators of adipocyte differentiation. They slog cooperatively to direct the final phase of adipocyte differentiation by activating the expression of adipocyte-specific genes, such as fatty acid synthetase, fatty acid-binding protein, leptin and adiponectin [<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096094">16</a>]. Insulin-regulated GLUT4 glucose transporters embedded in granules are transferred and merged to cell-face membrane and increase cellular glucose-uptake. As a result, lipid accumulation proceeds and adipocyte gets lipid droplet [<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096093">14</a>]. Recent investigations suggest that the lag between the appearance of C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>and Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>expression is due to the time required to synthesize the additional proteins that facilitate C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>activity [<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096114">16</a>]. Specifically, the transcription of Kruppel-like factor (Klf5) is activated by C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b4; </em>, and Klf5 in concert with these C/ebps contributes to the induction of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>[<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096095">17</a>]. It is likely that additional factors in parallel pathways are induced early and converge on Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>downstream of C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b4; </em>, such as Srebp1c transcription factor. Srebp1c may regulate adipocyte differentiation by increasing the transcriptional activity of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>, and Srebp1c is expression is significantly enhanced in response to insulin [<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096092">18</a>,<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096098">19</a>]. It has already been reported that the transcriptional network of adipocyte differentiation is dominated by three pathways (insulin, glucocorticoid and cAMP) [<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096091">16</a>].</p><p id="__p8" class="p p-last">This study was designed to evaluate the anti-adipogenic action of the bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins during differentiation of 3T3-L1 cells. Specifically, we aimed to identify the molecular mechanisms underlying the effects of bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins on the transcriptional network and explore the major pathways involved with their inhibitory effects.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec6title">Methods</h2><div id="__sec7" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Chemical reagents</h3><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">Bilberry extracts (containing 25% anthocyanidins) were purchased from (Tokiwa Phito Chemical, Chiba, Japan). Anthocyanidins (pelargonidin, cyanidin, delphinidin, peonidin, and malvidin) were purchased from Funakoshi Co., Ltd. (Tokyo, Japan). Since bilberry extracts and all anthocyanidins are oil soluble, hence they were dissolved in DMSO, and stored in the dark at 4&#x000b0;C and the measured pH was 8.0 approximately. For each experiment, cells received bilberry extracts or anthocyanidins premixed with the culture medium. The final concentration of DMSO in the medium was 0.1% throughout the experiments.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><h3>Cell culture and adipocyte differentiation</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">3T3-L1 preadipocytes (American Type Culture Collection, Manassas, VA) were grown to confluence in DMEM (Sigma-Aldrich, Tokyo, Japan) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum and penicillin (100 U/ml)/streptomycin (100 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml). Adipocyte differentiation was induced using an Adipogenesis Assay Kit (Chemicon International, Temecula, CA). On day 0, the cells were induced with initiation media (10 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml insulin, 1 <em>&#x003bc;</em>M dexamethasone and 0.5 <em>&#x003bc;</em>M IBMX in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum). On day 3, the initiation media was replaced with progression media (10 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml insulin in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum). On day 6, the progression media was replaced with maintenance media (DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum). From days 0 to 6, the cells were exposed to the chemical reagents (DMSO, bilberry extracts 100 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml and anthocyanidins 100 nM). The cells are incubated at 37&#x000b0;C with 5% CO<sub>2 </sub>throughout the experiments.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><h3>Oil Red O staining</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">On day 7, 3T3-L1 cells were fixed in fresh 10% formalin (pH 7.4) and stained with Oil Red O dye (Sigma-Aldrich, Tokyo, Japan). The lipid the staining was quantified by extracting the dye with 100% isopropanol and measuring the absorbance at 500 nm.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><h3>Quantitative real-time PCR</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first">Total RNA was isolated from 3T3-L1 cells using ISOGEN reagent (Nippon Gene, Tokyo, Japan). Total RNA was quantified using NanoDrop (Thermo Scientific) and reverse transcribed to cDNA using M-MLV Reverse Transcriptase Kit (Invitrogen Life Science Technologies, Tokyo, Japan) following the manufacturer's specifications. The expression level of genes in 3T3-L1 cells treated with bilberry extracts or anthocyanidins was assesed by quantitative RT-PCR (Stratagene Mx 3005 Sequence Detection System, Agilent Technologies, Tokyo, Japan). PCR amplification was performed using a QuantiTect SYBR Green PCR Kit (QIAGEN, Tokyo, Japan). The results are expressed as the fold increase relative to the controls after normalizing to the <em>&#x003b2;</em>-actin gene expression levels. Differentiation was initiated by treating the cells with or without bilberry extracts (0, 10, 50, 100 &#x003bc;g/mL). On day 7, we found that bilberry extracts markedly diminished the lipid accumulation in 3T3-L1 cells. Furthermore, bilberry extracts inhibited the differentiation of 3T3-L1 cells in a dose-dependent manner (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure1A).</span></span><span>1A</span></a>). On the other hand, bilberry extracts had no effect on cell proliferation (data not shown). In addition, bilberry extracts down-regulated the mRNA levels of Ppar <em>&#x003b3;</em>; a master regulator of adipocyte differentiation. Bilberry extracts had similar effects on the expression of adiponectin, which is a major adipocyte marker (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure1B).</span></span><span>1B</span></a>). These results showed that bilberry extracts are capable of inhibiting adipocyte differentiation and diminished lipid accumulation with concomitant down-regulation of Ppar <em>&#x003b3;</em>.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140034035564848" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3063807_1743-7075-8-14-1.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is 1743-7075-8-14-1.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/bin/1743-7075-8-14-1.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140034035564848" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Figure 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p17"><strong>Bilberry extracts inhibited 3T3-L1 adipocyte differentiation</strong>. We investigated the effects of the bilberry extracts on the expression of transcription factors that are associated with these three pathways. As shown in Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3em;">Figure2A,</span></span><span>2A</span></a>, treating cells with bilberry extracts decreased the mRNA levels of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b1; </em>which play key-roles in adipocyte differentiation. In addition, out of the five anthocyanidin tested, delphinidin was most effective in down-regulating Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and Srebp1c mRNA levels (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure3C</span></span><span>3C</span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140034038056688" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3063807_1743-7075-8-14-3.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is 1743-7075-8-14-3.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/bin/1743-7075-8-14-3.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140034038056688" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F3/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F3"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3">Figure 3</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p21"><strong>Anthocyanidins contained bilberry extracts that inhibited 3T3-L1 adipocyte differentiation</strong>. We examined the effects of delphinidin and bilberry extracts on phosphorylation of IRS1 by insulin stimuli. 3T3-L1 preadipocytes were treated with 100 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/mL bilberry extracts or 100 nM delphinidin with 100 nM insulin for 5 min. When delphinidin or bilberry extracts were added in culture media, the ratio of pIRS1/IRS1 was not increased by insulin treatment as compared with the DMSO control (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure4A).</span></span><span>4A</span></a>). Moreover, we compared the whole-genome expression profiles of adipocytes on day 2 (early stage of differentiation) under four different conditions (DMSO, bilberry, delphinidin, or cyanidin) by microarray analyses. The microarray analysis showed that 784 probes were commonly differentially expressed (fold change &#x0003e; 2) among the three conditions compared to the DMSO-treated cells. A subsequent GSEA analysis of these 784 probes revealed that altered-genes were associated with the insulin signaling pathway (KEGG: 4910, <a href="http://www.genome.jp/dbget-bin/get_pathway?org_name=hsa&#x00026;mapno=04910" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3063807&amp;issue-id=193959&amp;journal-id=272&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.genome.jp/dbget-bin/get_pathway?org_name=hsa&#x00026;mapno=04910</a> and were most significantly (p &#x0003c; 0.0001) overlapped with them in KEGG pathways (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure4B).</span></span><span>4B</span></a>). These data indicate that anthocyanidin and bilberry blocked insulin signal during adipocyte differentiation (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure4C</span></span><span>4C</span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140034037002688" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3063807_1743-7075-8-14-4.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is 1743-7075-8-14-4.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/bin/1743-7075-8-14-4.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140034037002688" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F4"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4">Figure 4</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p23"><strong>Bilberry and anthocyanidins inhibit the insulin signaling pathway</strong>. The data showed that bilberry extracts inhibited the adipocyte differentiation of 3T3-L1 cells in a dose-dependent manner and also bilberry extracts down-regulated the expression of three transcriptional factors; Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>, C/ebp <em>&#x003b1; </em>and Srebp1c. Next, we tested whether anthocyanidins; the major flavonoids of bilberry, had effects on adipocyte differentiation in a similar way. Indeed, anthocyanidin down-regulated the expression of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and Srebp1c genes. Therefore, in presence of isulin stimuli, the level of Srebp1c is elevated which in turn results in up-regulation of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and C/ebp<em>&#x003b1; </em>gene expression (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure4C).</span></span><span>4C</span></a>). These results made us to hypothesize that the major effects of anthocyanidins was to block insulin signal. Western blotting analysis showed that bilberry extracts and anthocyanidin decreased the tyrosine phosphorylation of IRS1. In addition, genome-wide microarray experiments and GSEA revealed that in the early differentiation stages, bilberry extracts or anthocyanidins altered expression of genes in the insulin pathway. These data indicate that 1) bilberry extracts inhibit adipocyte differentiation via the insulin pathway and 2) these effects are primarily mediated by the action of anthocyanidins.</p><p id="__p25">Recent investigations suggested that Srebp1c is the earliest transcription factors involved in adipocyte differentiation and Srebp1c is required to induce Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>downstream of C/ebp <em>&#x003b2; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b4; </em>[<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096096">22</a>], these results suggest that bilberry extracts-induced inhibition of adipocyte differentiation may be predominantly associated with unchanged Srebp1c expression, accompanied by a strong inhibition of Ppar <em>&#x003b3; </em>and C/ebp <em>&#x003b1; </em>in the transcriptional network. Notably, Kim et al. [<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096110">23</a>] demonstrated that ectopic expression of a dominant-negative Srebp1c inhibited preadipocyte differentiation, while overexpressing this protein significantly enhanced the adipogenic activity of Ppar <em>&#x003b3;</em>. In their subsequent study, it was reported that insulin stimuli significantly increases Srebp1c expression in adipocytes [<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096109">19</a>].</p><p id="__p26">More recently, Takikawa et al. [<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096113">21</a>] demonstrated that dietary bilberry extracts activate AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) in white adipose tissue. It was also reported that insulin-mediated activation of Akt can negatively regulate AMPK activity [<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096099">24</a>]. Therefore, it is deliberated that the inhibitory effects of anthocyanidin and bilberry extracts on IRS1 phosphorylation lead to Akt inactivation and AMPK activation. Additionally, Hafeez et al. [<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_230800179">25</a>] revealed that anthocyanidin; delphinidin induced apoptosis of prostate cancer cells and Lamy et al. [<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_215096089">26</a>] showed delphinidin inhibited phosphorylation of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) receptor-2 in human umbilical vascular endothelial cells. Our GSEA results indicated that anthocyanidins altered genes known to be linked with "Apoptosis" and "VEGF signaling pathway" (Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3.5em;">(Figure4C)</span></span><span>4C</span></a>) and these findings are consistent with other published reports. In essence of the data presented here and previous reports, it seems plausible that bilberry extracts or anthocyanidins might be interacting with the cellular receptors like IR or VEGFR. However, further investigation is required to validate this hypothesis.</p><p id="__p27" class="p p-last">To further elucidate the properties of bilberry extracts and anthocyanidins, we tested the inhibitory effects of five major anthocyanidins that are present in bilberry, including pelargonidin, cyanidin, delphinidin, peonidin, and malvidin, on 3T3-L1 differentiation. Although, all the five anthocyanidins effectively inhibited the lipid accumulation in 3T3-L1 cells, but the reasons for exhibiting differences in their inhibitory efficiencies was unclear. Perhaps, it may be from the number of hydroxyl groups present on the B-ring and the hydrophilic properties as shown in Figure <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3063807/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3em;">Figure3A.</span></span><span>3A</span></a>. The differences in their efficiencies displayed by these anthocyanidins warrants a more elaborate study to further elucidate the underlying mechanisms involved in their mode of action.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec19title">Conclusions</h2><p id="__p28" class="p p-first-last">We demonstrated that anthocyanidins-enriched bilberry extracts inhibited adipocyte differentiation via insulin pathway. These findings provide important insight and implications for preventing and treating obesity associated with metabolic syndrome.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec20title">Competing interests</h2><p id="__p29" class="p p-first-last">The authors declare that they have no competing interests.</p></div><div id="__sec21" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec21title">Authors' contributions</h2><p id="__p30" class="p p-first-last">RS and MTan contributed to design the study, analysis and interpretation of data, and drafting of the manuscript.
