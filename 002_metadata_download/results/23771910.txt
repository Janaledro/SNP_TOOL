id="idm140317437414880" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140317437414880title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="P1" class="p p-first-last">Early life and pre-conception environmental stimuli can affect adult health-related phenotypes. Exercise training is an environmental stimulus affecting many systems throughout the body and appears to alter offspring phenotypes. The aim of this study was to examine the influence of parental exercise training, or &#x0201c;exercise ancestry,&#x0201d; on morphological and metabolic phenotypes in two generations of mouse offspring. F0 C57BL/6 mice were exposed to voluntary exercise or sedentary lifestyle and bred with like-exposed mates to produce an F1 generation. F1 mice of both ancestries were sedentary and sacrificed at 8 wk or bred with littermates to produce an F2 generation, which was also sedentary and sacrificed at 8 wk. Small, but broad generation- and sex-specific effects of exercise ancestry were observed for body mass, fat and muscle mass, serum insulin, glucose tolerance, and muscle gene expression. F1 EX females were lighter than F1 SED females, and had lower absolute tibialis anterior and omental fat masses. Serum insulin was higher in F1 EX females compared to F1 SED females. F2 EX females had impaired glucose tolerance compared to F2 SED females. Analysis of skeletal muscle mRNA levels revealed several generation- and sex-specific differences in mRNA levels for multiple genes, especially those related to metabolic genes (e.g., F1 EX males had lower mRNA levels of <em>Hk2</em>, <em>Ppard</em>, <em>Ppargc1&#x003b1;</em>, <em>Adipoq</em>, and <em>Scd1</em> than F1 SED males). These results provide preliminary evidence that parental exercise training can influence health-related phenotypes in mouse offspring.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">Exercise, Pregnancy, Metabolism</span></div></div><div id="S1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S1title">Introduction</h2><p id="P2" class="p p-first">The beneficial effects of exercise training on metabolic health are well characterized. Among other outcomes, exercise training leads to increased mitochondrial oxidative capacity, which may be protective against a variety of chronic diseases (<a href="#R52" rid="R52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496428">Tseng <em>et al.</em>, 1995</a>). In addition, exercise training improves glucose homeostasis and fat oxidation (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496422">Hoppeler &#x00026; Fl&#x000fc;ck, 2003</a>; <a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496373">Fluck, 2006</a>). In contrast, lack of exercise (or sedentary lifestyle) is associated with chronic disease development and all-cause mortality (<a href="#R32" rid="R32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496378">Mokdad <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>).</p><p id="P3">Mounting evidence suggests early life (even pre-conception) events can affect adult health-related phenotypes, such as disease risk; this is referred to as the developmental origins of health and disease hypothesis (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode">Gluckman &#x00026; Hanson, 2006</a>). Maternal protein restriction results in offspring with lower birth weights and adult metabolic dysfunction (<a href="#R37" rid="R37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496381">Peixoto-Silva <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). Excess maternal caloric intake also affects offspring health, leading to excess adiposity (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496404">Bayol <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>) as well as reduced muscle force production (<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496437">Bayol <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) in exposed offspring compared to mothers consuming a normal diet. The existing literature is primarily focused on maternal factors, however there is emerging evidence that dietary and other environmental factors can also influence offspring health through the paternal line (<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496439">Ng <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>).</p><p id="P4">Exercise training is an environmental stimulus affecting many systems throughout the body, and it may be capable of inducing transgenerational modifications similar to these more commonly studied nutritional interventions. The clinical literature concerning the effect of maternal exercise on maternal, fetal, and child health has been reviewed elsewhere (<a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496364">Kalisiak &#x00026; Spitznagle, 2009</a>). In summary, exercise during pregnancy appears to have beneficial effects in the mother and fetus. Child health outcomes are less clear, with studies reporting greater, lower, or no difference in body weight between neonates whose mothers did or did not exercise during pregnancy (<a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496420">Kalisiak &#x00026; Spitznagle, 2009</a>). In those studies that observed decreased body weight, the difference appeared to be due to a lower percent body fat (<a href="#R8" rid="R8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496383">Clapp &#x00026; Capeless, 1990</a>). The impact of maternal exercise on adult health outcomes or the impact on multiple generations has not been studied in humans. Likewise in rodents, varying effects of maternal exercise have been observed. Maternal treadmill running has been linked to smaller litters in some (<a href="#R50" rid="R50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496429">Treadway <em>et al.</em>, 1986</a>) though not all (<a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496369">Monteiro <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>) studies, but has not been associated with fetal or offspring body mass or length (<a href="#R50" rid="R50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496380">Treadway <em>et al.</em>, 1986</a>; <a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496433">Monteiro <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). Similarly, maternal swimming has been associated with lower birth weight offspring in one (<a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496411">Pinto &#x00026; Shetty, 1995</a>) but not all studies (<a href="#R51" rid="R51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496410">Treadway &#x00026; Lederman, 1986</a>). Two recent studies documented beneficial adaptations in body composition and glucose and insulin dynamics in the mature offspring of dams who had access to a voluntary running wheel during the perinatal period (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496440">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>, <a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496399">2012<em>b</em></a>). To the best of our knowledge, only one study has examined the impact of maternal exercise over multiple generations of offspring; there, maternal swimming led to low-birth-weight pups in the first generation. This growth retardation was also observed in a second generation of rat pups born to the offspring of exercised dams, regardless of whether the first generation offspring were also exposed to exercise (<a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496388">Pinto &#x00026; Shetty, 1995</a>). Whether these observed changes in body size and/or composition are associated with metabolic health or function has not been elucidated. Additionally, the potential impact of paternal exercise has not been examined.</p><p id="P5" class="p p-last">Thus, we sought to determine how body morphology, metabolic phenotypes and skeletal muscle gene expression are affected by exercise ancestry in multiple generations of mouse offspring. Our aims were 1: determine body, fat, organ and skeletal muscle mass differences, 2: examine metabolic phenotypes such as glucose tolerance and circulating insulin and lipids, and 3: determine gene expression differences by skeletal muscle microarray and targeted gene expression analyses. We hypothesized that multiple generations of offspring from exercise ancestry would exhibit advantageous morphological, metabolic and gene expression phenotypes compared to offspring from a sedentary ancestry.</p></div><div id="S2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S2title">Methods</h2><div id="S3" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Ethical Approval</h3><p id="P6" class="p p-first-last">All animal procedures were performed in accordance with the National Institutes of Health guidelines and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at the University of Maryland.</p></div><div id="S4" class="sec"><h3>Animal Procedures</h3><p id="P7" class="p p-first">We performed a breeding experiment including three generations of animals that were either exposed to exercise or kept sedentary in the F0 generation. An overview of our experimental design and timeline is provided in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Figure 1</span></a>. A standard diet (Purina Prolab RMH 3000, 60% carbohydrate, 14% fat, 26% protein) and water were provided <em>ad libitum</em> for all generations in all conditions. 20 male and 20 virgin female 5-wk old C57BL/6 mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratories (Bar Harbor, ME); these mice made up the F0 generation. C57BL/6 is an inbred strain of mouse that was chosen based on their propensity to perform voluntary wheel running, their common use in research studies of exercise, nutrition, metabolism, and gene expression, and their fecundity. Until 8 weeks of age, these animals were kept sedentary (i.e., unexposed to voluntary running wheel; standard cage conditions) and group-housed with same-sex mice.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140317477735808" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms498738f1.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms498738f1.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/bin/nihms498738f1.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm140317477735808" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Figure 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p11">Experimental design (A) overview and (B) timeline.</p></div></div></div><p id="P8" class="p p-last">At 8 weeks of age, each sex was randomly split into two condition groups: exercise (EX) and sedentary (SED) (n=10 per group). F0 animals were placed into standard cages (F0 SED) or in cages with computer-monitored voluntary running wheels (F0 EX). F0 mice were housed individually in their respective cages for 10 weeks. Following this intervention males and females from like conditions (EX or SED) were randomly paired for mating. The F0 EX breeding pairs had continued access to the running wheel throughout the breeding period, however running activity could not be monitored during the breeding period as there were two mice in each cage. Males were removed after pregnancy was visually confirmed by abdominal distention, vaginal plug and/or and body weight changes. After removal, F0 males had continued access to the running wheel (EX) or SED condition until sacrifice at 20 weeks (a total of 12 weeks of wheel access). F0 females had continued access to the running wheel (EX) or SED condition during pregnancy and lactation and were sacrificed at 25 weeks after the F1 mice were weaned (a total of 17 weeks of wheel access). Eight of ten breeding pairs in each condition produced viable litters. All of the F0 animals that underwent the intervention period (EX or SED condition) were included in the F0 analyses. The pups resulting from this original mating were designated as F1. A random group of 10 male and 10 female F1 pups from each condition (EX and SED) were designated as F1 breeders and were mated at 8 weeks with F1 littermates to produce the F2 generation. Of the 10 breeding pairs per condition, 7 F1 EX and 9 F1 SED pairs produced viable litters. The remaining F1 pups were glucose tolerance tested and sacrificed at 8 weeks. F2 pups from each condition were glucose tolerance tested and sacrificed at 8 weeks. Body weight was monitored weekly for all animals. F1 and F2 offspring remained sedentary throughout the experiment. Following weaning at 21&#x02013;25 days, all F1 and F2 offspring were group-housed with same-sex littermates. We used 1&#x02013;7 offspring per litter for the 8-week analyses.</p></div><div id="S5" class="sec"><h3>Intraperitoneal Glucose Tolerance Test (IPGTT)</h3><p id="P9" class="p p-first-last">Glucose tolerance tests were performed on all F0, F1, and F2 mice at 8 (F1 and F2 generations), 20 (F0 males), or 25 (F0 females) weeks. For F0 EX mice, the voluntary running wheels were locked 36 hours prior to the glucose tolerance test to limit the effect of acute exercise. All animals were fasted overnight (12 hr) prior to glucose tolerance testing. Baseline blood glucose measurements were made and then each mouse was injected intraperitoneally with 2.0 mg of D-glucose (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) per gram of body mass. Blood glucose was measured 30, 60, 90, and 120 minutes after injection in all animals; blood glucose was also measured 15 min after injection in F2 males and females. Area under the curve for concentration vs. time was calculated using the linear trapezoidal rule. Blood glucose measurements were made using a rodent-specific glucometer (AlphaTRAK; Abbott Laboratories, Abbott Park, IL) on blood removed from a tail snip. Following the glucose tolerance test, wheels were unlocked and animals were returned to <em>ad libitum</em> food and water.</p></div><div id="S6" class="sec"><h3>Tissue &#x00026; Serum Collection</h3><p id="P10" class="p p-first-last">Animals were euthanized 2&#x02013;5 days following the glucose tolerance test. To limit the effects of acute exercise and feeding, running wheels were locked 24 hours and animals were fasted for 4 hours prior to euthanasia. Euthanasia was performed under isoflurance anesthesia; the method of euthanasia was exsanguination by cardiac puncture followed by removal of the heart. Heart, liver, omental fat, cerebellum, tibialis anterior (TA), extensor digitorum longus (EDL), soleus, plantaris, gastrocnemius, and quadriceps muscles were dissected, weighed, and flash frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at &#x02212;80&#x000b0;C until analysis. Approximately 1 mL of blood was obtained from a cardiac puncture and allowed to coagulate. The coagulated blood was centrifuged at 1750 &#x000d7; g for 15 minutes to obtain serum. Serum was removed to a fresh tube and stored at &#x02212;80&#x000b0;C until analysis.</p></div><div id="S7" class="sec"><h3>Serum Measures</h3><p id="P11" class="p p-first-last">Serum triglyceride and glycerol were measured using the Serum Triglyceride Determination Kit (TR0100; Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO). Serum insulin was measured following a 4-hour fast using a mouse-specific ELISA kit (80-INSMS-E01, ALPCO Diagnostics, Salem, NH).</p></div><div id="S8" class="sec"><h3>Tissue Preparation</h3><p id="P12" class="p p-first-last">The gastrocnemius muscle was chosen for analysis because it is a mixed-fiber type muscle (<a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode">Augusto <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>) and therefore may be more representative of the average mouse skeletal muscle than a predominantly fast or slow muscle. Whole gastrocnemius muscle was powdered in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was isolated with Trizol reagent (15596-026, Life Technologies, Grand Island, NY), DNase-treated, and quantified with the NanoDrop (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) spectrophotometer. Reverse transcription was performed with 1&#x003bc;g of total RNA with the High-Capacity cDNA RT kit (4368813, Life Technologies, Grand Island, NY).</p></div><div id="S9" class="sec"><h3>Microarray</h3><p id="P13" class="p p-first-last">Genome-wide analyses of gene expression were performed on subsets of F1 and F2 males. Total RNA was extracted from powdered gastrocnemius muscle using the RNeasy Fibrous Tissue Mini Kit (Qiagen, Germantown, MD). All samples were diluted to 98 ng/&#x003bc;l and pooled by generation and condition (5 pooled samples per array). A total of 8 arrays were performed (2 arrays each for F1 EX males, F1 SED males, F2 EX males, and F2 SED males). Microarray experiments were performed as two-color experiments using GeneChip Mouse Exon 1.0 ST Array chips (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA). The microarray gene expression data were imported from the probe cell intensity CEL files and the Affymetrix Expression Console software was used to calculate the summary measure of the probe level data. These microarray data have been deposited in the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3783594&amp;issue-id=227863&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>) website and can be accessed through GEO Series accession number <a id="__tag_336496442" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40469" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE40469"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE40469","term_id":"40469"}}</span>GSE40469</a>.</p></div><div id="S10" class="sec"><h3>Gene Expression</h3><p id="P14" class="p p-first-last">Real-time quantitative PCR was used to assess the gene expression level of adiponectin (<em>Adipoq)</em>, cell death-inducing DFFA-like effector c (<em>Cidec)</em>, and stearoyl-Coenzyme A desaturase 1 (<em>Scd1)</em> (n=10&#x02013;20 per group). Primer and probe sequences were designed for each gene&#x02019;s mRNA sequence using PrimeTime qPCR Assay designer (IDT). <em>18S</em> rRNA was used as an expression control for both real-time and gel-based PCR and did not differ between treatment groups. RT-PCR was used to measure the expression of metabolic genes (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma, coactivator 1 alpha, <em>Ppargc1a</em>; pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase, isozyme 4, <em>Pdk4</em>; peroxisome proliferator activated receptor alpha, delta, and gamma, <em>Ppara, Ppard</em>, and <em>Pparg</em>, aminolevulinate, delta-, synthase 1, <em>Alas1</em>; hexokinase 2, <em>Hk2</em>; cytochrome c, somatic, <em>Cycs</em>; citrate synthase, <em>Cs</em>; and cytochrome c oxidase subunit I, <em>Cox1</em>) (n=7&#x02013;10 per group). Primer and probe sequences and PCR conditions are available upon request. Products were visualized on 1.5% agarose gels using ethidium bromide. qPCR data were normalized to <em>18S</em> using the &#x02212;&#x00394;Ct method (<a href="#R43" rid="R43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496393">Schmittgen &#x00026; Livak, 2008</a>) and expressed as fold induction (2<sup>&#x02212;&#x00394;Ct</sup>) of mRNA expression compared to the corresponding EX group (1.0-fold induction). Relative band intensities from PCR gels were be analyzed with NIH ImageJ software and normalized to <em>18s</em>. Values are shown as means &#x000b1; standard error.</p></div><div id="S11" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Statistics</h3><p id="P15" class="p p-first-last">Two-tailed t-tests were used to compare body and tissue masses, serum measures, and IPGTT AUC between EX and SED groups within each sex and generation. A repeated-measures ANOVA was used to compare the IPGTT response between EX and SED groups within each sex and generation. One- (F1 male RT-qPCR analysis) and two- (all other analyses) tailed t-tests were used to compare gene expression between EX and SED groups within each sex and generation using SPSS version 18. Statistical significance was accepted at p &#x0003c; 0.05. A 2-stage ANOVA procedure was carried out on the 4 arrays of the F1 generation and F2 generations, respectively. Details about this procedure can be found in Lee (<a href="#R26" rid="R26" class=" bibr popnode">Lee, 2004</a>). Briefly, in the first-stage ANOVA, a one-way ANOVA model for EX vs. SED ancestry was fitted on the entire dataset, resulting in normalized estimates of gene expression centered by the ancestry factor. These normalized values were used in a second-stage ANOVA to identify which probe sets were significantly differentially expressed between EX and SED ancestries.</p></div></div><div id="S12" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S12title">Results</h2><p id="P16" class="p p-first">During the pre-breeding intervention period, F0 EX males ran an average of 6558 &#x000b1; 503 m &#x000d7; 24 hr<sup>&#x02212;1</sup> while F0 EX females ran a significantly greater (p &#x0003c; 0.05) average of 8378 &#x000b1; 533 m &#x000d7; 24 hr<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>. Running behavior was only measured prior to mating, though the F0 EX mice had continued access to the voluntary running wheel throughout breeding, pregnancy and lactation. To examine the effects of parental exercise, we characterized the body mass, various tissue masses, glucose tolerance, and serum insulin, glycerol, and triglyceride levels in the original parent generation (F0) and F1 and F2 offspring from EX and SED ancestries.</p><div id="S13" class="sec"><h3>F0 generation</h3><div id="S14" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Males </h4><p id="P17" class="p p-first-last">After the intervention period, F0 EX males weighed significantly less than F0 SED males (p=0.024; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). F0 EX males also had significantly less omental fat mass compared to SED males (p=0.003; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>), a difference that was maintained after normalizing to body mass (p=0.009; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). Additionally, when normalized to body mass, F0 EX males had greater EDL mass and lower omental fat mass compared to F0 SED males (p=0.021 and p=0.001, respectively; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). There were no other differences in organ or tissue mass in F0 males. Baseline blood glucose and glucose tolerance was not affected by the EX intervention in F0 males (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Figure 2A</span></a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140317477732496" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3783594_nihms498738f2.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms498738f2.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/bin/nihms498738f2.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140317477732496" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F2/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F2"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2">Figure 2</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p12">Blood glucose concentration during an intraperitoneal glucose tolerance test in: (A) F0 males, (B) F0 females, (C) F1 males, (D) F1 females, (E) F2 males, and (F) F2 females. After normalizing to body mass, the difference in TA mass was no longer significant, but omental fat mass remained lower in F1 EX females (p=0.017; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). No differences in baseline blood glucose concentration or glucose tolerance were observed between EX and SED in F1 females (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Figure 2D</span></a>), but serum insulin was significantly higher (55%) in F1 EX females compared to SED females (p=0.008; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3783594/table/T3/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T3" rid-ob="ob-T3" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 3</span></a>). Sample sizes were n=7&#x02013;10. *significantly different from EX within sex and generation (p &#x0003c; 0.05)</p></div></div></div></div></div><div id="S23" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S23title">Discussion</h2><p id="P28" class="p p-first">In this study, we sought to determine the effect of EX ancestry on the morphological and metabolic phenotypes of two generations of offspring. This study investigated the influence of maternal and paternal EX ancestry on anatomical characteristics and metabolic phenotypes, including skeletal muscle gene expression. Our results indicate broad effects of EX ancestry on various offspring phenotypes, including body mass, fat and muscle mass, fasting serum insulin, and glucose tolerance. Further, we observed effects of EX ancestry on the expression of several gastrocnemius muscle mRNAs through two generations of offspring.</p><p id="P29">Following the EX training period, F0 EX males were lighter with less omental fat mass and greater normalized muscle mass than SED males. F0 EX females had greater soleus mass than SED females but did not differ in fat or body mass. Other researchers have also observed lower body mass in male, but not female mice after a period of voluntary exercise (<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496385">De Bono <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>), though this is not universal as some have observed decreased body mass in both sexes (<a href="#R48" rid="R48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496374">Swallow <em>et al.</em>, 2005</a>) or no differences in either sex (<a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496412">Allen <em>et al.</em>, 2001</a>). There were no differences in glucose tolerance in males following the exercise intervention and a tendency for improved glucose tolerance in exercised females. Though forced exercise can induce improvements in glucose tolerance in mice (<a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496417">James <em>et al.</em>, 1983</a>), no other studies that we are aware of have observed an effect of voluntary exercise on glucose tolerance in non-obese mice on standard chow. Our voluntary wheel running approach alleviates the induction of systemic handling stress seen with forced running or swimming, however it does not allow us to control exercise volume or intensity. We anticipated that voluntary running wheel would be sufficient to produce significant adaptations as measurable changes in muscle fiber type content have been observed in comparable studies in the gastrocnemius muscle in the same mouse strain as used here (<a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496362">Allen <em>et al.</em>, 2001</a>).</p><p id="P30">F1 females with EX ancestry were lighter than those with SED ancestry at 8 weeks of age; no other differences in offspring body mass were observed. Maternal exercise training has previously led to offspring with lower (<a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496366">Pinto &#x00026; Shetty, 1995</a>) or not different (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496432">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>) body mass. Our study does not allow us to identify a specific mechanism leading to lower body mass in F1 EX females because F0 dams were exposed to exercise from pre-conception through gestation and lactation (e.g. epigenetic, in utereo, or lactational mechanisms). There does not appear to be a transgenerational effect (e.g., F2 offspring were unaffected) of parental exercise on body mass at 8 weeks of age in our study. Only one other study we are aware of has examined the effect of maternal exercise on offspring mass over multiple generations. <a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496394">Pinto and Shetty (1995)</a> found EX ancestry led to decreased body mass in both F1 and F2 offspring; however, the exercise intervention in that study was forced swimming, which is often questioned as a form of exercise in a rodent (<a href="#R3" rid="R3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496396">Baldwin, 1985</a>) and may have induced an additional maternal stress independent of the exercise stimulus. Maternal stress alone has induced both higher (<a href="#R45" rid="R45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_448687004">Schulz <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>) and lower (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496416">Patin <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>) offspring body mass in other studies.</p><p id="P31">Although we did not measure body composition, we examined individual tissue masses to identify potential differences in organ mass in the offspring. F1 males with EX parents had lower muscle masses than F1 males from SED parents, and F1 females from EX parents had lower TA and omental fat mass compared to F1 females from SED parents. Recent evidence suggests that the maternal environment can have significant effects on developmental partitioning of muscle, adipose tissue, and connective tissue precursors (<a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_448687003">Du <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>); however, we are unable to confirm if this would explain our differences. Interestingly, F1 EX males had lower muscle masses compared to F1 SED males, however, there were no corresponding differences in omental fat pad mass. <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496414">Carter <em>et al.</em> (2012<em>a</em>)</a> observed lower fat mass and higher lean mass percentages in mature (39 wk) male, but not female, offspring of exercised dams, while in a similar study, no body composition differences were observed (<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496376">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>b</em></a>). Though these results are not directly comparable due to the difference in offspring age and method of body composition assessment, it is interesting that we observed a contrasting effect, with lower fat mass in only female offspring. In the F2 offspring, EX females had lower relative muscle and omental fat pad mass compared to F2 SED females. The differential effects of EX ancestry on offspring body and tissue mass between sexes and generations suggest the observed differences may be induced through sex- and/or generation-specific mechanisms.</p><p id="P32">Glucose tolerance was affected only in F2 female offspring where those with EX ancestry had slightly reduced glucose tolerance than those with SED ancestry. It is possible that the greater fasting insulin concentration observed in F1 EX females could have led to impaired glucose disposal and thus hyperglycemia during pregnancy if insulin resistance developed in the F1 EX females. Mild maternal hyperglycemia during pregnancy has been linked with reduced glucose tolerance in rat offspring (<a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496398">Gauguier <em>et al. 1991</em></a>). Two recent studies have examined the influence of maternal exercise on glucose tolerance in healthy animals. In both, offspring from exercised dams had improved glucose tolerance as well as insulin sensitivity (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496425">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>, <a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496395">2012<em>b</em></a>). It is critical to note, however, that these improvements were not observed until offspring were 31&#x02013;32 weeks (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496365">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>) or 10 months of age (<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496382">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>b</em></a>). Together with our results this indicates that while the positive effects of exercise training on glucose tolerance may be transmittable to offspring, they are not yet apparent at a young age.</p><p id="P33">The effects of EX and EX ancestry on gene expression were examined by a combination of global mRNA profiling and targeted gene expression analysis of gastrocnemius muscle. Our study is the first to examine the effect of maternal exercise or exercise ancestry on skeletal muscle gene expression. Our microarray approach revealed a number of mRNA transcripts were differentially expressed as a function of EX ancestry in both generations, with a greater number of transcripts affected in the F2 compared to the F1 generation. Surprisingly, there was no overlap in differentially expressed transcripts between F1 and F2 offspring, again highlighting the difference between <em>in utero</em> versus transgenerational effects. Three of the differentially regulated transcripts (<em>Adipoq</em>, <em>Cidec</em>, and <em>Scd1</em>) have previously been associated with lipid metabolism and specifically intramuscular lipogenesis. <em>Adipoq</em> codes for the adipokine adiponectin, that when secreted from adipocytes promotes fatty acid oxidation and enhances insulin sensitivity (<a href="#R49" rid="R49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496379">Tomas <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>) in muscle. <em>Adipoq</em> mRNA is expressed in muscle (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496408">Delaigle <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>; <a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496372">Liu <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>), but it is unclear whether the resultant protein has similar effects to circulating adiponectin. <em>Adipoq</em> mRNA in muscle is associated with greater intramuscular fat (<a href="#R55" rid="R55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496426">Wang <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>) and lipotoxicity (<a href="#R11" rid="R11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496375">Delaigle <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>), but also enhanced insulin sensitivity (<a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496436">Liu <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>). <em>Cidec</em> codes for cell death-inducing DFFA-like effector c, which promotes apoptosis (<a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496421">Liang <em>et al.</em>, 2003</a>) and is upregulated in adipose tissue during adipogenesis (<a href="#R42" rid="R42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496419">Puri <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>). In muscle, <em>Cidec</em> mRNA expression is associated with de novo lipogenesis (<a href="#R56" rid="R56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_379293098">Wang <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). <em>Scd1</em> codes for steroyl-CoA desaturase-1, which enzymatically regulates the formation of monounsaturated fatty acids within the cell (<a href="#R25" rid="R25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496407">Kim <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). <em>Scd1</em> mRNA expression in muscle is associated with the increased intramuscular triglyceride concentrations observed with both obesity (<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_448687005">Hulver <em>et al.</em>, 2005</a>) and exercise training (<a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496401">Dobrzyn <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). These lipogenic transcripts were downregulated in F1 EX male offspring, with a similar pattern observed in the F0 and F2 generations. In females, we found the opposite result, an overall tendency towards increased expression of <em>Adipoq</em>, <em>Cidec</em>, and <em>Scd1</em> in EX/EX ancestry females. Recalling that F1 EX females had less omental fat than F1 SED ancestry females while there were no differences in fat mass in male offspring, we speculate that EX ancestry females preferentially decreased adipose tissue lipid storage compared to SED ancestry females while EX ancestry males preferentially decreased ectopic (including muscle) lipid storage.</p><p id="P34">In addition to the targets identified by the microarray, we examined a number of <em>a priori</em> metabolic gene expression targets. Direct exposure to EX did not lead to differences in basal expression of the <em>a priori</em> metabolic genes in F0 males and only <em>Pdk4</em> expression was significantly lower in EX females. Although altered expression levels of the selected genes have been observed following exercise in previous studies (<a href="#R41" rid="R41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496397">Puntschart <em>et al.</em>, 1995</a>; <a href="#R44" rid="R44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496424">Schmutz <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>), the lack of expression differences in our study is not surprising because sacrifice occurred 24 hours after the last bout of wheel running and in humans, the exercise-induced increase in transcription of some metabolic genes is transient and expression returns to baseline by 24 hours post-exercise (<a href="#R39" rid="R39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496400">Pilegaard <em>et al.</em>, 2000</a>). In F1 offpring, EX males tended to have lower expression levels of <em>Cs</em>, <em>Ppara</em>, and <em>Alas1</em>. If these gene expression differences reflect a more extensive effect of EX ancestry on skeletal muscle gene expression then perhaps the morphological differences seen in the F1 EX males (smaller soleus, EDL, and quadriceps muscle mass) can be partially explained by an impact of EX ancestry on skeletal muscle gene expression and tissue development. Interestingly, there were more significant effects of EX ancestry on metabolic gene expression in F2 offspring than F1 offspring. The magnitude of the difference between EX and SED ancestries, however, was smaller, which may explain why the differences in gene expression were not associated with gross morphological changes in the F2 generation. Alternatively, EX may uniquely affect skeletal muscle gene expression in the F2 offspring through the germ line whereas the gross morphological effects observed in the F1 generation may be related to a direct influence of exercise during the F1 <em>in utero</em> period.</p><p id="P35">Altered maternal nutrition and other stressors have been associated with epigenetic changes in a number of genes and tissues, however in many of these studies the associated physiological effects of these gene changes do not present until later in life or following a metabolic challenge such as a high fat diet (<a href="#R54" rid="R54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496406">Vickers, 2011</a>). Similarly, the lack of overt physiological phenotype observed in the present study may be related to the young age (8 wk) of our animals and/or their exposure to only typical cage conditions and diet. The body composition differences between offspring of sedentary and exercised dams observed by <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496431">Carter <em>et al.</em> (2012<em>a</em>)</a> were observed at 39&#x02013;40 weeks of age. Further, the authors note that the observed differences in glucose tolerance were not detectable until 7 months of age (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496418">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>). Thus we believe future studies should investigate the effects of EX ancestry on metabolic phenotypes such as glucose tolerance in animals following aging or a metabolic challenge.</p><p id="P36">We observed differences in gene expression patterns between males and females in multiple offspring groups. For several genes, the expression pattern was inverted between F1 males and F2 females, with <em>Alas1</em>, <em>Hk2</em>, <em>Ppard</em>, and <em>Ppargc1a</em> all lower in EX F1 male compared to SED while they were higher in F2 EX females compared to SED. Overall, EX ancestry led to primarily greater mRNA expression in female offspring and lower mRNA expression in male offspring. These patterns along with the body and tissue weight results demonstrate an apparent sex-specific effect of EX ancestry on offspring outcomes. Sex-specific environmental influences have been observed previously for offspring phenotypes in both humans (<a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496384">Stein <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>; <a href="#R30" rid="R30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496413">Lumey <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) and rodents (<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496409">Dunn &#x00026; Bale, 2009</a>; <a href="#R53" rid="R53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496415">van Straten <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>; <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496392">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>). In humans, prenatal exposure to famine was associated with increased BMI (<a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496387">Stein <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>) and blood lipids (<a href="#R30" rid="R30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496427">Lumey <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) in women, but not men. In mice, <em>in utero</em> protein restriction influenced the development of metabolic dysfunction in female, but not male offspring (<a href="#R53" rid="R53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496438">van Straten <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>). Additionally, maternal high fat diet induced greater body length in both sexes of second-generation offspring, but higher Igf1 levels in females only (<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496430">Dunn &#x00026; Bale, 2009</a>). With regard to exercise, voluntary maternal wheel running in the perinatal period led to higher lean and lower fat mass percentages in mature male, but not female offspring (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496363">Carter <em>et al.</em>, 2012<em>a</em></a>). One proposed mechanism for these sex-dependent observations is a difference in placental function between male and female fetuses. Chronic maternal stress in mice led to sex-specific differences in placenta gene expression where male placentas had greater expression levels of genes important to growth while female placentas were not affected (<a href="#R34" rid="R34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496371">Mueller &#x00026; Bale, 2008</a>). Several possible mechanisms could lead to this sex-specificity, such as an effect of the environmental stimulus on early development (gametogenesis or embryonic sexual differentiation). Alternately, the environment could interact with the offspring&#x02019;s own sex chromosomes or hormones later in development.</p><p id="P37">A critical difference between our study and the other studies of maternal exercise and offspring phenotypes is the inbred status of the mice used in our study. The rats used in the <a href="#R40" rid="R40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496367">Pinto and Shetty (1995)</a> study and the mice and rats used in the <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496370">Carter <em>et al.</em> (2012<em>a</em></a>; <a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496391">2012<em>b</em></a>) studies were both from outbred lines, while we studied C57Bl/6 mice, an inbred strain. Though there are additional confounding factors, the effect of maternal exercise in outbred lines appears to be more dramatic than the effect of exercise ancestry observed in the present study. Interestingly, other environmentally-induced transgenerational effects on offspring health have also been shown to be dependent on the inbred vs. outbred status of the rodent line used for the experiment (<a href="#R46" rid="R46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496405">Skinner <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>; <a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496434">Guerrero-Bosagna <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>). <a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496389">Guerrero-Bosagna et al. (2012)</a> found maternal vinclozolin treatment led to increased adult onset disease in an outbred, but not an inbred mouse model. This is the only study we are aware of to directly compare transgenerational phenotypes in an inbred and outbred mouse strain within the same study. The mechanisms contributing to this differential response between inbred and outbred lines of rodents have not yet been identified. However, we speculate that the stress of continued inbreeding may modify the epigenetic mechanisms responsible for manifestation of environmentally induced phenotypic changes, thus making the inbred organism less susceptible to epigenetic reprogramming of the germ line in response to environmental factors. Together these studies suggest a critical role of rodent strain when examining the role of the environment in modifying transgenerational phenotypes.</p><p id="P38">We would like to acknowledge some limitations to our study related to the breeding and pre-weaning methodology. The litter sizes reported reflect those offspring that were weaned from each breeding pair. We did not collect information on litter size, survival or body mass prior to weaning. Additionally, we recognize that the variability in litter size and weaning time could have affected early offspring nutrition. Overnutrition induced by culling litters during lactation leads to neonatal overgrowth and can induce the development of obesity and glucose intolerance (<a href="#R38" rid="R38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496423">Pentinat <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>) as well as a number of other cardiometabolic risk factors (<a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496390">Habbout <em>et al.</em>, 2013</a>). As noted previously, 1&#x02013;7 offspring per litter were used for analysis in this study. The use of multiple offspring per litter was unavoidable due to sample size limitations. However, it is important to note that the use of multiple offspring per litter could confound the findings due to the &#x0201c;litter effect&#x0201d;, where pups within litters are more similar to one another than pups between litters (<a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_336496386">Holson <em>et al.</em>, 1976</a>).</p><p id="P39" class="p p-last">In summary, EX ancestry affects various offspring phenotypes across two generations, but in a generation- and sex-dependent manner. We have observed effects at both the whole-body (body and tissue mass and glucose tolerance) and skeletal muscle gene expression levels, which together reflect a broad impact of EX ancestry. This study is the first to examine the effect of exercise ancestry on a broad range of metabolic phenotypes, however this study is descriptive in nature and the specific mechanisms responsible for the observed phenotypes were not elucidated. Future studies should examine the potential influences of maternal vs. paternal exercise, and their cooperative effects. Similarly, additional work should isolate the EX exposure to discrete phases (i.e. pre-conception, gestation, lactation) to provide further insight into potential mechanisms.</p></div><div id="unreferenced-ft" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><div class="unreferenced">​
