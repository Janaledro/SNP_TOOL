However, the true promise of this approach can not be estimated until the scientific community has robust baseline data describing variation in gene expression patterns in normal individuals. Using a large representative sample set of postmenopausal women (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;286) in the Norwegian Women and Cancer (NOWAC) postgenome study, we investigated variability of whole blood gene expression in the general population. In particular, we examined changes in blood gene expression caused by technical variability, normal inter-individual differences, and exposure variables at proportions and levels relevant to real-life situations. We observe that the overall changes in gene expression are subtle, implying the need for careful analytic approaches of the data. In particular, technical variability may not be ignored and subsequent adjustments must be considered in any analysis. Many new candidate genes were identified that are differentially expressed according to inter-individual (i.e. fasting, BMI) and exposure (i.e. smoking) factors, thus establishing that these effects are mirrored in blood. By focusing on the biological implications instead of directly comparing gene lists from several related studies in the literature, our analytic approach was able to identify significant similarities and effects consistent across these reports. This establishes the feasibility of blood gene expression profiling, if they are predicated upon careful experimental design and analysis in order to minimize confounding signals, artifacts of sample preparation and processing, and inter-individual differences.</p></div></div><div id="idm140551864047264" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140551864047264title">Author Summary</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">As a major defence and transport system, blood cells are capable of adjusting gene expression in response to various clinical, biochemical, and pathological conditions. Here, we expand our understanding about the nature and extent of variation in gene expression from blood among healthy individuals. Using a large representative sample of postmenopausal women (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;286) in the Norwegian Women and Cancer (NOWAC) postgenome study, we investigated blood gene expression changes due to normal inter-individuality (age, body mass index, fasting status), and exposure variables (smoking, hormone therapy, and medication use) at proportions and levels found in real life situations. Host genes were found to vary by inter-individual (i.e. fasting, BMI) and exposure (i.e. smoking) factors, and these gene lists may be used as a basis for further hypothesis development. Our study also establishes the feasibility of blood gene expression profiling for disease prediction, diagnosis, or prognosis, but underscores the necessity of care in study design and analysis to account for inter-individual differences and confounding signals.</p></div></div><div id="s1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s1title">Introduction</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first">There is growing evidence that transcriptome analysis of peripheral blood cells is a valuable tool for determining signatures related to disease <a href="#pgen.1000873-Twine1" rid="pgen.1000873-Twine1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628151">[1]</a>&#x02013;<a href="#pgen.1000873-Sharma1" rid="pgen.1000873-Sharma1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628162">[5]</a> and drug-response <a href="#pgen.1000873-Burczynski1" rid="pgen.1000873-Burczynski1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628194">[6]</a>. Differences in blood gene expression may also reflect the effects of a particular exposure, such as smoking <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lampe1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lampe1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628204">[7]</a>, metal fumes <a href="#pgen.1000873-Wang1" rid="pgen.1000873-Wang1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628147">[8]</a>, or ionizing radiation <a href="#pgen.1000873-Amundson1" rid="pgen.1000873-Amundson1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628171">[9]</a>. In our previous research, we studied gene expression profiles from whole blood related to hormone therapy (HT) use in postmenopausal women <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux1" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628157">[10]</a> and identified specific challenges raised by inter-individual variability when isolating signals associated with defined exposure levels. Although blood gene expression profiling promises molecular-level insight into disease mechanisms, there remains a lack of baseline data describing the nature and extent of variability in blood gene expression in the general population. Characterizations of this variation and the underlying factors that most influence gene expression amongst healthy individuals will play an important role in the feasibility, design and analysis of future blood-based studies investigating biomarkers for exposure, disease progression, diagnosis or prognosis <a href="#pgen.1000873-Wild1" rid="pgen.1000873-Wild1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628212">[11]</a>.</p><p id="__p5">Several studies <a href="#pgen.1000873-Debey1" rid="pgen.1000873-Debey1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628129">[12]</a>&#x02013;<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628134">[18]</a> have reported that technical variables such as collection, transportation, storage of blood samples, RNA isolation method and choice of microarray platform, in addition to biological effects, can influence gene expression profiles. These technical factors associated with the processing and preparation of human blood and subsequent microarray hybridization represent significant challenges in the analysis of variability.</p><p id="__p6">Furthermore, a few previous studies have used microarrays to analyze blood from healthy volunteers and found that inter-individual sample variation was associated with sex <a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628154">[18]</a>, age <a href="#pgen.1000873-Eady1" rid="pgen.1000873-Eady1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628187">[13]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628183">[18]</a>, the time of day the sample was taken <a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628172">[18]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Radich1" rid="pgen.1000873-Radich1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628137">[19]</a>, and the proportion of the different cell populations comprising the blood sample <a href="#pgen.1000873-Eady1" rid="pgen.1000873-Eady1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628139">[13]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628203">[18]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Cobb1" rid="pgen.1000873-Cobb1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628146">[20]</a>. However to date, all such studies have focused on gene expression profiles generated from a small set of samples not representative of the general population using different blood cell subtypes. For several reasons including the small sample sizes, these studies have been restricted to the analysis of a small number of variables simultaneously, thus ignoring possible interaction and confounder effects.</p><p id="__p7">Finally, an understanding of these causes of variability would represent a significant step forward in the identification and evaluation of the disease and disease risk biomarkers. Most if not all genes are involved in molecular pathways that provide mechanistic insight in response to exposure or disease development. Pathway depictions are usually simplified, ignoring interactions with other pathways, and we often have incomplete knowledge about the specific interplay of the many elements in almost any particular system.</p><p id="__p8" class="p p-last">Using a large representative sample set of postmenopausal women in the Norwegian Women and Cancer (NOWAC) postgenome study <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux2" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628186">[21]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Lund1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lund1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628143">[22]</a> processed via a standardized blood collection procedure and via an experimentally validated microarray platform <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux3" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628215">[23]</a>, we investigate here the baseline variability of whole blood gene expression profiles. This represents the first comprehensive cross-sectional analysis of blood gene expression changes related to multiple inter-individual and exposure variables, and opens the new research discipline of systems epidemiology <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lund2" rid="pgen.1000873-Lund2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628202">[24]</a>. In this setting, we investigated blood gene expression changes due to technical variability, normal inter-individuality, and exposure variables at proportions and levels relevant to real life situations, and establish that these effects are mirrored in the blood transcriptome.</p></div><div id="s2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s2title">Results</h2><div id="s2a" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Study design</h3><div id="s2a1" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Population characteristics </h4><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">Characteristics of women included in the analyses are described in <a href="#pgen.1000873.s002" rid="pgen.1000873.s002" class=" supplementary-material">Table S1A</a>. In average, smokers consumed 2.8 cigarettes (sd 3.8) before blood sampling and 10.2 cigarettes (sd 6.3) the day before. The mean body mass index (BMI) was 25.6 kg/m<sup>2</sup> (SD 4.2) with most women either of normal BMI (51.7&#x00025;) or overweight/obese (45.8&#x00025;). With respect to gene set enrichment analysis, the immunoglobulin gene set (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;36) had the lowest global test enrichment <em>p</em>-value (<em>p</em>-value&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.03), but a high false discovery rate (FDR&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.92). One publication <a href="#pgen.1000873-Eady1" rid="pgen.1000873-Eady1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628214">[13]</a> found a gene list (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;14 genes; N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;9 after mapping to our Applied Biosystems probe IDs) derived from blood and associated with age but was not significantly enriched in our dataset (global test <em>p</em>-value&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.45).</p></div></div></div><div id="s3" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s3title">Discussion</h2><p id="__p23" class="p p-first">Peripheral blood is an ideal surrogate tissue as it has the potential to reflect responses to changes in the immediate and distant environments by alterations of gene expression levels. Given the number of factors that influence gene regulation and expression, it is not surprising that often more than one strong signal is present in any given high-dimensional dataset. The external validity of NOWAC as a representative sample of the Norwegian female population has been verified in several methodological analyses and found to be acceptable <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lund3" rid="pgen.1000873-Lund3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628199">[35]</a>. Studies of the internal validity, including reliability, have been undertaken for dietary questions <a href="#pgen.1000873-Hjartaker1" rid="pgen.1000873-Hjartaker1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_585245986">[36]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Parr1" rid="pgen.1000873-Parr1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628138">[37]</a>, menopausal status, and use of HT <a href="#pgen.1000873-Hjartaker1" rid="pgen.1000873-Hjartaker1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_585245987">[36]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Waaseth1" rid="pgen.1000873-Waaseth1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628189">[38]</a>, whereas validation of variables measuring physical activity remain ongoing.</p><div id="s3a" class="sec"><h3>Inter-individual variability</h3><p id="__p24" class="p p-first">In addition to technical variability, substantial differences in gene expression profiles were identified between individuals with respect to exposure. Overall, the functional enrichment of significant single genes and gene set enrichment analyses show that high-throughput gene expression studies implicate similar (although not identical) underlying biology across several studies. Whereas age did not induce a large effect in blood gene expression for our cohort of postmenopausal women aged from 48 to 62 years, pathways and gene sets affected by smoking and, to a lesser extent both BMI and fasting, are numerous and interconnected. Some expression profiles associated with these variables may also be associated with other factors (e.g., lower levels of exercise, age). A host of new candidate genes for regulation by inter-individual (fasting, BMI) and exposure (smoking) factors were identified which could be used as a basis for hypothesis development.</p><p id="__p25">Several processes associated with smoking were involved in cardiovascular regulation by G-coupled receptors (i.e. purinergic, adrenergic beta-1, urotensin II or thromboxan A2 receptors) or protein activity (i.e. thrombospondin type-1, fibronectin type-3). Consistent with previous observations that smoking reduces olfactory sensitivity in a dose- and time-dependent manner <a href="#pgen.1000873-Frye1" rid="pgen.1000873-Frye1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628126">[39]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Katotomichelakis1" rid="pgen.1000873-Katotomichelakis1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628180">[40]</a>, we find that smoking significantly impairs blood gene expression of olfactory receptors. We also observed that smokers have deregulated gene expressions of several P450 cytochromes which catalyse mono-oxygenase activity that can both toxify and detoxify carcinogenic compounds. As established in normal lung <a href="#pgen.1000873-Schlager1" rid="pgen.1000873-Schlager1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628159">[41]</a> and rats <a href="#pgen.1000873-Orosz1" rid="pgen.1000873-Orosz1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628140">[42]</a>, smokers tend to have a small increase in NAD(P)H:(quinone-acceptor) oxidoreductase compared to non-smokers.</p><p id="__p26">Two previous studies <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lampe1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lampe1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628191">[7]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-vanLeeuwen1" rid="pgen.1000873-vanLeeuwen1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628176">[31]</a> have examined the effects of cigarette smoking on leukocyte gene expression in circulation and both of the associated signatures had the most significant enrichment scores over all gene sets considered here. Inflammatory responses previously associated with smoking <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lampe1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lampe1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628161">[7]</a> were up-regulated in the blood expression of smokers in our dataset. Lending support that smoking has immune and inflammatory effects, specific blood cell gene signatures <a href="#pgen.1000873-Eady1" rid="pgen.1000873-Eady1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628150">[13]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628164">[18]</a> (i.e. increased monocytes and decreased red blood cell and natural killer cell signalling) were differentially expressed according to smoking status. This is consistent with previous observations showing that the total numbers of peripheral leukocytes differ by smoking status <a href="#pgen.1000873-Jensen1" rid="pgen.1000873-Jensen1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628130">[43]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Schwartz1" rid="pgen.1000873-Schwartz1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628185">[44]</a>. Core genes up-regulated in non-smokers from the enriched hormone-related gene sets <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux1" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628188">[10]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Frasor1" rid="pgen.1000873-Frasor1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628125">[33]</a> were predicted to be involved in neuroactive ligand-receptor interactions like prostaglandin receptors. Elevated prostaglandin E2 synthesis has been previously reported in smokers in comparison with non-smokers <a href="#pgen.1000873-Badawi1" rid="pgen.1000873-Badawi1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628196">[45]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Beloqui1" rid="pgen.1000873-Beloqui1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628133">[46]</a>. The predicted gene network also reflects the effect of smoking on hormone levels with increased secretion of prolactin and glucagon <a href="#pgen.1000873-Kapoor1" rid="pgen.1000873-Kapoor1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628163">[47]</a>. Two pathways related to exercise <a href="#pgen.1000873-Connolly1" rid="pgen.1000873-Connolly1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628168">[32]</a> were also found up-regulated in non-smokers, which may simply be due to an underlying prevalence of active exercisers in non-smokers <a href="#pgen.1000873-Kvaavik1" rid="pgen.1000873-Kvaavik1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628149">[48]</a>.</p><p id="__p27">In our study, we found BMI class associated with blood gene expression changes involved in several immune processes including diabetes type I. It has been reported that several immune functions are dysregulated in obesity <a href="#pgen.1000873-Tanaka1" rid="pgen.1000873-Tanaka1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628175">[49]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Nieman1" rid="pgen.1000873-Nieman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628206">[50]</a> and both genetic and environmental factors such as obesity have been implicated as triggers in the pathogenesis of diabetes. The role of autoimmunity in the origins of type I diabetes is well-known, including a role in latent autoimmune diabetes in adults <a href="#pgen.1000873-Zimmet1" rid="pgen.1000873-Zimmet1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628210">[51]</a> and several observations suggest that autoimmunity may be part of type II diabetes <a href="#pgen.1000873-Syed1" rid="pgen.1000873-Syed1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628153">[52]</a>&#x02013;<a href="#pgen.1000873-Lewis1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lewis1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628152">[55]</a>. Finally, two pathways related to exercise <a href="#pgen.1000873-Connolly1" rid="pgen.1000873-Connolly1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628174">[32]</a> were also up-regulated in women with normal BMI which may be due to a higher prevalence of physical exercise than in overweight/obese women.</p><p id="__p28">Of all the variables considered, fasting was associated with the largest number of genes, but few genes were identified as core genes possibly due to the limited number of fasting women (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;28) at the time of blood sampling. Selection of core genes aims to select a subset of true positives which work together (possibly in similar pathways) towards significance of the set. The significant core genes associated with fasting were generally involved in gene expression regulation and chromatin modification <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dhalluin1" rid="pgen.1000873-Dhalluin1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628178">[56]</a>&#x02013;<a href="#pgen.1000873-Klug1" rid="pgen.1000873-Klug1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628197">[58]</a>. Much of our understanding of the effects of nutrition on chromatin structure has been gleaned from model organisms, especially <em>S. cerevisiae</em>, <em>C. elegans</em>, <em>Drosophila</em>, and mice <a href="#pgen.1000873-Garfinkel1" rid="pgen.1000873-Garfinkel1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628158">[59]</a>. In humans, two previous studies were unable to characterize acute effects of food intake in blood gene expression profiles <a href="#pgen.1000873-Eady1" rid="pgen.1000873-Eady1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628181">[13]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628160">[18]</a>. One putative 784-gene signature exists <a href="#pgen.1000873-Bouwens1" rid="pgen.1000873-Bouwens1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628145">[34]</a>, however only 49 genes associated with fasting overlap with this signature. This may simply be due to chance.</p><p id="__p29" class="p p-last">Due to a significant interaction between HT and MED within our profiles, further analyses with a larger sample size are needed in order to investigate the different categories of medications, HT regimens and hormone levels, as well as their interactions in blood.</p></div><div id="s3b" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Consistency with other studies</h3><p id="__p30" class="p p-first">Differences between the genes identified and the interpretation of results in the various studies discussed here are likely to have resulted from technical differences in the array platforms used, the subset of blood cells analyzed, and the chosen analytical procedures. Several studies <a href="#pgen.1000873-Debey1" rid="pgen.1000873-Debey1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628192">[12]</a>&#x02013;<a href="#pgen.1000873-Whitney1" rid="pgen.1000873-Whitney1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628123">[18]</a> examined how gene expression profiles of blood samples are affected by technical variables. Specific blood sample collection methods result in the isolation of different blood cell subpopulations. White blood cells have been defined as the most transcriptionally active of all cell types in blood and may give the most sensitive gene expression profiles in response to defined factors <a href="#pgen.1000873-Li1" rid="pgen.1000873-Li1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628177">[60]</a>. In large epidemiological studies, RNA stabilization is compulsory and PAXgene tubes have been found satisfactory to stabilize and enable RNA extraction from whole blood cells <a href="#pgen.1000873-Rainen1" rid="pgen.1000873-Rainen1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628170">[61]</a>. While high proportions of globin RNA could reduce sensitivity with respect to certain microarray platforms <a href="#pgen.1000873-Li1" rid="pgen.1000873-Li1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628209">[60]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Debey2" rid="pgen.1000873-Debey2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628190">[62]</a>,<a href="#pgen.1000873-Liu1" rid="pgen.1000873-Liu1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628205">[63]</a>, we previously investigated two globin reduction protocols and determined that they were not beneficial when Applied Biosystems (AB) microarrays are used <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux3" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628211">[23]</a>. We found that RNA extraction and one variable related to RNA degradation (i.e. time between blood collection and freezing) had a significant global effect on blood gene expression profiles. In addition to normalization preprocessing, our results suggest that technical variability should not be ignored and possible adjustment for technical sources of variability should be considered in any analysis. Techniques such as surrogate variable analysis <a href="#pgen.1000873-Leek1" rid="pgen.1000873-Leek1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_585245984">[64]</a> may adjust for hidden sources of heterogeneity and large-scale dependence in gene expression studies <a href="#pgen.1000873-Leek2" rid="pgen.1000873-Leek2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628144">[65]</a>. As an example in our study, 25 significant surrogate variables were highly correlated to the strongest identified technical sources of noise, array lot number (canonical correlation r<sup>2</sup>&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.95), time between blood collection and freezing (canonical correlation r<sup>2</sup>&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.62) and RNA extraction (canonical correlation r<sup>2</sup>&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;0.43).</p><p id="__p31">After adjustment for technical variability, our analysis demonstrates the ability to find significant similarities between studies by focusing on the biological implications of the gene sets from each individual study, rather than the specific single genes that met the criteria for significant differential expression in each individual study. They lend support to the idea that blood gene expression studies can indeed detect exposure-specific differences and that failure to consider this type of biological variation can result in the misidentification of genes when investigating predictive, diagnostic or prognostic signatures in blood.</p><p id="__p32" class="p p-last">In conclusion, this study extends the limited baseline information currently available that describes normal patterns of variation in blood gene expression. The data generated have been made freely available and should represent a useful resource for the design of future studies including power calculations. Our results confirm the feasibility of identifying signatures of inter-individual factors (e.g. fasting, BMI) and exposure factors (e.g. smoking) in blood-based gene expression profiles, and reinforces the need for proper study design, sample preparation, and technical analysis.</p></div></div><div id="s4" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="s4title">Methods</h2><div id="s4a" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Ethics statement</h3><p id="__p33" class="p p-first">We have received approval from the Regional Committee for Medical Research Ethics for the collection and storing of questionnaire information and blood samples. The informed consent formula explicitly mentions that the blood samples can be used for gene expression analyses as well as large-scale genotyping.</p><p id="__p34">All data are stored and handled according to the permission given by the Norwegian Data Inspectorate. The Directorate of Health and Social affairs (SHD) has given us an exemption from the confidentiality of information in national registers.</p><p id="__p35" class="p p-last">Before use of the biological material, a request has been sent to the regional ethical committee for Northern-Norway. Use of biological material requires permission according to laws pertaining to biotechnology and gene technology, both of which are administered by the SHD.</p></div><div id="s4b" class="sec"><h3>Subjects</h3><p id="__p36" class="p p-first-last">The women are part of the Norwegian Women and Cancer (NOWAC) study (<a href="http://uit.no/kk/NOWAC/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://uit.no/kk/NOWAC/</a>) consisting of 172471 women who were 30 to 70 years of age at recruitment from 1991 to 2006 <a href="#pgen.1000873-Lund1" rid="pgen.1000873-Lund1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628198">[22]</a>. The NOWAC postgenome cohort study <a href="#pgen.1000873-Dumeaux2" rid="pgen.1000873-Dumeaux2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628127">[21]</a> consists of approximately 50,000 women born between 1943 and 1957, randomly drawn in groups of 500 from the NOWAC registers, who gave blood samples between 2003 and 2006 and filled in a two-page questionnaire. The two-page questionnaire included questions regarding menopausal status, weight, height; past week exposure to smoking, HT, oral contraceptives, other MED, omega-3 fatty acid, soy or other dietary supplements; and details concerning blood specimen collection (date, hour, posture). Women included in the present study received a blood collection kit and an accompanying two-page questionnaire by mail in April 2005. Among the group of 500 women, 444 (89&#x00025;) returned both citrate and PAXgene blood RNA (PreAnalytiX GmbH, Hembrechtikon, Switzerland) tubes; 3.3&#x00025; declined to participate, 0.7&#x00025; had died or migrated and 7&#x00025; did not respond. Samples were included in the study according to the following inclusion criteria: the donor was postmenopausal (99 donors excluded), blood was successfully collected in one PAXgene tube and in two plasma collection tubes (8 donors excluded), and the samples were frozen within 3 days from blood collection (9 donors excluded). Based on these criteria, 328 PAXgene blood samples were included for RNA extraction.</p></div><div id="s4c" class="sec"><h3>RNA isolation and quality control</h3><p id="__p37" class="p p-first-last">PAXgene blood RNA tubes were thawed at room temperature for 4 h. 500 &#x000b5;L of blood was removed and stored on &#x02212;70&#x000b0;C for future use. Total RNA was isolated using the PAXgene Blood RNA Isolation Kit, according to the manufacturer's manual. RNA quantity and purity was assessed using the NanoDrop ND-1000 spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher Scientific, Wilmington, Delaware, USA). The absorbance ratio of 260 nm and 280 nm (A260/A280) was between 1.93 and 2.1 for all samples included for further analysis. The Experion automated electrophoresis system (BioRad, Hercules, CA, USA) and the RNA StdSens Analysis Kit was used to evaluate RNA integrity of a randomized 32&#x00025; of the samples, according to the instruction manual. The electropherograms were inspected for clear ribosomal peaks. We were not able to analyze any numerical criteria corresponding to electrophoresis patterns, because this information was not available. Thirty nine samples were excluded from further analysis due to insufficient RNA purity, yield or integrity. RNA samples were kept at &#x02212;70&#x000b0;C until further use.</p></div><div id="s4d" class="sec"><h3>Microarray-based profiling and image analysis</h3><p id="__p38" class="p p-first">After exclusion based on study design and RNA quality and quantity criteria, samples were analyzed using the Applied Biosystems (AB) expression array system (Foster City, Lousiana, USA). 500 ng total RNA was used for amplification by the NanoAmp RT-IVT labeling kit from AB for one round of amplification, in accordance with the manufacturer's manual. Briefly, the 1<sup>st</sup> strand of cDNA was synthesized by reverse transcription using the T7-oligo (dT) primer, followed by 2<sup>nd</sup> strand synthesis. The double-stranded cDNA was purified, and used as template for <em>in vitro</em> transcription (IVT). During IVT, digoxigenin (DIG)-labeled UTP was incorporated into the cRNA. The quantity and purity of the cRNA was measured on the NanoDrop ND-1000, and the cRNA was stored on &#x02212;70&#x000b0;C until further use. 10 &#x000b5;g of DIG-labeled cRNA was fragmented and hybridized to AB Human Genome Survey Microarray V2.0, in accordance with the Chemiluminescence Detection Kit Protocol.</p><p id="__p39" class="p p-last">The AB Human Genome Survey Microarray V2.0 contains 277 control probes and 32,878 probes for the interrogation of 29,098 genes. AB Expression System software was used to extract signal intensities, signal to noise ratios (S/N) and flagging.</p></div><div id="s4e" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Data analysis</h3><p id="__p40" class="p p-first">A total of 304 arrays including 15 technical replicates were analyzed. Data analysis was performed using R (<a href="http://cran.r-project.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://cran.r-project.org</a>), an open-source-interpreted computer language for statistical computation and graphics, and tools from the Bioconductor project (<a href="http://www.bioconductor.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.bioconductor.org</a>), adapted to our needs. Using R, we set the expression intensity to &#x0201c;missing&#x0201d; for genes with flagging value &#x0003e;8191 (threshold recommended by the microarray manufacturer). For a set of technical replicate arrays from the same subject, we excluded the array with the least number of probes that had a S/N exceeding 3. Furthermore, arrays (N&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;3) where less than 40&#x00025; of the probes had a S/N&#x02265;3 were also removed from the analysis. Individual probes were not considered, if the S/N exceeded 3 in less than 50&#x00025; of the samples. After sample and probe filtration, we proceeded with a log2 transformation, quantile normalization and imputation of missing values using 10-nearest neighbourhood method <a href="#pgen.1000873-Troyanskaya1" rid="pgen.1000873-Troyanskaya1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628128">[66]</a>. A total of 286 arrays and 16185 probes are analyzed. Microarray data have been deposited at Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO; <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo</a>) accession number <a id="__tag_569628216" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15289" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE15289"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE15289","term_id":"15289"}}</span>GSE15289</a>.</p><p id="__p41">The global ANCOVA <a href="#pgen.1000873-Mansmann1" rid="pgen.1000873-Mansmann1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628179">[25]</a> was carried out by comparison of linear models via the extra sum of squares principle to test for the univariate and multivariate association between global expression values and technical variables. All significant technical variables with a permuted <em>p</em>-value &#x0003c;0.001 identified in the ANCOVA multivariate analysis were included in the gene-wise linear model selection as random (array lot number, RNA extraction date) and fixed (time between blood collection and freezing) variables.</p><p id="__p42" class="p p-last">Forward-backward variable selection was used to select gene-wise model based on BIC. Linear mixed models were used to test the association of each gene with the significant technical and all biological variables. The z-score from the global test <a href="#pgen.1000873-Goeman1" rid="pgen.1000873-Goeman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628184">[26]</a> was used to select core probes that most strongly explain the difference between groups setting a FDR <a href="#pgen.1000873-Benjamini1" rid="pgen.1000873-Benjamini1" class=" bibr popnode">[67]</a> threshold which maximizes the discovery of true positives (weight&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;2) <em>versus</em> false positives (weight&#x0200a;=&#x0200a;1) associated with each variable. Gene set enrichment analysis was conducted using the global test <a href="#pgen.1000873-Goeman1" rid="pgen.1000873-Goeman1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628155">[26]</a>, which offers the opportunity to compare two or more groups while taking into account the association between probe sets as well as their individual effects. When testing several gene sets curated from the literature, we adjusted for multiple testing using FDR <a href="#pgen.1000873-Benjamini1" rid="pgen.1000873-Benjamini1" class=" bibr popnode">[67]</a>. Functional clustering and gene networks prediction were performed with the Database for Annotation, Visualization, and Integrated Discovery (DAVID) at <a href="http://david.abcc.ncifcrf.gov/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://david.abcc.ncifcrf.gov/</a>
<a href="#pgen.1000873-Dennis1" rid="pgen.1000873-Dennis1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_585245985">[27]</a>, and the Human Experimental/Functional Mapper (HEFalMp) <a href="#pgen.1000873-Huttenhower1" rid="pgen.1000873-Huttenhower1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_569628173">[28]</a> at <a href="http://function.princeton.edu/hefalmp" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2837385&amp;issue-id=185328&amp;journal-id=331&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://function.princeton.edu/hefalmp</a>, respectively.</p></div></div><div id="s5" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin"
