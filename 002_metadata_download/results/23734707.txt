Cellular alterations specific to &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 have been difficult to assess because &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 co-expression results in tetramer heterogeneity, including formation of contaminating WTp53 tetramers. Based upon the p53 tetramer structure, we expressed &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 as a single transcript that maintained tetramer architecture, ensuring a 2:2 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 stoichiometry. As expected, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers were stable and transcriptionally active <em>in vitro</em> and in cells, largely mimicking the function of WTp53 tetramers. Microarray analyses, however, revealed about 80 genes whose expression was altered 2-fold or more in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells. Moreover, global metabolomic profiling quantitated hundreds of biochemicals across different experiments (WTp53, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, plus controls). When evaluated collectively, these data suggested altered mTOR signaling and mitochondrial function&#x02014;each canonical regulators of longevity&#x02014;in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 vs. WTp53. Increased levels of free amino acids, increased expression of <em>IRS-1</em>, and decreased expression of <em>INPP5D/SHIP-1</em> suggested activated mTOR signaling in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells; this was confirmed upon comparative analyses of several mTOR pathway intermediates. We also observed changes in mitochondrial function in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells, which correlated with increased <em>MARS2</em> expression and elevated levels of carnitine, acetyl CoA, ATP, and Krebs cycle intermediates. Finally, elevated levels of succinate and 2-hydroxyglutarate indicate potential epigenetic means to propagate &#x00394;Np53:WTp53-induced gene expression changes to cell progeny. This may be especially important for aging, as biological effects manifest over time.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text"><em>DOT1L</em>, urea cycle, polyamines, glutathione, 2HG, epigenetic</span></div></div><div id="S1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S1title">Introduction</h2><p id="P3" class="p p-first">The role of p53 in mammalian aging is best exemplified by mouse studies with p53 truncation mutants (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738757">Maier <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>; <a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738787">Tyner <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). Both artificial and naturally occurring p53 isoforms that lack the N-terminal p53 activation domain, when expressed together with wild-type p53 (WTp53), cause a progeroid phenotype that includes early-onset osteoporosis, memory loss, and premature death (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738795">Maier <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>; <a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738804">Pehar <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>; <a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738770">Tyner <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). The molecular mechanisms by which &#x00394;Np53 cause accelerated aging remain incompletely understood. It is clear, however, that &#x00394;Np53 requires WTp53 to mediate its physiological effects; mice expressing only &#x00394;Np53 do not age rapidly (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738784">Maier <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>). Because p53 binds DNA as a tetramer, the formation of mixed &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 hetero-tetramers is believed to mediate the accelerated aging phenotype (<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738786">Courtois <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). Although &#x00394;Np53 has been well studied using knock-in mouse models, &#x00394;Np53 has not been thoroughly studied in human cells. Moreover, global gene expression and metabolic changes induced by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 have not been assessed in human cells or any model organism. One potential reason for this is that co-expression of &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 will confound analyses due to heterogeneity, including formation of &#x0201c;contaminating&#x0201d; WTp53 tetramers. As one consequence, it has been difficult to define cellular changes that are specifically dependent upon &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, because a means to examine a single entity with fixed &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 stoichiometry was not evident. For these reasons, the underlying mechanisms that link &#x00394;Np53 and its progeroid phenotype remain incompletely understood, even in model organisms.</p><p id="P4" class="p p-last">To further define the molecular pathways impacted by &#x00394;Np53 expression in human cells, we expressed 2:2 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers (i.e. two copies of &#x00394;Np53 and two copies of WTp53 per tetramer) in p53-null H1299 cells. Based upon the p53 tetramer structure (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738748">Okorokov <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>), we implemented a strategy that expressed &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 as a single transcript that maintained the native tetramer architecture, ensuring a 2:2 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 stoichiometry. These &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers were stable and transcriptionally active <em>in vitro</em> and in cells, largely mimicking the function of WTp53 tetramers. Microarray analyses identified 84 genes whose expression was up- or down-regulated 2-fold or more in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells compared with WTp53. This set of genes included many with established roles in longevity and metabolism. We next completed global metabolomic profiling, which identified and quantitated hundreds of biochemicals across different experiments (WTp53, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, plus controls). When evaluated collectively, the gene expression and metabolomics data provided clear predictions (validated via pathway analyses) about cellular processes disrupted by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression. Among the most striking were mitochondrial function and the mTOR pathway, each well-established regulators of longevity. Biochemical and cellular assays confirmed a hyper-active mTOR pathway and altered mitochondrial function in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 (vs. WTp53). We also uncovered metabolites whose elevated levels could contribute to heritable epigenetic changes upon &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression. Because &#x00394;Np53 is a naturally occurring isoform whose levels can increase with cell stress, the &#x00394;Np53-induced changes identified here could play a role in normal human aging.</p></div><div id="S2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S2title">Results</h2><div id="S3" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Expression and purification of 2:2 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers</h3><p id="P5" class="p p-first-last">The p53 activation domain (p53AD) resides in the N-terminus of the protein. Two separate activation motifs have been identified, with residues 14&#x02013;26 comprising p53AD1, and residues 41&#x02013;57 comprising p53AD2. Thus, the &#x00394;Np53 isoform lacks p53AD1 but retains p53AD2. The structure of the native p53 tetramer indicates that p53 assembles as a dimer of dimers, with the C-terminus of one p53 polypeptide interacting with the N-terminus of a second p53 protein (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738761">Okorokov <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>). The juxtaposition of the N- and C-termini suggested that a flexible linker could tether two p53 monomers to yield a 1:1 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 dimer (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S1</a>). Implementing the strategy outlined in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Figure 1A</span></a>, we expressed a tethered &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 protein as a single transcript in insect cells. Silver stain and western blot analysis (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1C</span></a>) revealed a molecular weight of approximately 100 kDa, consistent with a tethered &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 dimer. As shown in <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Figure S7</a>, most, but not all, genes probed by qRT-PCR showed similar changes in expression levels in Saos-2 cells compared with H1299 cells.</p></div><div id="S7" class="sec"><h3>The peptide linker itself does not alter biological function of p53</h3><p id="P11" class="p p-first">Collectively, the data in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Figure 1</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and2</span></span><span>2</span></a> indicated that the flexible peptide linker inserted between &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53, which was based upon the p53 tetramer structure (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738758">Okorokov <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>), did not affect the biological activity of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers. To further address whether the linker itself might impact p53 function, we expressed tethered WTp53 tetramers (i.e. WTp53:WTp53) in p53-null H1299 cells. In other words, WTp53 dimers were expressed as a single transcript using the same linker sequence used for &#x00394;Np53:WTp53. Microarray analyses were then completed in which we compared global gene expression changes induced by &#x0201c;standard&#x0201d; WTp53 vs. its tethered counterpart (i.e. WTp53:WTp53). These and other data, summarized in <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Figure S8A&#x02013;J</a>, indicate that the peptide linker itself does not appear to direct gene expression changes or otherwise alter WTp53 function, at least within the duration of these experiments.</p><p id="P12" class="p p-last">In agreement with these findings, we performed qRT-PCR experiments in H1299 cells co-expressing equal levels of &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 (i.e. &#x00394;Np53 + WTp53). As anticipated, and in agreement with data for p21 (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2D</span></a>), gene expression from these &#x0201c;&#x00394;Np53 + WTp53&#x0201d; cells was largely similar to &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S9 and S10</a>). Importantly, in cases where differences were observed, these could be attributed to tetramer heterogeneity (e.g. WTp53 tetramers) resulting from &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 co-expression (see legend, <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S8, S9 and S10</a>).</p></div><div id="S8" class="sec"><h3>Global metabolomics analyses</h3><p id="P13" class="p p-first">Whereas many genes up- or down-regulated in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells are directly or indirectly linked to aging, a significant fraction are involved in basic metabolic processes (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). Given the major role that p53 plays in regulating cell metabolism (<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738799">Feng &#x00026; Levine, 2010</a>; <a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738778">Vousden &#x00026; Ryan, 2009</a>), we compared the metabolomes of H1299 cells expressing WTp53 or &#x00394;Np53:WTp53. The metabolomics analysis utilized an analytical platform that combines gas chromatography (GC) and liquid chromatography (LC) mass spectrometry (GC-MS and LC-MS/MS) techniques (<a href="#R25" rid="R25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738775">Reitman <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>).</p><p id="P14" class="p p-last">Six biological replicate samples were prepared for metabolic profiling. In addition to WTp53 and &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, we evaluated mock and GFP vector only transfected H1299 cells. Because cell cycle changes or changes in cell state (e.g. apoptosis or senescence) could potentially alter metabolite levels, we probed samples to ensure viability and that each cell population was similar in cell state and cell cycle distribution (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S2B and S3</a>). As with microarray analyses, we also confirmed that p53 levels were consistent and comparable in WTp53 vs. &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 samples by quantitative western blot (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S2A</a>). A statistical summary of the biochemicals identified in each sample is shown in <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S3</a>; a complete list of the metabolites identified, including statistical parameters and absolute or relative quantitation, is provided in <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S4</a>.</p></div><div id="S9" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic changes associated with WTp53 support roles in tumor suppression and metabolism</h3><p id="P15" class="p p-first-last">Relative to control cells (WTp53 vs. GFP vector; <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S3</a>) 142 biochemicals were categorized as significantly altered in H1299 cells expressing WTp53 (p &#x02264; 0.05). As anticipated, the metabolic alterations induced by WTp53 support its known roles as a metabolic regulator and tumor suppressor (<a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738793">Vousden &#x00026; Ryan, 2009</a>). Although the primary focus of this study was how the &#x00394;Np53 isoform altered WTp53 function, we highlight the metabolic changes induced by WTp53 in <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S5</a>.</p></div><div id="S10" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic changes associated with &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 vs. WTp53</h3><p id="P16" class="p p-first">Compared with WTp53, the levels of 94 biochemicals were altered (p &#x02264; 0.05) in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Table S3</a>). As shown in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Figure 5C</span></a>, phosphorylated mTOR, phospho-S6K, and phospho-4EBP-1,2,3 were each elevated in Saos-2 cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 compared with WTp53. Taken together, the data in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3773055/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Figure 5</span></a> (see also <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Fig. S12</a>) demonstrate that &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression activates the mTOR pathway in human cells. This outcome is opposed to WTp53, which suppresses mTOR signaling (<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738756">Feng &#x00026; Levine, 2010</a>).</p></div></div><div id="S13" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S13title">Discussion</h2><p id="P24" class="p p-first">The p53 transcription factor is expressed in most cell types, plays important roles throughout development, and helps sustain basic cellular functions, such as stress response. Whereas myriad factors influence aging, this study focused on the naturally occurring human &#x00394;Np53 isoform, whose expression causes accelerated aging in mice (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738796">Maier <em>et al.</em>, 2004</a>; <a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738773">Tyner <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). To more thoroughly understand the distinct activities of human &#x00394;Np53, we focused on differences relative to WTp53. Our goal was not to distinguish direct from indirect effects of &#x00394;Np53, but rather to gain insight into the cellular pathways altered by its expression. A key feature of this study was analysis of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers with fixed 2:2 stoichiometry; this circumvented confounding issues associated with &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 co-expression and allowed clear delineation between WTp53 and &#x00394;Np53:WTp53. Another key feature was the combination of gene expression and metabolomics data, which allowed better assessment of pathways altered by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53. On their own, gene expression data correlate poorly with organismal aging. Age-associated changes in gene expression are heterogeneous and vary with cell or tissue type (<a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738779">Bahar <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>; <a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738766">Somel <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>). Also, age-associated gene expression changes are not consistent across model organisms (<a href="#R39" rid="R39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738747">Zahn <em>et al.</em>, 2007</a>). Signaling and metabolic pathways, however, are more consistently linked to aging across different cell types and even model organisms (<a href="#R32" rid="R32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738760">Vijg &#x00026; Campisi, 2008</a>). Collectively, our data suggest that in human cells, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 helps orchestrate changes in basic cellular processes that contribute to aging. In fact, mitochondrial function and mTOR signaling are linked to longevity in organisms as distinct as mammals and yeast (<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738794">Laplante &#x00026; Sabatini, 2012</a>; <a href="#R31" rid="R31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738792">Vendelbo &#x00026; Nair, 2011</a>).</p><p id="P25" class="p">We would like to point out some advantages and limitations of our study. Whereas the metabolomics analyses complemented the gene expression data and provide a wealth of information, they do not offer a fully comprehensive view of cellular alterations that accompany &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression. The total number of chemically distinct metabolites in human cells exceeds a few thousand (<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738771">Wishart <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>), whereas we identified several hundred in this study. We also cannot delineate the cellular location of each metabolite, or its lifetime within each cell. Furthermore, non-coding RNAs can affect gene expression and cell physiology, and it remains unclear how &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 affects expression of non-coding RNA genes. The effect of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 vs. WTp53 on H1299 cell growth, cell cycle, or senescence was essentially identical; however, a ~10% difference was observed in annexin V staining (early apoptosis). This could make some contribution to gene expression and metabolic differences between &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 and WTp53 cells; however, our analyses focused on large-scale changes (typically 2-fold or greater) such that it is unlikely a major contributing factor. Consistent with this, phenotypic changes (e.g. mitochondrial membrane potential or mTOR signaling) were insensitive to removal of annexin V-positive cells and were consistent at 24 or 41 hours following &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 or WTp53 expression. We acknowledge that the gene expression and metabolic changes induced by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 have similarities with aging studies in model organisms. Aging is controlled by a complex array of genetic, epigenetic, and environmental factors, however, and the data shown here focus only on &#x00394;Np53:WTp53-dependent changes. Thus, our results will not correlate with all aging studies completed in model organisms. Moreover, the bulk of our analysis was completed in a single cell type. Although this has obvious advantages (isogenic; identical culture conditions) and avoids physical and physiological heterogeneity inherent in young vs. old comparisons, the &#x00394;Np53:WTp53-induced changes identified here may not be identical in distinct cell types or contexts.</p><div id="S14" class="sec"><h3>Analysis of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers as a single entity</h3><p id="P26" class="p p-first">The cryo-EM structure of the WTp53 tetramer has provided unprecedented insight about its structural organization (<a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738774">Okorokov <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>) and revealed a straightforward means to link &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 as a single transcript while preserving p53 tetramer architecture. As expected, the flexible peptide linker&#x02014;which was longer than necessary to enable conformational variability&#x02014;did not notably affect the biological activity of p53, as tethered versions of WTp53 (i.e. WTp53:WTp53) mimicked phenotypic and gene expression changes induced by native WTp53 tetramers. Similarly, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 formed stable tetramers, bound p53 target sequences in cells, activated transcription similar to WTp53 in vitro, and displayed similar activity to co-expressed &#x00394;Np53 + WTp53 in cells. It has been postulated that translation of p53 generates homo-dimers, followed by post-translational tetramer formation (<a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738769">Nicholls <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). Based upon this model, the structural organization of 2:2 &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers would be slightly distinct, with one WT dimer merging with one &#x00394;Np53 dimer. Whether this assembly mechanism predominates in cells remains unclear, in part because few studies have directly addressed this question, likely due to its enormous practical and technical challenges. The similar gene expression changes induced by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 compared with &#x00394;Np53 + WTp53, however, suggest that either these potential architectural distinctions have little functional consequence, or that the linked &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 tetramers evaluated here represent a predominant tetramer architecture.</p><p id="P27" class="p p-last">Expression of linked &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 dimers to ensure a 2:2 tetramer stoichiometry offered several advantages over co-expression of &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 (i.e. &#x00394;Np53 + WTp53). Because &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 stoichiometry cannot be controlled with separate co-expression (even when each protein is present at similar levels), up to five distinct entities could form, ranging from zero to four copies of &#x00394;Np53 within any given tetramer. The strategy employed here avoided this heterogeneity, and ensured the presence of a single entity in which the &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 stoichiometry was kept constant. Equally important, our strategy ensured that gene expression and metabolomics data would not be confounded by the presence of contaminating WTp53 tetramers that result from &#x00394;Np53 and WTp53 co-expression. Thus, the gene expression and metabolomics comparison between &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 and WTp53 completed here enabled a clear contrast that would simply not be possible with any series of co-expression studies.</p></div><div id="S15" class="sec"><h3>Neomorphic function of human &#x00394;Np53</h3><p id="P28" class="p p-first">Mutations can cause a gain- or loss-of-function in a transcription factor that modify its effect on global gene expression patterns; such &#x0201c;neomorphic&#x0201d; mutations are well documented for p53 (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738803">Freed-Pastor &#x00026; Prives, 2012</a>). In general, the effects of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 vs. WTp53 in H1299 cells were largely indistinguishable. Only a subset of genes were differentially expressed, and only minor changes, if any, were observed upon assessing cell growth, apoptosis, cell cycle, or senescence. Potentially, &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 could gain functions that promote growth; however, this was not evident based upon our observations with cultured cells or from the gene expression and metabolomics data. Based upon our comparative analyses with WTp53, alterations in mTOR signaling and mitochondrial function appear to represent neomorphic outcomes of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression. Whereas WTp53 is a negative regulator of mTOR signaling (<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738790">Feng &#x00026; Levine, 2010</a>), this function appears to be lost with &#x00394;Np53:WTp53. Up-regulation of the mTOR pathway appears to result&#x02014;at least in part&#x02014;from altered gene expression (<em>IRS-1</em>, <em>INPP5D</em>) and metabolic changes (amino acids) in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53.</p><p id="P29">Potential mechanisms that underlie the mitochondrial changes (e.g. increased membrane potential) can also be deduced from the metabolic and gene expression data. In addition to carnitine and its role in mitochondrial fatty acid transport, we observed an up-regulation of <em>MARS2</em>, the mitochondrial tRNA<sup>met</sup> synthetase, which suggests enhanced translation of mitochondrial proteins in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells. Because translation initiates with the Met codon, it has been proposed that elevated levels of tRNA<sup>met</sup> up-regulate translation (<a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738764">Marshall <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). Increased translation of mitochondrial proteins, in particular those involved in electron transport and oxidative phosphorylation, could result in the observed increase in membrane potential and ATP. Mitochondrial function is also sensitive to iron. In &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells, we observed increased levels of glutathione and its derivatives, along with altered expression of the heme transporter <em>SLC46A1</em> and the iron reductase/iron import protein <em>CYBRD1</em>. Glutathione is essential for regulating iron metabolism (<a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738785">Kumar <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>), and increased levels of glutathione metabolites, coupled with changes in iron import-export factors, suggests that iron homeostasis might be disrupted in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells. Mitochondrial function is highly dependent upon iron, in part because many proteins and protein complexes important for electron transport contain iron-sulfur clusters. In fact, glutathione is required for proper biogenesis of iron-sulfur clusters (<a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738749">Lill, 2009</a>).</p><p id="P30" class="p p-last">Given the well-studied roles of the mTOR pathway and mitochondrial function in longevity, our results with &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 are consistent with its accelerated aging phenotype in mice and implicate the naturally occurring &#x00394;Np53 isoform as a potential regulator of human aging.</p></div><div id="S16" class="sec"><h3>Potential links between &#x00394;Np53 and normal physiological aging</h3><p id="P31" class="p p-first">A growing number of studies indicate that single metabolites are sufficient to trigger phenotypic changes in cells and within an organism. To cite a few examples, acetate can correct insulin signaling defects (<a href="#R27" rid="R27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738763">Shin <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>) and choline can promote atherosclerosis (<a href="#R34" rid="R34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738798">Wang <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). The metabolic changes induced by &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 expression are striking and encompass a limited set of pathways. We observed that amino acids, urea cycle intermediates, polyamines, and glutathione metabolites were significantly elevated in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, relative to WTp53. Numerous studies have linked these same metabolites to aging and longevity in mammalian model organisms; however, far fewer studies have addressed the metabolic changes that accompany human aging. A metabolomic study of human plasma isolated from healthy old vs. young donors shows clear similarities to our metabolomic comparison of &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 and WTp53 (<a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738752">Lawton <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). As seen in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells, amino acid levels were uniformly increased in old vs. young human plasma samples, as were polyamines, carnitine, TCA cycle, and urea cycle metabolites. Potentially, these shared features could reflect a distinct metabolic signature in aged human cells that is rapidly adopted upon &#x00394;Np53 expression. Much additional work will be needed to rigorously test this hypothesis.</p><p id="P32">Among the various models for mammalian aging, a common theme includes stochastic, heritable changes that gradually alter gene expression patterns and metabolic pathways (<a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738782">Martin, 2009</a>). Such mechanisms appear to represent a fundamental requirement for aging, as biological effects manifest over time. Because &#x00394;Np53 is a naturally occurring isoform, its levels could change with age. Existing gene expression data have limited value, however, as &#x00394;Np53 levels are controlled primarily by alternate translation (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738755">Candeias <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>; <a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738754">Ray <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>). In mice, relative levels of &#x00394;Np53 change with developmental state (<a href="#R30" rid="R30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738780">Ungewitter &#x00026; Scrable, 2010</a>), and several reports indicate that human &#x00394;Np53 protein levels change during cell stress (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738751">Bourougaa <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>; <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738768">Candeias <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>; <a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738789">Courtois <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a>). The links to cell stress imply that transient increases in &#x00394;Np53 levels can occur throughout the human lifespan.</p><p id="P33" class="p p-last">Could transient elevation of &#x00394;Np53 levels have chronic, long-term consequences? We note two potential mechanisms by which transient &#x00394;Np53 expression could initiate a self-reinforcing cascade, with possible implications for aging. First, it is established that ER stress increases relative levels of &#x00394;Np53 vs. WTp53 in human cells by favoring alternate translation initiation from an IRES within the WTp53 mRNA (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738746">Bourougaa <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). Elevated levels of 2-hydroxyglutarate (2HG) can independently induce an ER stress response (<a href="#R26" rid="R26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738781">Sasaki <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>). Because 2HG is elevated in &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 cells, &#x00394;Np53 could promote its own translation by inducing ER stress. Second, we observed elevated levels of succinate and 2HG in cells expressing &#x00394;Np53:WTp53, as well as increased expression of the histone H3K79 methyltransferase <em>DOT1L</em>. This suggests that DNA methylation and histone H3K79 methylation patterns may be globally altered by 3Np53 expression. Because 2HG or succinate can competitively inhibit demethylase enzymes that use &#x003b1;-ketoglutarate as a cofactor, elevated 2HG and succinate favors a general retention of methyl marks on both histones and DNA (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738788">Xiao <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>; <a href="#R38" rid="R38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738802">Xu <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). DNA methylation and histone H3K79 methylation are epigenetic regulators of transcription that may enable even transient 3Np53 expression to &#x0201c;lock in&#x0201d; gene expression patterns that propagate to cell progeny. Indeed, whereas both H3K79 methylation and DNA methylation marks are reversible, DNA methylation also appears to be heritable (<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738745">Bonasio <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). Future studies will focus on whether &#x00394;Np53:WTp53 alters patterns of DNA methylation and/or H3K79 tri-methylation genome-wide, and how this might contribute to the geriatric pathology of &#x00394;Np53.</p></div><div id="S17" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Accession Numbers</h3><p id="P34" class="p p-first-last">Microarray data were deposited in GEO (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3773055&amp;issue-id=227444&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>) under the accession number <a id="__tag_332738805" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE37271" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE37271"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE37271","term_id":"37271"}}</span>GSE37271</a>.</p></div></div><div id="S18" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S18title">Experimental Procedures</h2><div id="S19" class="sec sec-first"><h3>In vitro transcription</h3><p id="P35" class="p p-first-last">In vitro transcription reactions and purification of factors for the reconstituted transcription system were completed as described (<a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_332738743">Knuesel <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>).</p></div><div id="S20" class="sec"><h3>Microarray analyses</h3><p id="P36" class="p p-first-last">Total RNA from FACS sorted cells was extracted with the RNeasy kit (Qiagen) and was analyzed at the Genomics and Microarray Shared Resource (University of Colorado Cancer Center) for transcriptional profiling.
