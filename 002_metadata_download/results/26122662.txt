Whereas canonical NF-&#x003ba;B signaling is well studied, there is little information on the divergent noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway in the context of pancreatic islet dysfunction. Here, we demonstrate that pharmacological activation of the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B&#x02013;inducing kinase (NIK) disrupts glucose homeostasis in zebrafish in vivo. We identify NIK as a critical negative regulator of &#x003b2; cell function, as pharmacological NIK activation results in impaired glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in mouse and human islets. NIK levels are elevated in pancreatic islets isolated from diet-induced obese (DIO) mice, which exhibit increased processing of noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B components p100 to p52, and accumulation of RelB. TNF and receptor activator of NF-&#x003ba;B ligand (RANKL), two ligands associated with diabetes, induce NIK in islets. Mice with constitutive &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic NIK activation present impaired insulin secretion with DIO. NIK activation triggers the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B transcriptional network to induce genes identified in human type 2 diabetes genome-wide association studies linked to &#x003b2; cell failure. These studies reveal that NIK contributes a central mechanism for &#x003b2; cell failure in diet-induced obesity.</p></div></div><div id="idm139771995469104" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p3" class="p p-first">Inflammation has emerged as a key component of the metabolic syndrome. The canonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway is a well-studied regulator of inflammatory processes and is heavily implicated in insulin resistance and &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in diabetes. Inhibition of NF-&#x003ba;B improved glucose tolerance in human and animal studies (<a href="#bib59" rid="bib59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278736">Yuan et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib27" rid="bib27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278727">Hundal et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib20" rid="bib20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278690">Goldfine et al., 2013</a>). The I&#x003ba;B-like kinases IKK-&#x003b5; and TANK-binding kinase (TBK1) are downstream NF-&#x003ba;B targets involved in driving insulin resistance (<a href="#bib10" rid="bib10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278769">Chiang et al., 2009</a>) and blockade of IKK-&#x003b5; and TBK1 improves metabolic function in obesity (<a href="#bib44" rid="bib44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278728">Reilly et al., 2013</a>). Thus, canonical NF-&#x003ba;B is a central molecular pathway in obesity-dependent changes in the metabolic syndrome.</p><p id="__p4">A divergent arm of NF-&#x003ba;B signaling is the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway mediated by NF-&#x003ba;B&#x02013;inducing kinase (NIK), which is activated downstream of TNF receptor (TNFR) family members including TNFR2, receptor activator of NF-&#x003ba;B (RANK), lymphotoxin &#x003b2; receptor (LT&#x003b2;R), or CD40 upon ligand binding (<a href="#bib48" rid="bib48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278742">Sun, 2011</a>). NIK levels and hence its activation are controlled by an E3 ligase complex; TNFR-associated factor 2 (TRAF2) recruits the E3 ligases baculoviral IAP repeat-containing proteins 2 and 3 (BIRC2/3), whereas interactions with TRAF3 allows delivery of NIK to the complex (<a href="#bib51" rid="bib51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278755">Vallabhapurapu et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278729">Zarnegar et al., 2008</a>). In the absence of a ligand, NIK is ubiquitinated by this complex and targeted for proteasome-dependent degradation, thereby silencing the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway (<a href="#bib51" rid="bib51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278759">Vallabhapurapu et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278724">Zarnegar et al., 2008</a>). The noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B dimer regulates gene expression through binding to general, as well as specific, noncanonical &#x003ba;B target sites (<a href="#bib4" rid="bib4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278739">Bonizzi et al., 2004</a>); however, its targets and the phenotypic consequence of its activation are incompletely known. Studies show that noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway activation mediated through loss of TRAF2 or TRAF3 promotes expansion of B cells (<a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278719">Gardam et al., 2008</a>), and leads to a shift in the proportions of specific T cell subpopulations (<a href="#bib57" rid="bib57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278738">Xie et al., 2011</a>). Recently, activation of the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway has been linked to the metabolic syndrome (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278746">Choudhary et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib46" rid="bib46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278680">Sheng et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278715">Kiechl et al., 2013</a>). For example, NIK levels are high in muscle biopsies of obese patients, but decline after gastric bypass surgery, weight loss, and a decrease in insulin resistance (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278698">Choudhary et al., 2011</a>).</p><p id="__p5">Emerging evidence emphasizes a crucial role for pancreatic &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in type 2 diabetes (T2D). Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified a large number of loci associated with &#x003b2; cell function and survival (<a href="#bib16" rid="bib16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278681">Florez, 2008</a>; <a href="#bib23" rid="bib23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278702">Guan et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib49" rid="bib49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278721">Taneera et al., 2012</a>). Further, hyperglycemia (<a href="#bib14" rid="bib14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278703">Donath et al., 1999</a>), circulating inflammatory factors (<a href="#bib47" rid="bib47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278696">Spranger et al., 2003</a>), or amyloid production in the islet (<a href="#bib53" rid="bib53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278695">Verchere et al., 1996</a>) are deleterious for islets and contribute to T2D pathogenesis. This is indicated by diminished &#x003b2; cell mass (<a href="#bib6" rid="bib6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278761">Butler et al., 2003</a>), but also by reduced &#x003b2; cell secretory function in T2D patients (<a href="#bib15" rid="bib15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278707">Ferrannini and Mari, 2014</a>). Thus, &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in diabetes pathogenesis is now being acknowledged as a critical and major contributor in diabetes pathogenesis. However, the mechanisms for loss of &#x003b2; cell function and mass are incompletely known. Therefore, it is essential to elucidate factors that contribute to both insulin resistance in peripheral tissue and &#x003b2; cell failure.</p><p id="__p6" class="p">Core components of the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway have been identified in pancreatic islets (<a href="#bib39" rid="bib39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278711">Ortis et al., 2010</a>); however, the functional consequence of NIK and subsequent noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B activation in &#x003b2; cells is completely unknown. We demonstrate here that pharmacologically induced NIK disrupts glucose homeostasis in vivo in a zebrafish model and impairs glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in mouse and human islets. Additionally, a high-fat diet triggers &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic NIK activation and disruption of the E3 ligase control circuit regulating NIK impairs &#x003b2; cell insulin secretory capacity in a diet-induced obesity model in mice. Here, for the first time, we demonstrate a novel function for NIK and subsequent noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B activation as a negative regulator of pancreatic &#x003b2; cell function, impairing insulin secretion in diet-induced obesity. NIK activation in the metabolic syndrome is a feature of pancreatic &#x003b2; cells but also insulin-dependent peripheral tissues, thereby providing a tissue-spanning mechanism to link inflammation with multiple tissue failure in diabetes pathogenesis.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">RESULTS</h2><div id="__sec2" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Systemic monovalent 1 (MV1) administration impairs glucose homeostasis in zebrafish in vivo</h3><p id="__p7" class="p p-first">Aberrant BIRC function has been associated with many human cancers (<a href="#bib17" rid="bib17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278720">Fulda and Vucic, 2012</a>), leading to the development of a group of peptide-derived drugs called second mitochondria-derived activator of caspase (SMAC) mimetics, which induce the degradation of BIRC2 and BIRC3, thereby interfering with their ability to inhibit apoptosis (<a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278679">Varfolomeev et al., 2007</a>). Concomitantly, degradation of BIRC2 and BIRC3 elicits NIK accumulation, which leads to proteasome-dependent processing of p100 into p52 and hence activates the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway (<a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278697">Varfolomeev et al., 2007</a>).</p><p id="__p8" class="p">To test whether systemic elevation of NIK mediated by the SMAC mimetic MV1 would alter glucose homeostasis in vivo, we used a zebrafish model. The main organs required for metabolic control, such as the pancreas, islet, and insulin-sensitive tissue (muscle, liver) are conserved in zebrafish, rendering it an excellent model to study glucose homeostasis (<a href="#bib24" rid="bib24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278745">Hesselson et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib29" rid="bib29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278709">Jurczyk et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib45" rid="bib45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278688">Seth et al., 2013</a>). First, we showed that synthesized MV1 could induce NIK accumulation and processing of p100 into p52 in MDA-MB-231/Luc breast cancer cells, indicating activation of noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B signaling (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 A</span></a>), as described previously (<a href="#bib52" rid="bib52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278741">Varfolomeev et al., 2007</a>). We next injected MV1 into zebrafish larvae at 6 d postfertilization (dpf) and examined glucose levels after 6 h. WT larvae treated with MV1 exhibited increased glucose levels compared with vehicle (DMSO) controls (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 B</span></a>). This indicates that MV1-induced NIK activation disrupts glucose homeostasis in vivo in WT zebrafish.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139772029169168" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4516791_JEM_20150218_Fig1.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is JEM_20150218_Fig1.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/bin/JEM_20150218_Fig1.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139772029169168" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_fig1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1">Figure 1.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p9"><strong>MV1 disrupts glucose homeostasis in zebrafish and impairs &#x003b2; cell function in human and mouse islets.</strong> (A) Protein levels of NIK and p100 to p52 processing in the MDA-MB-231/Luc breast cancer cell line treated with increasing concentrations of MV1 were assessed by immunoblotting. Addition of MV1 to WT islets triggered p100 to p52 processing, indicating NIK activation (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 C</span></a>). MV1 had no effect on islet survival in 24-h in vitro cultures as assessed by calcein-AM uptake and ethidium homodimer DNA intercalation (unpublished data). In a glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS) assay, MV1-treated islets exhibited normal basal insulin output, but insulin secretion was reduced by &#x0223c;50% compared with controls when challenged with 20 mM glucose. Potassium chloride (KCl)&#x02013;stimulated secretion was normal, indicating normal insulin content (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 D</span></a>). To assess the effect of MV1-treatment in vivo, we transplanted either MV1- or vehicle-treated islets into diabetic syngeneic recipients. A glucose tolerance test (GTT) at post-operative day (POD) 3 revealed that, compared with controls, MV1-treated grafts exhibited impaired glucose tolerance (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 E</span></a>). Glucose tolerance was still slightly impaired at POD 7, but had completely resolved by POD 11 such that MV1-treated islets provided equivalent metabolic control to vehicle treated islets (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1, F and G</span></a>). Further, MV1-treated grafts exhibited normal morphology and insulin staining, as well as equivalent graft area determined by morphometry (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 H</span></a>). This indicates that acute activation of NIK results in a temporary &#x003b2; cell defect that is reversed upon drug-clearance and suppression of NIK.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h3>NIK activation impairs glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in human islets</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">We next assessed whether &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic activation of NIK would impair the function of human islets. Human islets treated with MV1 showed normal basal (2.8 mM) but defective glucose-stimulated (25 mM) insulin secretion (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig1" rid-ob="ob-fig1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig1"><span>Fig. 1 I</span></a>). Thus, activation of NIK in human islets resulted in impaired &#x003b2; cell function. These data highlight the importance of the NIK pathway and its contribution to &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in human islet physiology.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><h3>NIK is activated in islets in diet-induced obesity</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first">To elucidate whether NIK plays a role in islets in a model of glucose intolerance, a diet-induced obesity (DIO) model, C57BL/6 mice were placed on a 45% kcal high-fat diet (HFD). Islets from DIO mice exhibited activation of the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig2" rid-ob="ob-fig2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig2"><span>Fig. 2 E</span></a>), as indicated by the accumulation of NIK and increased phosphorylation of the NIK downstream target I&#x003ba;B kinase &#x003b1; (IKK&#x003b1;). Activated phospho-IKK&#x003b1; phosphorylates NF-&#x003ba;B p100, leading to its proteasome-dependent cleavage releasing active p52. As the antibody for Phospho-IKK not only identifies phospho-IKK&#x003b1; but also phospho-IKK&#x003b2;, we probed for IKK&#x003b2; downstream target I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1;, which is the canonical NF-&#x003ba;B inhibitor; I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1; levels were normal, indicating absence of canonical NF-&#x003ba;B activation in DIO islets.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="fig2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig2"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig2" rid-ob="ob-fig2"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm139771994662592" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4516791_JEM_20150218_Fig2.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is JEM_20150218_Fig2.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/bin/JEM_20150218_Fig2.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm139771994662592" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_fig2"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="fig2" rid-ob="ob-fig2">Figure 2.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p13"><strong>Pancreatic islets in a DIO model exhibit NIK hyperactivation and display a net &#x003b2; cell secretory defect.</strong> (A) Blood glucose and AUC were determined in 12 chow (dotted line) and 15 DIO (solid line) fed C57BL/6 WT mice (16 wk old) after i.p. injection of <span class="small-caps">d</span>-glucose. The effect of TNF and RANKL on noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B signaling in islets is unknown. TNF induced p100 to p52 processing and RelB accumulation in primary mouse islets, indicating NIK activation (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig2" rid-ob="ob-fig2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig2"><span>Fig. 2 F</span></a>). RANKL induced p100 to 52 processing and RelB accumulation in primary islets, indicating NIK activation, but did not activate canonical NF-&#x003ba;B signaling (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4516791/figure/fig2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="fig2" rid-ob="ob-fig2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_fig2"><span>Fig. 2 G</span></a>). Therefore, both TNF and RANKL activate NIK signaling in primary mouse islets. Additionally, islets isolated from HFD-fed mice show cell-intrinsic activation of NIK and activation of the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway with reduced insulin secretion.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><h3>Cell-intrinsic NIK activation negatively regulates &#x003b2; cell function in a model of diet-induced obesity</h3><p id="__p15" class="p p-first">The aforementioned data present a causal link between &#x003b2; cell dysfunction and NIK activation in DIO. To test this concept further, we used a genetic approach whereby &#x003b2; cells would express high levels of active NIK in a cell-intrinsic manner. NIK levels, and hence its activation, are controlled by an E3 ligase complex; TRAF2 recruits the E3 ligases BIRC2/3 while interactions with TRAF3 allow delivery of NIK to the complex (<a href="#bib51" rid="bib51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278750">Vallabhapurapu et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278751">Zarnegar et al., 2008</a>). In absence of a ligand, NIK is ubiquitinated by this complex and targeted for proteasome-dependent degradation thereby silencing the noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway (<a href="#bib51" rid="bib51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278760">Vallabhapurapu et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278733">Zarnegar et al., 2008</a>). Deletion of any one component, i.e., TRAF2, TRAF3, or the BIRC proteins, results in cell-intrinsic hyperactivation of noncanonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathway at a physiological level (<a href="#bib18" rid="bib18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278766">Gardam et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" rid="bib61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278737">Zarnegar et al., 2008</a>).</p><p id="__p16">To examine how &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic NIK activation would affect glucose homeostasis, we generated &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;specific TRAF2 knockout (&#x003b2;TRAF2) mice. TRAF2<sub>loxP/loxP</sub> were crossed with mice expressing Cre under the control of the rat insulin promoter (RIP Cre). In contrast, I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1; levels were not changed in &#x003b2;TRAF2 islets, indicating that IKK&#x003b2; and canonical NF-&#x003ba;B signaling were not activated. models.</div></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec14" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec14title">DISCUSSION</h2><p id="__p37" class="p p-first">We have identified NIK as a novel mechanism of &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in obesity. Acute NIK activation using a pharmacological approach disrupts glucose homeostasis in a zebrafish model, and induces &#x003b2; cell dysfunction in mouse islets, but also in human islets, highlighting the importance of the NIK pathway in human islet physiology as a negative regulator of &#x003b2; cell function. As we have demonstrated, pancreatic &#x003b2; cells in a model of obesity and glucose intolerance present with &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic NIK activation and a decrease in &#x003b2; cell insulin secretory capacity. We could demonstrate that NIK was induced by inflammatory cytokines TNF and RANKL in pancreatic mouse islets. Both cytokines constitute good candidates as NIK activators in a DIO mouse model and in human obesity. Both RANKL and TNF levels are elevated in mouse models of obesity and in T2D subjects (<a href="#bib26" rid="bib26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278712">Hotamisligil et al., 1995</a>; <a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278701">Kiechl et al., 2013</a>). Blockade of TNF or RANKL improved glucose tolerance in mouse models of obesity (<a href="#bib50" rid="bib50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278718">Uysal et al., 1997</a>; <a href="#bib31" rid="bib31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278716">Kiechl et al., 2013</a>). Further, TNF inhibition has been shown to alleviate the metabolic syndrome in several human studies (<a href="#bib3" rid="bib3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278757">Bernstein et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#bib35" rid="bib35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278767">Marra et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib7" rid="bib7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278722">Campanati et al., 2013</a>).</p><p id="__p38">Our data furthers studies showing that diet-induced obesity induces NIK in peripheral insulin-dependent tissues such as liver (<a href="#bib46" rid="bib46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278744">Sheng et al., 2012</a>) and muscle (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278683">Choudhary et al., 2011</a>). Further evidence for a crucial involvement of NIK in whole body glucose homeostasis comes from studies of NIK knockout mice, which are hypoglycemic and exhibit improved glucose tolerance on a chow diet (<a href="#bib46" rid="bib46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278691">Sheng et al., 2012</a>). Accumulation of NIK is associated with decreased glucose uptake in muscle (<a href="#bib11" rid="bib11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278756">Choudhary et al., 2011</a>) and increased hepatic glucose output by the liver (<a href="#bib46" rid="bib46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278758">Sheng et al., 2012</a>). Thus, NIK is emerging as a global molecular factor in obesity, therefore, these data highlight the possibility of systemic NIK inhibition as a potential therapeutic target to reduce hepatic glucose production and improve muscle uptake of glucose, while simultaneously preserving &#x003b2; cell function.</p><p id="__p39">As we showed for pancreatic islets in diet-induced obesity, NIK levels are increased in the livers of genetically (<em>db/db)</em> and diet-induced obese mice (<a href="#bib46" rid="bib46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278717">Sheng et al., 2012</a>). Hepatocyte-specific NIK overexpression increased hepatic glucose output, presumably by phosphorylation and concomitant elevation and activation of the transcription factor cAMP response element-binding (CREB) levels, which stimulates gluconeogenesis. This process is controlled by cAMP-dependent PKA phosphorylating and activating CREB. We identified <em>Pde7b</em> as a highly expressed gene in islets with constitutively activated NIK (&#x003b2;TRAF2 and &#x003b2;TRAF3 islets). <em>Pde7b</em>, which encodes for phosphodiesterase 7b (PDE7B), is involved in the conversion of cAMP to AMP, thereby decreasing cAMP secondary messenger activity (<a href="#bib25" rid="bib25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278700">Hetman et al., 2000</a>). cAMP regulates glucose sensing and insulin secretion in pancreatic &#x003b2; cells, through both PKA-dependent and-independent mechanisms (<a href="#bib2" rid="bib2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278749">&#x000c4;mmal&#x000e4; et al., 1994</a>; <a href="#bib32" rid="bib32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278725">Leclerc and Rutter, 2004</a>). Significantly, <em>Pde7b</em> and <em>Traf2</em> were both recently shown to be differentially methylated in a genome-wide analysis of human T2D pancreatic islets (<a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278705">Dayeh et al., 2014</a>). <em>Pde7b</em> was found to be differentially expressed in human T2D pancreatic islets as assessed by RT-PCR, and its overexpression caused a reduction in glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in vitro (<a href="#bib13" rid="bib13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278768">Dayeh et al., 2014</a>). This suggests that overactivation of NIK may provide a direct link between regulation of cAMP levels and CREB activity, both in liver and in islets.</p><p id="__p40" class="p p-last">Inflammation has emerged as a key factor in the pathogenesis of the metabolic syndrome acting at the level of muscle and liver to induce insulin resistance, at adipocytes to drive elaboration of cytokines, and at the level of the &#x003b2; cell. Key signaling pathways include the JNK and canonical NF-&#x003ba;B pathways. Here, we demonstrate NIK levels are elevated in pancreatic islets isolated from diet-induced obese mice, which exhibited decreased insulin secretory capacity. Further, several genetic mouse models of constitutive &#x003b2; cell&#x02013;intrinsic NIK activation present impaired glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in diet-induced obesity. Thus, NIK acts as a critical signaling node that regulates a transcriptional network to control glucose homeostasis. NIK activation in obesity is a feature of pancreatic &#x003b2; cells, liver and muscle providing a tissue-spanning mechanistic understanding of the global effects of obesity in the metabolic syndrome.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec15title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2><div id="__sec16" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec17" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Mouse strains. </h4><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">C57BL/6 mice were sourced from Australian BioResource (Mossvale, NSW, Australia). Brink, Garvan Institute, NSW, Australia) and Birc3<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice containing Birc2<sub>loxP/loxP</sub> (C57BL/6 background; gift from D. Free glucose levels were analyzed 6 h after injection using a fluorescent glucose assay kit (Biovision; <a href="#bib29" rid="bib29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278706">Jurczyk et al., 2011</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">In vivo studies. </h4><p id="__p43" class="p p-first-last">For islet transplantation, 150 hand-counted primary islet equivalents isolated from male and female donor mice were transplanted into syngeneic diabetic male C57BL/6 recipients as previously described (<a href="#bib21" rid="bib21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278689">Grey et al., 2003</a>). Mice in the DIO model were fed a diet providing 45% calories from fat (lard/safflower oil) ad libitum. Glucose tolerance tests (i.p. 2 g/kg and i.v. 1 g/kg body weight) after a 16-h fast were performed on female &#x003b2;TRAF2, &#x003b2;TRAF3, and &#x003b2;BIRC2/3 mice and respective floxed and RIP Cre controls mice as previously described (<a href="#bib8" rid="bib8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278704">Cantley et al., 2013</a>). Blood samples for insulin ELISA (mouse ultra-sensitive kit; Crystal Chem) were collected using 5-&#x000b5;l micro-capillary tubes (Drummond microcaps; Sigma-Aldrich). Insulin secretory capacity was determined by normalizing insulin levels or insulin secretion measured by AUC to total &#x003b2; cell area. Islets were isolated as previously described (<a href="#bib60" rid="bib60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278735">Zammit et al., 2013</a>), from male and female donors for GSIS, RNA extraction, and Western blot protein extraction. In vivo mouse physiology experiments comprise data from at least three independent cohorts.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Analysis of TRAF2 and TRAF3 mRNA expression in isolated islets. </h4><p id="__p44" class="p p-first-last">Isolated mRNA (QIAschredder, RNEasy Plus Mini kit; QIAGEN) was reverse transcribed (Quantitect Reverse Transcription kit; QIAGEN). mRNA expression was determined by Real Time-PCRs using FastStart SYBR Green Master Mix (Roche). PCR reactions were performed with the following primers and normalized to the house-keeping gene Cyclophillin A (CpH, sense 5&#x02032;-TGGACCAAACACAAACGGTTCC-3&#x02032; and antisense 5&#x02032;-ACATTGCGAGCAGATGGGGTAG-3&#x02032;; TRAF2, sense 5&#x02032;-TCTGCCTGACCAGCATCCTC-3&#x02032; and antisense 5&#x02032;-AATGCCTTCTTCATACAGGCCTTC-3&#x02032;; TRAF3, sense 5&#x02032;-CAGCGTGCCAAGAAAGCATC-3&#x02032; and antisense 5&#x02032;-CGCACAACCTCTGCCTTCAT-3&#x02032;). Initial denaturation was performed at 95&#x000b0;C for 10 s, followed by a three step cycle consisting of 95&#x000b0;C for 15 s (4.8&#x000b0;C/s, denaturation), 63&#x000b0;C for 30 s (2.5&#x000b0;C/s, annealing), and 72&#x000b0;C for 30 s (4.8&#x000b0;C/s, elongation). A melt-curve was performed after finalization of 45 cycles at 95&#x000b0;C for 2 min, 40&#x000b0;C for 3 min, and gradual increase to 95&#x000b0;C with 25 acquisitions/&#x000b0;C.</p></div><div id="__sec21" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Assessment of signaling pathway activation. </h4><p id="__p45" class="p p-first-last">Protein was extracted from islets or cell lines using a detergent-based lysis buffer. 30&#x02013;40 &#x000b5;g of protein per well were separated on an SDS-PAGE and protein levels were determined by Western Blot analysis using the following antibodies (expected protein size [kD] is indicated): TRAF2 (C-20; 50 kD), TRAF3 (H-122; 65 kD; Santa Cruz Biotechnology Inc.), I&#x003ba;B&#x003b1; (39 kD), NIK (125 kD), phospho-IKK&#x003b1;/&#x003b2; (85/87 kD), p100/p52 (120 &#x00026; 52kD), RelB (70 kD; Cell Signaling Technology), &#x003b2;-actin (42 kD; Sigma-Aldrich), anti&#x02013;rabbit IgG (horseradish peroxidase-conjugated; Thermo Fisher Scientific), and anti&#x02013;mouse IgG (and horseradish peroxidase-conjugated; Thermo Fisher Scientific). Signals were visualized using an enhanced chemiluminescence detection kit (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). Western blot signals were quantified using the ImageJ (NIH) histogram function. Subsequently, the band intensity was normalized to relative protein concentration using &#x003b2; actin as a reference.</p></div><div id="__sec22" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Immunohistochemistry and &#x003b2; cell area determination. </h4><p id="__p46" class="p p-first-last">Tissues were fixed in 10% buffered formalin and 5-&#x000b5;m paraffin sections were stained with hematoxylin-eosin. Serial consecutive sections in 100-&#x000b5;m intervals were stained for insulin (polyclonal anti&#x02013;rabbit; Cell Signaling Technology) and counter-stained with hematoxylin. &#x003b2; cell area was assessed by determining total insulin-positive area (ImageJ).</p></div><div id="__sec23" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">GSIS. </h4><p id="__p47" class="p p-first-last">Three human pancreatic islet isolates were prepared at the St. Vincent&#x02019;s Hospital Centre for Islet Isolation (Melbourne, Australia) from heart-beating deceased donors. GSIS was determined for islets ex vivo as described (<a href="#bib8" rid="bib8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_465278682">Cantley et al., 2013</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec24" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Whole pancreas insulin content. </h4><p id="__p48" class="p p-first-last">To determine whole pancreas insulin content, pancreata were collected and immediately immersed in ice-cold acidified ethanol. The tissue was homogenized with a tissue homogenizer (Heidolph Silent Crusher M) and total pancreatic insulin content was determined by insulin ELISA (mouse ultra-sensitive kit; Crystal Chem).</p></div><div id="__sec25" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Synthesis and purification of the MV1 compound. </h4><p id="__p49" class="p p-first-last">The synthetic routes of MV1 production are available upon request.</p></div><div id="__sec26" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Microarray. </h4><p id="__p50" class="p p-first-last">The quality of mRNA isolated from primary islets was evaluated by using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies). Samples were run in triplicates on a MoGene2.0 (GeneChip Mouse Gene 2.0 ST Array, Affymetrix) at the Ramaciotti Center (University of New South Wales, Australia). Raw data were deposited in the Gene Expression Omnibus database: accession no. <a id="__tag_465278773" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE68317" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE68317"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE68317","term_id":"68317","extlink":"1"}}</span>GSE68317</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec27" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Statistical analysis. </h4><p id="__p51" class="p p-first-last">Statistical analysis was performed using an unpaired two-tailed Students <em>t</em> test using Prism software (GraphPad Inc.). Error bars in all figures represent SEM as specified.</p></div><div id="__sec28" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Study approval. </h4><p id="__p52" class="p p-first-last">Animal studies were approved by the Garvan/St Vincent&#x02019;s Animal Ethics Committee (AEC) fulfilling all the requirements of the NH&#x00026;MRC and the NSW State Government.
