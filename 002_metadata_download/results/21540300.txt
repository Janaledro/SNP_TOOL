In this study we demonstrate that ROR&#x003b1;-deficient staggerer mice (ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>) fed with a high-fat diet (HFD) exhibited reduced adiposity and hepatic triglyceride levels compared with wild-type (WT) littermates and were resistant to the development of hepatic steatosis, adipose-associated inflammation, and insulin resistance. Gene expression profiling showed that many genes involved in triglyceride synthesis and storage, including Cidec, Cidea, and Mogat1, were expressed at much lower levels in liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. In contrast, overexpression of ROR&#x003b1; in mouse hepatoma Hepa1&#x02013;6 cells significantly increased the expression of genes that were repressed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> liver, including <em>Sult1b1</em>, <em>Adfp</em>, <em>Cidea</em>, and <em>ApoA4</em>. ChIP and promoter analysis suggested that several of these genes were regulated directly by ROR&#x003b1;. In addition to reduced lipid accumulation, inflammation was greatly diminished in white adipose tissue (WAT) of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed with an HFD. The infiltration of macrophages and the expression of many immune response and proinflammatory genes, including those encoding various chemo/cytokines, Toll-like receptors, and TNF signaling proteins, were significantly reduced in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> WAT. Moreover, ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed with an HFD were protected from the development of insulin resistance. ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice consumed more oxygen and produced more carbon dioxide, suggesting increased energy expenditure in this genotype. Our study indicates that ROR&#x003b1; plays a critical role in the regulation of several aspects of metabolic syndrome. Therefore, ROR&#x003b1; may provide a novel therapeutic target in the management of obesity and associated metabolic diseases.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">staggerer mice, obesity, metabolic syndrome, macrophage, insulin-resistance, diabetes, retinoid-related orphan receptors</span></div></div><div id="idm140681912249440" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p3" class="p p-first"><span class="small-caps">obesity is a major global</span> health concern. In the United States alone, 30% of the general population is obese and an estimated 66% of all adults are overweight (<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306472">40</a>). Obesity is associated with an elevated risk of several pathologies, including Type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), cancer, and airway disease (<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306458">18</a>, <a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306404">36</a>). The molecular links between obesity and these diseases are beginning to be understood. It is now well recognized that obesity is associated with chronic low-grade inflammation and that inflammatory processes play an important role in obesity and related pathologies (<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306485">18</a>, <a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306510">44</a>, <a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306454">53</a>). Infiltration of macrophages and various T-lymphocytes in hypertrophic adipose tissues have been considered as important early events in the development of obesity-associated complications (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306478">14</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306422">38</a>, <a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306511">57</a>) and the release of proinflammatory cytokines by adipose tissue plays a key role in the development of insulin resistance, NAFDL, and cardiovascular disease.</p><p id="__p4">Retinoid-related orphan receptors ROR&#x003b1;, &#x003b2;, and &#x003b3; (NR1F1&#x02013;3) constitute a subfamily of nuclear receptors that regulate gene transcription by binding as a monomer to ROR-response elements (RORE) in the regulatory region of target genes (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306503">15</a>, <a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306502">20</a>, <a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306499">35</a>). Recent studies have demonstrated that RORs function as ligand-dependent transcription factors (<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306486">20</a>, <a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306425">23</a>, <a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306505">30</a>, <a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306412">48</a>, <a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306479">55</a>). RORs have been implicated in the regulation of a wide variety of physiological processes, including cerebellar development, metabolism, circadian rhythm, cellular immunity, bone formation, hypoxia signaling, and inflammatory responses (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306398">4</a>, <a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306481">9</a>, <a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306480">11</a>, <a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306506">12</a>, <a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306441">16</a>, <a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306456">19</a>, <a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306497">20</a>, <a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306495">24</a>, <a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306396">29</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306470">31</a>&#x02013;<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306509">33</a>, <a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306397">42</a>, <a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306414">43</a>, <a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306448">47</a>, <a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306492">49</a>, <a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306496">54</a>, <a href="#B58" rid="B58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306471">58</a>, <a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306451">60</a>).</p><p id="__p5" class="p">In the present study, we used the staggerer mice (ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>), a natural mutant strain containing a deletion within the ROR&#x003b1; gene, to obtain further insights into the physiological roles of ROR&#x003b1; in energy homeostasis. We show that ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice, but not ROR&#x003b3;<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice, were less susceptible to age- and high-fat diet (HFD)-induced obesity and hepatic steatosis. Moreover, ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice were protected against insulin-resistance and adipose-associated inflammation. Gene expression profiling by microarray analysis revealed differential gene regulation in liver and white adipose tissue (WAT) between wild-type (WT) and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice maintained on an HFD. These include genes involved in hepatic triglyceride accumulation and inflammatory genes. Our study further supports evidence that ROR&#x003b1; plays a critical role in the regulation of lipid/energy homeostasis and metabolic syndrome. As ligand-dependent transcription factor, ROR&#x003b1; may provide a novel therapeutic target for the management of obesity and associated diseases, such as diabetes and NAFLD.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2><div id="__sec2" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Experimental animals. </h4><p id="__p6" class="p p-first-last">Heterozygous C57BL/6 staggerer (ROR&#x003b1;<sup>+/sg</sup>) mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratories (Bar Harbor, ME). ROR&#x003b3;<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> and double knockout ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>/ROR&#x003b3;<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> (DKO) were described previously (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306401">24</a>). Littermate WT mice were used as controls. Mice were supplied ad libitum with mash NIH-A31 formula (1:1 ratio with water). To study diet-induced obesity, 8 to 12 wk old male mice were fed a mash HFD (<a id="__tag_612306596" class="tag_hotlink" href="/nuccore/D12492" ref="/nuccore/D12492"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"D12492","term_id":"220376","term_text":"D12492"}}</span>D12492</a>; Research Diets, New Brunswick, NJ) for 6 wk, unless indicated otherwise. Adiposity was determined with an Piximus densitometer (Lunar, Madison, WI). All animal protocols followed the guidelines outlined by the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS).</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Cell culture and stable cell lines. </h4><p id="__p7" class="p p-first-last">Murine hepatoma Hepa1&#x02013;6 cells were cultured in DMEM with 10% FBS. Hepa1&#x02013;6(E) and Hepa1&#x02013;6(ROR&#x003b1;) cells, stably expressing Flag-ROR&#x003b1;, were established by infecting parental cells with pLXIN(Empty) (Clontech, Palo Alto, CA) and pLXIN-3xFlag-ROR&#x003b1; retrovirus, followed by G418 selection. The expression and nuclear localization of ROR&#x003b1; protein were confirmed by Western blot analysis and immunofluorescent staining, respectively. Samples of 5 separate clones were examined by quantitative real-time PCR (QRT-PCR) and chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) analysis.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">RNA isolation. </h4><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">RNA from mouse tissues was isolated with RNeasy mini or midi kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA) as described previously (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306416">24</a>). The quality and integrity of the RNA was assessed by a Bioanalyzer (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA) and agarose gel electrophoresis.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Histology and immunostaining. </h4><p id="__p9" class="p p-first-last">Adipose and liver specimens were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and paraffin-embedded, and tissue sections (5 &#x003bc;m) were stained with hematoxylin-eosin. To detect macrophages, sections of WAT were stained with antiactive caspase 3 (Promega, Madison, WI) or F4/80 antibody (Santa Cruz Biotechology, Santa Cruz, CA) and an avidin-biotin-peroxidase detection system. F4/80-positive cells in at least six randomly selected fields in sections from five different mice were counted.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Microarray analysis. </h4><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">Microarray analysis was performed with WAT and liver as described detail in supplementary information.<sup><a href="#FN2" rid="FN2" class=" fn">1</a></sup> The data discussed in this publication have been deposited in the National Center for Biotechnology Information's Gene Expression Omnibus at <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3132837&amp;issue-id=198371&amp;journal-id=631&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo</a> #<a id="__tag_612306514" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE23736" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE23736"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE23736","term_id":"23736"}}</span>GSE23736</a>.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">QRT-PCR. </h4><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">The RNA was reversed-transcribed using a high capacity cDNA archive kit according to the manufacturer's instructions (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). QRT-PCR reactions were performed as described previously (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306461">24</a>) with SYBRG and TaqMan systems. Primers are listed in Supplemental Table S1A. All results were normalized relatively to the 18S or GAPDH transcripts.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">ChIP assay. </h4><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">ChIP analysis was performed using a ChIP assay kit (Millipore, Billerica, MA) according to the manufacturer's protocol with minor modifications. Liver tissues from WT and <em>ROR&#x003b1;</em><sup><em>sg/sg</em></sup> mice were homogenized using a polytron PT 3000 (Brinkmann Instruments). After cross-linking with 4% formaldehyde at room temperature for 20 min, cross-linked chromatin was sheered by sonication and then incubated with anti-ROR&#x003b1; antibody (sc-6062; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA) at 4&#x000b0;C overnight. Antibody-chromatin complexes were collected with protein G agarose beads for 2 h. After sequential washes, complexes were eluted and reversed cross-linked by incubation with NaCl at 65&#x000b0;C overnight. After RNase A and proteinase K digestions, the ChIPed-DNA was purified. The fold amount of the ChIPed-DNA relative to each input DNA was determined by quantitative PCR (QPCR). All the QPCR reactions were carried out in triplicate. For ChIP assay using Hepa1&#x02013;6 cells, cells were cross-linked with 4% formaldehyde for 10 min and cross-linked chromatin sheered and immunoprecipitated as described above. Immunoprecipitation was performed by anti-Flag M2 affinity gel (Sigma-Aldrich). Primer sequences for ChIP-QPCR are listed in Supplemental Table S1B.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Reporter assays. </h4><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">To analyze the activity of the mouse Adfp promoter, the region between &#x02212;2043 to +64, which contains two putative ROREs, 5&#x02032;-TTTGTAGGTGA (RORE1) and 5&#x02032;-GAAAGAGGTCA (RORE2), was amplified and cloned into the luciferase reporter plasmid pGL4.10 (Promega) and referred to as pGL4.10-Adfp(<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306494">2</a>). To determine the role of these ROREs, the &#x0201c;GG&#x0201d; in each RORE was mutated to &#x0201c;AA&#x0201d; using QuickChange site-directed mutagenesis kit (Stratagene). The expression vectors 3xFlag-CMV10-ROR&#x003b1;4 and 3xFlag-CMV10-Rev-Erb&#x003b1; were described previously (<a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306447">52</a>). Reporter assays were performed in hepatoma Huh-7 cells as reported previously (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306463">25</a>, <a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306403">52</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Analysis of blood and liver lipids. </h4><p id="__p14" class="p p-first-last">Blood levels of glucose, cholesterol, triglycerides, and high-density lipoprotein (HDL) were determined using the Cobas Mira Classic Chemistry System (Roche Diagnostics Systems, Montclair, NJ). The chemical reagents for all assays were purchased from Equal Diagnostics (Exton, PA). Serum insulin levels were analyzed with an insulin radioimmunoassay kit from Millipore (St. Charles, MO). To measure liver lipid content, tissues were homogenized and lipids extracted as previously described (<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306435">61</a>). Lipids were then dissolved in a mixture of 60 &#x003bc;l of tert-butanol and 40 &#x003bc;l of Triton X-100/methanol (2:1) mix. Triglyceride and cholesterol levels were measured with Stanbio assay kits (Stanbio Laboratory, Boerne, TX) (<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306467">54</a>). Serum leptin levels were analyzed by Elisa using a kit from RayBiotech (Norcross, GA). Steatocrit was analyzed using the perchloric acid method as described (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306428">51</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">LabMaster metabolic analysis. </h4><p id="__p15" class="p p-first-last">Metabolic parameters including, oxygen consumption (VO<sub>2</sub>), CO<sub>2</sub> production (VCO<sub>2</sub>), and respiratory exchange ratio were analyzed with a LabMaster system (TSE Systems, Chesterfield, MO) using WT and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed either with a normal diet or HFD for 6 wk. The average values during light period and dark period were calculated. Student's <em>t</em>-test was used to calculate the <em>P</em> value.</p></div><div id="__sec13" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Glucose tolerance test and insulin tolerance test. </h4><p id="__p16" class="p p-first-last">After an overnight fast, WT and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice were injected intraperitoneally with glucose (2 g/kg) or insulin (0.75 U/kg) (Eli Lilly, Indianapolis, IN) to examine glucose tolerance test (GTT) and insulin tolerance test (ITT), respectively. Levels of glucose were analyzed every 20 min for up to 2 h with glucose test strips (Nova Biomedical, Waltham, MA).</p></div><div id="__sec14" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Isolation of the stromal-vascular fraction and flow cytometry analysis. </h4><p id="__p17" class="p p-first-last">To isolate the stromal-vascular fraction (SVF) cells, epididymal white adipose tissue (eWAT) was isolated from WT and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed with an HFD for 18 wk. After chopping and collagenase treatment for 2 h at 37&#x000b0;C, cell pellets were centrifuged and resuspended in DMEM containing 10% FBS. The cell suspension was filtrated through 70-&#x003bc;m mesh. After centrifugation, red blood cells were removed by treatment with 1&#x000d7; RBC lysis buffer (eBioscience, San Diego, CA). Remaining cells were centrifuged and washed two times in DMEM containing 10% FBS. Fluorescence-conjugated monoclonal antibodies for F4/80 (Invitrogen, Camarillo, CA) and CD11b (BD Biosciences) were used to detect macrophage population. CD3, CD4, and CD8 (BD Biosciences) were used for the analysis of T lymphocytes. Isotype control antibodies were used for negative control, and dead cells were excluded by 7-amino-actinomycin D or propidium iodine staining. Stained cells were analyzed with the BD LSR II Flow cytometer (Becton Dickinson) using FACSDiVa software.</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec15" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec15title">RESULTS</h2><div id="__sec16" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec17" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice were resistant to age-induced obesity. </h4><p id="__p18" class="p p-first">Previous studies indicated a role for RORs in the regulation of several metabolic pathways and energy homeostasis (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306460">1</a>, <a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306482">20</a>, <a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306493">24</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306508">31</a>). To study the metabolic functions of ROR&#x003b1; and ROR&#x003b3; further, we examined whether loss of ROR function had any effect on age-induced obesity. Histological analysis showed that WAT and brown adipose tissues (BAT) of 1 yr old male WT mice became hypertrophic in agreement with a recent report (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1<em>A</em></span></a>) (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306445">13</a>). In sharp contrast, 1 yr old male ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice were greatly resistant to the development of age-induced WAT and BAT hypertrophy (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1<em>A</em></span></a>). Moreover, analysis of the adiposity, measured by Piximus densitometry (dual energy X-ray absorptiometry), showed that ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice exhibited a significantly lower total body fat index compared with WT littermates (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1<em>B</em></span></a>). The expression of several other genes implicated in fatty acid homeostasis was also repressed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> liver. These included acyl-coenzyme A (CoA) thioesterases, <em>Acot3</em> and <em>Acot4</em>, which hydrolyze acyl-CoAs to free fatty acids and CoASH, fatty acid binding protein 5 (Fabp5), adipose differentiation-related protein (<em>Adfp</em>, also termed perilipin 2), lipin 2 (<em>Lpin2</em>), angiopoietin-like 4 (<em>Angptl4</em>), and fibroblast growth factor 21 (<em>Fgf21</em>).</p><p id="__p25">Among other changes, the expression of a several sulfotransferases, cytochrome P450 enzymes, and ATP-binding cassette proteins was significantly reduced in liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice compared with WT counterparts. <em>Sult1e1</em>, which was previously found to be greatly upregulated in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed with a normal diet (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306402">24</a>), was the most dramatically induced gene in the liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice. These data are consistent with previous studies showing that ROR&#x003b1; is involved in the regulation of phase I and II metabolic genes (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306457">24</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306417">31</a>). Several genes involved in circadian rhythm, including the nuclear receptor <em>Rev-Erb&#x003b1;</em>, and the clock genes <em>Per3</em> and <em>Cry2</em>, were downregulated in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) liver, consistent with the reported regulatory function of ROR&#x003b1; in circadian rhythm. The expression of the early growth response genes, <em>Egr1</em> and <em>Egr2</em>, was decreased more than fourfold in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice, while <em>PPAR&#x003b1;</em> and <em>PXR</em> expression was decreased by 50% (Supplemental Table S2). The expression of <em>Srebf</em>, <em>ERR&#x003b1;</em>, <em>LXR&#x003b1;</em>, and <em>Rip140</em>, known regulators of energy and lipid homeostasis, was not significantly altered in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> liver.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140681927768720" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3132837_zh70131136240003.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zh70131136240003.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/bin/zh70131136240003.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140681927768720" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F3/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F3"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F3/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3">Fig. 3.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p26">Reduced expression of several lipogenic genes in liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed an HFD. Apoptotic cells were stained with an antibody for active-caspase 3. <em>E</em>: the number of F4/80-positive cells were decreased in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) than WT(HFD) mice (WT, <em>n</em> = 5; ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>, <em>n</em> = 5). <em>F</em>: stromal-vascular fraction cells from eWAT of WT(HFD) and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) were examined by flow cytometry analysis as described in <span class="small-caps">materials and methods</span>. The percentage of the macrophage population (F4/80<sup>+</sup>CD11b<sup>+</sup> cells) was significantly reduced, but T cell population (CD3<sup>+</sup> cells) and CD4+ and CD8+ subpopulations were unaffected in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice (WT, <em>n</em> = 9; ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>, <em>n</em> = 5). <em>G, H</em>: induction of inflammatory genes and metabolic in WAT was greatly decreased in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) for 6 wk. <em>I</em>: induction of lipogenic genes in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice. Expression of <em>Adfp</em> and <em>Angptl4</em>, which are involved in the regulation of lipid metabolism (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306501">6</a>, <a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306415">27</a>), was repressed in both WAT and liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice (Supplemental Tables S2 and S3, <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7<em>H</em></span></a>). Despite the reduced adiposity, the expression of several genes involved in lipogenesis, including acetyl-Coenzyme A carboxylase a and b (<em>Acaca</em> and <em>Acacb</em>), acyl-CoA synthetase medium-chain family member 3 (<em>Acsm3</em>), and <em>Srebf</em> was enhanced in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3132837/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7<em>I</em></span></a> and Supplemental Table S3). Interestingly, Srebf has been reported to induce <em>Acaca</em> expression, and, therefore, the increase in <em>Srebf</em> might be causally related to the upregulation of <em>Acaca</em> in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) WAT. Among other changes, the expression of a several cytochrome P450 genes and steroid metabolic genes were altered in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) WAT (Supplemental Table S3) consistent with a role of ROR&#x003b1; in the regulation of several metabolic pathways (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306500">24</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306434">31</a>).</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec23" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec23title">DISCUSSION</h2><p id="__p37" class="p p-first">In this study, we show that loss of ROR&#x003b1; protects mice against age- and diet-induced metabolic syndrome, whereas ROR&#x003b3; null mice behaved as WT mice. Specifically, ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice remained lean when maintained on an HFD and showed a markedly reduced susceptibility to the development of hepatic steatosis, adipose tissue-associated inflammation, and insulin resistance. Our study extends previous observations (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306489">24</a>, <a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306483">31</a>) and supports the hypothesis that the nuclear receptor ROR&#x003b1; plays a critical role in the control of energy balance and lipid homeostasis.</p><p id="__p38">The liver of aged ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice and mice fed an HFD contained smaller lipid droplets and significantly lower levels of triglycerides compared with WT mice. The accumulation of hepatic triglycerides is regulated at multiple levels, including uptake, transport, synthesis, storage of lipids, and lipolysis. Our gene-profiling analysis revealed that the expression of a number of lipogenic genes was significantly reduced in the liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. These included <em>Cidec</em> and <em>Cidea</em>, which regulate triglyceride storage, the size of lipid droplets, and lipolysis and which have been reported to be highly induced in hepatic steatosis (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306429">17</a>, <a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306442">39</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306488">41</a>). In addition, the expression of several genes implicated in the main pathway of triglyceride synthesis (<a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306421">37</a>), including glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (<em>Gpam</em> or <em>Gpat1</em>) and acyl-glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase 9 (<em>Agpat9</em>), and <em>Mogat1</em>, which is part of an alternative, less-studied pathway of triglyceride synthesis, was significantly reduced in liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. The combined effects on the regulation of multiple genes may have accounted for the reduced lipid accumulation in the liver of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice.</p><p id="__p39">The reduced expression of several other genes, including <em>Adfp</em>, <em>Angptl4</em>, <em>Fgf21</em>, and <em>Gdf15</em>, may also have contributed to the decreased accumulation of hepatic triglycerides in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. The expression of several of these genes was reported to be induced in obesity and all four proteins have been implicated in the control of lipid and/or glucose homeostasis (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306405">7</a>, <a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306410">8</a>, <a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306439">10</a>, <a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306446">59</a>). Adfp (or perilipin 2) is a major lipid droplet protein present in cells that accumulate triglycerides, and loss of Adfp has been reported to reduce triglycerides levels and to relieve hepatosteatosis in leptin-deficient <em>ob/ob</em> mice (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306498">7</a>). Thus, the reduced levels of Adfp expression observed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice might contribute to the protection against hepatic steatosis in these mice.</p><p id="__p40">It is well recognized that obesity is associated with low-grade systemic inflammation and that inflammation plays a key role in obesity-linked pathologies, such as hepatic steatosis and insulin resistance (<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306400">18</a>, <a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306424">44</a>, <a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306409">53</a>). The accumulation of various immune cells, including macrophages and T lymphocytes, in adipose tissues and the production of a variety of proinflammatory cyto- and chemokines are critical events in the development of obesity-associated inflammation (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306413">14</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306449">38</a>, <a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306408">57</a>). Our study supports the growing recognition of a relationship between obesity and adipose tissue-associated inflammation. Our gene expression profiling showed that many proinflammatory and immune-regulatory genes, including those encoding various interleukins, chemokines, chemo/cytokine receptors, TNF-&#x003b1;, Toll-like receptors, and metalloproteinases, were expressed at significantly lower levels in the WAT of HFD-fed ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice compared with their WT counterparts, suggesting that ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice develop considerably less inflammation when challenged with an HFD. This was supported by the greatly reduced infiltration of macrophages in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice as indicated by the rare presence of CLS and the lower percentage of SVF-associated F4/80<sup>+</sup>/Cd11b<sup>+</sup> macrophages in WAT of HFD-fed ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. Moreover, the expression of several macrophage markers, including <em>F4/80</em>, <em>Mac-2</em>, <em>Mpeg1</em>, and <em>Msr1</em>, was significantly decreased in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. ATF3, a basic leucine zipper-type transcription factor, is one of the transcription factors whose expression was significantly reduced in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice (Supplemental Table S3). Although ATF3 expression is greatly increased in obese adipose tissue and correlates with macrophage accumulation, it functions as a negative feedback mechanism to attenuate obese-macrophage activation (<a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306399">50</a>). Recent studies have shown that WAT contains different macrophage populations. Obesity induces a shift from mannose receptor (Mrc1 or Cd206) positive, Cd11c<sup>+</sup> macrophages to Mrc1-negative macrophages that express Ccr2, 3, and 5. The expression of both Mrc1 and these chemokine receptors was downregulated in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> WAT, consistent with a reduction in all three macrophage populations.</p><p id="__p41">Although T-lymphocytes have been reported to play a role in recruiting macrophages to WAT (<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306491">38</a>), no significant difference was observed in the percentage of CD4<sup>+</sup> or CD8<sup>+</sup> T lymphocytes in WAT of WT(HFD) and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice. ROR&#x003b1; has been implicated in anti- as well as proinflammatory responses, including the inhibition of NF-&#x003ba;B signaling and induction of Th17 differentiation, respectively (<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306474">9</a>, <a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306443">60</a>). The diminished inflammatory response in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup>(HFD) mice is consistent with reports showing reduced inflammatory responses in allergen-induced airway inflammation and inhibition of experimental encephalomyelitis in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice (<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306437">19</a>, <a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306490">60</a>).</p><p id="__p42">ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice maintained on an HFD remained insulin sensitive and glucose tolerant, suggesting that these mice were less susceptible to obesity-induced Type 2 diabetes. The expression of a number of genes implicated in the regulation of insulin resistance was found to be downregulated in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. Loss of Adfp has been reported to improve insulin resistance in leptin-deficient <em>ob/ob</em> mice (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306477">7</a>). Thus, the reduced expression of Adfp observed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice may have contributed to the protection against insulin resistance. The interleukin-1 receptor antagonist (<em>Il1rn</em>) and <em>Ssp1</em> were among the genes most dramatically repressed in WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. <em>IL1rn</em> has been reported to be highly upregulated in WAT of obese humans and to regulate insulin sensitivity (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306430">21</a>, <a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306487">45</a>), while <em>Il1rn</em>-deficient mice exhibited decreased adiposity and increased energy expenditure (<a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306464">46</a>). <em>Ssp1</em> expression was found to be elevated in obesity, while neutralization of Ssp1 was shown to inhibit obesity-induced inflammation and insulin resistance (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306433">3</a>, <a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306473">28</a>). Thus, the downregulation of <em>Adfp, Il1rn, Ssp1</em>, and likely other genes may have collaboratively promoted insulin sensitivity in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice through their interrelated effects on inflammation, adipogenesis, and energy expenditure.</p><p id="__p43">Exogenous expression of ROR&#x003b1; in mouse hepatoma Hepa1&#x02013;6 significantly increased the expression of several genes that were found to be repressed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> liver, including <em>Sult1b1</em>, <em>Adfp</em>, <em>Cidea</em>, and <em>ApoA4</em>. It is likely that certain ROR&#x003b1;-responsive genes are regulated directly by ROR&#x003b1; through its interaction with ROREs in their respective regulatory region, while others are controlled by indirect mechanisms (<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306455">20</a>). A recent study identified <em>Fgf21</em> as a direct target of ROR&#x003b1; transcriptional regulation in HepG2 cells (<a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306420">56</a>). The regulation of <em>Fgf21</em> by ROR&#x003b1; is supported by our microarray analysis showing that expression of <em>Fgf21</em> is reduced in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> liver. Examination of the sequence of the upstream regulatory region of a number of ROR&#x003b1;-responsive genes identified putative ROREs in several genes. ChIP analysis indicated that ROR&#x003b1; protein was recruited to the RORE-containing promoter region of <em>Adfp</em> and <em>Sult1b1</em> in the liver of WT mice, but not in that of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. Moreover, ROR&#x003b1; was recruited to the promoter regions of <em>Adfp</em> and <em>Sult1b1</em> in Hepa1&#x02013;6(ROR&#x003b1;) cells. These observations suggested that <em>Adfp</em> and <em>Sult1b1</em> are direct target genes of ROR&#x003b1;. Interestingly, both ROR&#x003b1; and ROR&#x003b3; were recruited to the <em>Sult1b1</em> promoter, whereas the <em>Adfp</em> promoter was ROR&#x003b1;-selective. These data are consistent with our previous observations showing redundancy between ROR&#x003b1; and ROR&#x003b3; in the regulation of certain genes, while other genes are regulated in an ROR isotype-selective manner (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306476">24</a>). The direct regulation of <em>Adfp</em> by ROR&#x003b1; was supported by reporter gene analysis. ROR&#x003b1; was able to enhance the <em>Adfp</em> promoter activity, while mutation of RORE2 abolished this activation.</p><p id="__p44">Several studies have demonstrated an interrelationship between clock proteins and various metabolic functions (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306468">2</a>). The effect of ROR&#x003b1; on clock gene regulation could be a component of the mechanism by which ROR&#x003b1; controls metabolism. However, <em>Bmal1</em>, <em>Npas2</em>, and <em>Clock</em> expression were not changed in the liver and WAT of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice (52, and not shown). Interestingly, in contrast to ROR&#x003b1;, <em>Adfp</em> exhibits a strong oscillatory pattern of expression during circadian time with the lowest level of expression at ZT8&#x02013;12, a time at which Rev-Erb&#x003b1; is most highly expressed. We provide evidence that both Rev-Erb&#x003b1; and ROR&#x003b1; play a role in the circadian regulation of <em>Adfp</em> likely by competing for the same RORE.</p><p id="__p45">Obesity is a consequence of an imbalance between energy intake and expenditure. The decreased adiposity in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice is not due to reduced food intake nor due to an increase in lipid excretion. Indirect calorimetric analysis of 2 mo old WT and ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice fed either a normal or HFD showed that VO<sub>2</sub>, VCO<sub>2</sub>, and heat generation were significantly enhanced in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice on the basis of unadjusted data or those adjusted for free fat mass. The hypothesis of increased energy expenditure was also supported by the upregulation of <em>Ucp-1</em> in BAT and particularly in the skeletal muscle of ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice. Thus, the elevated energy expenditure observed in ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice may at least in part be responsible for the reduced weight gain and resistance to hepatic steatosis and insulin insensitivity.</p><p id="__p46" class="p p-last">In summary, our study demonstrates that ROR&#x003b1;<sup>sg/sg</sup> mice are protected from age- and diet-induced hepatic steatosis, obesity-associated inflammation, and insulin resistance. Loss of ROR&#x003b1; inhibited the hepatic expression of several genes implicated in lipid accumulation and storage as well as the expression of several inflammatory genes in WAT. We provide evidence indicating that some of these genes are regulated directly by ROR&#x003b1;. Recent studies have suggested that the activity of ROR&#x003b1; can be regulated by natural and synthetic compounds (<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306418">30</a>, <a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_612306436">55</a>), which raises hopes that ROR&#x003b1; may provide a novel therapeutic target in the management and prevention of obesity and related pathologies, such as diabetes.</p></div><div id="__sec24" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec24title">GRANTS</h2><p id="__p47" class="p p-first-last">This research was supported by the Intramural Research Program of the NIEHS (Z01-ES-101586).</p></div><div id="__sec25" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec25title">DISCLOSURES</h2><p id="__p48" class="p p-first-last">No conflicts of interest, financial or otherwise, are declared by the author(s).</p></div><div id="__sec26" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec26title">Supplementary Material</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="PMC_1">
