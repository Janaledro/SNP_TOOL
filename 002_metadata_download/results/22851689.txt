Disturbed homeostasis causes organ failure, growth retardation, and metabolic disorders. Here, we show that the <em class="genus-species">Drosophila melanogaster</em> activating transcription factor 3 (Atf3) safeguards metabolic and immune system homeostasis. Loss of Atf3 results in chronic inflammation and starvation responses mounted primarily by the larval gut epithelium, while the fat body suffers lipid overload, causing energy imbalance and death. Hyperactive proinflammatory and stress signaling through NF-&#x003ba;B/Relish, Jun N-terminal kinase, and FOXO in <em>atf3</em> mutants deregulates genes important for immune defense, digestion, and lipid metabolism. Reducing the dose of either FOXO or Relish normalizes both lipid metabolism and gene expression in <em>atf3</em> mutants. The function of Atf3 is conserved, as human ATF3 averts some of the <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> mutant phenotypes, improving their survival. The single <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3 may incorporate the diversified roles of two related mammalian proteins.</p></div></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">INTRODUCTION</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first">Animal growth and development rely on coordinated functions of body organs to balance energy consumption and storage under optimal or challenging conditions, such as food scarcity or pathogen attack. When adverse conditions are temporary, animals survive by lengthening development and postponing maturity, reducing their body size (<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464803">16</a>, <a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464833">65</a>). In contrast, prolonged starvation or chronic inflammation may exhaust energy reserves, causing death (<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464854">32</a>, <a href="#B69" rid="B69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_344693829">69</a>). The key nutrient-sensing, metabolic, and immune-signaling pathways are functionally conserved across phyla. In the fruit fly <em class="genus-species">Drosophila melanogaster</em>, as in vertebrates, insulin/insulin growth factor (IGF) signaling (IIS) primarily regulates growth and metabolic homeostasis (<a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464834">56</a>). The innate immune response of the fly relies on the Toll receptor and the immune deficiency (Imd) pathways (<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464911">35</a>). In response to bacterial or fungal infection, the Toll and Imd pathways engage downstream transcription factors of the NF-&#x003ba;B-like family (Dif, Dorsal, and Relish) that in turn trigger synthesis of a battery of antimicrobial peptides (AMPs).</p><p id="__p4">It has become evident that the metabolic and immune system responses are tightly interconnected. Selective activation of Toll signaling in the <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> fat body attenuates IIS to reduce nutrient stores and overall growth (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464890">14</a>). Mycobacterial infection causes energy wasting due to the systemic activation of the Forkhead transcription factor FOXO (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464896">15</a>). Activation of the Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) pathway is a well-established example of antagonistic regulation of IIS in both flies and vertebrates (<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464841">29</a>). Chronic inflammation, accompanied by high JNK activity, is at the heart of the metabolic syndrome and type 2 diabetes (<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464900">54</a>). Interestingly, genetic removal of IIS pathway components, starvation, and DNA damage all induce AMP expression in the absence of infection. This infection-independent AMP upregulation requires both FOXO (<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464857">5</a>) and Relish (<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464856">30</a>, <a href="#B70" rid="B70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464840">70</a>), further supporting the notion that maintenance of metabolic and innate immune balance are intimately linked.</p><p id="__p5">The <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> fat body and gut integrate metabolic and inflammatory signals to coordinate energy use. The fly alimentary tract digests and absorbs nutrients, while the fat body metabolizes and stores them. In addition, both organs can mount an immune response. The fat body requires both the Toll and Imd pathways for AMP production, whereas the gut immune response relies solely on Imd signaling (<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464888">35</a>, <a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464838">62</a>). In contrast to the low basal immune activity within the unchallenged fat body, the presence of commensal bacteria in the gut lumen keeps the gut epithelium permanently alert with activated nuclear Relish. In this case, additional factors, such as the homeobox gene <em class="genus-species">caudal</em> (<a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464820">49</a>), control AMP expression. Tightly regulated production of positive and negative Imd modulators prevents overgrowth of pathogens while preserving beneficial commensal bacteria. Uncontrolled inflammatory response of the intestinal epithelium has a dramatic impact on gut physiology and homeostasis in both flies and humans (<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464808">43</a>, <a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464915">47</a>). A failing epithelial barrier and excessive intestinal inflammation are considered major causes of human inflammatory bowel diseases (IBDs) and the autoimmune celiac disease and have been associated with the systemic inflammatory response syndrome and type 1 diabetes (<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464864">39</a>).</p><p id="__p6">This study implicates the <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> activating transcription factor 3 (Atf3) as an important link between immunity and metabolism. Atf3 belongs to the family of basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factors. In mammals, various stress signals, including cytokines, bacterial infection, starvation, and irradiation, induce expression of ATF3. As a homodimer, ATF3 can repress transcription, whereas ATF3/Jun heterodimers activate it (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464880">21</a>). Mammalian ATF3 has a paralog, Jun-dimerizing protein 2 (JDP2). Knockout mice for either gene are viable but suffer immune and metabolic defects. ATF3 controls the extent of Toll-like receptor (TLR)-induced immune response (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464893">17</a>, <a href="#B67" rid="B67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464914">67</a>) and can influence glucose levels by regulating &#x003b2;-cell viability and function (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464853">23</a>, <a href="#B76" rid="B76" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464800">76</a>). <em>Jdp2</em><sup><em>KO</em></sup> mutants develop extra adipose tissue and store surplus fat (<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464878">41</a>). Changes in ATF3 expression are associated with human diseases, including diabetes and cancer (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_712835848">22</a>).</p><p id="__p7" class="p p-last">Here, we show that <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3, a single ortholog of the ATF3 and JDP2 proteins, maintains both innate immune and metabolic homeostasis. Loss of <em>atf3</em> primarily affects the gut and fat body, resulting in a complex lethal phenotype of chronic inflammation, combined with a metabolic defect, manifested as simultaneous starvation and obesity. The mutant phenotype is partially alleviated either by reducing the activity of Relish or FOXO or by supplying human ATF3. We suggest that the single <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3 protein combines functions of the duplicated mammalian proteins.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec2title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Transgenic constructs. </h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">The genomic <em>atf3</em>::<em>egfp</em> fusion construct <em>atf3</em><sup><em>[gBAC]</em></sup> was generated from bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) CH321-51N24 (<a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464908">63</a>) in the attB-P[acman]-CmR vector (<em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Genomic Resource Center, Bloomington, IN). In line with these data, we detected ectopic activity of an <em>attacinA</em>::<em>gfp</em> transgenic reporter in the proventriculus of <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4A</span></a>), but not in the fat body. These results corroborate the notion that the larval gut is the primary site of deregulated immune and stress responses in <em>atf3</em> mutants.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140189689965616" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zmb9991096680004.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zmb9991096680004.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/bin/zmb9991096680004.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm140189689965616" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F4"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4">Fig 4</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p30">AMP expression, stress response, and microbiota are increased in the <em>atf3</em> mutant gut. Consistent with this correlation, the second GO cluster encompassed genes of the Imd and Toll innate immunity pathways. Expression of all three NF-&#x003ba;B/Relish-like transcription factors (Rel, Dl, and Dif) was increased in <em>atf3</em> mutants. In contrast, inhibitors of Imd signaling (PGRP-SC1a and PGRP-SC1b) and lysozymes (LysB to -E) were strongly underexpressed in <em>atf3</em>-deficient larvae. To examine possible changes in the intestinal microbiota, we cultured bacteria from control and <em>atf3</em>-deficient larvae and found that the latter contained more bacteria (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4C</span></a>). However, the elevated expression of AMP genes (e.g., <em>dro2</em>) persisted even when we reduced the populations of commensal bacteria by growing <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae on food with antibiotics (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4F</span></a> and data not shown), and the antibiotics failed to improve larval survival. Therefore, it is plausible that the chronic inflammatory reaction in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae arose independently of bacterial infection. However, we cannot exclude effects of bacterial species that could not be cultured under our conditions and that might resist the antibiotic treatment.</p></div><div id="__sec18" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline"><em>atf3</em> mutants are obese while experiencing starvation. </h3><p id="__p33" class="p p-first">Active immune response comes at a considerable energy cost, compromising development, growth, and fitness. A chronic inflammatory state is an important risk factor in the development of various metabolic disorders ranging from wasting and cachexia to obesity and insulin resistance (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464825">14</a>, <a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464866">15</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464912">32</a>, <a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464873">54</a>, <a href="#B69" rid="B69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_344693828">69</a>). The chronic inflammation and stress phenotype of the developmentally delayed, growth retarded, and eventually lethal <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> mutants resembles this situation.</p><p id="__p34">To address whether <em>atf3</em> mutants suffer from disrupted metabolic and energy homeostasis, we examined their nutrient reserves. Staining of tissues from feeding third-instar larvae for neutral lipids revealed that, in contrast to the uniform, moderately sized lipid droplets in the control fat body, adipose cells of <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae contained lipid droplets of variable size, with extremely large droplets prevailing (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5A</span></a>). Staining with Nile red detected enlarged lipid droplets also in the mutant midgut (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5B</span></a>). Compared to controls, feeding <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae had twice the amount of stored fat (TAG) and nearly as much DAG and FFA (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5C</span></a>). In contrast to lipids, neither circulating carbohydrates (trehalose and glucose) nor glycogen stores were significantly different between fed control and mutant larvae (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5D</span></a>; see Fig. S4A in the supplemental material). Therefore, <em>atf3</em> mutants are obese but not hyperglycemic. Respirometry showed that both control and <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae primarily metabolized carbohydrates (RQ, close to 1.0); however, a lower rate of gas exchange in <em>atf3</em> mutants indicated that their total metabolism was slower (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Fig. 5E</span></a>). (E and F) The abnormally large lipid droplets and high TAG levels in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae become normalized upon supply of the <em>UAS-atf3</em><sup><em>[A]</em></sup> transgene or expression of hATF3 in the fat body. Scale bars = 40 &#x003bc;m. The TAG content was determined using the colorimetric assay. (G) qRT-PCR analysis showing that hATF3 can completely or partially normalize expression of some (<em>rel</em> and <em>attD</em>) but not other (<em>dro2</em>) genes misregulated in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> mutant larvae. The data are means and SEM; <em>n</em> = 4; *, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.05; **, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.01; and ***, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.001.</p></div></div></div><p id="__p41" class="p p-last">Taken together, our data show that chronic inflammation in <em>atf3</em> mutants is associated with a complex metabolic phenotype characterized by obesity but not hyperglycemia. Despite elevated lipid storage and normal carbohydrate levels, <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae have a transcriptome pattern of starvation response even when fed <em>ad libitum</em>. However, upon food withdrawal, they activate starvation response genes and mobilize nutrient reserves.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Human ATF3 normalizes fat content and gene expression in <em>atf3</em> mutants. </h3><p id="__p42" class="p p-first-last">A single copy of the rescuing <em class="genus-species">Drosophila UAS-atf3</em><sup><em>[A]</em></sup> transgene (without a <em>Gal4</em> driver) reduced the abnormally large lipid droplets and high TAG levels. Strikingly, expression of hATF3 using <em>FB-Gal4</em> was capable of similar reduction of lipid droplet size and fat content in the mutants (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7E</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">andF).</span></span><span>F</span></a>). Expression of some (but not all) of the genes ectopically activated in an <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> background, such as <em>relish</em>, <em>attD</em>, or <em>CG5550</em>, was completely or partially normalized by hATF3 (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7G</span></a>). Therefore, hATF3 can partially substitute for its <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> ortholog in maintaining immune and metabolic homeostasis by modulating the expression of certain regulatory genes. These data strengthen our previous conclusion that at least some of the functions of <em>atf3</em> have been evolutionarily conserved despite the limited protein sequence homology.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Attenuation of immunity improves lipid metabolism and survival of <em>atf3</em> mutants. </h3><p id="__p43" class="p p-first">Suppressed activity of NF-&#x003ba;B has been shown to simultaneously reduce inflammation and improve insulin sensitivity in mouse obesity models (<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464802">9</a>, <a href="#B73" rid="B73" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464860">73</a>). In <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em>, the transcription factor FOXO is known to antagonize insulin signaling and stimulate expression of the AMP, <em>thor</em>, or <em>lip4</em> gene in response to JNK stress signaling, or starvation (<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464877">5</a>, <a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464823">57</a>, <a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464897">64</a>). To address to what extent the high inflammatory tone and associated metabolic anomalies contribute to <em>atf3</em> mutant phenotypes and lethality, we suppressed the function of NF-&#x003ba;B/Relish and FOXO in <em>atf3</em> mutants (see Fig. S5A in the supplemental material) and analyzed the gene expression profiles of third-instar larvae. Introducing either <em>foxo</em> (<em>foxo</em><sup>&#x00394;</sup><sup><em>94</em></sup> or <em>foxo</em><sup><em>25J13</em></sup>) or <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup> loss-of-function mutations into the <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup><em>/Y</em> background restored normal levels of 98 and 80 transcripts, respectively (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8A</span></a>; see Data set S1 in the supplemental material). They included numerous representatives of the polysaccharide/chitin metabolism and defense/immune response GO functional groups (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3A</span></a>), such as <em>dro2</em>, <em>CG32302</em>, <em>CG5550</em>, and <em>lip3</em>, which we subsequently verified by qRT-PCR (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8B</span></a>). Several of these genes (e.g., <em>IM3</em>, <em>CG32302</em>, <em>CG5550</em>, <em>totA</em>, <em>totC</em>, and <em>Muc18B</em>) have been previously shown to be induced by immune challenge in a Relish-dependent manner (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464876">8</a>, <a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464916">13</a>).</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140189678818304" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3457521_zmb9991096680008.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zmb9991096680008.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/bin/zmb9991096680008.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140189678818304" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F8"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8">Fig 8</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p44">Effect of reduced Relish and FOXO activity on gene expression, lipid storage, and survival in <em>atf3</em> mutants. These data indicated that some prominent changes in the gene expression profile in <em>atf3</em> mutants rely on the two important transcriptional regulators, Relish and FOXO. Nevertheless, lowering the dose of functional FOXO or Relish was insufficient to normalize the activity of some other genes (e.g., <em>attD</em> and <em>thor</em>) whose expression was misregulated by the loss of <em>atf3</em>, suggesting that mechanisms independent of FOXO and Relish are at play.</p><p id="__p46" class="p p-last">Importantly, along with the partially normalized gene expression profile, we observed improved lipid metabolism when FOXO or Relish activity was reduced in <em>atf3</em> mutants. While neither Relish nor FOXO heterozygosity affected lipid contents (see Fig. S5B in the supplemental material), the high TAG and DAG levels dropped to close to normal in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup><em>/Y foxo</em><sup><em>25J13</em></sup><em>/+</em> larvae (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8C</span></a>). Reducing Rel function also restored wild-type levels of TAG and DAG caused by <em>atf3</em> deficiency, but in addition, it significantly lowered the titer of free fatty acids. These results indicated that, besides transcriptional control of AMP genes, Relish may regulate lipid metabolism. Indeed, we found that <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup> larvae stored less TAG than wild-type larvae, whereas overexpression of an activated form of Relish (Rel-68) (<a href="#B68" rid="B68" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464824">68</a>) using either <em>FB-Gal4</em> or <em>C7-Gal4</em> resulted in elevated TAG levels (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8D</span></a>), thus mimicking the effect of <em>atf3</em> mutation. The normalized gene expression and the recovery of lipid homeostasis upon lowering Relish activity correlated with increased survival of <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup><em>/Y rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup><em>/+</em> males compared to their <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup><em>/Y TM6B/+</em> siblings (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8E</span></a>). In contrast, we did not rescue adult eclosion by reducing the dose of <em>foxo</em>.</p></div></div><div id="__sec21" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec21title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">DISCUSSION</h2><div id="__sec22" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Atf3 role in the gut or fat body. </h3><p id="__p47" class="p p-first">Loss of <em>atf3</em> broadly altered the gene expression program of the third-instar <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> larvae. The extent of changes is not surprising, given that Atf3 proteins are bZIP transcription factors that dimerize with several members of the Jun subfamily capable of gene repression or activation depending on the cellular context (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464810">21</a>). Atf3-Atf3 and Atf3-Jun dimers also occur in <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464813">51</a>). Mammalian ATF3 proteins influence other transcription factors, including NF-&#x003ba;B, Smad3, and p53 (<a href="#B58" rid="B58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464829">58</a>, <a href="#B71" rid="B71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464799">71</a>). We therefore presume that the extensive alteration of gene expression in <em>atf3</em> mutants resulted from a combined effect of multiple transcriptional regulators rather than Atf3 alone. Our transcriptome analyses in synthetic genotypes implicate Relish and FOXO in the complex transcriptional regulation by Atf3.</p><p id="__p48">The changes in <em>atf3</em> mutants primarily involved genes that have been functionally annotated with polysaccharide/chitin metabolisms and immune and stress responses. Some genes acting in lipid metabolism and starvation response also turned up. Although our RNA libraries were derived from whole larvae, the transcriptome analysis clearly highlighted genes active in the gut. Even the strong upregulation of starvation response lipase genes&#x02014;<em>lip3</em>, formerly ascribed to the fat body (<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464870">45</a>), and <em>CG6271&#x02014;</em>proved to arise from the gut (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3B</span></a>). The <em>atf3</em> mutant profile overlapped to a striking extent with gene expression changes induced in the adult gut by ingested pathogenic bacteria (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464815">8</a>). Ingestion of wheat germ agglutinin (<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464869">36</a>) was another insult to the gut with a transcriptome signature matching <em>atf3</em> deficiency. WGA disturbs PM integrity and permeability. Making the PM barrier less protective against pathogens and at the same time hindering nutrient uptake leads to infection and starvation (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464868">25</a>). Therefore, not surprisingly, a number of PM-associated genes have been found to depend on Relish (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464816">8</a>). In contrast to damage inflicted by WGA (<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464848">36</a>) and <em>Ecc15</em> (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464830">1</a>) bacteria, both the microvilli of the gut epithelium and the PM appeared intact in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae (see Fig. S6 in the supplemental material). Still, enrichment for 23 mRNAs encoding PM components and proteins facilitating chitin and glycosaminoglycan (GAG) modification (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3A</span></a>) indicates that by regulating PM-associated genes, Atf3 is important for proper functioning of the gut in immune defense and digestion. The GAG content is known to affect PM permeability (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464904">25</a>), which might contribute to the malnutrition and retarded growth in <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae. Interestingly, loss of a chitin-binding protein, Drosocrystallin (Dcy), has been recently shown to compromise PM integrity in adult <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> flies (<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464872">33</a>). <em>dcy</em> mutants are sensitive to entomopathogenic bacteria, likely due to increased permeability of the PM, and they also activate the Imd immune pathway. Another anomaly of the <em>atf3</em>-deficient gut is an elevated count of natural commensal populations, with <em class="genus-species">Acetobacter</em> sp. being highly amplified (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4D</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">andE).</span></span><span>E</span></a>). Growing evidence links intestinal microbiota with energy homeostasis and metabolic disorders, including obesity (<a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464817">55</a>, <a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464843">59</a>). How the altered bacterial composition contributes to the <em>atf3</em> mutant phenotype remains to be resolved.</p><p id="__p49">The gene expression program of fed <em>atf3</em> mutants partially overlapped (e.g., upregulated <em>lip3</em> or <em>CG6271</em>) with transcriptional changes induced by starvation. However, some typical starvation response enzymes, such as phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (PEPCK) or the TAG lipase Brummer, remained unchanged (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span>Fig. 3B</span></a>; see Data set S1 in the supplemental material) (<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464850">19</a>, <a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464828">42</a>, <a href="#B75" rid="B75" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464875">75</a>). Unlike under true starvation, the fed <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae had normal carbohydrate levels and accumulated surplus fat in markedly enlarged lipid droplets in their fat bodies instead of utilizing it to maintain energy balance. When actually deprived of food, they activated starvation response genes and depleted TAG reserves, indicating that the lipolytic control system (<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464889">20</a>) was functional.</p><p id="__p50" class="p">Clearly, both the gut and the fat body express Atf3, and both are affected by its loss. A malfunctioning gut necessarily perturbs metabolism in the fat body (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a>). Therefore, it is difficult to determine which of the two organs suffers the primary damage in the absence of Atf3. Based on the strong bias of the gene expression data, including expression of <em>atf3</em> itself, toward the gut, we surmise that it is primarily the gut that is insulted. The ensuing quasistarvation response is then due to a combined effect of inadequate nutrition and energy shunting to sustain the chronic inflammatory response in <em>atf3</em> mutants. We propose that the ectopic lipase activity misregulates lipid absorption and storage in enterocytes, resulting in excess DAG and FFA. DAG is taken up from the blood by the fat body and converted to TAG, causing lipid overload and obesity. The chronic elevation of DAG and FFA may further unbalance homeostasis due to their cytotoxic effects (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a>). It is well established that FFA trigger cellular stress and inflammation mediated by JNK and NF-&#x003ba;B (<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464839">54</a>). Activation of JNK and FOXO (<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464913">29</a>) might exacerbate the deregulated metabolic balance and contribute to growth repression of <em>atf3</em> mutant larvae.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140189684222384" class="figure"><img class="fig-image" alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zmb9991096680009.jpg" title="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zmb9991096680009.jpg" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/bin/zmb9991096680009.jpg" /></div><div id="largeobj_idm140189684222384" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F9"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9">Fig 9</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p51">Model for Atf3 function in maintaining basal metabolic and immune system homeostasis in <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> larvae. Atf3 ensures the normal digestive and immune function of the gut by controlling appropriate expression of genes involved in PM composition. At the same time, Atf3 prevents the epithelial immune system from overreacting, at least partly through regulating Relish. Loss of Atf3 function causes chronic inflammation accompanied by stress and starvation responses that are mediated by JNK and FOXO signaling. Elevated activity of lipases in the gut may cause imbalance in nutrient utilization. The fat body takes up superfluous DAG and FFA and stores extra fat. An increase in the gut microbiota may further exacerbate defects in digestion, nutrient absorption, and immune system activity.</p></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec23" class="sec"><p></p><h3 class="inline">Link between immune and metabolic homeostasis. </h3><p id="__p52" class="p p-first">Enhanced activity of Imd and Toll signaling effectors, such as Relish, AMPs, and IMs, in <em>atf3</em> mutants coincided with activation of wound healing and the stress-inducible JNK pathway, producing a complex inflammatory response. This response arose even when the bacterial load was reduced by antibiotic treatment, suggesting that the normal role of Atf3 is to maintain a basal level of defense in the gut cells (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a>). In the absence of Atf3, an unprovoked immune reaction ensues. Interestingly, an immune suppressor role for mouse ATF3 has emerged from a systems biology approach (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464806">17</a>). When highly expressed, ATF3 suppresses proinflammatory genes downstream of TLR signaling, such as <em>IL-6</em>, <em>IL-12b</em>, and <em>TNF-</em>&#x003b1;, by recruiting a histone deacetylase to their promoters containing an ATF3 binding site (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464879">17</a>, <a href="#B67" rid="B67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464809">67</a>). Consequently, <em>Atf3</em><sup><em>KO</em></sup> mice die under septic shock induced by lipopolysaccharide (LPS) injection, showing that ATF3 is required to prevent the immune system from overreacting once it has responded to a challenge (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464826">17</a>). Our experiments show that even moderate gain of Atf3 in wild-type larvae represses transcription of the immune response genes <em>relish</em> and <em>thor</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7A</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">andB),</span></span><span>B</span></a>), while expression of both genes is elevated in <em>atf3</em>-deficient mutants (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Fig. 6</span></a>). Therefore, we propose that Atf3 curbs the innate immune response partly by controlling basal levels of Relish (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Fig. 9</span></a>). While the role of <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3 in immune homeostasis might parallel that of mammalian ATF3, the functional mechanism in flies remains to be determined.</p><p id="__p53" class="p p-last">Regulation of immune and starvation responses is intimately linked, as the effectors of immune (Relish) and IIS (FOXO) pathways regulate largely overlapping sets of genes (<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464831">5</a>, <a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464884">15</a>, <a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464851">30</a>, <a href="#B70" rid="B70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464842">70</a>) (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8A</span></a>) and should, therefore, be considered regulators of both metabolic and immune homeostasis. This is also evident from increased TAG levels upon Relish overexpression and lean <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup> mutant larvae (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3457521/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8D</span></a>) and starvation-sensitive <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup> adults (see Fig. S5C in the supplemental material). The fact that attenuated function of Relish or, to a lesser extent, FOXO can remedy the adverse consequences of Atf3 deficiency for both excessive fat storage and the chronic inflammatory response implies a functional interaction between Atf3 and signaling pathways that depend on Relish and FOXO. Atf3 appears to interact with a function of Relish that does not depend on the Imd pathway, since neither mutation in the I&#x003ba;B kinase (IKK) regulatory subunit IKK&#x003b3; (<em>kenny</em>) nor expression of a negative Imd regulator, Pirk, improved the survival rate of <em>atf3</em> mutants. Finally, in genetic experimentation with the <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup> allele, we were cautious about a second mutation affecting a gene, <em>nmdmc</em>, that encodes a NAD-dependent tetrahydrofolate dehydrogenase (<a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464845">24</a>). Due to the complex metabolic phenotype of <em>atf3</em><sup><em>76</em></sup> larvae, we cannot rule out an effect of the <em>nmdmc</em> mutation on the observed interaction with <em>rel</em><sup><em>E20</em></sup>.</p></div><div id="__sec24" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h3 class="inline">An evolutionary parallel. </h3><p id="__p54" class="p p-first-last">While <em>atf3</em> is an essential single-copy gene in <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em>, mammalian genomes encode two Atf3 orthologs, ATF3 and JDP2, neither of which is required for viability in mice (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464821">23</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464807">41</a>). However, the knockout mouse models implicated each gene in the maintenance of metabolic and immune homeostasis. As discussed above, ATF3 has been established as a negative modulator of the TLR-induced cytokine gene expression (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464874">17</a>, <a href="#B67" rid="B67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464891">67</a>). JDP2 is required to control lipid storage via regulation of adipocyte differentiation, and its loss leads to obesity (<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464922">41</a>). Human ATF3 and JDP2 are 430 to 450 amino acids shorter than the <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> protein, and sequence homology with fly Atf3 is appreciable only in the central 70 residues constituting the bZIP domain. We show that despite the limited similarity, expression of hATF3 in the gut and fat body in developing <em>atf3</em> mutant larvae improves viability, reduces obesity, and partly restores the normal gene expression program, including downregulation of <em>relish</em>. As in mammals (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464855">28</a>), <em>atf3</em> itself can be induced by starvation in <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em>. Compared to defects caused by strong gain of <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3 (reference <a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464865">51</a> and this study), overexpression of hATF3 was not detrimental, suggesting that hATF3 lacks some functions of the fly protein. However, ectopic expression of ATF3 in the pancreas and liver in transgenic mice disturbed endocrine function and glucose homeostasis (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464849">2</a>, <a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_289464871">3</a>). Therefore, fine-tuning of <em>atf3</em> gene expression is critical for both mammals and flies. In summary, our results suggest that <em class="genus-species">Drosophila</em> Atf3 represents a common regulator of metabolic and immune homeostasis, a role comprising the functions of both duplicated mammalian proteins.</p></div></div><div id="__sec25" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec25title" style="text-transform: uppercase;">Supplementary Material</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="PMC_1">
