We aim to explore the molecular basis for individual variation in REE and fuel partitioning as reflected by RQ. We performed microarray studies in human vastus lateralis muscle biopsies from 40 healthy subjects with measured REE and RQ values. We identified 2,392 and 1,115 genes significantly correlated with REE and RQ, respectively. Genes correlated with REE and RQ encompass a broad array of functions, including carbohydrate and lipid metabolism, gene expression, mitochondrial processes, and membrane transport. Microarray pathway analysis revealed that REE was positively correlated with upregulation of G protein-coupled receptor signaling (meet criteria/total genes: 65 of 283) involved in autonomic nervous system functions, including those receptors mediating adrenergic, dopamine, &#x003b3;-aminobutyric acid (GABA), neuropeptide Y (NPY), and serotonin action (meet criteria/total genes: 46 of 176). Reduced REE was associated with an increase in genes participating in ubiquitin-proteasome-dependent proteolytic pathways (58 of 232). Serine-type peptidase activity (9 of 76) was positively correlated with RQ, while genes involved in the protein phosphatase type 2A complex (4 of 9), mitochondrial function and cellular respiration (38 of 315), and unfolded protein binding (19 of 97) were associated with reduced RQ values and a preference for lipid fuel metabolism. Individual variations in whole body REE and RQ are regulated by differential expressions of specific genes and pathways intrinsic to skeletal muscle.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">microarray, human, skeletal muscle, G protein-coupled receptor, ubiquitin, protein phosphatase 2A, mitochondrial function, respiratory quotient</span></div></div><div id="idm140271300282848" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p2" class="p p-first"><span class="small-caps">obesity</span> increases the risk of type 2 diabetes and the metabolic syndrome, a trait cluster consisting of insulin resistance, obesity, glucose intolerance, upper body fat distribution, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and dysfibrinolysis (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538708">23</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538624">41</a>). The pathophysiology of obesity represents an imbalance between a high-energy intake and/or low-energy expenditure. Prospective studies have shown that a reduced rate of energy expenditure is a risk factor for body weight gain and obesity (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538652">10</a>, <a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538654">13</a>, <a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538660">56</a>, <a href="#B59" rid="B59" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538649">59</a>). Total energy expenditure can be divided into three major components, namely, resting metabolic rate (RMR) or resting energy expenditure (REE), thermic effect of exercise (TEE), and thermic effect of food (TEF). Each component can be increased or decreased in response to environmental changes. REE is measured when the individual is at rest under basal conditions and comprises 60&#x02013;75% of total energy expenditure. There is marked individual variability in REE, which is due in large part to differences in body weight (<a href="#B56" rid="B56" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538642">56</a>, <a href="#B75" rid="B75" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538625">75</a>) and lean body mass (<a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538683">17</a>, <a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538648">24</a>, <a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538699">37</a>). However, even when REE is normalized by lean body mass, there remains significant variability in REE among individuals. The molecular basis for interindividual variability in energy expenditure is largely unknown.</p><p id="__p3">Skeletal muscle is a major insulin target tissue (<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538666">21</a>) and is responsible for &#x0223c;80% of insulin-responsive glucose uptake. It is also involved in glycogen storage, lipid oxidation, protein turnover, and nonshivering thermogenesis (<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538639">19</a>&#x02013;<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538655">21</a>). Thus muscle metabolism not only accounts for a significant proportion of total REE but also is a major determinant of individual variability in REE normalized for lean mass (<a href="#B78" rid="B78" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538693">78</a>). It is not surprising then that skeletal muscle metabolism plays a role in the pathogenesis of obesity since individuals with a &#x0201c;low&#x0201d; metabolic rate may be at higher risk of gaining weight (<a href="#B78" rid="B78" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538646">78</a>). Several factors might influence the variation in muscle energy expenditure, including thyroid hormone, muscle tone, sympathetic innervation, catecholamine levels (<a href="#B78" rid="B78" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538698">78</a>), and muscle fiber type composition (<a href="#B73" rid="B73" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538678">73</a>). Most studies in skeletal muscle have focused on changes in metabolic flux and enzyme activities. A few studies have addressed the impact of specific gene expression on energy expenditure (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538632">15</a>, <a href="#B53" rid="B53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538661">53</a>, <a href="#B55" rid="B55" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538687">55</a>). For example, uncoupling proteins (UCPs) play important roles in adaptive thermogenesis and energy expenditure in rodents (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538694">22</a>) and can be induced by cold exposure, food intake, &#x003b2;-adrenergic stimulation, and thyroid hormone (<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538679">31</a>, <a href="#B57" rid="B57" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538627">57</a>, <a href="#B58" rid="B58" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538637">58</a>, <a href="#B63" rid="B63" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538688">63</a>), while glucocorticoids inhibit UCP expression (<a href="#B71" rid="B71" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538706">71</a>). These studies in rodents should only be extrapolated cautiously to humans. In humans, muscle UCP2 and UCP3 mRNA levels are not related to REE (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538638">7</a>), and the role of these proteins in energy metabolism is not well understood (<a href="#B54" rid="B54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538643">54</a>). The full extent and importance of alterations in gene transcription in human skeletal muscle relevant to REE are still unknown, and no systemic analysis of the changes in muscle gene expression correlated to REE has been reported.</p><p id="__p4">The respiratory quotient (RQ), i.e., the ratio of the volume of carbon dioxide released to the volume of oxygen consumed by a body tissue, is used as an index of the ratio of carbohydrate to fat oxidation. RQ can be used to estimate fuel partitioning between fat and carbohydrate, as preferred substrates for energy generation and fuel preferences to generate REE also exhibit individual variation. We have reported that an exon 6 splice-junction polymorphism in the gene of UCP3 increases RQ and reduces basal lipid metabolism in Gullah-speaking African Americans (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538682">4</a>). However, differences in gene expression that could explain individual variation in RQ have not been extensively studied.</p><p id="__p5" class="p">Genes influencing REE and RQ could represent candidate genes for obesity, metabolic syndrome, and type 2 diabetes due to the involvement of these traits in energy balance and substrate oxidation. To examine this possibility, we performed high-throughput microarray analyses of human skeletal muscle biopsied from 40 healthy nondiabetic individuals with REE and resting substrate oxidation rates measured by indirect calorimetry. We report here: <em>1</em>) REE and RQ are correlated with differential expressions of specific subsets of genes and pathways in skeletal muscle, and molecular regulation intrinsic to skeletal muscle can predict differences in whole body REE and RQ; <em>2</em>) REE is enhanced via the upregulation of receptors and pathways that accommodate a greater effect of the sympathetic nervous system (SNS), whereas decreased REE is associated with greater activity of catabolic processes; <em>3</em>) major determinants of fuel partitioning include serine-type peptidase activity, protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), mitochondrial function and cell respiration, and unfolded protein binding.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">METHODS AND MATERIALS</h2><div id="__sec2" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Human Subjects</h3><p id="__p6" class="p p-first">Microarray studies were performed in 40 healthy normoglycemic individuals, age 21&#x02013;55 yr (37.15 &#x000b1; 9.08 yr). Detailed clinical characteristics of subjects are shown in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>. After an overnight fast, and while at the GCRC, REE was measured by indirect calorimetry using a Deltratrac metabolic monitor (Deltratrac II, SensorMedics, Yorba Linda, CA). Measurements were performed in supine position and began 30 min after each subject was awakened from overnight sleep. The instrument was calibrated by ethanol combustion tests on a monthly basis and against standard gases before each test. Expired air was collected using the adult-size ventilated canopy system for 20 min after a 10-min equilibration. Whole body oxygen consumption (&#x00056;&#x00307;<span class="small-caps">o</span><sub>2</sub>) and CO<sub>2</sub> production (&#x00056;&#x00307;<span class="small-caps">co</span><sub>2</sub>) were calculated by measuring gradients across the face and the flow rates of air using Haldane transformation. In our laboratory, the coefficient of variation between REE measured in a walk-in room calorimeter and the Deltratrac metabolic monitor is 7.3%. Energy expenditure and substrate oxidation rates were determined from the RQ value and the tables of Lusk and Du Bois (<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538633">43</a>). Under aerobic conditions, respiration of fat gives an RQ of 0.7, respiration of protein gives an RQ of 0.9, and respiration of carbohydrate gives an RQ of 1.0. A mixed diet of fat and carbohydrate results in an average value between these numbers.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><h3>Insulin Sensitivity</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">In vivo insulin sensitivity was assessed using the euglycemic-hyperinsulinemic glucose-clamp technique at a maximally effective steady-state serum insulin concentration as previously described (<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538644">30</a>). After a 12-h fast, a catheter was inserted into the brachial vein to administer insulin, glucose, and K<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>. A dorsal hand vein was cannulated in a retrograde manner and kept in a warming device (65&#x000b0;C) to provide arterialized venous blood for sampling. For maximally stimulating glucose uptake and suppressing hepatic glucose production, regular insulin (Humulin; Eli Lilly, Indianapolis, IN) was administered at a rate of 200 mU&#x000b7;m<sup>&#x02212;2</sup>&#x000b7;min<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>, producing a mean steady-state insulin concentration of 3,480 &#x000b1; 138 pmol/l, which is maximally effective for stimulating glucose uptake into skeletal muscle. Plasma glucose was clamped at 5.0 mmol/l for at least 3 h, and maximal glucose uptake for each individual was calculated from the mean glucose infusion rate over the final three 20-min intervals. Whole body glucose uptake was calculated on the basis of the glucose infusion rate corrected for changes in the glucose pool size, assuming a distribution volume of 19% body weight and a pool fraction of 0.65. Glucose uptake was normalized per kilogram of lean body mass (excluding bone mass) determined by dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry to yield the glucose disposal rate (GDR) per kilogram of lean body mass.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><h3>Measurement of Plasma Glucose and Serum Insulin Levels</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">Plasma glucose was measured by the glucose oxidase method using a glucose analyzer (YSI 2300; Yellow Springs Instruments, Yellow Springs, OH). Serum insulin levels were measured using an electrochemiluminescence immunoassay (Roche Diagnostics, Mannheim, Germany).</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="sec"><h3>Muscle Biopsies</h3><p id="__p14" class="p p-first-last">Percutaneous needle biopsies of the vastus lateralis (&#x0223c;400 mg) were performed as previously described (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538626">28</a>, <a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538695">29</a>, <a href="#B76" rid="B76" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538647">76</a>). Muscle tissue was then blotted on a sterile cloth, placed immediately into liquid N<sub>2</sub>, and then transferred to a &#x02212;80&#x000b0;C freezer for storage.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><h3>RNA Extraction and Microarray Hybridization</h3><p id="__p15" class="p p-first-last">RNA was isolated by phenol/chloroform extraction using the Ultraspec RNA isolation Kit (Biotecx Laboratories, Houston, TX) followed by a DNase I treatment. RNA quality from each biopsy was examined by the A260/A280 absorbance ratio and by electrophoresis of RNA on agarose formaldehyde gels. Our RNA samples had an A260/A280 ratio of 1.8&#x02013;2.1, and no evidence of RNA degradation was detected on agarose gels in the samples that underwent analysis. In vitro transcription was accomplished using double-stranded cDNA and the MEGAscript T7 System (Ambion, Houston, TX) in the presence of biotinylated UTP and CTP to generate labeled antisense RNA. cRNA was separated from unincorporated nucleotides using RNeasy spin columns (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). The cRNA was then resuspended in diethyl pyrocarbonated-treated (DEPC) water for microarray study. Microarray analyses of human skeletal muscle biopsies were conducted using the Affymetrix Hu95A chips as previously described (<a href="#B76" rid="B76" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538702">76</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><h3>Statistical Data Analysis</h3><div id="__sec9" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Clinical data. </h4><p id="__p16" class="p p-first-last">To explore the optimal approach for normalizing REE measurements, we performed simple ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression analysis to determine the associations between REE and different anthropometric and metabolic variables, including BMI, total fat mass, percentage fat, LBM, and RQ. This was followed by multiple regression models using REE as the dependent variable and BMI, total fat mass, percentage fat, LBM, and RQ as independent variables.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Microarray data. </h4><p id="__p17" class="p p-first">We first normalized the data using quantile normalization (<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538676">11</a>) and then obtained the probe set intensity using robust multi-array average (RMA) (<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538636">35</a>) as implemented in the <em>affy</em> package in Bioconductor (<a href="http://www.bioconductor.org/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.bioconductor.org/</a>). We also generated present/absent calls for each probe set based on the Wilcoxon signed-rank test using the mass5call function in the <em>affy</em> package to filter out low-expression genes based on present calls (<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538704">45</a>).</p><p id="__p18">For identifying differentially expressed genes, we used ordinary least-squares regression analysis to test for correlation between gene expression and REE or RQ values. The gene expression values were regressed on these two clinical phenotypes at each probe set separately with or without sex and age as covariates. The regression analysis indicated that <em>P</em> values from the unadjusted and adjusted analyses are nearly the same, which suggests strongly that sex and age do not significantly affect the expression level of genes that are involved in regulating resting energy expenditure and respiratory quotient (data not shown). Therefore, we chose to present the analysis data without adjustment for sex and age. The individual was dropped from that regression analysis if either phenotype or expression was missing. For pathway analyses, nominal <em>P</em> value &#x0003c;0.05 was deemed as statistically significant. We also computed the posterior true positive probabilities (PTP) and posterior false positive probabilities (PFP) based on a mixture model approach using the High Dimension Biology Statistical Analysis (HDBStat!) software (<a href="http://www.ssg.uab.edu/hdbstat/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ssg.uab.edu/hdbstat/</a>) (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode">3</a>). The association between gene expression data and the human clinical data analysis was conducted using SAS statistical software (version 9.1.3).</p><p id="__p19" class="p">Genes that reached statistical significance for differential expression were subjected to PubMed search for their identified functions. Functional inferences are based on the authors' interpretation of literature referenced in Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/entrez/query.fcgi?db=OMIM" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/entrez/query.fcgi?db=OMIM</a>&#x00026;itool=toolbar) and Stanford Source (<a href="http://smd.stanford.edu" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://smd.stanford.edu</a>) pertaining to each gene. For further standardized analyses of gene function, we used Gene Ontology (GO) for gene annotations (<a href="http://www.geneontology.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.geneontology.org</a>). GoArray and FuncAssociate analysis programs were used for statistical analyses of effects on gene categories in the context of the domain knowledge represented in the Gene Ontology Database (<a href="http://goarray.med.yale.edu/GOArray/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://goarray.med.yale.edu/GOArray/</a>) (<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538659">5</a>, <a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538657">9</a>, <a href="#B52" rid="B52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538707">52</a>, <a href="#B77" rid="B77" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538681">77</a>). The schematic overview of the data analysis is shown in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1</span></a>.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140271307067248" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3070475_zdg0021194350001.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zdg0021194350001.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/bin/zdg0021194350001.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140271307067248" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3070475/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Fig. 1.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p20">Schematic overview figure of data analysis. EDR, expected discovery rate; FDR, false discovery rate; REE, resting energy expenditure; RQ, respiratory quotient.</p></div></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Quantitative Reverse Transcriptase-Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR)</h3><p id="__p21" class="p p-first-last">Total RNA was extracted from tissues using RNeasy mini kit and treated with DNase I (Qiagen, CA). RNA quality from each biopsy was examined by the A260/A280 absorbance ratio and by electrophoresis of RNA on agarose formaldehyde gels. RNA quality was assessed using the RNA 6000 Nano LabChip kit (Agilent Bioanalyser 2100, Agilent Technologies). Our RNA samples have an A260/A280 ratio of 1.8&#x02013;2.1. A total of 1 &#x003bc;g of RNA was reverse transcribed per reaction using first-strand complementary DNA synthesis with random primers (SuperScriptTM III reverse transcriptase kit, Invitrogen). qPCR was performed using the ABI Prism 7700 (Applied Biosystem, CA) and sequence-specific PCR primers and TaqMan probes for: O-linked <em>N</em>-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) transferase (UDP-<em>N</em>-acetylglucosamine:polypeptide-<em>N</em>-acetylglucosaminyl transferase) (OGT) [probe (5&#x02032;-ACCCCCATGGTGACTATGCCAGGA-3&#x02032;) and primers (5&#x02032;-CACACCACAGGGATGGATGTC-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-TGCAACTCGAGAAGCAAGAGTCT-3&#x02032;)]; thyroid hormone receptor, &#x003b1; (THRA) [probe (5&#x02032;-AGCAGAGCCACTTCCGTGTCATCCA-3&#x02032;) and primers (5&#x02032;-CGAACTGGGCAAGTCACTCTCT-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-CCGAGCGGTCTGTTGACATT-3&#x02032;)]; nuclear receptor subfamily 1, group D, member 1 (NR1D1) [probe (5&#x02032;-CCCACACACTTTACACAGTAACACCATGCCAT-3&#x02032;) and primers (5&#x02032;-CACCAGCAACATCACCAAGCT-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-CAGGCGTGCACACCGTAGT-3&#x02032;)]; and protein phosphatase 1, regulatory (inhibitor) subunit 3A (PPP1R3A) [probe (5&#x02032;-CACAGCAGATGTGTCTCATTCACCAAGGA-3&#x02032;) and primers (5&#x02032;-CTGAGCGAACATACCGCAATC-3&#x02032; and 5&#x02032;-CTAAAGCGCTGCCTTCACTAGTC-3&#x02032;)]. To quantify transcripts for genes of interest, we used GAPDH transcript as an internal control, and each sample was normalized with respect to GAPDH transcript content (Applied Biosystem). All assays were performed in duplicate, and the average values were used for analysis.</p></div></div><div id="__sec12" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec12title">RESULTS</h2><p id="__p22" class="p p-first">In simple regression analyses using demographic, anthropometric, and metabolic characteristics of the study subjects, REE was highly correlated with LBM (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = 0.85, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.001) and also with BMI (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = 0.48, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.01), but the correlation between REE and percentage fat did not reach statistical significance. When both LBM and BMI were included in the same multiple regression model, BMI lost significance (<em>P</em> &#x0003e; 0.05) while LBM explained most of the variance in REE (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = 0.73, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.001). Mean RQ was not correlated with either REE (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = &#x02212;0.12), body adiposity (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = &#x02212;0.07), or LBM (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = &#x02212;0.14) (<em>P</em> = NS). For purposes of examining the relationship between energy expenditure and muscle gene expression, REE rates were normalized per kilogram of LBM.</p><div id="__sec13" class="sec"><h3>Number of Gene Transcripts Associated with REE and RQ</h3><p id="__p23" class="p p-first-last">We have deposited the raw microarray data into GEO public database following MIAME standards (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE22309" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=<a id="__tag_611538710" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE22309" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE22309"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE22309","term_id":"22309"}}</span>GSE22309</a></a>). To identify genes that were differentially expressed as a function of REE or RQ, we used a regression model written as gene expression <em>j</em> = &#x003b2;<sub>0</sub><em>j</em> + &#x003b2;<sub>1</sub><em>j</em> &#x000d7; <em>X</em> (phenotype, either REE or RQ), where <em>j</em> represents probe set number. Using the mixture model approach of Allison et al. (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode">3</a>), the number of genes that were truly significantly correlated with REE or RQ was estimated by fitting the mixture model to the distribution of <em>P</em> values. Supplementary Tables 1A and 1B, available with the online version of this article, show significant positive and negative correlations, respectively, between gene expression values and REE/LBM, including <em>P</em> values from the regression model, posterior true positive probabilities (PTP), and posterior false positive probabilities (PFP). For a particular gene, PTP is an estimate of the proportion of genes that are truly differentially expressed among the genes with an observed <em>P</em> value smaller than the observed <em>P</em> value of this particular gene. PFP is an estimate of the proportion of genes that do not have a true differential expression among the genes with an observed <em>P</em> value smaller than the observed <em>P</em> value of this particular gene. Supplementary Tables 2A and 2B, available with the online version of this article, show significant positive and negative correlations between gene expression values and RQ, respectively.</p></div><div id="__sec14" class="sec"><h3>Differential Expression by Functional Categories</h3><p id="__p24" class="p p-first">The microarrays used in the present work allowed us to analyze mRNA levels encoding 12,626 genes and expressed sequence tags (ESTs) detected in human skeletal muscle. Of these genes, 1,280 were positively correlated to REE/LBM, while 1,112 were negatively correlated to REE/LBM. The number of genes significantly correlated with RQ was much smaller than those with REE/LBM: 442 genes were positively correlated to RQ, whereas 673 genes were negatively correlated to RQ.</p><p id="__p25">Gene Ontology annotations, Stanford Source (<a href="http://smd.stanford.edu" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://smd.stanford.edu</a>), and PubMed (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3070475&amp;issue-id=195559&amp;journal-id=629&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>), were used to assign these candidate genes into different functional categories. Because individual variability in REE is heavily dependent on differences in LBM, REE values were normalized for LBM in each subject. More than 85% of genes on the microarray did not significantly correlate to REE. Approximately 15% of the genes were differentially expressed as a function of REE/LBM in a statistically significant manner, indicating possible bases for orchestrated regulation of cellular functions that determine variation in energy metabolism. The analysis of these data has elucidated patterns of transcriptional regulation and biochemical pathways in skeletal muscle that affect whole body REE and fuel partitioning.</p><div id="__sec17" class="sec"><h3>Positive Regulation of REE</h3><p id="__p39" class="p p-first">Our microarray data indicate that increased gene expression of receptors that are involved in rhodopsin-like receptor and G protein-coupled receptor signaling pathways is associated with increased REE. This includes genes encoding adrenergic receptors, dopamine receptors, G protein-coupled receptors, serotonin receptors, opioid receptors, GABA-A receptors, glutamate receptors, and nicotinic cholinergic receptors. Most of these receptors function in synaptic transmission processes. In particular, genetic expression of &#x003b1;-adrenergic receptors (ADRA1&#x003b2;) and &#x003b2;-adrenergic receptors (ADRB3) was found to be positively correlated to REE. These receptors are keys for promulgating effects of the SNS. Activation of SNS pathways is known to augment each of the major components of energy expenditure: REE (<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538677">26</a>, <a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538692">65</a>), the thermic effect of food (<a href="#B61" rid="B61" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538663">61</a>), spontaneous physical activity (<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538634">16</a>), and 24-h RQ (<a href="#B64" rid="B64" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538684">64</a>). In particular, &#x003b2;-adrenergic receptor signaling influences total daily energy expenditure via modulation of REE in humans (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538685">8</a>, <a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538635">42</a>, <a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538703">47</a>, <a href="#B74" rid="B74" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538701">74</a>). Catecholamines can increase REE via &#x003b2;3-adrenergic receptor (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538670">51</a>), and &#x003b2;3-agonists act on cognate receptors in skeletal muscle (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538650">6</a>), brown adipose tissue (<a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538686">27</a>), and white adipose tissue to promote weight loss and improve insulin sensitivity (<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538651">33</a>, <a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538668">51</a>, <a href="#B60" rid="B60" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538641">60</a>). Systemic administration of isoproterenol (a sympathomimetic &#x003b2;-adrenergic receptor agonist) also results in a dose-dependent increase in energy expenditure in humans, accompanied by an increase in RQ and carbohydrate oxidation rate (<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538675">12</a>). Intravenous propranolol (a &#x003b2;-adrenergic receptor antagonist) infusion caused an acute decrease in REE (<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538669">47</a>). It is also important to consider that obesity is generally characterized by reduced SNS activity (<a href="#B51" rid="B51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538674">51</a>, <a href="#B69" rid="B69" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538673">69</a>). For example, Pima Indians prone to obesity have lower rates of muscle sympathetic activity compared with weight-matched Caucasians (<a href="#B65" rid="B65" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538689">65</a>) along with lower energy expenditure (<a href="#B67" rid="B67" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538667">67</a>), and low SNS activity was associated with poor weight loss outcomes in obese individuals treated with a dietary restriction intervention (<a href="#B68" rid="B68" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538631">68</a>). The current data indicate that it is not only the activity of effluent SNS pathways that can regulate energy metabolism; in addition, the end-organ response (i.e., skeletal muscle) can also impact REE via intrinsic changes in gene regulation, namely the upregulation of adrenergic receptors in muscle.</p><p id="__p40" class="p p-last">Regarding other receptors, dopamine receptors D<sub>2</sub> and D<sub>4</sub> were also positively correlated with REE. This is consistent with the observation that bromocriptine, a dopamine receptor D<sub>2</sub> agonist, has been shown to increase oxygen consumption and REE in humans (<a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538691">40</a>). In addition, a loss-of-function mutation of the human dopamine receptor D<sub>2</sub> gene has been associated with reduced energy expenditure (<a href="#B66" rid="B66" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538665">66</a>). Serotinin receptor expression (HTR1B, HTR1D, HTR1E, HTR4, HTR7) was also positively correlated with REE. While the mechanism linking serotonin receptor upregulation and REE is not clear, this would be predicted to augment serotonin (or 5-hydroxytryptamine) action, which has been shown to positively regulate glucose transport in rat skeletal muscle (<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538623">32</a>). Similarly, the association between expression of opioid receptors (opioid receptor kappa 1, opioid receptor mu 1, opioid receptor sigma 1, opioid growth factor receptor) and REE is deserving of more research, given that opioid receptors in the central nervous system regulate both appetite (<a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538671">44</a>) and REE (<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538697">36</a>). We also noted that upregulation of genes encoding receptor ligands influences REE. For example, increased expression of both neuropeptide Y (NPY) and neuropeptide FF-amide peptide precursor (NPFF) were associated with increased energy expenditure. NPY is an important sympathetic neurotransmitter involved in local neurovascular regulation of skeletal muscle energy metabolism, and NPFF plays a role in the regulation of heart rate and blood pressure. Thus norepinephrine and NPY have been shown to act synergistically in causing a reduction in body weight associated with an increase in resting metabolic rate (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538653">1</a>).</p></div><div id="__sec18" class="sec"><h3>Negative Regulation of REE</h3><p id="__p41" class="p p-first-last">Ubiquitin-proteasome-dependent proteolytic pathways were negatively correlated to REE, including genes encoding ubiquitin-conjugating enzymes, ubiquitin-specific proteases, and proteasomal proteins. In skeletal muscle, the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway is rapidly up- and downregulated during cycles of muscle atrophy and muscle accretion (<a href="#B72" rid="B72" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538696">72</a>) and is the major mechanism leading to increased muscle protein breakdown during starvation (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538656">25</a>). Starvation is also characterized by reduced energy expenditure and substrate metabolism by muscle (<a href="#B34" rid="B34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538700">34</a>), and prolonged starvation can lead to decrements in metabolic rates of up to 20&#x02013;30% (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538709">14</a>, <a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538640">48</a>). Upregulation of genes related to the ubiquitin-proteasome system in 24-h-fasted rats suggests that the ubiquitin-proteasome system in starvation is rapidly activated in skeletal muscle even before overt proteolysis (<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538628">18</a>, <a href="#B49" rid="B49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538664">49</a>). Our results in isocaloric individuals suggest that the ubiquitin-proteasome system could negatively modulate REE and explain differences in REE among healthy individuals, as well as mediate both a further decrement in REE and protein catabolism during periods of starvation.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="sec"><h3>Positive Regulation of RQ</h3><p id="__p42" class="p p-first-last">The skeletal muscle is able to switch between carbohydrates and fat as fuel substrates depending on energy requirements. However, even under basal conditions, there exists wide individual variation in fuel preferences. Apart from the impact of diet composition, RQ is also influenced by recent energy balance, sex, adiposity, and, importantly, familial aggregation of obesity (<a href="#B70" rid="B70" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538690">70</a>, <a href="#B79" rid="B79" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538705">79</a>). Genes that are positively correlated with RQ will alter macronutrient fuel partitioning in favor of carbohydrates by promoting carbohydrate oxidation or impairing lipid oxidation. Increased expression of genes encoding serine-type peptidase activity is associated with high RQ values, including serine proteases (PRSS3, PRSS16, PRTN3), dipeptidylpeptidase (DPP6), chymotrypsin (CTRL), and elastase 2 (ELA2, ELA2A). Serine proteases cleave and inactivate musclin, a muscle-derived protein that impairs glucose metabolism (<a href="#B50" rid="B50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538672">50</a>), but any relevance to our current results is speculative. To our knowledge, we are the first to report a correlation between serine-type peptidases and muscle fuel partitioning, and this is an area deserving of future study.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Negative Regulation of RQ</h3><p id="__p43" class="p p-first">Genes that favor lipid oxidation or reduce carbohydrate oxidation in basal muscle will lower RQ. We observed that the major functional categories responsible for preferential fat oxidation included the protein phosphatase 2A complex, mitochondrial function, and cellular respiration, due to differential expression of genes including protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), ATP synthase, H<sup>+</sup> transporters, mitochondrial F0 complex, NADH dehydrogenase (ubiquinone), succinate-CoA ligase (alpha subunit of mitochondrial succinyl CoA synthetase), translocase of outer mitochondrial membrane, ubiquinol-cytochrome <em>c</em> reductase, and heat shock protein. PP2A acts to dephosphorylate signaling molecules and can blunt insulin stimulation of glucose metabolism (i.e., glucose uptake, glycogen synthesis). The genes pertaining to mitochondrial function and cell respiration would clearly increase capacity for fatty acid oxidation, resulting in reduced RQ values.</p><p id="__p44" class="p p-last">There are two notable limitations of the study that deserve mention. First, it is important to keep in mind that the muscle biopsy contains cell types other than myocytes, including cells comprising the microvasculature, adipocytes, and connective tissue. Thus, while mRNA from muscle cells will predominate, differential gene expression could be due to changes in other cell types. Second, the method used for normalization of Affymetrix array data can have a major impact on the results (<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538629">39</a>, <a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538630">46</a>, <a href="#B62" rid="B62" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538680">62</a>). We used RMA in our manuscript, which is the most commonly chosen normalization method (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_611538662">2</a>). However, a gold standard method for microarray data normalization has not been yet defined. And third, we only chose to present the gene categories that are identified by both GoArray and FuncAssociate pathway programs, which could have resulted in an overconservative analysis. In any event, the data identified biochemical pathways and processes in skeletal muscle that predict individual differences in resting energy expenditure and fuel partitioning. Importantly, neuroregulation of REE does not only depend on central (autonomic) nervous system activity, but also, individual variation in REE is determined by differential expressions of neurotransmitter receptors intrinsic to skeletal muscle, which can affect the end-organ metabolic response. The correlations we investigated looked for linear associations between the response and gene expression. More complicated associations might either be missed or exaggerated when fitting a linear model.</p></div></div><div id="__sec21" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec21title">GRANTS</h2><p id="__p45" class="p p-first-last">This work was supported by grants from the National Institutes of Health (<span>DK-038764</span>;, <span>DK-083562</span>;, and <span>HL-055782</span> to W.
