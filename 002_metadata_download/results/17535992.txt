Author manuscript; available in PMC 2009 Jun 8.</div><div></div><div>Published in final edited form as:</div><div style="margin-left:1em"><div class="fm-vol-iss-date"><a href="/entrez/eutils/elink.fcgi?dbfrom=pubmed&amp;retmode=ref&amp;cmd=prlinks&amp;id=17535992" target="pmc_ext" ref="reftype=publisher&amp;article-id=2692663&amp;issue-id=179336&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CFront%20Matter&amp;TO=Content%20Provider%7CArticle%7CRestricted%20Access&amp;rendering-type=normal"><span class="cit">J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2007 Aug; 92(8): 3268–3277. </span></a></div><span class="fm-vol-iss-date">Published online 2007 May 29. </span> <span class="doi">doi:  <a href="//dx.doi.org/10.1210%2Fjc.2007-0642" target="pmc_ext" ref="reftype=other&amp;article-id=2692663&amp;issue-id=179336&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CFront%20Matter&amp;TO=Content%20Provider%7CCrosslink%7CDOI&amp;rendering-type=normal">10.1210/jc.2007-0642</a></span></div></div><div class="inline_block four_col va_top show-overflow align_right"><div class="fm-citation-ids"><div class="fm-citation-pmcid"><span class="fm-citation-ids-label">PMCID: </span><span>PMC2692663</span></div><div class="fm-citation-manuscriptid"><span class="fm-citation-ids-label">NIHMSID: </span><span>NIHMS33540</span></div><div class="fm-citation-pmid">PMID: <a href="/pubmed/17535992">17535992</a></div></div></div></div></div><h1 class="content-title">Molecular Evidence for a Link between the N363S Glucocorticoid Receptor Polymorphism and Altered Gene Expression</h1><div class="half_rhythm"><div class="contrib-group fm-author"><a href="/pubmed/?term=Jewell%20CM%5BAuthor%5D&amp;cauthor=true&amp;cauthor_uid=17535992">Christine M. Jewell</a> and  <a href="/pubmed/?term=Cidlowski%20JA%5BAuthor%5D&amp;cauthor=true&amp;cauthor_uid=17535992">John A. Cidlowski</a></div><div style="display:none" class="contrib-group aff-tip"></div></div><div class="fm-panel small half_rhythm"><div class="fm-authors-info fm-panel hide half_rhythm" id="idm140566360222640_ai" style="display:none"><div class="fm-affl" lang="en" id="A1">Laboratory of Signal Transduction, National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences, National Institutes of Health, Department of Health and Human Services, Research Triangle Park, North Carolina 27709</div><div id="CR1">Address all correspondence and requests for reprints to: John A. Cidlowski, National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences, P.O. Box 12233, MD F3-07, Research Triangle Park, North Carolina 27709. E-mail: <a href="mailto:dev@null" data-email="vog.hin.shein@1swoldic" class="oemail">vog.hin.shein@1swoldic</a>.</div></div><div class="togglers"><a href="#" class="pmctoggle" rid="idm140566360222640_ai">Author information <span>►</span></a> <a href="#" class="pmctoggle" rid="idm140566360222640_cpl">Copyright and License information <span>►</span></a> <a href="/pmc/about/disclaimer/">Disclaimer</a></div><div class="fm-article-notes fm-panel half_rhythm"></div><div class="fm-cpl-info fm-panel hide half_rhythm" id="idm140566360222640_cpl" style="display:none"><div class="fm-copyright half_rhythm"><a href="/pmc/about/copyright/">Copyright notice</a> </div></div></div><div id="pmclinksbox" class="links-box whole_rhythm"><div class="small"><div>The publisher's final edited version of this article is available  at <a href="/entrez/eutils/elink.fcgi?dbfrom=pubmed&amp;retmode=ref&amp;cmd=prlinks&amp;id=17535992" target="pmc_ext" ref="reftype=publisher&amp;article-id=2692663&amp;issue-id=179336&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CFront%20Matter&amp;TO=Content%20Provider%7CArticle%7CRestricted%20Access&amp;rendering-type=normal">J Clin Endocrinol Metab</a></div><div>See other articles in PMC that <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/citedby/">cite</a> the published article.</div></div></div></div><div class="sec"></div><div id="idm140566354502096" lang="en" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140566354502096title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><div id="S1" class="sec sec-first"><h2>Context</h2><p id="P1" class="p p-first-last">A single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) in the human glucocorticoid receptor (hGR) N363S (rs6195) has been the focus of several clinical studies, and some epidemiological data link this SNP to increased glucocorticoid sensitivity, coronary artery disease, and increased body mass index. However, molecular studies <em>in vitro</em> using reporter gene expression systems have failed, for the most part, to define a link between this polymorphism and altered glucocorticoid receptor function.</p></div><div id="S2" class="sec"><h2>Objective</h2><p id="P2" class="p p-first-last">The objective of this study was to address the biological relevancy of N363S SNP in GR function by establishing stable U-2 OS (human osteosarcoma) cell lines expressing wild-type hGR or N363S and examining these receptors under a variety of conditions that probe for GR activity including human gene microarray analysis.</p></div><div id="S3" class="sec"><h2>Design</h2><p id="P3" class="p p-first-last">Functional assays with reporter gene systems, Western blotting, and human microarray analysis were used to evaluate the activity of wild-type and N363S GR in both transiently and stably expressing cells. In addition, quantitative RT-PCR was used to confirm the microarray analysis.</p></div><div id="S4" class="sec"><h2>Results</h2><p id="P4" class="p p-first-last">Functional assays with reporter gene systems and homologous down-regulation revealed only minor differences between the wild-type hGR and N363S receptors in both transiently and stably expressing cell lines. However, examination of the two receptors by human gene microarray analysis revealed a unique gene expression profile for N363S.</p></div><div id="S5" class="sec sec-last"><h2>Conclusions</h2><p id="P5" class="p p-first-last">These studies demonstrate that the N363S SNP regulates a novel set of genes with several of the regulated genes supporting a potential role for this GR polymorphism in human diseases.</p></div></div></div><div id="idm140566360402544" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="P6" class="p p-first">GLUCOCORTICOID RECEPTORS (GR) ARE expressed in almost all cells and are necessary for life. GR signaling at the physiological level is regulated predominantly by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis where endocrine, neural, and cytokine signaling converge in the periventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus and regulate the synthesis and release of CRH. CRH stimulates the release of ACTH from the anterior pituitary, which in turn induces synthesis and secretion of cortisol by the adrenal cortex. Although most cortisol is bound to corticosteroid-binding globulin in the blood, approximately 10% remains free and is the biologically active form of the hormone (<a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613264">1</a>). Upon exposure to stress, cortisol levels are increased and affect almost all physiological systems including carbohydrate, lipid, and protein metabolism as well as the cardiovascular system, the immune system, behavior, bone density, and cell growth in addition to the regulation of the HPA axis itself (<a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613273">2</a>). Cortisol also has been found to be elevated in obese individuals, and dysregulation of the HPA axis was found to be more pronounced in individuals with central obesity (<a href="#R3" rid="R3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613218">3</a>). In addition, sensitivity to glucocorticoids, as measured clinically by dexamethasone suppression tests, varies greatly among individuals (<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613221">4</a>).</p><p id="P7">Both natural and synthetic glucocorticoids exert their physiological and pharmacological effects through binding to the intracellular GR, which upon activation by glucocorticoids, activate or repress the transcription of target genes. Several polymorphisms in the GR gene (NR3C1) have been reported in the normal population, and these genetic variations may influence an individual&#x02019;s response to glucocorticoids. One such single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) at amino acid 363, which changes the codon from asparagine (N) to serine (S), was initially identified in a study of Dutch kindred who presented with hypercortisolism and half the number of GR per cell (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613220">5</a>). In another Dutch study, a group of 216 elderly individuals were examined for the N363S polymorphism, and 13 heterozygotes (6% of the group) were identified (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613212">6</a>). Interestingly, these carriers exhibited an increased sensitivity to exogenously administered glucocorticoids as well as an increased insulin response and increased body mass index (BMI) (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613250">6</a>). In addition, a study using the Trier Social Stress Test, which assesses cortisol and ACTH responses to psychosocial stress, showed that N363S carriers had significantly increased salivary cortisol responses to stress (<a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613257">7</a>). In two studies of Australians (all Caucasian of British descent), Lin <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R8" rid="R8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613231">8</a>, <a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613228">9</a>) confirmed this association of increased BMI with the N363S polymorphism. In a separate study by Lin <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613230">10</a>) on subjects of Anglo-Celtic descent with coronary artery disease (CAD), they reported that the frequency of the S363 allele was 0.04 in a healthy normal-weight control group but rose to 0.15 in patients with CAD. This association rose even higher in patients with unstable angina (0.45), suggesting a role for the N363S polymorphism in the underlying cause of CAD (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613276">10</a>). In a severely obese Italian population, N363S was associated with increased BMI, resting energy expenditure, and food intake (<a href="#R11" rid="R11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613256">11</a>). A study by Roussel <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613244">12</a>) on French subjects with type 2 diabetes mellitus also reported an increase in BMI in subjects carrying the N363S polymorphism. Furthermore, Dobson <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613261">13</a>) also showed an association with increased waist-to-hip ratio in male N363S carriers but no associations with BMI, blood pressure, or serum cholesterol levels. However, other reports by Halsall <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode">14</a>), Echwald <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R15" rid="R15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613214">15</a>), Rosmond (<a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613263">16</a>), and Buemann <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613224">17</a>) have shown no association between N363S and increased BMI. Interestingly, the N363S variant did not occur in Japanese subjects (<a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613219">18</a>) and was of extremely low prevalence in a study performed on South Asians living in the United Kingdom (<a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613235">19</a>).</p><p id="P8">Biochemical and molecular biological studies examining the ability of N363S to bind ligand or mediate transcriptional activation of transfected glucocorticoid-responsive promoters have been largely negative with no differences observed between the variant and the wild-type receptor (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613253">5</a>, <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613251">6</a>, <a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613240">20</a>, <a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613267">21</a>), although recently, Russcher <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613238">22</a>) found a significant but small (8%) increase in transactivation of a GRELuc reporter gene by the N363S GR. Additionally, Russcher <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613227">22</a>) showed that there was no difference between wild-type and the variant in dexamethasone-mediated repression of the NF-<em>&#x003ba;</em>B p65 subunit. Thus, the molecular basis for the altered phenotype seen in some patients carrying the N363S SNP remains undefined.</p><p id="P9" class="p">In this study, we employed functional assays to analyze potential differences between wild-type human GR (hGR) and the N363S variant on gene activation and gene repression in both transiently transfected and stable cell lines expressing either wild-type or N363S GR. Under all conditions evaluated, only minimal differences were observed in the ability of N363S and wild-type hGR to regulate transiently transfected reporter genes. In contrast, examination of this polymorphism by microarray analysis showed, for the first time, that there are significant differences between wild-type hGR and the N363S SNP in their ability to regulate gene expression selectively. Several of these genes may define the link between the N363S SNP and human disease.</p></div><div id="S6" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S6title">Materials and Methods</h2><div id="S7" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Cell culture and transfection</h3><p id="P10" class="p p-first-last">COS-1 cells (African Green Monkey kidney cells) were cultured in DMEM-H (GIBCO, Grand Island, NY) supplemented with a mixture of 10% FCS:CS (fetal calf serum:calf serum), 2 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> glutamine, 100 IU penicillin, and 100 mg/ml streptomycin. U-2 OS (human osteosarcoma) cells were maintained in DMEM/F-12 supplemented with 10% FCS:CS, 2 m<span class="small-caps">m</span> glutamine, and penicillin/streptomycin, and selected clones were maintained in the same media with the addition of 200 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml Geneticin and 200 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml hygromycin. All cells were maintained in a humidified, 5% CO<sub>2</sub> atmosphere.</p></div><div id="S8" class="sec"><h3>U-2 OS cell lines stably expressing wild-type hGR and N363S</h3><p id="P11" class="p p-first-last">To establish the U-OFF parental cell line, the well characterized BD Clontech regulatory plasmid pTet-Off was transfected into U-2 OS cells (human osteosarcoma). These cells were chosen because they do not have detectable levels of GR (<a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613232">23</a>). Tet-Off regulates genes responsive to tetracycline, so to generate the tetracycline-responsive plasmid, pTRE2hGR<em>&#x003b1;</em>, <em>Mlu</em>I and <em>Eco</em>RV ends were generated onto the coding region of hGR<em>&#x003b1;</em> using PCR amplification of the pCMVhGR<em>&#x003b1;</em> plasmid. The pTRE2hyg vector was digested with <em>Mlu</em>I and <em>Eco</em>RV, and the two DNAs were ligated (<a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613217">23</a>). Site-directed mutagenesis was then performed to make pTRE2N363S. The wild-type pTRE2hGR<em>&#x003b1;</em> and the pTRE2N363S plasmids were individually transfected into the U-OFF cells, and selection was initiated by supplementing the U-OFF growth media with 500 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml hygromycin. After 4 wk, several colonies, selected for expression of wild-type hGR or N363S, were transferred to 12-well plates and maintained in growth media containing 200 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml hygromycin. The receptor levels for each of these cell lines were then compared using Western blot analyses with the well-characterized anti-GR antibody no. 57 (<a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613268">24</a>). One cell line expressing wild-type hGR and one cell line expressing N363S GR at comparable levels were chosen for functional assays, microarray analysis, and initial quantitative RT-PCR studies. In addition, two other cell lines expressing wild-type hGR and two other cell lines expressing N363S GR were analyzed using quantitative RT-PCR. All cell lines were maintained as described above.</p></div><div id="S9" class="sec"><h3>Transient transfections and luciferase assays</h3><p id="P12" class="p p-first-last">Cells were plated in six-well plates at approximately 80% confluency 1 d before transfection. Transient transfections were carried out with FuGene 6 reagent (Roche Diagnostics Corp., Indianapolis, IN). After 18-24 h, the transfection media were removed, replaced with fresh medium supplemented with FCS:CS stripped of endogenous glucocorticoids, and treated with dexamethasone or vehicle (H<sub>2</sub>O) at the concentrations described. Twenty-four hours after treatment, cells were harvested in 400 <em>&#x003bc;</em>l Reporter Gene Assay Lysis buffer (Roche) per well. Cellular debris was pelleted at maximal speed in a refrigerated micro-centrifuge, and the supernatant was assayed for total protein content by Bradford assay (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA). The luciferase activity was measured by using the 96-well plate format with an MLX automated microtiter plate luminometer from Dynex (Thermo Lab-systems, Helsinki, Finland) and corrected to the amount of total protein.</p></div><div id="S10" class="sec"><h3>Western blotting</h3><p id="P13" class="p p-first-last">Cell extracts prepared for luciferase assays were denatured in a sodium dodecyl sulfate buffer and then separated on precast 8% Trisglycine gels (Invitrogen, San Diego, CA) and transferred to nitrocellulose membrane (0.2 <em>&#x003bc;</em>m). The membranes were blocked in Tris-buffered saline/0.5% Tween (TBS-T) containing 10% nonfat milk for a 1 h at room temperature, washed in TBS-T, then incubated with anti-GR antibody no. 57 (1:1000) and anti-<em>&#x003b2;</em>-actin (Chemicon, Temecula, CA) (1:10,000) for 1 h at room temperature (<a href="#R24" rid="R24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613277">24</a>). After additional washing in TBS-T, the blots were incubated with peroxidase-conjugated antirabbit and anti-mouse secondary antibodies (1:20,000) for 1 h at RT. Bands were visualized using ECL reagents (Amersham, Piscataway, NJ). To quantitate the amount of receptor in each band, the wild-type hGR and N363S GR signals were quantitated densitometrically using NIH Image analysis software and normalized to the <em>&#x003b2;</em>-actin signal for each band.</p></div><div id="S11" class="sec"><h3>Microarray analysis</h3><p id="P14" class="p p-first-last">Microarray analysis was carried out using Agilent human 1Av2 arrays (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA). U-2 OS cells stably expressing either wild-type hGR or N363S were cultured for 24 h in media containing charcoal-stripped serum and subsequently treated for 6 h with 10 n<span class="small-caps">m</span> dexamethasone or vehicle (H<sub>2</sub>O), and total RNA was isolated using the QIAGEN RNeasy midi kit (QIAGEN, Valencia, CA). RNA samples were then amplified using the Agilent low-RNA-input fluorescent linear amplification kit protocol. Total RNA (0.5 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g) was then labeled with Cy3 or Cy5 following the manufacturer&#x02019;s protocol. Two-color comparison was then carried out using 750 ng each of the Cy3- and Cy5-labeled cRNA, which were mixed and fragmented using the Agilent <em>in situ</em> hybridization kit protocol. Hybridizations were performed for 16 h in a rotating hybridization oven using the Agilent 60-mer oligo microarray processing protocol. After this same protocol, slides were washed and then scanned with an Agilent scanner. Data were obtained using the Agilent Feature Extraction software (version 7.1), using defaults for all parameters. Each RNA sample was analyzed in duplicate (for a total of eight chips per analysis), and two experimental samples of RNA, isolated at different times, were analyzed separately. The gene lists from each analysis were then combined and further analyzed for genes common between the two individual microarray analyses. The microarray data discussed in this manuscript have been deposited in NCBI&#x02019;s Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO, <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=2692663&amp;issue-id=179336&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/</a>) (<a href="#R25" rid="R25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613226">25</a>) and is accessible through GEO accession no. <a id="__tag_172613280" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE5796" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE5796"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE5796","term_id":"5796"}}</span>GSE5796</a>.</p></div><div id="S12" class="sec"><h3>Real-time PCR analysis</h3><p id="P15" class="p p-first-last">Three stably expressing cell lines each of wild-type hGR and N363S were cultured for 24 h in charcoal-stripped serum medium and then treated with 10 n<span class="small-caps">m</span> dexamethasone or vehicle (H<sub>2</sub>O) for 6 h, and total RNA was isolated using the QIAGEN RNeasy mini kit. Real-time PCR was performed using the 7900HT sequence detection system predesigned primer/probe sets available from Applied Biosystems (Foster City, CA) and following the manufacturer&#x02019;s instructions. The signal obtained from each gene primer/probe set was normalized to that of the unregulated housekeeping gene cyclophilin B primer/probe set (also available from Applied Biosystems). Each primer/probe set was analyzed with at least three different sets of RNA.</p></div><div id="S13" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Transient transfections and quantitative RT-PCR analysis</h3><p id="P16" class="p p-first-last">U-2 OS cells were plated in 100-mm dishes at approximately 80% confluency 1 d before transfection. Cells were transfected with TransIt-LT1 reagent (Mirus, Madison, WI) as described by the manufacturer using 25 <em>&#x003bc;</em>l TransIt-LT1 and 2.5 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g DNA per dish. After 18-24 h, the transfection medium was removed, replaced with charcoal-stripped serum medium, and further incubated for 24 h at 37 C. The cells were then treated with 10 n<span class="small-caps">m</span> dexamethasone or vehicle (H<sub>2</sub>O) for 6 h. Total RNA was isolated, and quantitative RT-PCR analysis was performed as described.</p></div></div><div id="S14" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S14title">Results</h2><div id="S15" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Analysis of the N363S hGR polymorphism</h3><p id="P17" class="p p-first">To analyze the function of N363S compared with wild-type hGR, we took a two-prong experimental approach. First, to recapitulate and confirm previous studies in several laboratories (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613236">5</a>, <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613213">6</a>, <a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613275">20</a>-<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613260">22</a>), we transiently transfected COS-1 cells with either hGR or N363S. Second, we generated stable human cell lines expressing either wild-type hGR or the N363S polymorphism by stably transfecting the receptors into U-OFF cells (U-2 OS cells, human osteosarcoma), which do not have detectable levels of GR (<a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613271">23</a>). After culturing the transfected cells for up to 4 wk in selective media containing 500 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml hygromycin, several colonies of cells expressing either hGR or N363S were transferred to 12-well plates and maintained in 200 <em>&#x003bc;</em>g/ml hygromycin. These stable cell lines were sequenced to confirm that the cells were expressing either hGR or N363S, and one wild-type (hGR-B5) and one N363S (N363S-A2) cell line expressing comparable GR protein levels as assessed by Western blot were chosen for further evaluation.</p><p id="P18">Both the transiently transfected COS-1 cells and the stably expressing U-2 OS cell lines were then assayed for their ability to activate gene expression. To determine whether there was any difference in glucocorticoid-mediated down-regulation of the wild-type or N363S expressed proteins, both hGR and N363S transiently expressing COS-1 cells and stably expressing U-2 OS cells were treated with increasing amounts of dexamethasone (0-1000 n<span class="small-caps">m</span>) for 24 h. <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Figure 1, E and F</span></a>, shows a representative Western blot of hGR and N363S proteins that have been probed with the anti-GR antibody and anti-<em>&#x003b2;</em>-actin as well as a graphic representation of the data from at least three experiments with the protein levels normalized to <em>&#x003b2;</em>-actin. The U-2 OS stably expressing cell lines, hGR-B5 and N363S-A2, were cultured for 24 h in charcoal-stripped serum media and then treated with 10 n<span class="small-caps">m</span> dexamethasone or vehicle for 6 h, and total RNA was isolated and subjected to microarray analysis. Each experimental hybridization consisted of four comparison groups and was run in duplicate: hGR CON (vehicle-treated control) <em>vs.</em> N363S CON, hGR DEX (treated with 10 n<span class="small-caps">m</span> dexamethasone) <em>vs.</em> N363S DEX, hGR CON <em>vs.</em> hGR DEX, and N363S CON <em>vs.</em> N363S DEX. Those genes shown in <em>green</em> are significantly repressed and those in <em>red</em> are significantly induced. The mean range represented is 2.5-fold induced to 2.5-fold repressed. This analysis revealed that when N363S is compared with wild-type (hGR CON <em>vs.</em> N363S CON and hGR DEX <em>vs.</em> N363S DEX), we see a striking change in the pattern of gene regulation reflecting that expression of the N363S polymorphism is regulating a novel set of genes even in the absence of added glucocorticoid. Furthermore, treatment with dexamethasone affects this gene regulation and reveals additional differences between genes regulated by wild-type hGR- and N363S-expressing cells in the presence of dexamethasone (hGR CON <em>vs.</em> hGR DEX and N363S CON <em>vs.</em> N363S DEX). Additional analysis of these data using human chromosome mapping and comparing genes affected by the glucocorticoid-treated wild-type-expressing cells (hGR CON <em>vs.</em> hGR DEX) and the treated N363S-expressing cells (N363S CON <em>vs.</em> DEX) shows the physical position of the genes with known loci (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2B</span></a>). Thus, when one uses the correct endogenous genes selectively regulated by the N363S polymorphism, one can recapitulate data from the stable cell lines in a transient system. This discovery will allow us to elucidate the molecular basis for this polymorphism&#x02019;s altered gene expression.</p></div></div><div id="S17" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S17title">Discussion</h2><p id="P27" class="p p-first">In this study, we have analyzed the differences between wild-type hGR and the hGR SNP N363S that has been associated with higher glucocorticoid sensitivity, increased insulin response to dexamethasone, CAD, and obesity, specifically, with an increase in BMI (<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613215">6</a>, <a href="#R8" rid="R8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613259">8</a>, <a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613248">10</a>-<a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613242">12</a>). Under our experimental conditions, the data presented here revealed no major differences between wild-type hGR and the N363S polymorphism when transactivation and transrepression were assayed in cells transiently transfected with synthetic reporter genes comprised of consensus hormone or transcription factor elements (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Figs. 1, A and C</span></a>). These results support most of the previous reports (<a href="#R5" rid="R5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613233">5</a>, <a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613247">6</a>, <a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613258">20</a>, <a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613229">21</a>), although recently Russcher <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613266">22</a>) demonstrated that N363S had a small but significant increase in transactivating capacity on a GRE-LUC reporter. In addition to the transactivation and transrepression assays, we analyzed each GR for its ability to undergo hormone-mediated down-regulation in a dose-dependent manner, and again, we found no difference between the N363S polymorphism and wild-type hGR (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1E</span></a>). Because transient transfection assays can be variable from one experiment to the next, they may not reveal subtle differences when comparing the expression and function of two different proteins when one uses synthetic reporter elements. Therefore, we created stable cell lines overexpressing either hGR or N363S to eliminate some of this potential variability.</p><p id="P28">Lu and Cidlowski (<a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613249">23</a>) in our laboratory analyzed the differences between the wild-type human GR and its isoforms using stable cell lines expressing either wild-type hGR or each isoform. Using this same expression system, we produced stable cell lines expressing either wild-type hGR or the N363S SNP. Even though creation of the stable cell lines gave us cells expressing equal amounts of hGR and N363S proteins (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1B</span></a>), thus reducing some of the variability associated with transient transfection assays, the functional assays with the stable cell lines revealed little or no difference between wild-type hGR and N363S (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1, B, D, and F</span></a>). These data also argue against the 363 asparagine to serine change becoming an additional site for phosphorylation of the GR protein because our laboratory has previously shown that the greatest differences in transactivation of the mGR phosphorylation site mutants was seen using the simple GRE2-TATA promoter (<a href="#R29" rid="R29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613252">29</a>). In addition, the amino acid sequences surrounding the serine site do not correspond to the published consensus sequence (<a href="#R30" rid="R30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613234">30</a>).</p><p id="P29">Therefore, we used gene array analysis to determine whether there was differential regulation between the two cell lines in the absence and presence of dexamethasone. Although we cannot rule out that residual glucocorticoids were still present in the cells at the time of our analysis, our microarray results show, for the first time, that the N363S polymorphism can regulate a novel set of genes in comparison to wild-type hGR both in the absence and presence of dexamethasone (Figs. <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -3em;">(Figs.2</span></span><span>2</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F3/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F3" rid-ob="ob-F3" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F3"><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and3).</span></span><span>3</span></a>). These data were confirmed using several populations of cells expressing different levels of receptor (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC2692663/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4</span></a>). Together these data suggest that the N363S phenotype may be the result of differential effects on gene regulation. Interestingly, the effect of the polymorphism, in the absence of dexamethasone, could be recapitulated in a transient transfection system which will now allow us to dissect the molecular basis for altered gene expression.</p><p id="P30">One interesting family of genes involved in the response to external stimulus (which includes immune response) is the SAAs. These acute-phase proteins, precursors of amyloid A (AA), which is involved in the pathogenesis of AA amyloidosis, are produced primarily in the liver and increase many-fold during inflammation. During the acute inflammatory response, the circulating levels of SAA rise dramatically within 24 h of an inflammatory stimulus (<a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613278">28</a>). SAA along with C-reactive protein (CRP) are the major acute-phase protein responders and are regulated by cytokines such as IL-1, IL-6, and TNF, although how they are regulated is not precisely known (<a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613239">28</a>). Because SAA increases where tissue damage has occurred with subsequent inflammation, patients who have suffered myocardial infarctions also have elevated SAA levels (<a href="#R28" rid="R28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613265">28</a>). This has led to SAA being investigated as a prognostic indicator, along with CRP, for acute myocardial infarction (<a href="#R31" rid="R31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613223">31</a>). Interestingly, the SAAs were among the genes most highly up-regulated in the N363S-expressing cells. However, it remains unclear whether the increased SAA levels in the U-2 OS cell lines expressing the N363S SNP can be associated with the increase in CAD in carriers of the SNP as reported by Lin <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613254">10</a>), although clearly, further study is now warranted.</p><p id="P31">Although CRP and IL-6 have been previously associated with parameters of obesity, only recently has SAA also been investigated in relation to body composition (<a href="#R32" rid="R32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613270">32</a>). In a large study in both men and women in the area of Augsburg, Germany, both total adiposity (fat mass, BMI) and abdominal adiposity (waist circumference and waist-to-hip ratio) were measured, and these measurements correlated with markers of systemic inflammation CRP, SAA, fibrinogen, and IL-6. Their study discovered that adiposity was strongly associated with these markers of systemic inflammation in both men and women and that this correlation, especially for CRP, was even stronger in women. Other studies have not only associated SAA and CRP with obesity but also found that weight loss significantly decreased the levels of SAA and CRP (<a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613262">33</a>-<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613245">35</a>). Of these studies, Poitou <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R34" rid="R34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613269">34</a>) found that SAA1, SAA2, and SAA4 transcripts were overexpressed in the sc white adipose tissue of obese women compared with the lean controls. In addition, Gomez-Ambrosi <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613241">35</a>) found high levels of SAA mRNA transcripts in the omental adipose tissue of morbidly obese patients undergoing gastric bypass surgery. In all three of these studies, weight loss whether by diet (<a href="#R33" rid="R33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613237">33</a>, <a href="#R34" rid="R34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613216">34</a>) or gastric bypass (<a href="#R35" rid="R35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613222">35</a>) was associated with a significant decrease in SAA. Finally, O&#x02019;Brien and Chait (<a href="#R36" rid="R36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_172613246">36</a>) suggest that SAA and CRP may be chronically elevated in individuals with conditions such as atherosclerosis, insulin resistance, diabetes, and obesity leading to an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. Again, although these findings are tantalizing, we cannot yet draw a direct correlation between the obesity phenotype observed in N363S carriers with an increase in SAA levels observed in our stably expressing U-2 OS cells.</p><p id="P32" class="p p-last">There were many other genes that were differentially regulated by the N363S SNP compared with wild-type GR, with the majority of the genes involved in biological processes such as cell growth/maintenance and nucleic acid metabolism. However, the most notable aspect of our study is that the N363S SNP does regulate a novel set of genes in comparison with wild-type hGR. Additional studies will be needed to correlate specific genes with the N363S phenotype and provide a molecular understanding of the ability of the polymorphism to selectively regulate these genes.</p></div><div id="S18" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S18title">Acknowledgments</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p1">We thank Jennifer Collins, Danica Ducharme, and Sherri
