sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140513963042848title">Abstract</h2><!--article-meta--><div><p id="P1" class="p p-first-last">Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have reproducibly associated variants within introns of <em>FTO</em> with increased risk for obesity and type-2 diabetes (T2D) <sup><a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984331">1</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R3" rid="R3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984356">3</a></sup>. While the molecular mechanisms linking these noncoding variants with obesity are not immediately obvious, subsequent studies in mice demonstrated that <em>FTO</em> expression levels influence body mass and composition phenotypes <sup><a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984367">4</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984358">6</a></sup>. Yet, no direct connection between the obesity-associated variants and <em>FTO</em> expression or function has been made <sup><a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984365">7</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984347">9</a></sup>. Here, we show that the obesity-associated noncoding sequences within <em>FTO</em> are functionally connected, at megabase distances, with the homeobox gene <em>IRX3</em>. The obesity-associated <em>FTO</em> region directly interacts with the promoters of <em>IRX3</em> as well as <em>FTO</em> in the human, mouse, and zebrafish genomes. Furthermore, long-range enhancers within this region recapitulate aspects of <em>IRX3</em> expression, suggesting that the obesity-associated interval belongs to the regulatory landscape of <em>IRX3</em>. Supporting this, obesity-associated SNPs are associated with expression of <em>IRX3</em>, but not <em>FTO</em>, in human brains. Directly linking <em>IRX3</em> expression with regulation of body mass and composition, <em>Irx3-</em>deficient mice exhibit a 25&#x02013;30% reduction in body weight, primarily through the loss of fat mass and increase in basal metabolic rate with browning of white adipose tissue. Furthermore, hypothalamic expression of a dominant negative form of <em>Irx3</em> reproduces the metabolic phenotypes of <em>Irx3</em>-deficient mice. Our data posit that <em>IRX3</em> is a functional long-range target of obesity-associated variants within <em>FTO</em>, and represents a novel determinant of body mass and composition.</p></div></div><div id="idm140513979478272" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="P2" class="p p-first">Noncoding variation in single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) within a 47 kb region of high linkage disequilibrium (LD) in introns 1 and 2 of <em>FTO</em> remains the strongest genetic association with risk to polygenic obesity in humans <sup><a href="#R1" rid="R1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984392">1</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984346">4</a></sup>. Individuals homozygous for risk alleles of the associated SNPs weigh approximately 3 kilograms more than individuals homozygous for non-risk alleles, underscoring the significant phenotypic impact of these common variants <sup><a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984371">2</a></sup>. Multiple follow-up studies directly implicated <em>FTO</em> as a gene controlling body mass and composition. Null <em>Fto</em> alleles in mice result in lean phenotypes, with reciprocal models overexpressing <em>FTO</em> displaying increased body weight <sup><a href="#R4" rid="R4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984354">4</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984337">6</a></sup>. Lacking, however, is direct evidence that enhancers within the obesity-associated <em>FTO</em> introns are connected with regulation of <em>FTO</em> expression. Toward that end, expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) analyses have systematically failed to show association between the obesity-associated SNPs and <em>FTO</em> expression in human tissues <sup><a href="#R7" rid="R7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984339">7</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R9" rid="R9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984376">9</a></sup>.</p><p id="P3">To directly chart the cis-regulatory circuitry within the <em>FTO</em> locus, we used circular chromosome conformation capture followed by high throughput sequencing (4C-seq). We carried out 4C-seq in whole mouse embryos (E9.5) and in adult (8 weeks) mouse brains, since previous work suggest that brain <em>FTO</em> expression modulates metabolic parameters <sup><a href="#R6" rid="R6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984370">6</a>, <a href="#R10" rid="R10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984397">10</a></sup>. We profiled the genomic interactions with promoters of genes located within a megabase window around the obesity-associated SNPs (mm9, chr8:93,725,000&#x02013;94,725,000), including <em>Fto</em> and <em>Rpgrip1l</em>, and <em>Irx3</em>, a half-megabase downstream. The difference in interaction patterns among genes was striking (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1a</span></a>). The <em>Fto</em> promoter chiefly participates in genomic interactions proximal to the gene promoter. While in mouse embryos these interactions include the obesity-associated intronic region (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>), no such interactions are detected in adult mouse brains (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Extended Data Fig. 1</span></a> and <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 1</a>). In marked contrast, the promoter of <em>Irx3</em> participates in numerous long-range interactions across a broad genomic region encompassing nearly 2 megabases, including robust interactions with the obesity-associated interval within <em>FTO</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1a</span></a>). We confirmed these interactions between <em>Irx3</em> and the <em>Fto</em> obesity-associated region using chromatin conformation capture (3C) in adult mouse brains (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Extended Data Fig. 2c</span></a>). These data suggest the obesity-associated interval is likely part of the regulatory landscape of <em>Irx3</em>. We next inferred that the long-range interactions between the obesity-associated <em>FTO</em> intron and <em>IRX3</em> represent a conserved feature in vertebrate genomes. Inspecting the human ENCODE dataset, we observe that MCF-7 cells display the same pattern of long-range looping between the obesity-associated interval and <em>IRX3</em>, but not <em>FTO</em>, assayed by ChIA-PET (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Extended Data Fig. 2a</span></a>). This is further corroborated by recent HiC data in human fibroblasts<sup><a href="#R11" rid="R11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984336">11</a></sup> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F6/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F6" rid-ob="ob-F6" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F6"><span>Extended Data Fig. 2b</span></a>). We also observed a similar pattern of chromatin interactions in zebrafish embryos, assayed by 4C-Seq (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F5/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F5" rid-ob="ob-F5" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F5"><span>Extended Data Fig. 1c</span></a>). Together, these data posit that the obesity-associated <em>FTO</em> intron is mediating functional interactions with <em>IRX3</em> in the human, mouse, and zebrafish genomes.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140513972168656" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4113484_nihms566189f1.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms566189f1.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/bin/nihms566189f1.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140513972168656" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F1"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1">Figure 1</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Long-range interactions in the <em>IRX3</em>/<em>FTO</em> locus</strong><p id="__p5"><strong>a,</strong> Mouse embryo 4C-seq interactions emanating from each promoter are displayed as links across the circle (darker link implies greater significance). We observed that the degree of evolutionary conservation in noncoding sequences of the <em>FTO</em>-<em>IRX</em> locus (chr16:53731249&#x02013;54975288) places it on the top 2% of similarly-sized genomic intervals, suggesting the presence of many functional noncoding elements spread over the region (see Methods). More specifically, ENCODE data suggest that the 47-kb obesity-associated interval is fraught with cis-regulatory elements, evidenced by an abundance of enhancer-associated chromatin marks, DNase hypersensitive sites, and transcription factor binding events across this genomic region (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>). We tested three human DNA fragments from the 47-kb obesity-associated region for their putative enhancer properties using an <em>in vivo</em> mouse reporter assay. These fragments overlap enhancer-like chromatin marks in multiple cell lines. All three fragments displayed enhancer activity in neonatal (P0&#x02013;P1) mice. Interestingly, two of the fragments drove strong reporter gene expression in lungs (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>). <em>Irx3</em> is highly expressed during lung development (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>) <sup><a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984383">12</a>, <a href="#R13" rid="R13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984380">13</a></sup>, whereas <em>Fto</em> expression in lungs is limited (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Extended Data Fig. 3a</span></a> and references <sup><a href="#R2" rid="R2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984360">2</a>, <a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984385">14</a>&#x02013;<a href="#R16" rid="R16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984344">16</a></sup>). Consistent with our data, a previous report has shown that the <em>FTO</em> obesity-associated region contains enhancers that drive <em>Irx3</em>-like expression in zebrafish <sup><a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984379">17</a></sup>.</p><p id="P5">Our data suggest that the regulatory landscape of <em>IRX3</em> spreads over megabase distances, whereas <em>FTO</em> expression is primarily regulated by regions proximal to its promoter. To test this, we engineered a human Bacterial Artificial Chromosome (BAC) spanning 162 Kb of the <em>FTO</em> locus, including its promoter and the 47-Kb obesity-associated region (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Extended Data Fig. 3</span></a>). We recombineered a reporter cassette at the <em>FTO</em> translation start and generated transgenic mice harboring the engineered BAC. Transgenic mice expressed the reporter gene in multiple tissues, recapitulating the endogenous expression pattern of <em>FTO</em><sup><a href="#R14" rid="R14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984394">14</a></sup>. We next determined that a 1.2 Kb region corresponding to the <em>FTO</em> promoter is sufficient to recapitulate most of the <em>FTO</em> expression pattern (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Extended Data Fig. 3</span></a>). In contrast, a 2.8-kb region corresponding to the <em>IRX3</em> promoter does not recapitulate any of the endogenous expression patterns of <em>IRX3</em>, suggesting that <em>IRX3</em> expression relies on long-range cis-regulatory elements. A total of 15 human sequences in the broader <em>FTO-IRX</em> locus have been characterized as <em>in vivo</em> enhancers in mouse <sup><a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984387">17</a>, <a href="#R18" rid="R18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984364">18</a></sup>, driving reporter expression in <em>Irx3</em>-expressing tissues, including eye, limb, brain, neural tube, and branchial arches <sup><a href="#R12" rid="R12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984351">12</a>, <a href="#R19" rid="R19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984391">19</a></sup> (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 2</a>). While these enhancers lie outside the obesity association interval, they are consistent with our chromatin looping data pointing to a broad <em>Irx3</em> regulatory landscape, extending into <em>Fto</em>. Further supporting these findings, the genomic interval spanning <em>FTO</em> and <em>IRX3</em> has been proposed to be part of a single genomic regulatory block, based on the presence of extended synteny across deep phylogenies and a high density of highly conserved noncoding elements <sup><a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984390">17</a></sup>, as well as patterns of CTCF binding and chromatin interactions <sup><a href="#R20" rid="R20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984340">20</a></sup>. Under these models, our data support the notion that the broad expression patterns of <em>FTO</em> are primarily regulated by elements proximal to the promoter, and that <em>IRX3</em> is endowed with an ancient, extensive cis-regulatory circuitry extending into <em>FTO</em>.</p><p id="P6">We next determined that gene expression studies in human brains corroborate our chromatin looping data, showing that the obesity-associated SNPs are associated with expression levels of <em>IRX3</em>, but not <em>FTO</em>. Our data and that of others <sup><a href="#R17" rid="R17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984381">17</a></sup> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1b</span></a>) demonstrate that the obesity-associated SNPs overlap several <em>in vivo</em> enhancers, raising the possibility that allelic variants may disrupt enhancer activity and alter expression of their target gene(s). To test this, we carried out eQTL mapping in human brain samples. <em>FTO</em> and <em>IRX3</em> are highly expressed in multiple regions of the brain including cerebellum and hypothalamus (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Extended Data Fig. 4</span></a>). Utilizing a dataset of 153 brain samples from individuals of European ancestry <sup><a href="#R21" rid="R21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984350">21</a></sup>, represented by cerebellum, we found significant association between 11 SNPs previously associated with increased body mass index (BMI), and expression of <em>IRX3,</em> but not <em>FTO</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2a</span></a>, <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 3</a>). These common SNPs have minor allele frequencies ranging from 42.5 to 47.5% and are in strong LD with each other (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F9/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F9" rid-ob="ob-F9" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F9"><span>Extended Data Fig. 5</span></a>). In all cases, such as for SNP rs9930506 (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2b</span></a>), the allele associated with increased BMI is also associated with increased <em>IRX3</em> expression. Further analysis, utilizing the GIANT Consortium dataset of SNPs associated with BMI in 249,796 individuals <sup><a href="#R22" rid="R22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984349">22</a></sup>, reveals that among SNPs significantly associated with <em>IRX3</em> expression in brain or adipose tissue, only those associated with <em>IRX3</em> in brain show highly significant associations with BMI (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2c</span></a>). Taken together, our data directly ties the noncoding genetic variation within <em>FTO</em> to tissue-specific modulation of <em>IRX3,</em> but not <em>FTO</em>, expression in human brain.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140513974986928" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4113484_nihms566189f2.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms566189f2.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/bin/nihms566189f2.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140513974986928" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F2"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F2/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2">Figure 2</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>BMI-associated SNPs are eSNPs for <em>IRX3</em>, not <em>FTO</em>, expression in human brain</strong><p id="__p6"><strong>a,</strong> SNPs associated with BMI in the <em>FTO</em> locus. 43 SNPs associated with <em>IRX3</em> expression (eSNPs) are shown in red, including 11 also associated with BMI. To investigate a possible role of <em>Irx3</em> expression in the hypothalamus mediating the body composition and energy homeostasis phenotypes in Irx3KO mice, we utilized <em>Rosa26<sup>EnR-Irx3</sup></em> conditional transgenic mice expressing a dominant negative form of <em>Irx3</em> (<em>EnR-Irx3</em>) by crossing with <em>Ins2-Cre</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F13/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F13"><span>Extended Data Fig. 9a, b</span></a>)<sup><a href="#R23" rid="R23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984378">23</a>,<a href="#R25" rid="R25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984366">25</a></sup>. This design allows for disrupting <em>Irx3</em> function while preserving the architecture of genomic interactions between the <em>Irx3</em> promoter and long-range regulatory sequences including the <em>Fto</em> obesity-associated region. Remarkably, <em>EnR-Irx3;Ins2-Cre</em> mice phenocopy the physiological, histological, and molecular metabolism phenotypes of germline Irx3KO mice (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4a&#x02013;d</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F13/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F13" rid-ob="ob-F13" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F13"><span>Extended Data Fig. 9c, d</span></a>). Energy expenditure was significantly higher in <em>EnR-Irx3;Ins2-Cre</em> mice with similar food intake and locomotor activity (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4e</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Extended Data Fig. 10a&#x02013;e</span></a>). Notably, these mice also exhibited &#x0201c;browning&#x0201d; of WAT as well as activation of BAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4f</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F14/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F14" rid-ob="ob-F14" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F14"><span>Extended Data Fig. 10f&#x02013;g</span></a>). Together, our data support the notion that the hypothalamic expression of <em>Irx3</em> regulates energy homeostasis and body composition.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig The functional link between <em>FTO</em> and body mass regulation originated from animal models, unequivocally demonstrating that variation in <em>FTO</em> expression results in variation in body mass phenotypes. But to assess the null expectation that genetic manipulations in mice may result in variation in these parameters, we searched among 7,556 targeted mouse gene knock-out models for evidence of alterations in body size, mass, and growth (See Methods). Nearly one third (2,166; 29%) of gene knock-outs in mice show alteration of these phenotypes, underscoring that animal models alone are not sufficient to definitively establish a functional relationship between a given gene and long-range noncoding variants (<a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 4</a>). It is the aggregate of our data on chromatin looping, patterns of <em>in vivo</em> enhancer function, human eQTL mapping and mouse model data that collectively place <em>IRX3</em> as an important target gene of the genetic association with human obesity. These observations have direct implication on how to interpret the associations characterized for thousands of noncoding variants with human disease and related quantitative traits. We observe more than 150 genomic regulatory blocks, similar to that containing <em>FTO</em> and <em>IRX3</em>, overlapping with noncoding SNPs associated with human disease and complex traits in GWAS (Methods and <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 5</a>), highlighting the need for a careful experimental pipeline to define the target gene(s) for each disease-associated noncoding SNP.</p><p id="P11" class="p">Our data posit that the obesity-associated SNPs within <em>FTO</em> are functionally connected with regulation of <em>IRX3</em> expression, and that <em>IRX3</em> is an important determinant of body mass and composition. <em>IRX3</em> encodes a transcription factor highly expressed in brain. Significance (p-value) was evaluated using the <em>t</em>-statistic from the regression.</p></div><div id="S12" class="sec"><h3>eSNP Association with BMI</h3><p id="P29" class="p p-first">We downloaded genome-wide association data from the Genetic Investigation of Anthropometric Traits<sup><a href="#R45" rid="R45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984341">45</a></sup> (GIANT) consortium website (<a href="http://www.broadinstitute.org/collaboration/giant/index.php/GIANT_consortium_data_files#BMI_.28download_GZIP.29" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.broadinstitute.org/collaboration/giant/index.php/GIANT_consortium_data_files#BMI_.28download_GZIP.29</a>). We extracted the p-values for those SNPs in the locus with significant association (p&#x0003c;0.05) with IRX3 or FTO expression in adipose or cerebellum. We plotted the distribution of association p-values for these expression-associated SNPs (eSNPs). rs9930506 is highly associated with BMI (p=1.41e-53, N=123541). Recombination rates are estimated from the International HapMap Project.</p><p id="P30" class="p p-last"><strong>Statistical Analyses</strong> were done in the R statistical software (<a href="http://cran.r-project.org/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://cran.r-project.org/</a>). Regional plots were done by repurposing an R script retrieved from the Broad Institute (<a href="http://www.broadinstitute.org/diabetes/scandinavs/figures.html" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.broadinstitute.org/diabetes/scandinavs/figures.html</a>).</p></div><div id="S13" class="sec"><h3>Mice</h3><p id="P31" class="p p-first-last">All animal experimental protocols approved by Animal Care Committee of the Toronto Centre of Phenogenomics conformed to the standards of the Canadian Council on Animal Care. <em>Irx3</em>-deficient (Irx3KO) mice <sup><a href="#R47" rid="R47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984369">47</a></sup> and Ins2-Cre mice <sup><a href="#R48" rid="R48" class=" bibr popnode">48</a></sup> were described previously. <em>Rosa26</em><sup>EnR-Irx3</sup> conditional transgenic mice (EnR-Irx3) was generated using pROSA26PA gene-targeting vector as previously described <sup><a href="#R49" rid="R49" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984396">49</a></sup>. Mice were maintained on 12-hour light/dark cycles and provided with food and water <em>ad libitum</em>. For diet-induced obesity studies, 8 week old male mice were subjected to 45% high-fat diet (Research Diets) for 10 weeks. Body weight was measured every week from 4 to 18 weeks of age, and body composition was analyzed using a EchoMRI device (Echo Medical Systems) at 18 weeks of age. Body mass index (BMI) was calculated by dividing body weight (g) by body length (mm) squared (BMI = body weight/body length<sup>2</sup>).</p></div><div id="S14" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic phenotyping experiments</h3><p id="P32" class="p p-first-last">Energy expenditure was evaluated via indirect calorimetry (Oxymax System, Columbus Instruments) over periods of 24 hour. Briefly, energy expenditure was calculated by multiplying oxygen consumption (VO<sub>2</sub>) by the calorific value (CV = 3.815 + 1.232 &#x000d7; respiratory exchange ratio) and corrected by lean mass. Locomotor activity and food intake were also measured simultaneously. For glucose and insulin tolerance tests, mice were subjected to intraperitoneal bolus injection of glucose (1mg/g of body weight) or insulin (1.5mU/g) after fasting for overnight (14&#x02013;16 hours) or 6 hours with water <em>ad libitum</em>, respectively. Blood glucose levels were measured at the indicated intervals. For histological analysis, tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and embedded in paraffin. 5 &#x003bc;m sections were stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&#x00026;E). <em>Irx3</em> expression in brain and lung was examined using <em>Irx3lacZ</em> knockin mice with beta-galactosidase staining.</p></div><div id="S15" class="sec"><h3>Gene and protein expression analyses</h3><p id="P33" class="p p-first">Total RNA was extracted from fat tissues or hypothalamus using RNeasy Lipid Tissue Kit (Qiagen), and cDNA was synthesized from 2 &#x003bc;g of RNA using M-MLV reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen) with oligo(dT). Gene expression assay was conducted using SYBR Green methods on Viia7 (Applied Biosystems), and relative Ct values were normalized by &#x003b2;-actin. Primer sequences are available upon request.</p><p id="P34" class="p p-last">Western blot analysis was performed as previously described <sup><a href="#R50" rid="R50" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984393">50</a></sup>. The same amount of protein extracted from hypothalamus or brown adipose tissues was loaded, which was confirmed by &#x003b2;-actin as a loading control. Antibodies used are as follows: Ucp1 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology), Irx3 (generated in-house), and &#x003b2;-actin (Calbiochem).</p></div><div id="S16" class="sec"><h3>Statistics</h3><p id="P35" class="p p-first-last">All results are expressed as mean &#x000b1; S.E.M. Statistical significance of differences among groups was determined by Student&#x02019;s t-test or ANOVA with post hoc analysis, Student-Newman-Keuls using Sigma Stat (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL). Differences at <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.05 were considered statistically significant (*, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.05 vs. WT or control; #, <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.05 vs. WT, HFD). Following numbers of mice/samples were used for comparison; Body weight measurement for Irx3 mice (ND, WT/KO: n = 32/25; HFD, WT/KO: n = 12/7) and EnR-Irx3 mice (control/mutant: n = 9/12); Body composition analysis for Irx3 mice (ND WT/KO: n = 21/12; HFD WT/KO: n = 8/5) and EnR-Irx3 mice (control/mutant: n = 9/12); Glucose tolerance test for Irx3 mice (ND 8wk, WT/KO: n = 8/5; ND 18wk, WT/KO: n = 10/6; HFD 18wk, WT/KO: n = 12/7) and EnR-Irx3 mice (control/mutant: n = 5/8); Insulin tolerance test for Irx3 mice (WT/KO: n = 12/7); Indirect calorimeter analysis for Irx3 mice(ND WT/KO: n = 7/5; HFD WT/KO: n = 8/4) and EnR-Irx3 mice(control/mutant: n = 5/7), and Gene expression analysis for Irx3 mice (WT/KO: n = 10/7) and EnR-Irx3 mice (control/mutant: n = 5/7).</p></div><div id="S17" class="sec"><h3>Mouse Body Mass Phenotypes</h3><p id="P36" class="p p-first">To calculate the fraction of knockout mice that display a phenotype affecting body mass, we consulted the Mouse Genome Informatics (MGI) database<sup><a href="#R51" rid="R51" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984368">51</a></sup>.</p><p id="P37">First, to determine the number of gene knockouts that result in a body mass phenotype, we needed to define a more precise phenotype. The Mammalian Phenotype Ontology<sup><a href="#R52" rid="R52" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984374">52</a></sup> (<a href="http://www.informatics.jax.org/searches/MP_form.shtml" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.informatics.jax.org/searches/MP_form.shtml</a>) specifies a controlled vocabulary by which all mice in the MGI database are classified. Within this framework, (we believed) the most applicable is &#x0201c;abnormal postnatal growth/weight/body size&#x0201d; (MP:0002089).</p><p id="P38">Next, we crafted a query using the MouseMine portal<sup><a href="#R53" rid="R53" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984338">53</a></sup> (<a href="http://www.mousemine.org" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.mousemine.org</a>) to access the MGI database, using MP:0002089 as the search criteria in the &#x0201c;Mammalian phenotypes --&#x0003e; Mouse genotypes&#x0201d; template (<a href="http://www.mousemine.org/mousemine/template.do?name=MPhenotype_MFeature&#x00026;scope=all" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.mousemine.org/mousemine/template.do?name=MPhenotype_MFeature&#x00026;scope=all</a>). We asked for all targeted alleles on chromosomes 1&#x02013;19, X and Y, reasoning that many non-targeted (i.e. random, chemical and radiation-induced) mutations have not been precisely mapped, making it difficult if not impossible to ensure that they are not counted multiple times. Also, in the context of our study, we are discussing the chance that a targeted knockout would give a specific phenotype, so these types of alleles are most relevant. Finally, we excluded alleles present only as cell lines, resulting in 2166 unique genes.</p><p id="P39" class="p p-last">To determine the total number of targeted mutations present in the MGI database, we performed a search identical to that above but omitting the MP:0002089 term, resulting in 7556 unique genes.</p></div><div id="S18" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Genomic Regulatory Blocks</h3><p id="P40" class="p p-first">Genomic regulatory blocks are regions under strong selection that contain a number of syntenic highly conserved sequences, believed to regulate a syntenic target gene (&#x0201c;anchor gene&#x0201d;, the gene that is the raison d&#x02019;&#x000ea;tre of the GRB). There are different approaches to determine GRBs, usually identifying syntenic genes and non-coding conserved regions that span a region and determining the boundaries of the region.</p><p id="P41">GWAS identified SNPs that might affect non-coding regulatory elements that, by their turn, regulate specific target genes. Assignment of regulatory elements to targets is still a challenge, the simplest method being to assign them to the nearest gene. In the case of FTO, because the GWAS SNP occurs in an FTO intron, this gene was considered to be the target.</p><p id="P42">The notion put forward here is that when regulatory elements occur within a GRB, which is the case for FTO/IRX3/IRX5, it is likely that they target the anchor gene (IRX3/IRX5), given its importance. <a href="#SD1" rid="SD1" class=" supplementary-material">Supplemental Table 5</a> contains an annotation of GRBs and their anchor genes and the GWAS SNPs within the GRB as well as annotation regarding whether the nearest gene is the anchor gene or not. In those cases in which the nearest gene differs from the anchor gene, we propose that the anchor gene be used as the putative target and additional experimentation as shown in this work should be performed to precisely determine which is the target gene for the GWAS-affected regulatory element.</p><p id="P43">Genomic regulatory blocks were obtained from UCNEbase (<a href="http://ccg.vital-it.ch/UCNEbase/" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://ccg.vital-it.ch/UCNEbase/</a>)<sup><a href="#R54" rid="R54" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_509984359">54</a></sup>. Human UCNE clusters (hg19) were downloaded from <a href="http://ccg.vital-it.ch/UCNEbase/data/download/clusters/hg19_clusters_coord.bed" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=4113484&amp;issue-id=240913&amp;journal-id=319&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://ccg.vital-it.ch/UCNEbase/data/download/clusters/hg19_clusters_coord.bed</a> and are referred here as GRBs.</p><p id="P44" class="p p-last">The hg19 GWAS catalog from the UCSC Genome browser was downloaded and the tagging SNPs contained in GRBs with more than 1 gene (making SNP assigning doubtful) were kept for further annotation. SNPs occurring in exons were removed. The nearest gene was considered to be the RefSeq gene whose transcription start site was the nearest, or the gene where the SNP is contained.</p></div></div><div id="S25" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="S25title">Extended Data</h2><!--fig ft0--><!--fig @position="anchor" mode=article f4--><!--fig mode="anchored" f5--><div class="fig  anchored whole_rhythm" id="F5"><h3>Extended Data Figure 1</h3><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140513972763424" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=4113484_nihms566189f5.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is nihms566189f5.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/bin/nihms566189f5.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140513972763424" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F5/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><!--caption a8--><strong>Long range interactions in mouse and zebrafish</strong><p id="P46">4C-seq data for the Irx3/<em>Fto</em> locus, visualized with the UCSC genome browser. <strong>a,</strong> Data (also shown in the circular plot in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC4113484/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1</span></a>) generated using whole mouse embryos (E9.5), showing the frequency of interactions with the promoter of <em>Irx3</em> (blue, top) or <em>Fto</em> (magenta, bottom).
