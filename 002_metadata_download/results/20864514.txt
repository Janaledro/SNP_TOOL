In this study, we investigate whether TAK1 functions as a regulator of lipid and energy homeostasis and has a role in metabolic syndrome.</p></div><div id="__sec2" class="sec"><h2>RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS</h2><p id="__p2" class="p p-first-last">We generated TAK1-deficient (TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>) mice to study the function of TAK1 in the development of metabolic syndrome in aged mice and mice fed a high-fat diet (HFD). (Immuno)histochemical, biochemical, and gene expression profile analyses were performed to determine the effect of the loss of TAK1 expression on lipid homeostasis in liver and adipose tissues. In addition, insulin sensitivity, energy expenditure, and adipose-associated inflammation were compared in wild-type (WT) and TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice fed a HFD.</p></div><div id="__sec3" class="sec"><h2>RESULTS</h2><p id="__p3" class="p p-first-last">TAK1-deficient (TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>) mice are resistant to the development of age- and HFD-induced metabolic syndrome. Histo- and biochemical analyses showed significantly lower hepatic triglyceride levels and reduced lipid accumulation in adipose tissue in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice compared with WT mice. Gene expression profiling analysis revealed that the expression of several genes encoding proteins involved in lipid uptake and triglyceride synthesis and storage, including Cidea, Cidec, Mogat1, and CD36, was greatly decreased in the liver and primary hepatocytes of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. Restoration of TAK1 expression in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> hepatocytes induced expression of several lipogenic genes. Moreover, TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice exhibited reduced infiltration of inflammatory cells and expression of inflammatory genes in white adipose tissue, and were resistant to the development of glucose intolerance and insulin resistance. TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice consume more oxygen and produce more carbon dioxide than WT mice, suggesting increased energy expenditure.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec sec-last"><h2>CONCLUSIONS</h2><p id="__p4" class="p p-first-last">Our data reveal that TAK1 plays a critical role in the regulation of energy and lipid homeostasis, and promotes the development of metabolic syndrome. TAK1 may provide a new therapeutic target in the management of obesity, diabetes, and liver steatosis.</p></div></div></div><div id="idm140127771744704" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p5" class="p p-first">Obesity is a major health-care concern in Westernized cultures that affects &#x0223c;30% of the general population in the U.S. (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895346">1</a>,<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895395">2</a>). A strong etiologic link has been found between obesity and several obesity-associated diseases, including insulin-resistance, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and nonalcoholic fatty liver disease. There is considerable evidence indicating that systemic low-grade inflammation associated with obesity plays a pivotal role in the pathogenesis of metabolic syndrome (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895324">3</a><a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895323"></a><a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895388"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895352">6</a>). In particular, the infiltration of macrophages and T lymphocytes in hypertrophic adipose tissue and the production of proinflammatory cytokines are important early events in the development of obesity-associated complications (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895364">6</a><a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895333"></a><a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_229304665"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_266461203">9</a>).</p><p id="__p6">TAK1 (TR4, NR2C2), together with the closely related transcription factor TR2 (NR2C1), form a subclass of the nuclear receptor superfamily (<a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895348">10</a><a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895374"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895371">12</a>). TAK1 is highly expressed in several tissues, including the testis, brain, kidney, liver, and adipose tissue. Although TAK1 is still considered to be an orphan receptor, recent reports suggest that certain fatty acids and eicosanoids bind to and enhance the transcriptional activity of TAK1, thereby suggesting that TAK1 might function as a lipid sensor (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895389">13</a>,<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895339">14</a>). Although the precise physiologic functions of TAK1 remain poorly understood, characterization of TAK1-deficient mice have suggested a role for TAK1 in cerebellar development and reproductive functions (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895343">15</a><a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895336"></a><a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_304184147"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895344">18</a>). More recent studies have provided evidence suggesting a role for TAK1 in lipid metabolism and gluconeogenesis (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895393">14</a>,<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895362">19</a><a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895380"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895325">21</a>).</p><p id="__p7" class="p">In the present study, we used a TAK1-deficient (TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>) mouse model to obtain further insights into the physiologic roles of TAK1 in energy homeostasis. We show, for the first time, that male TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice are resistant to the development of age- and high-fat diet (HFD)-induced obesity and are protected against obesity-linked hepatic steatosis, white adipose tissue (WAT)-associated inflammation, and insulin resistance. Our study reveals that the TAK1-signaling pathway plays a critical role in the regulation of lipid and energy homeostasis and metabolic syndrome. Because TAK1 functions as a ligand-dependent transcription factor, it may provide a novel therapeutic target in the management and prevention of obesity and associated pathologies.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec5title">RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS</h2><div id="__sec6" class="sec sec-first"><h3>TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice.</h3><p id="__p8" class="p p-first">A schematic view and detailed information on the knockout strategy and mice are provided in supplementary Fig. 1 in the online appendix available at <a href="http://diabetes.diabetesjournals.org/cgi/content/full/db10-0628/DC1" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3012170&amp;issue-id=192927&amp;journal-id=582&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://diabetes.diabetesjournals.org/cgi/content/full/db10-0628/DC1</a>. TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice were bred into a C57BL/6 background for &#x0003e;8 generations. Mice were supplied ad libitum with National Institutes of Health-A31 formula and water. Mice that were 8 to 12 weeks old were fed a high-fat diet (HFD; <a id="__tag_374824690" class="tag_hotlink" href="/nuccore/D12492" ref="/nuccore/D12492"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"D12492","term_id":"220376","term_text":"D12492"}}</span>D12492</a>, Research Diets, New Brunswick, NJ) for 6 weeks, unless indicated otherwise. All animal protocols followed the guidelines outlined by the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences.</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><h3>Cell culture and viral infection.</h3><p id="__p10" class="p p-first-last">Primary hepatocytes were isolated using a Hepatocyte Isolation System (Worthington Biomedical, Lakewood, NJ). To generate adenovirus, TAK1WT and TAK&#x00394;AF2, a mutant lacking the AF2 domain, were cloned to pShuttle-IRES-hrGFP-1 vector and then transferred into AdEasy-1 (Stratagene, LA Jolla, CA). Adenovirus was then generated according to the manufacturer's protocol. Hepa1&#x02013;6/Emp, Hepa1&#x02013;6/TAK1, and Hepa1&#x02013;6/TAK&#x00394;AF2 cells were generated by infection with retrovirus containing the empty vector pLXIN, pLXIN-TAK1, or pLXIN-TAK1&#x00394;AF2, respectively. After selection in G418, separate clones were isolated. All cells were maintained in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium containing 10% FBS.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><h3>Histology and immunostaining.</h3><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">Adipose and liver specimens (<em>n</em> = 6) were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, paraffin-embedded, and tissue sections (5 &#x003bc;m) stained with hematoxylin-esosin. The average diameter of white adipocytes was calculated from 20&#x02013;30 cells/field and 3 fields/section. For the detection of macrophages, sections of white adipose tissue (WAT) were stained with an F4/80 antibody (Santa Cruz, CA) and avidin-biotin-peroxidase detection system.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><h3>RNA isolation, microarray analysis, and QRT-PCR.</h3><p id="__p12" class="p p-first-last">RNA isolation, microarray analysis, and QRT-PCR were carried out as described previously (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895330">22</a>). Total RNA from individual mice (<em>n</em> = 4&#x02013;10) in each group was analyzed as indicated. Details are listed in supplementary Table 1.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec"><h3>Biochemical assays.</h3><p id="__p13" class="p p-first-last">Blood levels of free fatty acids, &#x003b2;-hydroxybutyrate, glucose, cholesterol, triglycerides, and HDL were determined using the Cobas Mira Classic Chemistry System (Roche Diagnostics Systems, Montclair, NJ). The chemical reagents for all assays were purchased from Equal Diagnostics (Exton, PA). Serum insulin levels were analyzed with an insulin radioimmunoassay kit (Millipore, St. Charles, MO). To measure liver lipid content, tissues were homogenized and lipids extracted as previously described (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_447005477">23</a>). Triglyceride and cholesterol levels were measured with Stanbio assay kits (Stanbio Laboratory, Boerne, TX). Total ketones were analyzed with an Autokit (Waco Chemical GmbH, Neuss, Germany).</p></div><div id="__sec11" class="sec"><h3>Metabolic analysis.</h3><p id="__p14" class="p p-first-last">Wild-type (WT) and TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice were fed either a normal diet or HFD for 18 weeks and their oxygen consumption, CO<sub>2</sub> production, and respiratory exchange ratio were analyzed with a LabMaster system (TSE Systems, Chesterfield, MO). All values were measured every 5 min for 3 days. The average of the values during the circadian time or light period and dark period were calculated and presented. <em>P</em> values were calculated using the Student <em>t</em> test.</p></div><div id="__sec12" class="sec sec-last"><h3>Isolation of the stromal-vascular fraction and flow cytometry analysis.</h3><p id="__p15" class="p p-first-last">Stromal-vascular fraction (SVF) was isolated from epididymal white adipose tissue (eWAT) of mice fed with a HFD for 18 weeks and analyzed by flow cytometry with anti-F4/80 antibody (Invitrogen, Camarillo, CA), and anti-CD3, CD4, CD8, and CD11b antibodies (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA) as described (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895359">6</a>). Cells were costained with 7-amino-actinomycin D (7-AAD) or propidium iodine to exclude dead cells. Cells were analyzed with a BD LSR II Flow cytometer (Becton Dickinson) using FACSDiVa software as previously described (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895387">6</a>).</p></div></div><div id="__sec13" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec13title">RESULTS</h2><div id="__sec14" class="sec sec-first"><h3>Generation of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice.</h3><p id="__p16" class="p p-first-last">To obtain further insights into the role of TAK1 in vivo, we generated TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mutant mice in which TAK1 was functionally inactive (supplementary Fig. 1). Increased mortality of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> embryos was noted (supplementary Table 2). Although at 2 to 3 months the surviving TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice were slightly underweight, they were healthy and had a normal appearance and life span. Analysis of multiple organ tissues did not identify any gross anatomical or histologic abnormalities in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice.</p></div><div id="__sec15" class="sec"><h3>TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice are resistant to age-induced hepatic steatosis.</h3><p id="__p17" class="p p-first">TAK1 is highly expressed in several tissues that are critical in lipid and energy homeostasis (supplementary Fig. 2). TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice remain lean and show reduced adiposity and hepatic steatosis during aging or when fed a HFD. Moreover, TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice are protected against the development of age- and diet-induced adipose tissue-associated inflammation, insulin resistance, and glucose intolerance. These observations indicate that the nuclear receptor TAK1 plays a critical role in the control of energy balance and lipid homeostasis.</p><p id="__p39">Livers of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice showed a reduced lipid accumulation compared with their WT littermates. Hepatic triglyceride accumulation is controlled at several levels, including fatty acid uptake, synthesis and storage of triglycerides, fatty acid oxidation, and lipolysis. Gene expression profiling revealed a great number of differences in gene expression between livers from 1-year-old WT and TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice, including genes that are critical in the regulation of lipid, fatty acid, carbohydrate, and xenobiotic metabolism, and gene transcription (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3012170/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). The expression of many of these genes has been reported to be elevated in hepatic steatosis (<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895378">31</a>,<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895381">32</a>). One of these genes is CD36, which encodes a multifunctional protein implicated in angiogenesis, immunity, and in several metabolic disorders, such as obesity, hepatic steatosis, and insulin resistance (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895397">28</a>,<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895386">33</a>). In several cell types, including adipocytes and hepatocytes, CD36 facilitates long-chain fatty acid uptake. Thus, the reduced CD36 expression observed in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver may lead to diminished hepatic fatty acid uptake and, at least in part, be responsible for the resistance to hepatic steatosis.</p><p id="__p40">Cidea and Cidec were also among the genes that were the most dramatically downregulated in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. Cide proteins promote triglyceride accumulation within lipid droplets and regulate lipolysis, and their expression correlates positively with the development of obesity and hepatic steatosis (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895354">25</a>,<a href="#B34" rid="B34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895396">34</a>,<a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895392">35</a>). Deficiency in Cidea or Cidec in mice resulted in increased energy expenditure and lipolysis, and yielded a lean phenotype in mice and resistance to diet-induced obesity (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895349">25</a>,<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895390">26</a>,<a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895379">36</a>). Therefore, the repression of these genes in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice may also have contributed to the reduction in hepatic triglyceride levels and resistance to hepatic steatosis in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. Although the expression of Cidea and Cidec, as well as CD36, was greatly repressed in the liver of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice, TAK1 did not appear to regulate the expression of these genes in WAT, suggesting a tissue-dependent regulation.</p><p id="__p41">Mogat1, another gene that was dramatically downregulated in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver, is part of an alternative, less-studied pathway of triglyceride synthesis. The main pathway of triglyceride synthesis is catalyzed by glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (GPAT), acyl-glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferases (AGPATs), and diacylglycerol transferase (DGAT) in the final step of synthesis (<a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895356">37</a>). The expression of DGAT1 was not altered; however, the expression of GPAT1 and AGPAT6 was significantly reduced in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver. The latter is interesting because AGPAT6-deficiency has been reported to cause lipodystrophy and resistance to obesity (<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895332">38</a>). Thus, the lower levels of Mogat1, GPAT1, and AGPAT6 expression may be part of the mechanism by which triglyceride synthesis and storage is reduced in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver. Thus, the regulation of several genes with functions related to fatty acid uptake (<em>CD36</em>), triglyceride synthesis (<em>Mogat1</em>, <em>GPAT1</em>, <em>AGPAT6</em>), and storage (<em>Cidea</em>, <em>Cidec</em>) suggests that TAK1 affects several aspects of lipid accumulation. In contrast, no significant changes in fatty acid oxidation were observed.</p><p id="__p42">In contrast to aged mice, 4- to 5-month-old mice fed with a normal diet did not show histologic signs of hepatic steatosis; however, the hepatic expression of Cidea, Cidec, Mogat1, CD36, and Retn was significantly lower in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice than WT littermates. Consistent with a previous study (<a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895357">19</a>), young TAK1 KO mice were also more glucose tolerant and insulin sensitive than WT mice (supplementary Fig. 4). These observations suggest that TAK1 affects changes in hepatic gene expression and insulin sensitivity at an early age.</p><p id="__p43">Energy and lipid homeostasis is under the control of a complex network of transcription factors and coregulators (<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895367">32</a>,<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895366">39</a><a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895370"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895335">41</a>). Deficiencies in many of these factors have been associated with resistance to diet-induced obesity. For example, mice deficient in the nuclear receptors COUP-TFII and ERR&#x003b1;, or the coregulator RIP140 exhibit a lean phenotype; however, the expression of these genes was unaltered in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver. Because TAK1 itself functions as a transcription factor, one might expect that some of the differentially expressed genes be regulated directly by TAK1. Indeed, a recent report showed that TAK1 regulates <em>CD36</em> transcription in macrophages by binding to TAK1 response elements in the <em>CD36</em> gene promoter (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895358">14</a>), suggesting that <em>CD36</em> is a direct TAK1 target gene. <em>CD36</em> is also a known target of several other nuclear receptors, including PPAR&#x003b3;, LXR, and PXR (<a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895322">42</a>). Although the expression of PXR and LXR was unchanged, the expression of PPAR&#x003b3; was reduced by 50% in liver of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. Therefore, hepatic CD36 expression might be regulated by TAK1 directly as well as indirectly through modulation of PPAR&#x003b3; expression (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3012170/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>D</em>). The coregulators RIP140 and PGC-1&#x003b1;, and the receptor PPAR&#x003b3; have also been implicated in the regulation of Cidec (<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895355">29</a>,<a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895338">42</a>,<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895368">43</a>). TAK1 might cooperate with these transcriptional modulators to regulate the expression of these genes. Moreover, the downregulation of the transcription factor Srebf1, which promotes triglyceride synthesis (<a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895347">44</a>), may contribute to the reduced lipid accumulation in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> liver.</p><p id="__p44">Our data also demonstrated that the expression of several lipogenic genes was dramatically decreased in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> primary hepatocytes compared with WT hepatocytes. Restoration of TAK1 expression in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> hepatocytes by Ad-TAK1 induced the expression of Mogat1, Cidea, and Cidec, whereas empty virus or expression of an inactive form of TAK1 had little effect on their expression level. Moreover, downregulation of TAK1 in Hepa1&#x02013;6 cells by TAK1 siRNAs suppressed Cidec, whereas stable expression of TAK1-induced Cidec expression. These data indicate that these changes in gene regulation by TAK1 are hepatocyte cell autonomous and not a response to changes in other tissues. Whether these TAK1-responsive genes are direct targets of TAK1 transcriptional regulation needs further study.</p><p id="__p45">Recent studies have provided evidence indicating that TAK1 functions as a ligand-dependent transcription factor. Certain fatty acids, including &#x003b3;-linoleic acid and &#x003b3;-linolenic acid, as well as several eicosanoids, have been shown to activate TAK1-mediated transcription, suggesting that TAK1 might function as a fatty acid sensor (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895350">13</a>,<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895391">14</a>). Consistent with this hypothesis, we speculate that during aging or when fed a HFD, elevated levels of fatty acids may result in increased activation of TAK1 and enhanced expression of TAK1-responsive genes, such as CD36, that promote fatty acid uptake and triglyceride accumulation, and subsequent obesity (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3012170/figure/F8/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F8" rid-ob="ob-F8" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F8"><span>Fig. 8</span></a><em>D</em>). Hence, one could speculate that TAK1-selective antagonists would inhibit the expression of these genes and might be useful for the management of metabolic syndrome.</p><p id="__p46">In addition to hepatic steatosis, adiposity is greatly reduced in aged TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> and TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>(HFD) mice compared with WT mice. The adipocytes in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice were significantly smaller than in WT mice, suggesting reduced storage of triglycerides. Obesity is well known to be associated with chronic, low-grade inflammation, and there is considerable evidence that inflammation, insulin resistance, and aberrant lipid metabolism are interlinked in metabolic syndrome (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895331">3</a><a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895337"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895361">5</a>,<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_266461202">9</a>). Hypertrophy of adipose tissues and infiltration of inflammatory cells have been recognized as important early events in the development of obesity-linked pathologies. The molecular process of the recruitment and function of macrophage infiltration is not fully understood; however, the release of various cytokines by adipose tissue is likely part of the recruitment of various immune cells (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895369">6</a><a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895382"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_229304667">8</a>). In contrast to WT mice, TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice are protected against the development of age- and diet-induced adipose tissue-associated inflammation, as indicated by reduced infiltration of macrophages and T lymphocytes. Crown-like structures were rarely observed in WAT of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice and the macrophage markers, F4/80 and Mac-2, were expressed at significantly lower levels. In addition, the expression of several proinflammatory genes, including <em>Saa3</em>, <em>Mmp-12</em>, <em>Il1rn</em>, and <em>Tlr8</em>, were also reduced in adipose tissues of TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. T lymphocytes have also been implicated in the development of obesity-associated complications (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895383">6</a><a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895326"></a>&#x02013;<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_229304664">8</a>,<a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_219905734">30</a>). CD8<sup>+</sup> effector T cells have been reported to exhibit an essential role in the initiation and maintenance of adipose tissue inflammation, including macrophage recruitment, during obesity. The observed reduction in the number of CD8<sup>+</sup> cells in SVF might be linked to the diminished infiltration of macrophages and inflammatory response in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. Moreover, the reduced WAT inflammation in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice may in part be responsible for the preservation of the insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance observed in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice. In this regard, the repression of Il1rn expression in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> WAT is particularly interesting because upregulation of this gene has been reported to be associated with obesity whereas Il1rn KO mice have been shown to be resistant to obesity (<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895360">45</a>,<a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_205895341">46</a>). Therefore, repression of this gene may contribute to the resistance to obesity observed in TAK1 KO mice.</p><p id="__p47">Finally, two important factors in energy balance are food intake and energy expenditure. Although their relative food intake was slightly higher than their WT littermates, TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice exhibited a lean phenotype compared with WT mice. Furthermore, TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice showed a significant increase in energy expenditure as indicated by increased oxygen consumption and CO<sub>2</sub> production rates. The increase in energy expenditure by TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice is consistent with the elevated expression of UCP1 in BAT. UCP1 diverts energy derived from mitochondrial electron transport chain and generation of ATP into heat production. Thus, the elevated energy expenditure observed in TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup>(HFD) mice may at least in part be responsible for the reduced weight gain and resistance to hepatic steatosis and insulin insensitivity.</p><p id="__p48" class="p p-last">In summary, in this study we show for the first time that TAK1<sup>&#x02212;/&#x02212;</sup> mice are protected against age- and HFD-induced obesity, hepatic steatosis, adipose tissue-associated inflammation, and insulin resistance. As a ligand-dependent nuclear receptor, TAK1 might provide a novel therapeutic target in the management and prevention of obesity and related pathologies, such as diabetes.</p></div><div id="__sec22" class="tsec sec"><a id="supplementary-material-sec"></a><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec22title">Supplementary Material</h2><!--/article/body/sec/--><div class="sec suppmat" id="PMC_1">
