Surprisingly, PVAT has been reported to share characteristics of both brown and white adipose, but a detailed direct comparison to interscapular brown adipose tissue (BAT) has not been performed. Here we show by full genome DNA microarray analysis that global gene expression profiles of PVAT are virtually identical to BAT, with equally high expression of <em>Ucp-1</em>, <em>Cidea</em>, and other genes known to be uniquely or very highly expressed in BAT. PVAT and BAT also displayed nearly identical phenotypes upon immunohistochemical analysis, and electron microscopy confirmed that PVAT contained multilocular lipid droplets and abundant mitochondria. Compared with white adipose tissue (WAT), PVAT and BAT from C57BL6/J mice fed a high-fat diet for 13 wk had markedly lower expression of immune cell-enriched mRNAs, suggesting resistance to obesity-induced inflammation. Indeed, staining of BAT and PVAT for macrophage markers (F4/80 and CD68) in obese mice showed virtually no macrophage infiltration, and FACS analysis of BAT confirmed the presence of very few CD11b<sup>+</sup>/CD11c<sup>+</sup> macrophages in BAT (1.0%) compared with WAT (31%). In summary, murine PVAT from the thoracic aorta is virtually identical to interscapular BAT, is resistant to diet-induced macrophage infiltration, and thus may play an important role in protecting the vascular bed from inflammatory stress.</p></div><div class="sec"><strong class="kwd-title">Keywords: </strong><span class="kwd-text">obesity, microarray, macrophages, lipid droplet proteins, diabetes</span></div></div><div id="idm140357413362336" class="tsec sec headless whole_rhythm"><p id="__p2" class="p p-first"><span class="small-caps">the burgeoning prevalence</span> of obesity and diabetes throughout the world threatens to accelerate the incidence of associated cardiovascular diseases, including coronary artery disease, hypertension, and congestive heart failure. Although the mechanism by which obesity causes vascular disease is not fully understood, insulin resistance and the metabolic syndrome are thought to play a major role (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399897">7</a>, <a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_326453073">14</a>, <a href="#B27" rid="B27" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399865">27</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399906">41</a>). In human obesity as well as mouse models of obesity, enlarged adipocytes in visceral adipose tissue (VAT) become insulin resistant and fail to effectively store excess triglyceride through decreased capacity for lipogenesis and increased lipolysis (<a href="#B6" rid="B6" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399879">6</a>, <a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399828">7</a>, <a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_326453071">14</a>, <a href="#B24" rid="B24" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399826">24</a>, <a href="#B40" rid="B40" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399824">40</a>). These changes in adipose function lead to infiltration of immune cells, which are thought to cause chronic low-grade inflammation in obese subjects with the metabolic syndrome (<a href="#B8" rid="B8" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399850">8</a>, <a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_326453074">14</a>). Lipid overload in the face of decreased capacity of adipocytes to store triglycerides leads to ectopic lipid deposition in liver and muscle resulting in systemic insulin resistance (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_326453072">14</a>, <a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399831">22</a>). Prolonged obesity with these associated disorders is thought to be the major cause of &#x003b2;-cell failure and type 2 diabetes in humans.</p><p id="__p3">It is now well established that expansion of VAT, rather than subcutaneous adipose tissue (SAT), confers a high risk for the metabolic syndrome and incident cardiovascular disease (<a href="#B7" rid="B7" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399893">7</a>, <a href="#B10" rid="B10" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399834">10</a>, <a href="#B41" rid="B41" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399869">41</a>). Hypotheses for this observation include increased expression of angiotensinogen and complement, a greater rate of monocyte infiltration, and increased IL6 secretion in VAT compared with SAT (<a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399846">42</a>). It has also been postulated that the elevated rates of lipolysis in VAT compared with SAT render the former less able to sequester triglycerides away from liver and muscle (<a href="#B20" rid="B20" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399876">20</a>). These and other important differences between VAT and SAT have stimulated interest in the discovery of additional adipose depots that might have similar or increasingly pathologic functions in obesity. One such adipose depot is perivascular adipose tissue (PVAT), which was recently proposed to be a potential link between diabetes and cardiovascular disease (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399873">4</a>, <a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399889">13</a>, <a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399896">17</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399904">32</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399855">38</a>). The outermost connective tissue or adventitia surrounding an artery, including PVAT, may be prone to the same inflammation as VAT, and the idea that vascular disease could be impacted and perhaps promoted by factors in the adventitia has long-standing support (<a href="#B25" rid="B25" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399877">25</a>, <a href="#B30" rid="B30" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399853">30</a>, <a href="#B35" rid="B35" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399840">35</a>, <a href="#B36" rid="B36" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399871">36</a>).</p><p id="__p4">Initial studies (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399895">4</a>) of PVAT from human coronary arteries surprisingly detected expression of some BAT-specific genes (<em>PRDM16</em>, <em>UCP-1</em>, and <em>CPT1B</em>) to a degree that appeared intermediate to white adipose tissue (WAT) and BAT. These studies suggested PVAT might be an example of the recently described &#x0201c;brite&#x0201d; adipose tissue, white adipose that retains many of its usual characteristics but also displays some expression of BAT genes, including <em>Ucp-1</em> (<a href="#B31" rid="B31" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399847">31</a>). Interestingly, in rodents, PVAT surrounding the abdominal aorta displays characteristics of WAT, whereas PVAT surrounding the thoracic aorta appears quite different and is known to express some BAT genes (<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399886">11</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399836">32</a>). Although thoracic PVAT showed similarities to BAT in morphology and expressed <em>Ucp-1</em>, a direct detailed genomic comparison to true interscapular BAT has not yet been made (<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399901">11</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399880">32</a>). The extent to which thoracic PVAT in mice is more similar to human coronary PVAT, which displays only partial similarity to BAT, or to true interscapular BAT is an important question since mice are frequently used as models of human cardiovascular disease.</p><p id="__p5" class="p">We addressed this question by directly comparing genome-wide expression of thoracic PVAT to brown and white adipose depots from mice fed a normal diet (ND) or high-fat diet (HFD) for 13 wk. Remarkably, PVAT and BAT had virtually identical gene expression profiles. Furthermore, compared with the infiltration of macrophages into VAT of obese mice and the increased expression of macrophage-enriched genes in this tissue, thoracic PVAT and BAT were strikingly resistant to inflammation induced by high-fat feeding, as shown by microarray, immunohistochemistry, and FACS analysis. We conclude that PVAT surrounding the murine thoracic aorta is virtually identical to interscapular BAT and, like BAT, is resistant to inflammation under HFD conditions.</p></div><div id="__sec1" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec1title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2><div id="__sec2" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec3" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Animal studies. </h4><p id="__p6" class="p p-first-last">Male C57BL6/J mice were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME). All animals were fed normal chow until 8 wk of age. Animals were then divided into two groups; one fed normal chow and one fed HFD (45 kcal% fat; <a id="__tag_667175968" class="tag_hotlink" href="/nuccore/D12451" ref="/nuccore/D12451"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"D12451","term_id":"767753","term_text":"D12451"}}</span>D12451</a>; Research Diets) for 13 or 20 wk. Animals were fed ad libitum with free access to water and housed in the University of Massachusetts Medical School (UMASS) Animal Medicine facility with a 12:12-h light-dark cycle. Animals were weighed weekly for the duration of the diet study. Intraperitoneal glucose tolerance testing (GTT) was performed as previously described (<a href="#B33" rid="B33" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399868">33</a>). Area under the curve for the GTT was calculated using the trapezoidal method (<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399908">39</a>). At the completion of the HFD, mice were fasted for 6 h and then euthanized with CO<sub>2</sub> inhalation and bilateral pneumothorax. SAT (inguinal), VAT (epididymal), and BAT (interscapular) were harvested and snap frozen in liquid nitrogen. Blood was drawn via cardiac puncture into heparin-coated tubes, and the circulatory system was perfused with ice-cold PBS. Thoracic PVAT directly adjacent to the lesser curvature of the aortic arch was then harvested and snap frozen in liquid nitrogen. All experiments were performed in accordance with protocols approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee at UMASS Medical School.</p></div><div id="__sec4" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Insulin levels. </h4><p id="__p7" class="p p-first-last">Blood was collected via cardiac puncture at the time of euthanasia into heparin-coated microcentrifuge tubes and centrifuged at 3,000 <em>g</em> for 15 min. Serum was then decanted and stored at &#x02212;80&#x000b0;C. Fasting insulin levels were measured in duplicate with 10 &#x003bc;l of serum using a rat/mouse insulin ELISA (Millipore, St. Charles, MO) according to the manufacturers&#x02032; instructions.</p></div><div id="__sec5" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Quantitative PCR. </h4><p id="__p8" class="p p-first-last">Adipose tissue was isolated as previously described, snap frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at &#x02212;80&#x000b0;C. Tissues were homogenized and total RNA was isolated with RNA mini lipid kits (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). Two-hundred fifty nanograms of total RNA were reverse transcribed with the Bio-Rad iScript cDNA synthesis kit (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). cDNA was diluted 1:5, and 2.5 &#x003bc;l was used in a 12.5-&#x003bc;l reaction volume; each reaction was performed in duplicate. Real-time quantitative (q)PCR was performed with a Bio-Rad C1000 Thermal Cycler and SYBR Green Master Mix (Bio-Rad) using the following cycle parameters: 95.0&#x000b0;C for 3 min, 95.0&#x000b0; for 0:10 min, 60.0&#x000b0;C for 0:15 min, and 72.0&#x000b0;C for 0:30 min for 40 cycles. Expression was normalized to the reference gene <em>36b4</em> and expressed as relative to expression in SAT of ND mice using the 2<sup>&#x02212;&#x00394;&#x00394;Ct</sup> method (<a href="#B26" rid="B26" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399857">26</a>). Melt curve analysis and agarose gel electrophoresis were performed to determine the specificity of the PCR reaction products. Primer sequences were as follows: <em>Ucp-1</em> for AGGCTTCCAGTACCATTAGGT and <em>Ucp-1</em> rev CTGAGTGAGGCAAAGCTGATTT; <em>Acrp30</em> for TGTTCCTCTTAATCCTGCCA and <em>Acrp30</em> rev CCAACCTGCACAAGTTCCCTT; <em>36b4</em> for TCCAGGCTTTGGGCATCA and <em>36b4 rev</em> CTTTATCAGCTGCACATCACTCAGA; <em>Cidea</em> for ATCACAACTGGCCTGGTTACG and <em>Cidea</em> rev TACTACCCGGTGTCCATTTCT; and <em>PPAR</em>&#x003b3;<em>2</em> for ATGGGTGAAACTCTGGGAG and <em>PPAR</em>&#x003b3;<em>2</em> rev GTGGTCTTCCATCACGGAGA.</p></div><div id="__sec6" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Microarray analysis. </h4><p id="__p9" class="p p-first">RNA was isolated from SAT, VAT, BAT, and PVAT as previously described. RNA concentrations were determined using a Nanodrop 2000 Spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher, Wilmington, DE). The RNA quality was assessed using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). Only samples with a RNA integrity number &#x0003e;7.5 and normal 18- and 28-s fractions on microfluidic electrophoresis were used. RNA from two mice per tissue and diet was pooled for a total of 250 ng total RNA template for cDNA synthesis and in vitro transcription using the Ambion WT expression kit (Ambion, Carlsbad, CA). Second-strand cDNA was then labeled with the Affymetrix WT terminal labeling kit, and samples were hybridized to Affymetrix Mouse Gene 1.0 ST arrays (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA). Gene chip expression array analysis for individual genes was performed as previously described (<a href="#B39" rid="B39" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399867">39</a>), filtering for <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.05 and a fold change of &#x0003e;2. Three biological replicate hybridizations per tissue and diet were performed, for a total of 24 hybridizations. Robust multiarray average was adopted in the UMASS Microarray Computational Environment (MACE) to preprocess raw oligonucleotide microarray data. The algorithm was implemented as a function of the R package Affy (<a href="#B2" rid="B2" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399839">2</a>), which is part of the Bioconductor project (<a href="#B12" rid="B12" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399887">12</a>) using the statistical computing language R (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). All statistical calculations are performed using the R statistical computing environment, and the results are stored in a relational database. The preprocessed data are stored as base 2 log transformed real signal numbers and are used for fold-change calculations and statistical tests and to determine summary statistics. Mean signal values and SDs are computed for each gene across triplicate experiments and stored in the database. The fold change of expression of a gene in two experiments is the ratio of mean signal values from these experiments and is always a number greater than one. If the ratio is less than one, the negative value of the inverse ratio is stored as fold change. All downregulated genes therefore have a negative fold change value, and upregulated genes have a positive fold change. In both cases, this value is greater or equal than one.</p><p id="__p10" class="p p-last">To determine differential expression of genes in two hybridization experiments, MACE internally conducts a Student's <em>t</em>-test with the expression signal values of the two hybridizations for all genes in the set. The <em>t</em>-test value and test <em>P</em> value are stored in MACE and can be queried through the MACE user interface. The <em>P</em> values stored and displayed as a result of a query are not adjusted for multiple testing. The data discussed in this publication have been deposited in NCBI's Gene Expression Omnibus (<a href="#B9" rid="B9" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399856">9</a>) and are accessible through GEO Series Accession No. <a id="__tag_235399914" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28440" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28440"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE28440","term_id":"28440","extlink":"1"}}</span>GSE28440</a> (<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28440" ref="reftype=extlink&amp;article-id=3197360&amp;issue-id=201406&amp;journal-id=625&amp;FROM=Article%7CBody&amp;TO=External%7CLink%7CURI&amp;rendering-type=normal" target="_blank">http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=<a id="__tag_235399912" class="tag_hotlink" href="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28440" ref="/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE28440"><span class="tag-json" style="display:none">{"type":"entrez-geo","attrs":{"text":"GSE28440","term_id":"28440"}}</span>GSE28440</a></a>).</p></div><div id="__sec7" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Histology and immunohistochemistry. </h4><p id="__p11" class="p p-first-last">Adipose tissue samples (<em>n</em> = 3 per group) from normal and HFD animals were fixed in 4% formalin for immunohistochemistry. Briefly, samples were embedded in paraffin, sectioned, and stained with a rat anti-mouse F4/80 (ABd Serotec, Raleigh, NC; 1:50 dilution) or rat anti-mouse CD68 primary antibody (ABd Serotec; 1:40 dilution). Staining was visualized with a horseradish peroxidase-linked rabbit anti-rat secondary antibody. Staining with the secondary antibody alone was performed as a negative control. Images were taken with a Zeiss Microscope and PixeLINK SE Software.</p></div><div id="__sec8" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Transmission electron microsopy of adipose tissue samples. </h4><p id="__p12" class="p p-first">Adipose tissue samples were fixed in 2.5% glutaraldehyde in PBS (pH 7.2). Tissue samples were dissected into 1-mm blocks, and the tissues washed three times and left overnight in fresh buffer. The next morning the samples were postfixed for 1 h in 1% osmium tetroxide. The fixed adipose tissue samples were then washed again in the PBS and dehydrated through a graded series of ethanol to 100% and transferred through two changes of propylene oxide and finally into a 50:50 (vol/vol) mixture of propylene oxide:epoxy resin (SPON 812/Araldite 502) and left overnight to infiltrate. The following morning the adipose tissue samples were processed through two changes of fresh epoxy resin and embedded, allowing the blocks to polymerize 48 h at 70&#x000b0;C.</p><p id="__p13" class="p p-last">Blocks of tissue were then selected, cut out, and attached to blank epoxy stubs with a drop of Super Glue. Ultrathin sections were cut on a Reichart-Jung ultramicrotome using a diamond knife. The sections (64-nm thick) were collected and mounted on copper support grids and contrasted with lead citrate and uranyl acetate and examined on a Philips CM 10 transmission electron microscope at 80-Kv accelerating voltage.</p></div><div id="__sec9" class="sec"><p></p><h4 class="inline">FACS analysis. </h4><p id="__p14" class="p p-first-last">Mice were euthanized, and BAT and VAT were removed and treated with 4 and 2 mg/ml collagenase, respectively, in 4% BSA in PBS at pH 7.4 for 1 h. Samples were filtered through 200- and 30-&#x003bc;M spectra mesh sequentially and stained with CD31 (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, NJ; 1:1,000 dilution), CD11b (BD Biosciences; 1:200 dilution), and CD11c (BD Biosciences; 1:200 dilution) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Samples were run on a BD LSRII flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) and analyzed in Flow Jo, gating for CD31-negative cells.</p></div><div id="__sec10" class="sec sec-last"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Statistical analysis. </h4><p id="__p15" class="p p-first-last">All values are shown as means &#x000b1; SE. For experiments other than the microarray analyses, the Student's <em>t</em>-test for two-tailed distributions with equal variances was used for comparison between two groups; for comparisons greater than &#x02265;3 groups, two-way ANOVA followed by the Bonferroni correction was used. Differences &#x0003c;<em>P</em> &#x02264; 0.05 were considered significant. Data were entered into Microsoft Excel, and statistical analyses were performed with Graph Pad Prism 5.</p></div></div></div><div id="__sec11" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec11title">RESULTS</h2><div id="__sec12" class="sec sec-first-last"><div id="__sec13" class="sec sec-first"><p></p><h4 class="inline">Cidea and Ucp-1 are highly expressed in BAT and PVAT independent of obesity. </h4><p id="__p16" class="p p-first">HFD mice (<em>n</em> = 12) gained more weight than ND mice (<em>n</em> = 12) beginning at 3 wk and remained significantly more obese until the end of the study (30.9 &#x000b1; 0.75 vs. 42.08 &#x000b1; 0.78 g; <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.001). At the end of 13 wk, HFD mice were more glucose intolerant as shown by an elevated fasting blood glucose concentration (136.4 &#x000b1; 1.22 vs. 185.8 &#x000b1; 21.4 mg/dl; <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.02), elevated fasting insulin levels (2.1 &#x000b1; 0.64 vs. 5.4 &#x000b1; 1.03 ng/ml; <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.02), and increased area under the curve for the intraperitoneal GTT (29,352 &#x000b1; 753 vs. 38,983 &#x000b1; 1,928 min&#x000b7;mg<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>&#x000b7;dl<sup>&#x02212;1</sup>; <em>P</em> &#x0003c; 0.01).</p><p id="__p17" class="p">Analysis of mRNA from adipose tissue samples by qPCR showed that <em>Cidea</em> and <em>Ucp-1</em> were highly expressed in PVAT and BAT compared with SAT. Remarkably, the relative expression between these two depots was similar for both genes (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1</span></a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>). Interestingly, <em>Cidea</em> expression was reduced &#x0003e;50% by HFD in both PVAT and BAT; this effect was not observed for <em>Ucp-1</em> (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1</span></a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>). With respect to other markers of adipose differentiation, <em>PPAR</em>&#x003b3;<em>2</em> expression was slightly greater in VAT, PVAT, and BAT than SAT and significantly reduced by HFD in these depots (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1<em>D</em></span></a>). Adiponectin (<em>Acrp30)</em> expression was significantly greater in VAT and decreased with HFD (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F1/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F1" rid-ob="ob-F1" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F1"><span>Fig. 1<em>C</em></span></a>); expression of this gene was similar in all other depots. Therefore, PVAT expresses <em>Cidea</em> and <em>Ucp-1</em> to an equivalent degree as BAT, suggesting that it may be more similar to BAT than brite adipose tissue. As expected, expression of white adipose-enriched genes was significantly greater in SAT and VAT compared with PVAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). In other gene categories, expression of the adipokines <em>Lep</em>, <em>Retn</em>, <em>Rbp4</em>, and <em>Adipoq</em> was higher in SAT and VAT, whereas the receptors for <em>Adipoq</em> (<em>Adipor1/Adipor2</em>) were more highly expressed in PVAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). In regards to lipid metabolism genes, those involved in synthesis or storage (<em>Fads1</em>, <em>Fads3</em>, and <em>Cidec</em>) were expressed to a greater extent in SAT and VAT, whereas those functioning in lipid oxidation (<em>Gpam</em>, <em>Acss1</em>, and <em>Acsl5</em>) were more highly expressed in PVAT and BAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). Surprisingly, expression of immune cell-enriched genes, such as chemokines (<em>Ccl5</em> and <em>Ccl8</em>), T-cell receptors (<em>Cd3g</em>, <em>Cd3d</em>), and macrophage markers (<em>Cd68, Emr1</em>) was significantly greater in SAT and VAT compared with PVAT and BAT even under ND conditions (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). Given this dramatic difference in expression of immune cell genes in white compared with brown adipose depots, we compared the effects of high-fat feeding on changes in expression of immune cell-enriched genes in the four adipose depots.</p><p id="__p28" class="p"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a> shows the fold changes in expression for a series of selected immune cell enriched genes when comparing ND with HFD conditions in PVAT, BAT, SAT, and VAT depots. VAT demonstrated the greatest fold increase in expression of immune cell genes after high-fat feeding (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). Although the other depots exhibited some trends for changes in gene expression between ND and HFD fed mice, none of them met statistical significance (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>). Taken together, the data in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Tables 1</span></a> and <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span style="position: relative;text-decoration:none;">&#x200B;<span class="figpopup-sensitive-area" style="left: -1.5em;">and2</span></span><span>2</span></a> reveal a remarkably similar global gene expression profile between PVAT and BAT. These two adipose depots show no significant differences in expression in the majority of genes previously shown to be enriched in brown adipocytes, white adipocytes, or immune cells. In addition, the data in <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a> are particularly revealing in that the levels of expression of immune cell genes, as reflected by their probe set signals, are uniformly higher in both SAT and VAT compared with PVAT and BAT in both ND and HFD conditions. These data imply that the PVAT and BAT depots are relatively resistant to inflammation induced by high-fat feeding compared with WAT depots.</p><!--table ft1--><!--table-wrap mode="anchored" t5--><div CD68 and F4/80 markers define total macrophage populations in adipose tissue but do not differentiate between cells at various levels of activation (<a href="#B43" rid="B43" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399872">43</a>, <a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399861">45</a>, <a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399899">46</a>). Therefore, after exclusion of CD31<sup>+</sup> endothelial cells from the stromal vascular fraction, we chose to doubly stain cells for CD11b and CD11c, the latter being a marker of classical (M1) macrophage differentiation (<a href="#B45" rid="B45" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399837">45</a>). Eleven weeks of HFD resulted in a striking and significant enrichment of CD11b<sup>+</sup>/CD11c<sup>+</sup> macrophages in VAT compared with that observed for BAT (31.7 vs. 1.03%; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7<em>A</em></span></a>). As we failed to observe macrophage recruitment into BAT with 11 wk of HFD, we extended the duration of the HFD to 20 wk (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F7/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><span>Fig. 7<em>B</em></span></a>). This prolonged HFD duration resulted in a marked, significant increase in CD11b<sup>+</sup>/CD11c<sup>+</sup> macrophages in VAT, while BAT showed no enrichment of this cell population. Unfortunately, we were unable to perform FACS on PVAT due to the small quantity of this tissue in the aortic arch. However, the results of FACS analysis confirm that VAT is enriched with M1 macrophages after 11 and 20 wk of HFD, while BAT was resistant to this effect.</p><!--fig ft0--><!--fig mode=article f1--><div class="fig iconblock whole_rhythm clearfix" id="F7" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F7"><a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F7/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7"><!--fig/graphic|fig/alternatives/graphic mode="anchored" m1--><div data-largeobj="" data-largeobj-link-rid="largeobj_idm140357420460848" class="figure"><a class="inline_block ts_canvas" href="/core/lw/2.0/html/tileshop_pmc/tileshop_pmc_inline.html?title=Click%20on%20image%20to%20zoom&amp;p=PMC3&amp;id=3197360_zh40101100540007.jpg" target="tileshopwindow"><div class="ts_bar small" title="Click on image to zoom"></div><img alt="An external file that holds a picture, illustration, etc.&#10;Object name is zh40101100540007.jpg" title="Click on image to zoom" class="tileshop" src="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/bin/zh40101100540007.jpg" /></a></div><div id="largeobj_idm140357420460848" class="largeobj-link align_right" style="display: none"><a target="object" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F7/?report=objectonly">Open in a separate window</a></div></a><div class="icnblk_cntnt" id="lgnd_F7"><div><a class="figpopup" href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F7/" target="figure" rid-figpopup="F7" rid-ob="ob-F7">Fig. 7.</a></div><!--caption a7--><div class="caption"><p id="__p35">BAT is resistant to inflammation after 11 and 20 wk of HFD. <em>A</em>: after 11 wk of HFD, the stromal vascular fraction was isolated from VAT and BAT and stained with CD31-PE, CD11b-PerCp Cy5.5, and CD11c-PE Cy7. Results represent 3 independent experiments.</p></div></div></div></div></div></div><div id="__sec18" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec18title">DISCUSSION</h2><p id="__p36" class="p p-first">A major finding of the present studies is that PVAT from the murine thoracic aorta displays a gene expression pattern that is nearly identical to interscapular BAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4</span></a>; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>; Supplemental Tables S3 and S4). The similarity of gene expression between PVAT and BAT includes identical expression levels of the brown adipocyte genes <em>Cidea</em>, <em>Ucp-1</em>, <em>Dio2</em>, and <em>Prdm16</em>. Only 0.79% of genes represented on chip were differentially regulated between PVAT and BAT in ND conditions at the stringency level used. Interestingly, immunoglobulin genes were expressed to a greater extent in PVAT; this has also been demonstrated in human coronary PVAT (<a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399843">28</a>). We hypothesize that this due to the presence of vascular-associated lymphoid tissue (<a href="#B1" rid="B1" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399842">1</a>), although we have been unable to visualize such tissue using B-cell-specific markers (data not shown).</p><p id="__p37">In contrast to these subtle differences between PVAT and BAT, major differences between PVAT and WAT were observed in the expression of genes previously shown to be highly and selectively expressed in BAT or WAT (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>). Our data thus clarify and definitively substantiate previous indications that mouse thoracic PVAT is similar to BAT (<a href="#B3" rid="B3" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399910">3</a>, <a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399838">11</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399859">32</a>). Importantly, these previous studies demonstrate that PVAT can have characteristics of both BAT and WAT but this dichotomy largely depends on the anatomical context, that surrounding the abdominal aorta resembling WAT and that surrounding the thoracic aorta BAT (<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399878">11</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399841">32</a>). Our studies directly comparing thoracic PVAT gene expression (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>; Supplemental Tables S1&#x02013;S4; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F4/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F4" rid-ob="ob-F4" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F4"><span>Fig. 4</span></a>) and histological appearance (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/figure/F2/" target="figure" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="F2" rid-ob="ob-F2" co-legend-rid="lgnd_F2"><span>Fig. 2</span></a>) to mouse interscapular BAT show unequivocally that these two adipose depots are virtually identical. This appears different from human coronary PVAT, which demonstrates an intermediate phenotype between white and brown adipose (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399845">4</a>).</p><p id="__p38">After 13 wk of high-fat feeding, VAT displayed the expected dramatic increase in expression of many immune cell-enriched genes, while thoracic PVAT and BAT did not. This was somewhat surprising because some previous reports indicated that increased infiltration of macrophages and T cells into both PVAT (<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399832">32</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399829">38</a>, <a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399902">42</a>) and BAT (<a href="#B16" rid="B16" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399903">16</a>, <a href="#B18" rid="B18" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399860">18</a>) could be detected in mouse models. Xu et al. (<a href="#B46" rid="B46" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399909">46</a>) reported that genetic and diet-induced obesity resulted in chronic inflammation in WAT but not BAT. Thus the extent of BAT inflammation may greatly depend upon the mouse strain and conditions used. Our data are consistent with the results of Xu et al., suggesting BAT is comparatively resistant to inflammation in C57BL6/J mice after HFD feeding.</p><p id="__p39">In contrast to our present findings of very low inflammation of PVAT in mice after 13 wk of HFD, some investigators have proposed that PVAT is an intrinsically proinflammatory depot (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399890">4</a>, <a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399911">17</a>, <a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399852">28</a>). For example, humans with established coronary artery disease have higher mRNA and protein levels of IL-1&#x003b2;, IL-6, MCP-1, and TNF-&#x003b1; in epicardial adipose than in paired SAT samples. More recently, Chatterjee et al. (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399830">4</a>) published data regarding human coronary PVAT and murine thoracic PVAT, the same anatomic fat depot we have studied here. In control mice, relative mRNA expression of <em>Acrp30</em>, <em>Ppar</em>&#x003b3;, and <em>Fabp4</em> was lower in PVAT than SAT. With 2 wk of HFD feeding, expression of these adipocyte-specific genes further declined, while expression of the proinflammatory genes <em>Lep</em> and <em>Mip1</em>&#x003b1; increased (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399823">4</a>). The authors concluded that compared with SAT, PVAT was poorly differentiated and intrinsically proinflammatory and hence possibly an etiologic factor in the development of vascular disease (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399858">4</a>).</p><p id="__p40">Our findings are in contrast to the above (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399835">4</a>) in that expression of <em>Acrp30</em> and <em>Ppar</em>&#x003b3;<em>2</em> was not different in thoracic PVAT compared with other fat depots. A possible explanation for the difference in expression of <em>Acrp30</em> and <em>Ppar</em>&#x003b3;<em>2</em> between the two studies is that there may be diet- or age-dependent changes in the expression of these two genes; Chatterjee et al. (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399844">4</a>) used a shorter duration HFD and younger mice. For example, it may be that younger and older mice than those studied herein (21 wk) are prone to inflammation of PVAT. Two studies (<a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399881">32</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399870">38</a>) that have reported obesity associated inflammation of abdominal and femoral PVAT used 40- and 22-wk-old mice, respectively. Therefore, it is likely that many factors including age, strain, diet, and specific anatomic location influence whether or not PVAT becomes inflamed. Finally, although Chatterjee et al. (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399851">4</a>) did not report expression of BAT genes in their mouse model, their failure to observe significant expression of immune cell-specific genes <em>Cd68</em> and <em>Cd3</em> by qPCR does concur with our results.</p><p id="__p41">Importantly, both we and Chatterjee et al. (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399864">4</a>) have studied PVAT from the thoracic aortic arch, as this a segment of the aorta prone to atherosclerosis in both humans and mice. Although PVAT was originally hypothesized to signal in a &#x0201c;vasocrine&#x0201d; fashion to influence vascular tone (<a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399862">47</a>), it has subsequently been implicated in adventitial inflammation, which might promote atherosclerosis (<a href="#B4" rid="B4" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399820">4</a>, <a href="#B17" rid="B17" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399849">17</a>, <a href="#B28" rid="B28" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399884">28</a>, <a href="#B42" rid="B42" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399888">42</a>, <a href="#B47" rid="B47" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399898">47</a>); our study was designed to test these latter hypotheses.</p><p id="__p42">The overall evidence from the literature combined with our present work suggests that PVAT can have different characteristics depending on its anatomical location. Thus BAT-like adipose surrounds the thoracic aorta and WAT surrounds the abdominal aorta (<a href="#B11" rid="B11" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399827">11</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399905">32</a>). As such, we suggest that PVAT surrounding the abdominal aorta, like its visceral fat counterpart, is prone to the dysregulated adipocyte biology of obesity and subsequent inflammation. This idea has been verified by Takaoka <em>et al.</em> (<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399894">38</a>) who demonstrated that PVAT surrounding the femoral artery is WAT that has beneficial properties in lean conditions that are mediated by the paracrine effects of adiponectin (<em>Acrp30</em>). In obese states, inflammation and macrophage infiltration of PVAT surrounding the femoral artery result in decreased adiponectin secretion and increased <em>Tnf</em>&#x003b1; expression, both of which facilitate pathological neointimal hyperplasia in response to vessel injury (<a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399891">38</a>). In humans, neointimal hyperplasia contributes to the pathophysiology of coronary artery disease. For example, diabetes and obesity are associated with increased rates of in-stent restenosis following percutaneous intervention due to neointimal hyperplasia (<a href="#B29" rid="B29" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399833">29</a>). A scenario in which white PVAT is beneficial in lean conditions, but becomes dysfunctional in obese conditions, is analogous to the current model of obesity-induced insulin resistance (<a href="#B14" rid="B14" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_326453070">14</a>). These and other reports support the concept that PVAT with white adipose features conforms to this model (<a href="#B13" rid="B13" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399885">13</a>, <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399822">32</a>, <a href="#B38" rid="B38" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399821">38</a>, <a href="#B44" rid="B44" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399875">44</a>). The important contribution of our work is that brown PVAT surrounding the thoracic aorta appears to be resistant to obesity-induced inflammation and hence may offer protection from the associated changes to the arterial adventitia.</p><p id="__p43">The finding that PVAT and BAT display little or macrophage infiltration under HFD conditions, while WAT does, raises the interesting question: what is unique to WAT that causes infiltration of macrophages? A recent study by Kosteli et. al (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399863">22</a>) may shed insight into this important question. Using caloric restriction of previously HFD fed mice, the authors showed that rates of adipocyte lipolysis correlate with macrophage infiltration. These studies suggest that the chronic elevation in WAT lipolysis observed in obesity causes increased release of fatty acids, which serve to stimulate macrophage infiltration (<a href="#B22" rid="B22" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399882">22</a>). This hypothesis offers a potential explanation for why BAT and BAT-like PVAT fail to attract immune cells. The unique function of BAT and PVAT to rapidly metabolize fatty acids via high capacity for &#x003b2;-oxidation likely results in relatively low rates of local free fatty acid release from the cells compared with WAT (<a href="#B48" rid="B48" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399892">48</a>). A second possibility is that the fatty acid species released by BAT and PVAT may also afford an anti-inflammatory effect. The ratio of saturated to polyunsaturated fatty acids has been shown to increase in the VAT and abdominal PVAT of high-fructose fed rats (<a href="#B34" rid="B34" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399866">34</a>). Thus under these conditions WAT may release relatively higher levels of saturated fatty acids, which may be more active in stimulating macrophage chemotaxis (<a href="#B23" rid="B23" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399907">23</a>, <a href="#B37" rid="B37" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399874">37</a>). Finally, abdominal or white PVAT may secrete proinflammatory adipokines or cytokines. We have found that although white adipose (SAT and VAT) has greater expression of adiponectin, which has anti-inflammatory effects, it also has greater mRNA expression of adipokines that correlate with vascular disease (<em>Rbp4</em> and <em>Resistin</em>; <a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T1/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T1" rid-ob="ob-T1" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 1</span></a>; Refs. <a href="#B5" rid="B5" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399900">5</a>, <a href="#B19" rid="B19" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399848">19</a>). Furthermore, abdominal PVAT has been shown to secrete greater amounts of the chemokine MCP-1 than thoracic PVAT; our data also confirm that <em>Mcp-1</em>(<em>Ccl-2</em>) mRNA is increased in VAT but not thoracic PVAT after high-fat feeding (<a href="/pmc/articles/PMC3197360/table/T2/" target="table" class="fig-table-link figpopup" rid-figpopup="T2" rid-ob="ob-T2" co-legend-rid=""><span>Table 2</span></a>; Ref. <a href="#B32" rid="B32" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399854">32</a>). The comparatively greater diameter of white adipocytes in VAT and abdominal PVAT may be one factor responsible for increased <em>Mcp-1</em> transcription (<a href="#B15" rid="B15" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399825">15</a>, <a href="#B21" rid="B21" class=" bibr popnode tag_hotlink tag_tooltip" id="__tag_235399883">21</a>).</p><p id="__p44" class="p p-last">In summary, results from this present study demonstrate that PVAT surrounding the thoracic aorta is effectively BAT, as shown by light and electron microscopy, and full genome expression analysis. Thoracic PVAT and interscapular BAT are resistant to inflammation induced by 13 and 20 wk of HFD, as shown by reduced expression of immune cell-enriched genes, immunohistochemistry with macrophage markers, and FACS analysis for activated macrophages. This work provides an important mandate to study expression of BAT genes in human PVAT from patients with and without vascular disease. In light of the recent discovery of functional BAT in adult humans, and given the known beneficial metabolic and herein described anti-inflammatory properties of BAT, promotion of a BAT phenotype in the perivascular niche may have important effects in preventing vascular diseases such as hypertension and atherosclerosis.</p></div><div id="__sec19" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec19title">GRANTS</h2><p id="__p45" class="p p-first-last">We thank Hardy Kornfeld and the Millennium MD/PhD Program.</p></div><div id="__sec20" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="__sec20title">DISCLOSURES</h2><p id="__p46" class="p p-first-last">No conflicts of interest, financial or otherwise, are declared by the author(s).</p></div><div id="idm140357426133088" class="tsec sec"><h2 class="head no_bottom_margin" id="idm140357426133088title">ACKNOWLEDGMENTS</h2><div class="sec"><p id="__p47">We thank the Morphology Core Facility and the Genomics Core Facility of the UMASS Medical School Diabetes and Endocrinology Research Center (DK-32520) for assistance with histology and microarray analyses, respectively.
